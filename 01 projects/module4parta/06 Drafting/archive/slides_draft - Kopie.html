My Formulation of Tommy's Case

Formulation begins with what we observe, then asks why — and holds open the question of what we may not yet know.
Tommy, age 7, is referred because of aggression toward peers, impulsivity, poor frustration tolerance, and difficulty concentrating. He also shows emerging shame and withdrawal. His teachers describe his reactions as "over the top." A surface reading labels him difficult. A formulation asks what is producing this presentation — and whether the explanation is complete.

Tommy's difficulties are best understood through a formulation-based lens: cumulative adversity has plausibly shaped his developmental trajectory across biological, cognitive, social, and emotional domains. But these adversities should not be treated as a complete explanation. Co-occurring neurodevelopmental needs may have been overlooked. His current difficulties therefore represent elevated risk rather than fixed destiny — particularly in the presence of emerging protective relationships at school.

Central argument — held across all five slides
Applying a single causal frame prematurely — whether "bad behaviour" or "it's all ACEs" — risks misunderstanding Tommy and shaping support around the wrong hypothesis. This formulation is deliberately provisional.

Which Adversities Matter Most?

Tommy's vignette maps to 6+ ACE categories (Felitti et al., 1998). What matters analytically is not the count alone — it is where these adversities cluster, how they interact, and when they occurred in development.
Prenatal adversity
Maternal cortisol from domestic violence, low mood, and substance use crosses the placenta. Alcohol exposure creates plausible FASD risk. Poor nutrition and fragmented antenatal care compound this. The stress-response system begins calibrating before birth.
Disrupted and frightening caregiving
Mary's caregiving combined warmth with fear, emotional unavailability, and physical aggression. NICU separation removed consistent co-regulation at a sensitive period. No coherent relational pattern was available for Tommy to learn from.
Abuse and neglect
Direct exposure to verbal and physical aggression, unmet care needs, and periods of emotional abandonment — occurring throughout the developmental window where self-regulation, trust, and emotional cognition are forming. The child's own home became a source of threat.
Poverty and community stress
Chronic economic precarity, 6+ house moves, community-level adversity, and social isolation reduced Mary's buffering capacity. These are not background noise. They are the conditions that made consistent caregiving structurally difficult.
These adversities do not simply add up — they interact and compound. Each one further eroded the relational conditions Tommy needed at precisely the developmental periods when those conditions matter most.

How Might These Adversities Affect Development?

Not a diagnosis — a developmental hypothesis. Research links cumulative adversity to effects across all three domains. Crucially, they interact: each domain shapes the others.
The following represents plausible effects based on Tommy's profile and the research evidence — framed as hypothesis, not certainty. The key word throughout is consistent with.
Biological / Neurodevelopmental
A stress-response system shaped by threat
Chronic early adversity is associated with a stress-regulation system calibrated toward hypervigilance rather than exploration (Shonkoff & Garner, 2012). This is not a disorder — it is a biological adaptation to an environment of unpredictability. The research term is latent vulnerability: a sensitised system that responds to future stressors more intensely than it otherwise would. Prenatal alcohol exposure may have compounded this through additional effects on neurodevelopment.
Cognitive-Regulatory
Attention, impulse control, and learning under stress
Early chronic stress is consistently associated with difficulties in executive function, sustained attention, and impulse regulation — the capacities Tommy's teachers are observing. His difficulty focusing and acting before processing are consistent with this pathway. However, the same profile is also characteristic of ADHD and FASD — a critical point addressed in Slide 4.
Social-Emotional
Attachment, trust, and reading relationships
Inconsistent caregiving — warmth and fear from the same person — is associated with a disorganised attachment pattern (Main & Hesse, 1990): no coherent relational strategy, high activation under threat. Tommy's internal working model (Bowlby, 1969) appears to code adults as unpredictable and safety as fragile — which explains why neutral peer interactions register as hostile and why school relationships are so effortful.
These domains interact: stress biology disrupts cognitive regulation; impaired regulation disrupts social-emotional learning; disrupted attachment increases stress reactivity. The effects are systemic, not modular.
Developmental effects across all three domains — but does this explanation account for everything? → Slide 4 raises what an ACE-only formulation risks missing.

What Must Not Be Overlooked?

A formulation based solely on ACEs may be incomplete — and an incomplete formulation shapes the wrong response.

Tommy's attention difficulties, impulsivity, and social-communication challenges could reflect trauma-related dysregulation, a neurodevelopmental condition such as ADHD, effects of prenatal alcohol exposure (FASD), or — most likely — a combination of these. These presentations overlap clinically and cannot be distinguished without comprehensive assessment. The critical point is that no such assessment appears to have occurred.

No developmental assessment Mary systematically avoided healthcare appointments — she felt judged and withdrew. This disengagement, itself a consequence of the adversity and social conditions she experienced, has removed opportunities for developmental screening throughout Tommy's life.
Diagnostic overshadowing The curriculum (M4) explicitly notes that ACEs can delay recognition of autism or ADHD, and that maltreated children should receive full neurodevelopmental assessment. When one explanation is prominent, co-occurring needs tend to go unrecognised — a documented clinical risk.
Risk of misreading dysregulation as conduct Without this nuance, practitioners — including teachers — risk interpreting Tommy's dysregulation as deliberate misconduct. This shapes disciplinary rather than supportive responses, and risks deepening the very relational ruptures that are maintaining his difficulties.
A neuroaffirmative approach acknowledges that some of Tommy's difficulties may have a neurodevelopmental basis requiring support independent of trauma-informed intervention — and that both may be needed simultaneously.
Overshadowing acknowledged — does elevated risk mean a fixed outcome? → Slide 5 considers why the trajectory remains open to change.

Why Is Outcome Not Fixed?

The ACE–outcome relationship is dose-response and probabilistic — not deterministic. Tommy's trajectory carries elevated risk; it is not sealed.
Protective factors are already visible in the vignette
Mary's warm and responsive moments — Peek-A-Boo, babbling, outdoor play, expressing pride in Tommy — are serve-and-return interactions that build neural connections and begin revising his relational template, even within an inconsistent relationship. Calm and predictable teachers provide felt safety (Siegel, 2012): a regulated adult presence that keeps Tommy within a window of tolerance where PFC-mediated learning is possible. The LSA's emotion-naming sessions function as co-regulation: naming internal states activates language pathways, and over time builds Tommy's own regulatory capacity. The shift from hitting to verbal communication is direct evidence of this working. School meals provide physiological stability — the precondition for relational repair to take effect.
Resilience is environmental, not individual character
Research consistently shows that resilience in adversity-exposed children emerges from ordinary relational conditions — not exceptional individual traits (Masten, 2001). The mechanism is not Tommy's strength; it is the consistency of the adults around him. This reframes the question: not "is Tommy resilient enough?" but "can the conditions that are already producing change be sustained and extended?" — at school, in the family, and in the community structures that determine whether Mary herself can be a buffer.
Tommy's presentation is not the endpoint of a fixed trajectory. It is the current expression of a developmental pathway shaped by cumulative adversity — a pathway that remains open to change wherever consistent, attuned relationships are available. This formulation therefore points not toward labelling, but toward understanding — and toward the kinds of comprehensive assessment and relational support that can meaningfully shift the trajectory.

Evidence & Sources
Central argument
Formulation-based lens. ACEs plausibly shaped trajectory but are not the complete explanation. Co-occurring neurodevelopmental needs may be overlooked. Elevated risk, not fixed destiny.
Felitti et al. (1998)
ACE study foundation: 10 categories, dose-response relationship with health and mental health outcomes across the lifespan.
Formulation approach
Clinical formulation: a hypothesis about how difficulties developed and are maintained. Preferable to premature diagnosis in complex developmental presentations. Provisional and revisable.
Achenbach (1966)
Internalising/externalising taxonomy: both tracks visible in Tommy's presentation. Externalising (aggression, impulsivity) currently primary. Internalising (shame, withdrawal, hypervigilance) emerging. Both carry distinct long-term risk profiles.
Felitti et al. (1998)
Dose-response: 4+ ACEs significantly elevate risk. Tommy's profile: 6+ categories. But the count is not the analysis — the timing, clustering, and interaction of adversities is.
Felitti et al. (1998) — ACE clusters
Original 10 categories grouped analytically: prenatal adversity, disrupted caregiving, abuse/neglect, community/poverty stress. Each cluster selected for developmental impact at sensitive periods.
Bronfenbrenner (1979)
Ecological systems theory: required by the assignment. Informs the poverty/community cluster — macro-level adversity reduces parental buffering capacity, which is the mechanism by which community stress becomes relational harm.
Interaction, not addition
Key analytical point: adversities compound each other. Poverty makes consistent caregiving harder; DV increases maternal cortisol; substance use reduces emotional availability. The clusters are not independent.
Shonkoff & Garner (2012)
Toxic stress: sustained, unbuffered HPA activation. Latent vulnerability as the biological trace. Evidence-based — not deterministic language ("may be associated with," "consistent with").
Main & Hesse (1990)
Disorganised attachment: the specific pattern produced by frightening/frightened caregiving. No coherent strategy available. Most strongly associated with later social-emotional and regulatory difficulties.
Bowlby (1969)
Internal Working Model: cognitive-emotional template from primary caregiver, generalised to all relationships. Tommy's template — adults as unpredictable — is the social-emotional consequence of disorganised attachment.
Domain interaction
Stress biology → disrupts cognitive regulation → disrupts social-emotional development. The three domains are not parallel — they feed into each other. This is the "systemic" point on the slide.
Diagnostic overshadowing
Well-documented clinical phenomenon: a prominent presenting explanation (here, ACEs) draws attention away from co-occurring conditions. In trauma contexts: ADHD and FASD are most commonly overlooked.
FASD overlap
SIGN guidance (M4 W2 P29): FASD profile overlaps significantly with ADHD and with trauma responses — attention, impulsivity, social difficulties. Prenatal alcohol exposure is already established in Tommy's vignette.
Neuroaffirmative framing
Acknowledges neurodevelopmental differences as valid presentations requiring support independent of (or alongside) trauma-informed approaches. Not pathologising — accepting that some difficulties may not be solely trauma-derived.
Felitti et al. — probabilistic
Dose-response is not determinism: adversity elevates risk but does not guarantee disorder. The same research that documents risk also documents variability — the mediating variable is the protective environment.
Shonkoff — serve-and-return
Mary's warm moments as partial relational repair. Even inconsistent warmth has documented protective value when it produces responsive serve-and-return exchanges.
Siegel (2012)
Felt safety: regulated adult presence keeps child within window of tolerance. Teachers and LSA providing this is direct vignette evidence.
Masten (2001)
Ordinary magic: resilience as environmental output, not individual trait. The adults' consistency is the protective factor — not Tommy's character.
