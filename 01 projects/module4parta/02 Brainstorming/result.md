## 2. Source Material: Tommy Vignette

### Community level (macrosystem)

- **Economic collapse:** Major employer relocated → widespread unemployment, financial instability, uncertain incomes
- **Stress transmission:** Living on limited/uncertain income is a chronic community-level stressor
- **Substance use:** Highly prevalent in adults and teenagers; normalised from school age
- **Crime:** Area known for high crime rates
- **Tight-knit community:** Partial protective buffer; social capital present despite adversity

### Family level (mesosystem / microsystem)

**Prenatal period:**
- Domestic violence: Mary's partner volatile, verbally and physically abusive, worse when drunk
- Frequent house moves → inconsistent antenatal care across different health districts
- Mary's coping: alcohol and drug use during pregnancy; poor diet
- Maternal mood: persistently low during pregnancy
- Mary's own history: 15 foster placements; some carers neglectful or abusive; parents both young with traumatic childhoods → intergenerational trauma pattern

**Postnatal / early years:**
- Tommy born prematurely → NICU stay (low birth weight, breathing difficulties)
- Limited daily support for Mary post-discharge
- Mary felt judged at healthcare appointments → stopped attending health visitor and medical appointments for Tommy
- Mary's continued substance use and low mood → emotional unavailability; care needs not consistently met
- No qualifications → precarious, variable employment → Tommy regularly left with different, unfamiliar adults

**Mary's warm moments (present but inconsistent):**
- Peek-A-Boo and responding to babbling as if Tommy were speaking
- Outdoor play when safe to do so
- Expressing pride in Tommy; moments of feeling very loved

**Mary's overwhelmed moments:**
- Shouting "horrible things" at Tommy; sometimes hitting him
- Tommy left feeling scared, unsure, and increasingly ashamed

### Individual / child level (microsystem)

**Early development:**
- Premature birth; low birth weight; NICU; breathing difficulties
- Risk of FASD spectrum (alcohol exposure in utero)
- No consistent attachment figure in earliest months

**School age:**
- Attention and focus difficulties
- Impulsive behaviour
- "Over the top" emotional reactions
- Initial aggression: hitting other children when overwhelmed or feeling disrespected
- Responded quickly to emotion-naming sessions with LSA
- Received free school meals and vitamins

**Trajectory shift:**
- Learning Support Assistant: emotion coaching, naming feelings, verbal expression of experience
- Developed positive relationships with calm, predictable teachers
- Growing self-awareness and improved capacity for self-regulation over time

### Protective factors summary

| Factor | Level | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Mary's attuned moments (Peek-A-Boo) | Family | Serve-and-return; neural connection building |
| Tight-knit community | Community | Social capital, partial economic buffer |
| Free school meals and vitamins | Individual | Physical stability, nutrition |
| LSA emotion coaching | Individual | Emotion recognition, verbal regulation |
| Calm, predictable teachers | School | Corrective relational experience; felt safety |
| Tommy's neuroplasticity | Individual | Responsiveness to safe adult relationships |

### ACE count (Felitti et al. 1998 categories)

Tommy's case maps onto 6+ ACE categories: domestic violence, substance abuse in household, mental illness in household, emotional abuse, physical abuse, and neglect — plus extended models that include community-level adversity and housing instability.

## 3. Five Brainstorming Options

---

### Option 1 — Relational Disruption → Relational Repair

**Central thread:** Every ACE in Tommy's life disrupted the relational environment he needed for safe development. Every protective factor worked by partially restoring relational safety.

**Through-line logic:**
Every slide connects to the same question: *how did this disrupt (or restore) the relational environment?*

- Prenatal domestic violence disrupted Mary's capacity to be a safe relational base before birth
- NICU separation interrupted the earliest attachment window
- Mary's inconsistency → disorganised attachment: Tommy has no coherent relational strategy
- School: LSA and teachers = corrective relational experiences → partial restoration
- Conclusion: Tommy's trajectory depends entirely on what happens in the relational environment next

**Theoretical anchors:**
Bowlby (1969), Main & Hesse (1990) — disorganised attachment; Shonkoff / Harvard CoDC — toxic stress and absence of buffering adult; Siegel (2012) — felt safety, interpersonal neurobiology; Masten (2001) — resilience as access to ordinary relational supports; Bronfenbrenner (1979) — ecological frame

**Slide architecture:**
1. Tommy's ACEs at three ecological levels
2. Biological and neurological impact (HPA, amygdala, PFC, epigenetics)
3. Cognitive, social, emotional, and mental health effects
4. Protective factors and relational repair
5. ACE-outcome synthesis — dose-response, neuroplasticity, what comes next

**Why this earns A+:**
- Generates an ACE → mechanism → outcome → reversal chain for every claim (Analysis/Evaluation criterion)
- Prevents the presentation becoming a catalogue of facts
- All theoretical frameworks map elegantly onto one through-line
- The thread is explicit, memorable, and assessor-friendly
- Allows clear, relevant conclusions on every slide

**Risks:**
- Needs disciplined delivery to avoid sprawl
- Must not reduce to "relationships fix everything" — requires mechanistic precision

**Grade prediction: A+**

---

### Option 2 — Toxic Stress as the Unifying Mechanism

**Central thread:** Shonkoff's toxic stress model is the single biological mechanism that links all of Tommy's ACEs to his developmental outcomes. The key variable is the absence of a consistent buffering adult.

**Through-line logic:**
All ACEs produce the same upstream event: sustained cortisol elevation without a regulatory co-regulator. This triggers: HPA axis sensitisation → amygdala hyperactivation → PFC suppression → downstream cognitive, emotional, social, and health effects.

**Theoretical anchors:**
Shonkoff et al. (2012) — toxic stress framework; McEwen (2007) — allostatic load; Perry (2006) — neurosequential model; Meaney & Szyf (2005) — epigenetic programming; Selye (1956) — stress response origins

**Slide architecture:**
1. Tommy's ACEs as chronic stress activators
2. The toxic stress mechanism (HPA → brain → epigenetics)
3. Downstream outcomes (cognitive, emotional, social, mental health)
4. When buffering is present: serve-and-return, protective factors
5. Intervention window: neuroplasticity and stress-buffering relationships

**Why this earns A:**
- Single mechanistic frame gives strong coherence
- Excellent fit for biological/neurological content
- Less elegant fit for community-level ACEs and social outcomes
- Risks becoming overly biological; harder to address social/cognitive domains with equal depth

**Risks:**
- Social and community dimensions feel like add-ons rather than integral
- May appear to reduce everything to neurobiology
- Harder to address the full scope of the brief ("biological, social, cognitive, mental health")

**Grade prediction: A**

---

### Option 3 — Developmental Timing and Critical Windows

**Central thread:** The prenatal period and early childhood are the most sensitive developmental windows. Tommy's ACEs were front-loaded into precisely these critical periods, creating disproportionate and lasting biological and psychological effects.

**Through-line logic:**
Timing matters as much as dose. Prenatal HPA programming, NICU separation at the earliest attachment window, and maternal emotional unavailability in months 0–12 represent disruptions to the periods of maximum neurological plasticity and sensitivity. Later ACEs operate on an already-sensitised system.

**Theoretical anchors:**
Knudsen (2004) — critical and sensitive periods in neuroscience; Barker (1990) — developmental origins of health and disease; Meaney & Szyf (2005) — epigenetic programming; Shonkoff — prenatal toxic stress; Main & Hesse — attachment window

**Why this earns A/B:**
- Scientifically sophisticated; excellent for demonstrating knowledge (30% criterion)
- Strong fit for biological/neurological slides
- Difficult to integrate protective factors naturally (they came later; how do they connect to a "timing" frame?)
- Risks being heavy on biology; Communication criterion may suffer if slides read like a lecture

**Grade prediction: A/B**

---

### Option 4 — ACE Dose-Response: Adversity, Trajectory, and the Limits of Determinism

**Central thread:** Felitti et al.'s (1998) dose-response relationship shows that Tommy's 6+ ACE categories place him at 4–12× elevated risk for poor outcomes. But dose is real without being deterministic: neuroplasticity and protective factors can alter trajectory.

**Through-line logic:**
Use the ACE pyramid (Felitti/Anda) as the organising spine. Show Tommy moving down through the pyramid: adversity → disrupted neurodevelopment → social/emotional impairment → early risk behaviours (aggression). Then show how the pyramid can be interrupted at each level.

**Theoretical anchors:**
Felitti et al. (1998) — original ACE study; Anda et al. (2006) — dose-response evidence; Bronfenbrenner — ecological risk accumulation; Masten — resilience

**Why this earns B/A:**
- Directly uses the core ACE literature (strong Knowledge criterion)
- Good for showing breadth of risk knowledge
- Weaker as an Analysis/Evaluation vehicle — dose-response is correlational, not mechanistic; lacks the ACE → mechanism → conclusion chain the rubric rewards
- Can slide toward being a list of ACEs with risk statistics

**Grade prediction: B/A**

---

### Option 5 — Intergenerational Transmission of Adversity

**Central thread:** Tommy's ACEs did not begin with Tommy. They began with Mary's own ACE-saturated childhood (15 foster placements, abuse, neglect, traumatised parents) — and transmitted through biological, psychological, and social pathways to affect Tommy before he was born.

**Through-line logic:**
Mary's unresolved trauma → disorganised attachment in her own childhood → limited mentalisation capacity → inconsistent, sometimes frightening caregiving → Tommy's disorganised attachment and ACEs. Community deprivation as the context that intensified transmission at every level.

**Theoretical anchors:**
Fonagy et al. — mentalisation and intergenerational transmission; Main & Hesse — disorganised attachment and unresolved loss; Fraiberg (1975) — "ghosts in the nursery"; Meaney & Szyf — epigenetic transmission

**Why this earns A/B:**
- Sophisticated and analytically distinctive
- Shows understanding beyond the vignette surface
- Risks going beyond evidence in the vignette (we don't have Mary's biological outcomes; we cannot verify epigenetic transmission from the case data)
- Harder to cover Tommy's full developmental picture (biological, cognitive, social) in 5 slides
- Risk of spending too much time on Mary rather than Tommy

**Grade prediction: A/B**

## 5. Recommendation

**Choose Option 1: Relational Disruption → Relational Repair.**

The through-line "every ACE disrupted the relational environment Tommy needed; every protective factor partially restored it" is the only option that satisfies all three rubric criteria simultaneously:

- **Analysis/Evaluation (50%):** Generates an ACE → mechanism → outcome → reversal chain for every claim. Every slide earns marks on the highest criterion.
- **Knowledge (30%):** All canonical theoretical frameworks (Bronfenbrenner, Shonkoff, Main & Hesse, Siegel, Masten, Felitti) map onto a relational narrative without strain.
- **Communication (20%):** A single unifying question makes the presentation coherent, memorable, and easy to follow — exactly what "slides echo what is being said" requires.

The other options are solid, but they either front-load biology at the expense of social/cognitive breadth (Options 2, 3), lack mechanistic depth (Option 4), or risk going beyond the evidence available in the vignette (Option 5).

Option 1 also leaves room for a genuinely analytical closing slide: Tommy's story is not finished. What happens in the relational environment next — at individual, family, and community level — will determine whether neuroplasticity enables recovery or whether risk compounds.
