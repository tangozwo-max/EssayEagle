## The idea in one line
Resist **over-medicalising** Tommy's ACE-related struggles (labels/disorders/"therapy for everything"); instead
respond with a **trauma-informed "what happened to you, not what's wrong with you" / reparation** approach that
repairs his context (stability, belonging, relationships).

## Two halves of the same argument

### 1. The caution — over-medicalisation / "therapeutic ethos"
Critics warn that schooling's growing focus on mental health can tip into a *therapeutic ethos* that pathologises
ordinary distress, multiplies vague "disorders," and can **diminish resilience and autonomy**. For Tommy, the risk
is reading every ACE-linked behaviour as a clinical symptom to be diagnosed/medicated, rather than an
understandable adaptation to adversity.
- **Source:** Ecclestone, K. and Hayes, D. (2019) *The Dangerous Rise of Therapeutic Education*. 2nd edn. London:
  Routledge. (1st edn 2009.)
- Curriculum echo: Montessori argued "many issues traditionally seen as **medical were educational**" (P64).

### 2. The constructive flip — "reparation" / formulation / trauma-informed
The historical counter-model: treat at-risk children **not as the problem, but as individuals who have faced
hardship**, and *repair* their environment — stability, belonging, routine (Aichhorn's "personal drawers,"
regular meals). This is the ancestor of today's **trauma-informed, attachment-aware** practice and of
**formulation over diagnosis** ("what happened to you," not "what's wrong with you").
- **Source (historical):** Aichhorn, A. (1935) *Wayward Youth*. New York: Viking Press. (orig. 1925; principle of
  **reparation**.) Secondary/curriculum source: Winship, G.
- **Source (modern equivalent — formulation):** the 5Ps formulation model — predisposing-factor question is
  literally *"What has happened to the young person in the past?"* (Macneil et al.; Anna Freud Centre; Kempen
  "a road map of how they came to be where they are") — `M4 W4 P46`.
- **Source (trauma & the brain — directly Tommy-relevant):** latent vulnerability / threat–reward–memory systems,
  and recovery with the right support (Anna Freud Centre; Cross et al.) — `M4 W2 P32`.

## Why it fits the Tommy presentation
- **Critical-evaluation criterion (highest weight):** lets you weigh a strength against a limitation — ACE-awareness
  is vital, *but* avoid the over-medicalising trap — and reach a defensible position (formulation/reparation).
- **Individual → family → community** mapping the brief asks for: reparation is precisely about repairing the
  *systems* around the child, not just treating the child.
- **Protective factors / resilience:** the "reparation" response *is* the protective-factor story (belonging,
  stable relationships, routine), which the brief explicitly wants.

## Placement decision (2026-06-08)
The deck's spine ("Relational Disruption → Relational Repair") already *is* the reparation idea — Slides 4–5.
What's missing is its critical-evaluation counterweight. Split the two points:

- **Over-medicalisation / diagnostic overshadowing → Slide 3 (school-age behaviours).** This is where Tommy's
  aggression + inattention appear — the exact behaviours liable to be fast-tracked to ADHD/ODD/conduct-disorder
  labels. One evaluative sentence amplifies the claim already on that slide ("his behaviours were not choices, but
  the output of a nervous system") and sets up the Slide-4 repair turn:
  *"Tommy's aggression and inattention read as ADHD or conduct disorder — but these are adaptations to toxic stress,
  not primary disorders. The risk is diagnostic overshadowing: medicalising the symptom and missing the story. A
  formulation lens asks 'what happened to you?' before 'what's wrong with you?'"* → cite Ecclestone & Hayes.
  (If the evolving deck has a dedicated complexity/overshadowing slide, that's an equally good home — same logic.)
- **"ACEs are probabilistic, not deterministic" → the ACE slide (Slides 1–2, with Felitti).** A *prediction* caveat,
  distinct from over-medicalising. The deck already says "outcomes are not deterministic" on Slide 5 — pull that
  principle forward to where the ACEs are introduced. Support: McCrory & Viding (latent vulnerability) — recovery
  is possible with the right support.
- **NOT the opening "presenting issues" slide** — too early; no behaviours yet to anchor the caution.

Rubric rationale: this is the highest-weight move (critical evaluation), and it tightens the Slide 3→4 flow rather
