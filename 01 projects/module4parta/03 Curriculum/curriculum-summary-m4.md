## M4 W1 — Attachment and Development

### Key Concepts
- **Attachment (Bowlby, 1969)**: Evolutionary drive to seek proximity to caregivers under threat; primary caregiver relationship is the template for all subsequent relationships
- **Stages of attachment**: Pre-attachment (0-6 wks) → Attachment-in-making (6 wks-7 mths) → Clear-cut attachment (7-24 mths) → Goal-corrected partnership (24+ mths)
- **Attachment types**: Secure (55% population); Insecure-avoidant (22%); Insecure-ambivalent/resistant (8%); Disorganised (15%) — disorganised highest risk for MH problems
- **Disorganised attachment (Main & Hesse, 1990)**: Caregiver is simultaneously source of fear and source of comfort; child has no coherent strategy; associated with highest rates of later psychopathology
- **Internal Working Model (IWM, Bowlby)**: Mental scripts based on early caregiving experience; generalise to all future relationships; set expectation of whether adults are reliable
  - Secure IWM: "Adults are available; I am worthy of care"
  - Insecure/Disorganised IWM: "Adults are unpredictable; I cannot depend on others" → hyperactivation as only available strategy
- **Mentalizing (Fonagy, M4 W1 P02)**: Ability to understand mental states — own and others'; developed through attuned caregiving; impaired mentalizing = difficulty interpreting others' intentions, reading faces, predicting consequences of own behaviour → neutral cues misread as hostile
- **Anna Freud Centre (curriculum)**: IWM described as "scripts" based on early unpredictable caregiving that a child brings into school and all new relationships

### Key Terms
- **Serve-and-return (Shonkoff)**: Back-and-forth interactions between caregiver and infant that build neural connections; each exchange strengthens social-emotional architecture
- **Co-regulation**: Caregiver providing an external regulatory framework for child's stress response; precondition for child's self-regulation development
- **Felt safety (Siegel, 2012)**: Child's subjective sense of being safe with a caregiver; activates learning and social engagement rather than survival mode
- **Still Face Experiment (Tronick)**: Demonstrates infant's acute sensitivity to caregiver emotional withdrawal; 2-minute withdrawal produces measurable distress

### Brain Development (P06)
- **Synaptic pruning**: Ages 2-10; strengthens used pathways, eliminates unused ones — experience-dependent
- **90% brain development by age 6**: Critical window for relational and emotional architecture
- **PFC vs ventral striatum**: PFC (executive function, impulse control) develops last; ventral striatum (reward/risk-taking) develops earlier → adolescence = elevated risk-taking, impulsivity
- **Shonkoff quote**: "The quality of a child's earliest experiences shapes the architecture of the developing brain" — early relationships are literally structuring neural circuits

### Curriculum-Assignment Links (Tommy)
- Tommy's IWM = key explanatory mechanism for school behaviours
- Mentalizing impairment explains hostile attribution bias and peer conflict
- Co-regulation by LSA = evidence-based mechanism of change

## M4 W2 — Risk Factors, Resilience, NDCs, and Trauma

### Risk Factor Framework (P18-P20)
- **Individual risk factors**: Genetics, temperament, NDCs, physical health, cognitive capacity
- **Family risk factors**: Parental MH, DV, substance use, poverty, parenting style, bereavement
- **Community risk factors**: Deprivation, crime, lack of services, discrimination, community violence
- **Risk is cumulative**: ACEs operate through dose-response relationship — each additional ACE compounds risk

### Domestic Abuse as Risk (P22)
- Independent risk factor for infant/child MH (beyond maternal depression)
- **Prenatal**: Increased miscarriage, low birth weight, preterm birth
- **Infancy**: Toxic stress affects HPA development; disrupts caregiver-infant attachment
- **School age**: Emotional dysregulation, aggressive behaviour, peer difficulties, school avoidance
- **Toxic stress syndrome**: Chronic exposure without support network; contributes to anxiety/depression; affects lifelong relationship patterns

### Protective Factors and Resilience (P24)
- **Ungar & Theron (curriculum)**: Resilience is NOT an innate trait — it depends on environmental resources
  - "Focus on improving the child's environment so that resilience is unnecessary"
  - Resilience is contentious term — many children's behaviour under adversity is adaptive not resilient
- **Children's limited agency**: Resilience in children is complex because they cannot control their environment
- **Practical implication**: Protective factors work by modifying the environment, not the child; interventions should target systems around the child
- **Masten (2001) "Ordinary magic"**: Resilience processes are not extraordinary; they are ordinary relational processes that become visible against a backdrop of adversity

### Neurodevelopmental Conditions (NDCs) and ACEs (P26-P30)
- **"Double jeopardy" (Gajwani & Minnis, curriculum P28)**: ACEs increase risk of NDCs; NDCs increase vulnerability to further adversity
- **Maltreated children are nearly 10x more likely to have 3+ NDCs** (curriculum data)
- **Zhou et al. (curriculum P28)**: ACEs significantly delay ADHD and autism diagnosis — children with trauma histories wait longer for neurodevelopmental assessment
- **Curriculum directive (P28 verbatim)**: "Maltreated children should receive full neurodevelopmental assessments" — not assuming ACE-driven behaviour is purely relational
- **Diagnostic overshadowing**: Risk of attributing all symptoms to trauma/abuse when an NDC may co-exist

### FASD (P29)
- **Variable presentation**: Domains affected include attention, impulse regulation, social cognition, hyperactivity, memory, adaptive behaviour
- **SIGN guidance (curriculum)**: Underdiagnosed partly because of "overshadowing of prenatal alcohol exposure by other causes of neurodevelopmental delays"
- **Continuum**: Not all children with prenatal alcohol exposure develop FASD; but any prenatal alcohol exposure is risk
- **Tommy relevance**: Mary's substance use during pregnancy = FASD risk; same presentation profile as ADHD

### Trauma and Neurobiological Development (P31-P32)
- **Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs, Felitti et al., 1998)**: 10 categories; dose-response relationship with MH outcomes; 6+ categories = dramatically elevated lifetime risk
- **Toxic stress model (Shonkoff & Garner, 2012)**: Three types: Positive (brief, manageable), Tolerable (buffered by a caring adult), Toxic (sustained, unbuffered)
- **Latent vulnerability (curriculum P32 — formal definition)**: "Increased risk of future MH disorders in children who have experienced abuse or neglect, even in the absence of current symptoms"
  - Affects memory systems, threat-detection systems, reward systems
  - Sensitises brain to future adversity — subclinical now, may manifest later
  - Not immediately visible as disorder; requires longitudinal monitoring
- **Sensitive periods (curriculum P32)**: Extended by unpredictable environments — windows of heightened neuroplasticity remain open longer under chronic stress; means intervention can still work, but also means continued adversity continues to harm
- **HPA axis reactivity**: Calibrated to environmental threat level; children in chronic threat develop hyperreactive HPA axis → cortisol dysregulation → amygdala hyperactivation → hippocampal effects → PFC underdevelopment
- **Interoception (curriculum P32)**: Impaired body-sensing under chronic stress; children with trauma histories may have difficulty reading their own physiological states — relevant to emotional dysregulation
- **Neurodivergent note (curriculum P32)**: Children with sensory processing differences or NDCs may have additional interoception challenges

### Key Terms W2
- **HPA axis**: Hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis; cortisol-producing stress response system; calibrated in early childhood
- **Amygdala hyperactivation**: Chronic threat experience → over-sensitive threat detector → false positives
- **Hippocampal effects**: Stress hormones affect memory consolidation and contextual memory
- **Intergenerational transmission**: ACE history in parents (especially mother) predicts ACE exposure in children through parenting capacity, relationship patterns, mental health

## M4 W3 — Continuum of Need and Interventions

### Continuum of Need Framework (P35)
Four levels of provision (widely used in UK health, education, social care):

| Level | Descriptor | Example for Tommy |
|---|---|---|
| **Universal** | Available to all; no assessment needed; promotes wellbeing, prevents escalation | Free school meals + vitamins; calm school environment; PSHE |
| **Selective/Targeted** | For those with additional needs or ACE risk; no diagnosis needed; early help | Named worker; emotion coaching; parental support (Mary) |
| **Indicated** | For those showing early signs; targeted early intervention | LSA emotion-naming sessions; trauma-informed classroom |
| **Specialist** | Severe/complex/enduring needs; requires diagnosis; multi-agency | CAMHS referral; residential support |

- **Movement between levels**: Individuals move up and down as circumstances change
- **Early intervention principle**: Intervening at lower levels prevents escalation to specialist need

### Mental Health Promotion and Early Intervention (P36, P39-P41)
- **Prevention > treatment**: Most MH disorders begin before adulthood — earlier intervention has greater impact per resource invested
- **Psychoeducation**: Key component at all levels; families need to understand what is happening
- **Sure Start (case study P40)**: Community-based early years programme; evidence base for improvement of parenting practices, child school readiness, reduction of abuse
- **Family interventions**: Evidence strongest for parenting skills at lower levels of the continuum

## M4 W4 — Formulation, Disorders, and Treatments

### Formulation-Based Approach (P45-P46)
- **Assessment → Formulation → Intervention → Evaluation**: Core clinical process cycle
- **Formulation**: Making sense of a person's difficulties using a combination of theory, research, and clinical data; NOT a diagnosis — a hypothesis about causes, mechanisms, and maintaining factors
- **5Ps model**: Presenting problems / Predisposing factors / Precipitating factors / Perpetuating factors / Protective factors
- **Kempen quote (curriculum P46)**: "A formulation is a road map of somebody's difficulties" — helps choose intervention, not just label
- **Formulation vs diagnosis**: Formulation is individualised and contextual; diagnosis is categorical; curriculum explicitly prefers formulation approach for children with complex histories
- **Cultural acknowledgement (P47)**: Context including culture, language, faith, family structure must inform formulation

### Common MH Disorders in C&A (P49-P54)
**Depression (P50)**
- Prevalence: Low in children, sharp increase in adolescence (especially females)
- **Presentation in younger children**: More somatic complaints; irritability rather than sadness; impact seen in academics, social, family
- **Adolescent-specific symptoms (Rice et al.)**: Irritable mood, weight/appetite changes, insomnia/hypersomnia, fatigue
- **Risk of recurrence**: ~50% of adolescents with depression have another episode; 3x more likely to have adult depression
- **Psychosocial impacts**: Lower educational attainment, welfare dependence, interpersonal difficulties
- **ACE link**: ACEs dramatically increase risk; dose-response confirmed; Tommy's profile = elevated depression risk

**Anxiety (P51)**
- Often co-morbid with depression and behavioural disorders
- Curriculum link: Latent vulnerability + threat-detection hyperactivation → anxiety as downstream effect

**Oppositional Defiant Disorder / Conduct Disorder (P53)**
- **ODD curriculum directive**: "Should not be made unless there has been a thorough and multidisciplinary assessment... considering the child's context and different diagnoses and explanations"
- **Linked to**: NDCs, attachment insecurity, trauma history — ODD presentation is frequently a symptom not a disorder
- **Tommy risk**: ACEs + attachment disruption = elevated ODD/CD profile; but diagnosis requires multidisciplinary assessment eliminating NDC/trauma explanations first
- **Internalising vs externalising**: Tommy shows both — aggression/impulsivity (externalising), emerging shame/social withdrawal (internalising)
- **Behavioural interventions**: Parenting skills training has strongest evidence at lower levels

### Treatments (P55-P61)
- **CBT**: Adapted for children — concrete, visual, shorter sessions, include family; evidence for anxiety and depression
- **Psychodynamic therapy**: For adolescents with relational difficulties; insight into relational patterns
- **Systemic family therapy**: Whole-family approach; aligns with Tommy's need for Mary to be supported
- **DBT**: For adolescents with self-harm; teaches emotion regulation, distress tolerance, interpersonal effectiveness
- **Trauma-informed care**: Not a separate therapy but a framework — understand behaviour as adaptive response to threat; avoid retraumatisation; prioritise felt safety
