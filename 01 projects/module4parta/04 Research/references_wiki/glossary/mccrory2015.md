## Key Claims
- Childhood maltreatment creates 'latent vulnerability': neurocognitive adaptations to early adversity that remain dormant but increase psychiatric risk under later stress.
- Altered threat processing (heightened vigilance) is one exemplar system — adaptive in a maltreating environment but maladaptive long-term.
- Reward processing and memory systems are also candidate latent vulnerability domains.
- Latent vulnerability can be operationalised as a third intervention point: after risk identification but before psychiatric disorder emerges (indicated prevention).
- Explains why maltreatment survivors may appear healthy until exposed to specific stressors that activate the vulnerability.

## Numerical Findings
- Theoretical paper; no primary data
- Draws on neurocognitive literature showing heightened amygdala reactivity and altered prefrontal regulation in maltreated children

## Study Design
Conceptual/theoretical paper; reviews neurocognitive evidence and proposes a three-stage preventive psychiatry model (risk identification → latent vulnerability indexing → indicated prevention).

## Limitation
No primary empirical data; causal direction of neurocognitive changes not established; operationalisation of latent vulnerability in clinical practice remains underdeveloped at time of publication.
