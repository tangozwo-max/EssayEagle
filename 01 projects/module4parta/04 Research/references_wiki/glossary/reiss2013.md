## Key Claims
- Lower socioeconomic status is consistently associated with higher rates of mental health problems in children and adolescents across multiple studies.
- The SES–mental health gradient is evident across diverse mental health outcomes (internalising, externalising, and general difficulties).
- Associations are robust across different SES indicators (income, parental education, occupational status).
- Findings hold across multiple countries and measurement approaches, suggesting a universal mechanism.
- SES operates through multiple pathways: material deprivation, psychosocial stress, parenting quality, and reduced access to services.

## Numerical Findings
- Systematic review; specific effect sizes depend on included studies
- Consistent gradient: low-SES children show higher mental health problem prevalence across all outcomes reviewed

## Study Design
Systematic review; searched multiple databases for studies examining SES and mental health outcomes in children/adolescents; multiple SES indicators and mental health measures included.

## Limitation
Heterogeneity of SES measures and mental health outcomes across included studies limits meta-analytic synthesis; mostly cross-sectional studies; limited longitudinal evidence at time of publication.
