## Key Claims
- Cumulative childhood risk (multiple co-occurring risk factors) is associated with elevated internalising and externalising mental health problems in children.
- Child care quality acts as a moderating or compensatory factor: higher-quality child care partially buffers the effect of cumulative risk on children's mental health development.
- The relationship between risk exposure and mental health follows a dose-response pattern — each additional risk factor compounds adverse outcomes.
- Findings are drawn from the Norwegian Mother, Father and Child Cohort Study (MoBa) linked with the Medical Birth Registry of Norway (MBRN), providing high statistical power and population-level coverage.

## Numerical Findings
- MoBa + MBRN registry linkage — one of the largest birth cohorts globally (>100,000 pregnancies recruited 1999–2008)
- Published May 2023; *Journal of Applied Developmental Psychology*, 86, Article 101546
- Open access (CC BY 4.0)

## Study Design
Large-scale prospective birth cohort using Norwegian national registry data (MoBa + MBRN). Examines cumulative childhood risk and child care quality as predictors of children's mental health trajectories. Register linkage allows control for a wide range of confounders.

## Limitation
Norwegian population sample — generalisability to populations with different welfare state structures (e.g., UK, US) requires caution. Observational design precludes causal inference. Child care quality measured via structural proxies rather than direct observation in registry data.
