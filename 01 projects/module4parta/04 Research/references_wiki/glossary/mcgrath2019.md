## Key Claims
- Family-based CBT with ERP is highly effective for childhood OCD — large effect sizes for OCD symptom reduction at posttest (g=1.00) and follow-up (g=1.69).
- Family accommodation (FA) is critically implicated in OCD maintenance and treatment outcomes: FA is significantly correlated with OCD severity and poorer treatment response, and may mediate OCD symptom outcomes.
- The number of family factors targeted in treatment significantly moderates FA outcomes (z=2.21, p=0.03): the greater the number of family variables addressed, the greater the reduction in accommodation — but this moderation did NOT apply to OCD symptom outcomes on C/Y-BOCS.
- 60% of patients in the largest OCD RCT (POTS) failed to achieve remission on ERP-based CBT alone, highlighting the need for family variable targeting.
- 99% of parents report participating in at least one accommodation behaviour; 77.1% report daily FA — making FA near-universal in families of children with OCD.
- FA is negatively reinforcing for parents: accommodating temporarily reduces both child distress and parental anxiety, explaining its persistence despite its long-term harm.

## Numerical Findings
- k=37 eligible studies; N=1,727 OCD participants
- Pooled effect sizes at posttest: OCD symptoms g=1.00; FA g=not specified (large)
- At follow-up: OCD symptoms g=1.69; FA g=1.98
- POTS (largest OCD RCT): 60% of ERP-CBT participants failed to achieve remission
- 99% of parents: ≥1 FA behaviour; 77.1% daily FA (Flessner et al. 2011)
- Moderator for FA outcomes: number of family factors targeted (z=2.21, p=0.03)

## Study Design
Systematic review and meta-analysis; extensive literature search for eligible family-based CBT studies for childhood OCD; moderator analyses examined degree of parental involvement and number of family factors targeted; primary outcomes: OCD symptom severity (C/Y-BOCS) and FA measures.

## Limitation
Heterogeneity across studies in FA measurement instruments and family involvement operationalisation; publication bias possible; follow-up periods varied; relatively modest k for moderator analyses; most studies from Western clinical settings; no formal comparison to non-family-based control conditions in some studies.
