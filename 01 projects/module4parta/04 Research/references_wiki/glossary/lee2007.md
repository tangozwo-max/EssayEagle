## Key Claims
- Maltreatment does not cause aggression directly but via three mediating pathways: (a) overactivated cognitive biases (especially threat-detection); (b) compromised emotion regulation; (c) neurobiological hardwiring of these biases as the prefrontal cortex (PFC) develops.
- Physical abuse specifically generates a hostile attributional bias and hypervigilance for threat cues, producing reactive aggression; neglect generates emotion-regulation deficits due to absence of emotional learning opportunities, also producing aggression via a different route.
- Physical and neglect subtypes produce similar externalising outcomes but via distinct mechanisms — this has different implications for intervention design.
- Neglect may have more lasting negative impact on cognitive, academic, and social functioning than physical abuse, particularly in childhood and early adolescence (Erickson & Egeland, 2002).
- Executive functions (EFs) — especially inhibition, attentional flexibility, and planning — continue developing into adolescence and are specifically vulnerable to disruption by maltreatment-induced threat biases.
- Maltreated children show heightened vigilance to aggressive stimuli from preschool age, interfering with normative attentional and executive development.

## Numerical Findings
- Theoretical review; no primary data
- Cites evidence that 75% of violent/delinquent adults report childhood trauma
- Nationally representative US study (Finkelhor et al.): physical abuse and neglect had strongest associations with anger and aggression — stronger than sexual victimisation or witnessing family violence
- Neglected Romanian adoptees: EF deficits on attentional and social tasks (Chugani et al., 2001)
- Children aged 5–12 with neglect history: decreased verbal learning capacity in registration and retrieval

## Study Design
Narrative theoretical review; proposes a conceptual model linking maltreatment type → neurocognitive mechanism (threat bias vs. emotion-regulation deficit) → aggression outcome, integrating cognitive, developmental, and neurobiological literatures.

## Limitation
Explicitly speculative and theoretical; hypotheses intended to generate future empirical research rather than support firm conclusions; does not distinguish severity or chronicity of maltreatment; age-of-onset effects not adequately addressed; SIP literature (Dodge's model) integrated but differentiation from EF deficits remains underdeveloped.
