## Key Claims
- Early childhood (0–5 years) is a critical and sensitive period for brain development; experiences during this time have lasting impacts on cognitive, social-emotional, and health outcomes.
- The brain's architecture is built through a continuous process of gene–environment interaction; early relationships are a primary architect.
- Toxic stress (strong, frequent, or prolonged adversity without adequate buffering) disrupts the architecture of the developing brain.
- Serve-and-return interactions (responsive caregiving) are essential for building neural connections for learning, behaviour, and health.
- Comprehensive National Research Council synthesis; represents scientific consensus across neuroscience, developmental psychology, and child health.

## Numerical Findings
- NRC synthesis report; integrates findings from hundreds of studies across disciplines
- Established the 90% brain development by age 5 estimate widely used in policy

## Study Design
National Research Council systematic evidence synthesis; commissioned report; integrates neuroscience, developmental psychology, paediatrics, and social science.

## Limitation
No PDF available; 2001 publication — neuroscience has advanced considerably; some specific estimates have been refined; 'critical period' framing has been nuanced by later plasticity research.
