## Key Claims
- Social conditions (especially socioeconomic status) are 'fundamental causes' of disease because they determine access to resources that protect against risk factors across time.
- Even when specific risk factor mechanisms are addressed, SES inequalities in health persist because resources are redirected to the next available risk pathway.
- The SES–health gradient is reproduced across all causes of death — suggesting a mechanism above specific diseases.
- Resources include money, knowledge, power, prestige, and beneficial social connections — all more available to high-SES individuals.
- Addressing fundamental social causes (not just proximate risk factors) is necessary for durable health equity.

## Numerical Findings
- Theoretical paper; no primary data
- Evidence drawn from mortality statistics showing persistent SES gradients across decades and diseases

## Study Design
Theoretical/sociological paper; proposes fundamental cause theory; analyses mortality data to demonstrate persistence of SES health gradients despite medical advances.

## Limitation
Theoretical — the mechanism by which SES 'causes' disease is not fully specified at the biological level; the model is primarily sociological and does not address individual-level variation; published 1995, though highly influential and still widely cited.
