## Key Claims
- ACEs (Adverse Childhood Experiences) are strongly and dose-dependently associated with adult health risk behaviours and leading causes of death.
- The more ACE categories a person experienced, the greater their risk for poor adult outcomes (graded dose-response relationship).
- ACEs are common: two-thirds of the study population reported at least one ACE; more than one in five reported three or more.
- ACE categories include: emotional, physical, and sexual abuse; emotional and physical neglect; household dysfunction (domestic violence, substance abuse, mental illness, parental separation, incarceration).
- ACEs in childhood fundamentally shape adult physical and mental health through biological embedding during critical developmental periods.

## Numerical Findings
- n = 17,337 adult members of Kaiser Permanente health plan; predominantly middle-class, white, college-educated
- ACE score strongly predicts adult risk for heart disease, cancer, stroke, liver disease, COPD, depression, suicide attempts, substance abuse
- Graded dose-response: each additional ACE category increases risk; ACE score ≥4 doubles or quadruples risk for many outcomes

## Study Design
Large cross-sectional epidemiological study; retrospective ACE assessment via questionnaire; linkage to medical records for health outcomes; regression analyses controlling for demographics.

## Limitation
Retrospective self-report of childhood adversity; predominantly white, middle-class, insured sample (underestimates ACE prevalence/impact in more disadvantaged groups); cross-sectional design limits causal inference.
