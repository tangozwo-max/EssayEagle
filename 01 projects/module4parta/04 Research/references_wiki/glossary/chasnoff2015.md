## Key Claims
- 86.5% of children with FASD in this clinical sample had never been correctly diagnosed or had been misdiagnosed prior to referral.
- 80.1% of children with FASD criteria had never received any prenatal alcohol-related diagnosis (missed diagnosis rate).
- FASD is frequently misidentified as ADHD, behavioural disorder, or attachment disorder — masking the neurodevelopmental aetiology.
- Missed and incorrect diagnosis delays appropriate intervention, compounding functional impairment in foster and adopted children.
- Neurocognitive damage indicators (learning disorders, communication disorders, intellectual disability) were also under-recognised.

## Numerical Findings
- n = 547 children assessed at a children's mental health centre
- 156 met FASD criteria; 125 (80.1%) had never been diagnosed; 31 previously identified (10 misdiagnosed within spectrum)
- Missed diagnosis rate: 80.1%; misdiagnosis rate: 6.4%

## Study Design
Retrospective analysis of comprehensive multidisciplinary diagnostic evaluations; McNemar's test for dependent proportions to compare pre/post-assessment diagnosis rates.

## Limitation
Single specialist centre (referral bias); retrospective design; sample limited to foster/adopted children who reached specialist assessment (iceberg effect — may underrepresent community prevalence).
