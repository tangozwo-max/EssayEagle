## Key Claims
- Low SES has consistent, bidirectional, and developmentally stable effects on child physical and psychosocial health, operating from the prenatal stage onwards.
- Three mediating pathways connect SES to health: structural/material factors (housing, nutrition, healthcare access), behavioural factors (diet, activity, smoking), and psychosocial factors (parental wellbeing, stress, warmth).
- Child-based SES indicators (subjective perceived social position; school career trajectory) independently predict health outcomes beyond parent-based SES, and are especially strongly associated with self-reported health and life satisfaction.
- Financial instability (alternating between poverty and non-poverty across the first 10 years) confers the highest risk for behavioural difficulties at age 11, even exceeding persistent poverty — reflecting that unpredictability itself is harmful.
- Behavioural interventions targeting individual family practices (nutrition supplements, parenting skills) show limited long-term effects; area-level and structural interventions are needed to address SES-related health inequalities.

## Numerical Findings
- Narrative review of studies published 2018–2020 (Current Opinion format — narrative, not systematic)
- Multiple family-based SES indicators (income, maternal education, occupational composite) linked to higher BMI in children
- Girls show stronger BMI/SES associations than boys in some studies
- Low parental education + high-crime area: both contributed independently to higher BMI in 7-year-olds
- Financial instability group (poverty alternating) had higher rate of behavioural difficulties at age 11 than permanently poor group
- Lower SES linked to: cognitive ability, mood problems, disordered gambling, lower self-control, risk-taking, bullying

## Study Design
Narrative review (Current Opinion format); covers physical health (obesity, cardiovascular risk, injuries, myopia), psychosocial health (quality of life, behavioural difficulties, cognitive abilities), resilience, and intervention evidence, drawing from cohort studies and cross-sectional studies primarily from Western countries.

## Limitation
Narrative (not systematic) review; high risk of selection bias; study-level heterogeneity in SES measurement (composite scores, area-level, parent-based, child-based); intervention effect sizes not meta-analytically pooled; predominantly Western European and North American literature — generalisability limited.
