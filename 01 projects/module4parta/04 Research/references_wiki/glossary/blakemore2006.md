## Key Claims
- Adolescence is a period of significant neural restructuring: grey matter decreases (pruning) and white matter increases (myelination) continue through puberty and into early adulthood.
- The prefrontal cortex (PFC) is among the last regions to mature, explaining adolescent limitations in executive function (planning, impulse control, decision-making).
- Social cognition (mentalising, perspective-taking) develops significantly during adolescence and correlates with medial PFC development.
- Changes in neural architecture during adolescence map onto commonly observed adolescent behaviours (risk-taking, peer-orientation, emotional reactivity).
- Histological and neuroimaging studies converge on similar developmental trajectories of grey/white matter across adolescence.

## Numerical Findings
- Review article; synthesises histological and brain imaging (structural/functional MRI) studies
- Grey matter peak: approximately 11–12 years (frontal/parietal); followed by adolescent pruning
- White matter increases linearly into adulthood

## Study Design
Narrative review; reviews histological, structural MRI, and functional MRI studies of adolescent brain development; discusses implications for executive function and social cognition.

## Limitation
Narrative (not systematic) review; 2006 data — adolescent neuroscience has advanced significantly since publication; individual variation in developmental timing not addressed.
