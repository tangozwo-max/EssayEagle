## Key Claims
- Resilience in development is 'ordinary magic' — it arises from basic human adaptive systems, not exceptional qualities, when those systems are protected and functioning.
- The most important protective systems include: attachment relationships, cognitive development, self-regulation, motivation/efficacy, and community/cultural connections.
- Resilience research has shifted from identifying rare 'resilient individuals' to understanding universal adaptive systems that promote positive outcomes under adversity.
- Disrupting or damaging fundamental developmental systems (especially early attachment) produces the greatest risk for maladaptation.
- Policy implications: protect and restore fundamental adaptive systems rather than expecting extraordinary individual characteristics.

## Numerical Findings
- Review article; synthesises decades of resilience research
- No specific effect sizes reported; draws on multiple longitudinal studies (Kauai, Minnesota longitudinal study, others)

## Study Design
Narrative review published in American Psychologist; synthesises the first generation of resilience research and proposes a second-generation framework focused on adaptive systems.

## Limitation
Narrative review; 2001 — resilience science has advanced considerably since; 'ordinary magic' framing may understate the role of structural inequalities in determining access to protective systems.
