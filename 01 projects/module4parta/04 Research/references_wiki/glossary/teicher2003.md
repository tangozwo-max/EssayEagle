## Key Claims
- Early stress and childhood maltreatment produce lasting structural and functional changes in the developing brain, particularly in stress-response systems (HPA axis, limbic system).
- Specific brain regions affected: amygdala (heightened reactivity), hippocampus (reduced volume), prefrontal cortex (reduced regulatory capacity), corpus callosum (reduced integration).
- Early adversity produces a stress-sensitive neural phenotype — an organism calibrated for a dangerous environment, which is maladaptive in safe contexts.
- These neurobiological changes are not irreversible but represent embedded risk that increases vulnerability to psychiatric disorder.
- Maltreatment-related neurobiological changes are related to HPA axis dysregulation, elevated cortisol, and altered stress reactivity patterns.

## Numerical Findings
- Review; synthesises neuroimaging, neurobiological, and clinical studies
- Hippocampal volume reductions of 5–12% reported in maltreatment/stress studies
- HPA axis dysregulation: elevated cortisol or blunted cortisol response documented

## Study Design
Narrative review; integrates animal models, human neuroimaging (structural and functional MRI, EEG), and HPA axis studies to map neurobiological consequences of early adversity.

## Limitation
2003 publication — neuroimaging resolution and analysis methods have advanced considerably; some animal model findings may not fully translate to humans; stress effects are dose/timing-dependent and individual variation is high.
