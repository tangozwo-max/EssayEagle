## Key Claims
- Acute laboratory stress selectively impairs sustained attention — but not inhibitory control — in young children (mean age 5.4 years), consistent with the hypothesis that stress biases cognition toward bottom-up environmental monitoring at the expense of effortful attention.
- Children of mothers with higher parenting stress (Parenting Stress Index, PSI) show significantly greater attentional impairment following acute stress, demonstrating that caregiving environment moderates children's stress-response vulnerability.
- The PSI 'parental distress' subscale was most strongly associated with elevated sympathetic nervous system (SNS) reactivity in children post-stressor.
- Autonomic nervous system (ANS) reactivity — specifically SNS engagement — partially mediates the link between parenting stress and post-stress cognitive impairment.
- The preschool period is a critical developmental window for the emergence of children's independent regulatory capacity; caregivers buffer or amplify physiological and cognitive stress responses at this stage.

## Numerical Findings
- N = 84 mother–child dyads; children M = 5.38 years (SD = 0.85, range 4.20–6.71)
- Stress group: N = 58 (33 female); Control group: N = 26 (14 female)
- 74 children with complete behavioural data (49 Stressor, 25 Control) after exclusions for task non-completion
- Stressor: unfamiliar adult with stern affect; matching task deliberately impossible to complete in 2 min
- Go/No-Go task: zoo-themed, 2 × 60-trial blocks; Go accuracy = sustained attention; No-Go accuracy = inhibitory control
- ANS (SCL, RSA) measured via electrodes throughout baseline and post-stressor

## Study Design
Between-groups experimental design; children randomly assigned to acute stress (unfamiliar adult + social threat task) or control condition; Go/No-Go task administered pre- and post-stressor; ANS physiology (SNS/PNS) measured throughout; parenting stress assessed via maternal PSI report.

## Limitation
Small sample (N = 84); laboratory stressor may not generalise to chronic or interpersonal real-world stress; predominantly community-recruited, largely non-clinical sample; limited ethnic and socioeconomic diversity not reported; no follow-up — acute stress effects may differ from those of cumulative chronic stress exposure.
