## Key Claims
- Reactive and proactive aggression are highly intercorrelated (r = .68) — suggesting measurement instruments struggle to separate them — but show meaningfully different associations with psychosocial adjustment at the meta-analytic level.
- Reactive aggression is more strongly and uniquely linked to internalising problems, emotional dysregulation/ADHD symptoms, low prosocial behaviour, and peer rejection than proactive aggression.
- Proactive aggression is positively associated with peer-reported social preference (r = .14–.15), reflecting its instrumental, goal-oriented nature and possible link to social dominance.
- Both forms are similarly associated with delinquent behaviour and victimisation by peers, making simple type-based distinctions unreliable for antisocial outcome prediction.
- The intercorrelation between types is partly a measurement artifact — distinguishing them requires purpose-built instruments, not standard omnibus scales.

## Numerical Findings
- k = 42 studies included; total N = 20,266 participants aged under 18
- Proactive/reactive intercorrelation: r = .68
- Six psychosocial adjustment domains examined: internalising, externalising/ADHD, prosocial behaviour, peer preference, peer victimisation, delinquency
- Reactive aggression uniquely predicted internalising and ADHD symptoms in incremental validity tests
- Peer-reported proactive aggression: r = .14–.15 with social preference

## Study Design
Meta-analysis of published and unpublished empirical studies; moderation analyses examined whether measurement type, informant, age, and sex affected differential effect sizes.

## Limitation
Publication bias likely; heterogeneity across measurement instruments makes pooled estimates noisy; high intercorrelation limits clean incremental validity tests; few studies used instruments specifically designed to distinguish functions of aggression; community samples dominate — clinical population findings may differ.
