## Key Claims
- All standard SES indicators (household income, parental education, parental unemployment) independently predict mental health problems in children at 2-year follow-up.
- Parental education moderates the impact of stressful life situations: children with higher-educated parents show fewer mental health problems even when stressful events occur.
- Children with low SES face multiple stressful life situations and are at higher risk for developing mental health problems.
- Mental health problems at baseline are the strongest predictor of problems two years later (stability of difficulties).
- Stressful life situations between baseline and 2-year follow-up — combined with low parental education — significantly predict child mental health outcomes.

## Numerical Findings
- BELLA cohort (mental health module of KiGGS): n = 2,111 participants aged 7–17 at baseline
- Three measurement points (2003–2006, 2004–2007, 2005–2008)
- Hierarchical multiple linear regression; SDQ (parent-report) as outcome measure

## Study Design
Prospective longitudinal cohort (BELLA/KiGGS); nationally representative German sample; hierarchical multiple regression modelling SES indicators, stressful life situations, and their interactions.

## Limitation
Effect sizes are small; SES measured at baseline only; stressful life situations measured via count (not severity); German nationally representative sample may not generalise internationally.
