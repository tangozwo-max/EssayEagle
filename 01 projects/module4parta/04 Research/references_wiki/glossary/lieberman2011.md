## Key Claims
- Labelling (affect labelling/name-it-to-tame-it) reduces subjective emotional responses to negative emotional stimuli.
- Affect labelling, reappraisal, and distraction all reduce emotional reactivity but through different pathways and to different degrees.
- The labelling effect operates through increased prefrontal (right ventrolateral PFC) regulation of amygdala activity.
- Even incidental affect labelling (without instruction) reduces the subjective experience of emotional arousal.
- Implication for therapy: language-based emotion regulation strategies can modulate limbic system responses — supporting the value of psychoeducation and naming feelings.

- Experimental study; adult sample

## Study Design
Experimental; within-subjects fMRI paradigm; participants viewed emotional images under labelling, reappraisal, and distraction conditions; subjective ratings and neural responses measured.

## Limitation
Adult laboratory sample — generalisability to children and clinical populations requires validation; laboratory emotional stimuli differ from real-life emotional situations; individual differences in language ability not controlled.
