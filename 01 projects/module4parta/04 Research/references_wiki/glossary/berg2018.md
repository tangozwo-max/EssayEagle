## Key Claims
- Children with ASD who experience adversity (ACEs) receive significantly delayed autism diagnoses and delayed entry into specialist services compared to children without adversity.
- A dose–response relationship exists: each additional ACE category independently predicts longer time to diagnosis and service initiation.
- Adversity compounds structural barriers (access, parental awareness, clinician recognition) already present for ASD families, widening the window of untreated developmental need.
- The co-occurrence of adversity and ASD creates compounding developmental vulnerability — delays mean children go longer without therapeutic support during critical developmental windows.
- Relevant to understanding how social disadvantage (chaotic environments, parental mental health difficulties) may delay recognition of neurodevelopmental needs in children like Tommy.

## Numerical Findings
- N = 1,624 children with ASD; data from the National Survey of Children's Health (NSCH) 2011–2012
- 1–2 ACEs: time ratio (TR) = 1.17 for diagnosis delay (17% longer), TR = 1.22 for service entry delay (22% longer)
- ≥3 ACEs: TR = 1.23 for diagnosis delay (23% longer), TR = 1.27 for service entry delay (27% longer)
- ~20% of ASD children in the sample experienced ≥3 ACEs

## Study Design
Secondary analysis of NSCH survey data; parametric accelerated failure time (AFT) log-logistic models used to estimate time-to-diagnosis and time-to-services, adjusting for demographic and clinical covariates.

## Limitation
Cross-sectional/retrospective survey design; adversity measured by parent report, subject to recall bias; NSCH captures a broad population snapshot rather than clinical trajectories; no information on type or severity of specific adversity; confounding by SES, race/ethnicity, and healthcare access not fully controlled.
