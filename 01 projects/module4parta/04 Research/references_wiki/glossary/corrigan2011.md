## Key Claims
- Complex childhood trauma produces autonomic dysregulation: survivors are easily triggered into extreme arousal states (sympathetic hyperarousal or parasympathetic hypoarousal) by trauma reminders.
- The Window of Tolerance (WoT; Siegel, 1999) conceptualises the functional arousal zone within which a person can engage effectively — trauma narrows this window, making regulation outside normal range more likely.
- The periaqueductal grey (PAG) mediates instant defence responses: dorsolateral PAG activates fight/flight; ventrolateral PAG activates freeze/submissive collapse.
- Ascending monoaminergic tracts — particularly mesocortical, mesolimbic, and nigrostriatal dopaminergic pathways — drive sustained mood and arousal dysregulation following trauma.
- Dysfunctional behaviours (self-harm, substance use, compulsive rituals) function as attempts to regulate an autonomic system chronically driven outside the WoT; they are coping strategies, not disorders of will.
- Pharmacological interventions targeting dopaminergic and noradrenergic pathways may complement trauma-focused psychological therapies.

## Numerical Findings
- Theoretical/review paper; no primary data collected
- Cites animal models of depression (pain, isolation, social defeat) to infer dopaminergic pathway changes
- WoT model derived from clinical observation and neurobiological literature rather than quantitative trials

## Study Design
Theory and pharmacology review; integrates autonomic neuroscience, trauma psychology (Window of Tolerance model), animal models of stress-induced mood changes, and clinical pharmacology to propose intervention targets for complex trauma sequelae.

## Limitation
Primarily theoretical; animal model extrapolation to human complex trauma is speculative; the WoT model lacks standardised operationalisation for empirical testing or clinical trials; pharmacological recommendations based on indirect evidence only; paper predates refinements in polyvagal theory and updated trauma neuroscience.
