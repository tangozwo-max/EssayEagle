## Key Claims
- Preterm birth is a significant risk factor for neurodevelopmental difficulties including cerebral palsy (CP), cognitive impairment, developmental coordination disorder (DCD), learning difficulties, and behavioural/emotional problems.
- The earlier and more severe the prematurity, the greater the risk: very preterm (<32 weeks) and extremely low birth weight (ELBW, <1000g) infants face the highest rates of major impairment.
- Moderate-to-late preterm infants (32–36 weeks) comprise ~75% of all preterm births and carry substantial — but often unrecognised — risks for ADHD, learning difficulties, and school failure.
- Neurological injury patterns (IVH, PVL) mediate outcomes, but caregiver quality and NICU environment also shape developmental trajectories independently.
- NICU hospitalisation disrupts early parent–infant bonding, adding a psychosocial risk layer on top of the biological risk.

## Numerical Findings
- CP prevalence: ~14% at 22–27 weeks GA; ~6% at 28–31 weeks; <1% at 32–36 weeks
- DCD rates: 9.5–51% in VPT/VLBW vs 5–6% in general population
- Cognitive impairment (IQ < 70): 4–47% across GA bands 22–34 weeks
- Mean IQ ~10.9 points lower in preterm vs full-term controls
- 72% of ELBW adolescents show school difficulties
- Late-preterm (34–36 weeks) = ~75% of all preterm births

## Study Design
Narrative review; synthesises literature on preterm birth outcomes across neurological (CP, IVH, PVL), cognitive (IQ, learning), behavioural (ADHD, autism spectrum symptoms), and emotional domains, stratified by gestational age and birth weight.

## Limitation
Narrative (not systematic) review with potential selection bias; 2010 publication — neonatal intensive care and survival rates have improved since; outcomes vary markedly by gestational age band, which is not always clearly distinguished across studies; long-term adult outcomes and caregiver quality variables are underemphasised.
