## Key Claims
- ACEs are transmitted intergenerationally through biological (epigenetic), psychological (parenting behaviour, mental health), and social (poverty, instability) pathways.
- Unresolved parental trauma disrupts sensitive caregiving, increasing children's risk for attachment difficulties and ACEs.
- Protective factors can break the intergenerational cycle: sensitive caregiving, parental mental health support, stable housing, and social support are key buffers.
- Prevention must target parents as well as children — addressing parental ACEs is as important as protecting children from current ACEs.
- Masten's resilience systems framework (ordinary magic) integrates well with intergenerational ACE prevention models.

## Numerical Findings
- Review article; synthesises biological, psychological, and social research on intergenerational transmission
- No single effect sizes; draws on multiple longitudinal datasets

## Study Design
Narrative review in Clinical Psychology Review; integrates developmental, clinical, and public health literatures on ACE transmission and prevention across generations.

## Limitation
Narrative review — selective citation possible; mechanisms of biological/epigenetic transmission are not yet fully established; prevention programme evidence base is still developing.
