## Key Claims
- Fathers play a significant long-term role in children's anxiety development — independent of the established role of mothers — specifically through the quality of father–child attachment security.
- Lower attachment security to fathers in first grade (age 6) predicted which children maintained anxiety into adolescence (age 15), across a 9-year follow-up.
- Restriction of maternal autonomy granting (parental overcontrol) in middle childhood (age 10) predicted persistence of child anxiety into adolescence.
- Partner conflict explained the longitudinal association between earlier and later parental anxiety — a novel finding suggesting interparental conflict transmits parental anxiety over time.
- Family processes (partner conflict, attachment security, autonomy granting) function as mediating mechanisms that translate initial parent/child anxiety into sustained anxiety across development.
- The study corrects a major gap in the anxiety transmission literature: most prior research focused exclusively on mothers, underestimating paternal contributions to the long-term maintenance of child anxiety.

## Numerical Findings
- N = 661 children; NICHD Study of Early Child Care and Youth Development (USA)
- 9-year longitudinal study: assessments at Grade 1 (age ~6), Grade 5 (age ~10), and age 15
- Longitudinal path model (including both fathers and mothers) was a good fit for the data
- Lower father–child attachment security at Grade 1 → child maintained anxiety to age 15
- Maternal restriction of autonomy granting at Grade 5 → child anxiety persisted to age 15
- Partner conflict (at Grade 1) → mediated association between early and later parental anxiety

## Study Design
Secondary longitudinal analysis of the NICHD SECCYD cohort; path analysis model tested with both fathers and mothers included simultaneously; family process variables (partner conflict, parent–child attachment security, parental autonomy granting) examined as mediators of change in child and parental anxiety across three developmental time points.

## Limitation
Sample attrition across 9 years (from original NICHD cohort); anxiety measured by parent/teacher report rather than diagnostic interview; parental autonomy granting and attachment measured at only one time point in childhood; generalisability limited by predominantly White, two-parent NICHD cohort composition; path model assumes certain directional relationships that may not capture full bidirectionality.
