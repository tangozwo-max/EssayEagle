## Key Claims
- Disorganised attachment in infancy is robustly associated with caregiver frightened/frightening behaviour (Main & Hesse) and with unresolved parental loss or trauma.
- Disorganised attachment significantly predicts later psychopathology, dissociation, and difficulties with self-regulation and social relationships.
- Prevalence of disorganisation is elevated in high-risk and maltreatment samples; it is not random variation but a meaningful clinical indicator.
- Disorganised attachment involves a collapse of organised attachment strategies — the child cannot resolve the dilemma of a caregiver who is simultaneously a source of fear and comfort.
- Signed by 30+ leading attachment researchers as a consensus statement; carries unusual weight as expert consensus.

## Numerical Findings
- Review; synthesises decades of attachment research
- Disorganised attachment prevalence: ~15% in low-risk samples; substantially higher in maltreatment/high-risk samples
- Predictive validity for dissociation and psychopathology: medium-to-large effect sizes in meta-analyses cited within

## Study Design
Multi-author consensus review/position paper; synthesises empirical literature on disorganised attachment from infancy to adulthood; addresses clinician and policy implications.

## Limitation
Consensus paper format — potential for shared theoretical commitments among authors; some predictive pathways from infant disorganisation to adult outcomes involve long developmental spans with many moderating factors.
