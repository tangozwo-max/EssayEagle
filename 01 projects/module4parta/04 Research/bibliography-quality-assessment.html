Tier 2–3 Sources — Why These Carry Limited Evidentiary Weight

11 of 43 sources (26%) are Tier 2 or 3. These are appropriate for framing and contextualising arguments but must never serve as primary evidence for empirical claims. The rule: every T2–T3 citation needs a named T4+ empirical partner in the same or adjacent sentence. If you cannot name it, the T3 source is carrying weight it cannot bear.

Cannot carry evidentiary weight for any statistical or clinical claim. Use to introduce a clinical concern or frame the dual-jeopardy concept. Bowlby (1997) T3 Theory Book Foundational theoretical treatise (original 1969); no primary empirical data; no systematic search; author's interpretive synthesis of ethology and clinical observation. Cannot validate any empirical claim independently. Use only to introduce attachment theory and Internal Working Model concept. Pair with Granqvist et al. (2017) T6 for empirical attachment evidence.
Bronfenbrenner (2009) T3 Theory Book Ecological systems framework (original 1979); no primary data; conceptual architecture only; does not address neurobiological mechanisms. Reprint of a 45-year-old text; bioecological extensions (Bronfenbrenner & Morris 2006) are not captured here. Use to introduce and name the ecological systems framework. Every ecological risk claim must cite a T4+ empirical source (e.g. Poulain 2020 for SES, Garner & Yogman 2021 for community/family factors).
Weerasekera (1993) T3 Theory Descriptive formulation model (4Ps); no empirical testing reported; describes a clinical tool rather than research findings. 1993 — no update of this specific paper since publication. Cite only to name and attribute the 4Ps formulation structure. Do not use to support any claim about outcomes or effectiveness of formulation.
McCrory & Viding (2015) T3 Theory Theoretical framework paper proposing the latent vulnerability model; no new primary data; framework awaits full empirical testing. Valuable conceptual contribution but is not empirical evidence itself. Cite to introduce the latent vulnerability framework and sensitised neural systems concept. Pair with McLaughlin (2019) T7 SR for neurobiological evidence of adversity effects.
Link & Phelan (1995) T3 Theory Sociological theory paper (fundamental cause theory); 1995; theoretical proposition rather than primary empirical test. Discipline of sociology, not developmental psychology — transferability must be stated, not assumed. Use only to name and frame structural/SES mechanisms as fundamental causes. Pair with Reiss et al. (2019) T6 or Poulain et al. (2020) T4 for empirical SES–mental health evidence.
Corrigan et al. (2011) T3 Theory Theory/narrative review proposing an autonomic dysregulation model; limited original data; interpretive synthesis. Window of Tolerance as a concept predates this paper and derives from Siegel — proper attribution requires care. Cite for the autonomic dysregulation framing of trauma response. Pair with Teicher et al. (2003) T6 Landmark for neurobiological evidence of HPA and stress-response dysregulation.
Glover (2015) T3 Chapter Book chapter; author-selected literature review; no systematic search protocol; editorial volume format. Evidence selection reflects author's interpretive lens and the volume's framing, not an independent synthesis. Use to introduce prenatal stress mechanisms and HPA programming concepts. Pair with Tadanki et al. (2025) T4 review or Arpino et al. (2010) for broader evidence.
Macneil et al. (2012) T3 Commentary Scholarly commentary in BMC Medicine; no primary data; argues for case formulation over diagnosis through selective literature review. Author position paper — not an evidence synthesis of formulation outcomes. Appropriate to cite as justification for using a formulation approach. Do not use to claim that formulation improves clinical outcomes (no data in this paper supports that).
Achenbach & Rescorla (2001) T3 Manual Assessment instrument manual; documents tool development and psychometric norms; not a primary research study. Appropriate for methodological citation only — does not report findings about child development or intervention outcomes. Cite only when referencing the CBCL/ASEBA as an assessment tool: "measured using the CBCL (Achenbach & Rescorla, 2001)." Do not use to support substantive claims about child mental health patterns.
