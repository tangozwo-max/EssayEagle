Tommy Presentation Cut Plan

A focused cut plan for the 10-minute version. The aim is to preserve the academic story, keep the analysis strong, and remove visible detail that slows delivery or repeats points already made elsewhere.

Core principle

Cut detail, not structure

Through-line to keep: Tommy’s presentation is best understood as the cumulative result of prenatal, relational, and social adversity, with protective relationships offering a realistic opportunity to shift trajectory.

Keep 5 content slidesAim for 4 spoken points max per slideMove weaker points into narration

Fast answer
Slide Keep Cut or compress Why
1. Presenting issues Keep the visual and the core thesis that behaviour is adaptation, not simple misconduct. Fold “possible risk pathway” into speech instead of keeping it as a separate visible section. This slide should frame the case quickly, not split attention between two right-side arguments.
2. Predisposing factors Keep cumulative ACEs and the individual-family-community structure. Reduce each risk-layer box to 2 bullets; say “ACEs interact and compound” aloud instead of on the slide. The ecological logic is essential to the brief, but the current text density is slowing the story.
3. Developmental impact Keep HPA/stress, attention-learning, attachment-trust, and mental-health risk. Cut “overall health” from the visible slide. Overall physical-health risk is true, but it is not central enough for a 10-minute case presentation.
4. Risk of complexity Keep the 5P idea and four big mechanisms. Cut separate bullets on impaired regulation disrupting social development, negative relational experiences as self-fulfilling prophecy, and teacher/school trigger detail as stand-alone items. This slide is the most overloaded and repeats material already covered on Slides 2 and 3.
5. Protective factors Keep partial maternal protection, predictable teachers, ACEs are probabilistic, and the final synthesis. Cut school meals as a separate bullet, “ordinary magic” as a named on-slide concept, and adolescent neuroplasticity as a stand-alone bullet. These are useful ideas, but they cost explanation time and work better in speaker notes.

Protective factors and opportunity

Keep: the message that not all is fixed, and that relationships buffer risk.

Trim hard: leave only the most intuitive and case-linked protective factors on screen.

  • Cut school meals as a separate bullet.
If time is very tight

One-sentence rule

If a visible bullet cannot be explained in one spoken sentence, it should probably be cut from the slide and kept only in notes.
