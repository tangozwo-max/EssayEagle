Content block 1

Executive function difficulties

Your current idea: If executive function is affected, Tommy may struggle with emotional regulation, impulsivity, and concentration.

Tighter academic statement: Early adversity is associated with poorer executive functioning, including inhibitory control, working memory, and cognitive flexibility; chronic stress may disrupt prefrontal systems that support regulation of behaviour, emotion, and attention.

How it fits Tommy: This supports linking impulsivity, concentration problems, and reactive behaviour to stress-related executive-function vulnerability rather than deliberate non-compliance.

Best use in presentation: Keep the statement concise on the slide; explain the prefrontal/executive-function link in your narration.

Content block 2

Low sustained attention under stress

Your current idea: Constant stress keeps threat systems activated, making it harder to focus and learn.

Tighter academic statement: Persistent stress can impair sustained attention in children, and stress-related changes in amygdala–prefrontal circuitry are one plausible mechanism through which concentration and learning are disrupted.

How it fits Tommy: This supports a formulation in which hypervigilance and poor concentration interfere with classroom learning and emotional self-regulation.

Best use in presentation: Avoid saying the amygdala is “constantly firing”; say stress may bias attention toward threat and away from learning tasks.

Content block 3

Impaired mentalizing and reduced trust

Your current idea: Tommy may struggle to read others’ mental states and therefore misread interactions.

Tighter academic statement: Mentalizing refers to understanding behaviour in terms of underlying thoughts, feelings, wishes, and intentions; where early relationships are unsafe or inconsistent, children may show reduced capacity to interpret others’ mental states accurately, especially under stress.

How it fits Tommy: This gives a bridge between relational trauma and later interpersonal misunderstandings, social defensiveness, or rapid escalation with adults and peers.

Best use in presentation: Keep this as a brief explanatory sub-point unless you have a strong direct source from Fonagy or later review literature in your reference list.

Content block 4

Internal working models

Your current idea: Adults become experienced as unpredictable and safety as fragile.

Tighter academic statement: Early attachment experiences help shape internal working models of self and others, including expectations about whether caregivers are responsive, comforting, and safe.

How it fits Tommy: This supports interpreting distrust, guardedness, or expectation of inconsistency as learned relational expectations rather than isolated symptoms.

Best use in presentation: This is a good theory sentence for your narration; use Tommy’s vignette examples to make it concrete.

Content block 5

Hostile attribution bias

Your current idea: Tommy may read neutral interactions as hostile.

Tighter academic statement: Children exposed to harsh, threatening, or inconsistent environments may be more likely to interpret ambiguous social cues as hostile, which can increase aggressive or defensive responding.

How it fits Tommy: This gives a direct explanation for overreacting to peers or adults when social cues are unclear.

Best use in presentation: Use one short phrase on the slide, then explain that hostile attribution is a bias in interpreting ambiguous intent.

Content block 6

Serve-and-return and disorganized attachment

Your current idea: Missing serve-and-return may contribute to attachment problems; ideally connect this to disorganized attachment.

Tighter academic statement: Responsive serve-and-return interactions are important for early brain development, while disorganized attachment is more specifically linked to frightened, frightening, highly dysregulated, or otherwise alarming caregiving and breakdowns in the child’s strategy for seeking safety.

How it fits Tommy: This allows you to distinguish between general non-responsive caregiving and the stronger claim that disorganized attachment is associated with fear, alarm, or contradiction in the caregiving relationship.

Best use in presentation: Treat these as related but not identical. Only merge them into one sentence if your final source explicitly does so.

Suggested slide wording
  • Attention and learning: Early adversity is associated with poorer executive function and sustained attention, which may contribute to Tommy’s impulsivity, concentration problems, and difficulties learning in stressful environments.
  • Trusting relationships: Inconsistent or frightening caregiving may shape internal working models in which adults are experienced as unpredictable and safety as fragile.
  • Social interpretation: Tommy may be more likely to misread ambiguous interactions as threatening, increasing defensive or aggressive responses.
  • Attachment: Limited responsive caregiving may reduce opportunities for serve-and-return development, while frightening or contradictory caregiving may increase risk for disorganized attachment-related behaviours.
