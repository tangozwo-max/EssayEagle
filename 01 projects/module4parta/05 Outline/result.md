# Slide Outline — Tommy Presentation: Relational Disruption → Relational Repair

**Assignment:** Module 4 Part A — 10-min recorded presentation  
**Through-line:** Every ACE in Tommy's life disrupted the relational environment he needed. Every protective factor worked by partially restoring it.  
**Slide structure:** Title + 5 content slides + Reference slide  
**Timing:** ~2 min per content slide

## Opening Hook (spoken over title slide — 15 sec)

> "Tommy is seven years old. He hits other children, struggles to focus, and reacts to small frustrations with explosive intensity. A different assessor might label him difficult. This presentation argues he is predictable — and that everything we see in Tommy was set in motion before he was born."

This frames the through-line immediately and signals analytical intent.

## Slide 1 — An Ecological Inheritance of Risk

**Slide title:** *An Ecological Inheritance of Risk*

**Central argument:** Tommy's relational disruption did not begin with Tommy. It began a generation earlier and filtered through every level of his ecology before he was born.

### Visual
Three-tier ecological table (Bronfenbrenner, 1979):

| Level | Tommy's context | Relational impact |
|---|---|---|
| Macrosystem / Exosystem | Employer relocation → unemployment; high community crime and substance use | Chronic financial stress; normalised high-risk coping; reduced social capital |
| Mesosystem / Microsystem | DV; Mary's substance use; 6+ address changes; volatile partner | Home defined by unpredictability and threat — the anti-conditions for secure attachment |
| Individual / Intergenerational | Mary's 15 foster placements → no secure attachment model; no qualifications | Mary cannot provide what she never received — intergenerational transmission of relational fragility |

**Key analytical move:** The third row is the critical one. Mary's ACE history is not background — it is the mechanism by which community-level stress becomes Tommy's relational reality. 6+ ACE categories (Felitti et al., 1998) are present before Tommy is school-age.

**Spoken close:** "Tommy's ACE score tells us how many categories of risk are present. What Bronfenbrenner shows us is that those risks do not exist in isolation — they cascade inward, from community to family to the first relationship Tommy will ever have."

**References this slide:** Bronfenbrenner (1979); Felitti et al. (1998)

## Slide 2 — Biology Under Siege

**Slide title:** *Biology Under Siege*

**Central argument:** The relational disruption translated into biological change before Tommy drew his first breath. His stress-response system was shaped by an environment of threat — not by choice, but by biology.

### Visual
Linear chain diagram: **Prenatal → Birth → Infancy → School-age biology**

**Spoken content, beat by beat:**

1. **Prenatal environment:** Mary's chronic cortisol — produced by DV, low mood, substance use — crossed the placenta. Alcohol exposure created FASD risk across cognitive, behavioural, and emotional domains. Tommy's HPA axis was being calibrated to expect danger before he had a name.

2. **Birth:** Preterm, low birth weight, NICU. The NICU represents a biologically critical period without a buffering adult (Shonkoff). Without a consistent co-regulator during sensitive periods of brain development, the infant's stress system has no external reference point to regulate against — it remains at high activation.

3. **Toxic stress model (Shonkoff):** Distinguish tolerable stress (buffered by a caring adult) from toxic stress (sustained, unbuffered activation). Tommy experienced the latter. The result is a *latent vulnerability* — biological changes to the brain's memory, threat, and reward systems that do not necessarily produce disorder immediately, but sensitise the brain to future adversity.

4. **Brain consequences:** Amygdala hyperactivation (threat over-detection), hippocampal effects (memory and emotional regulation), PFC underdevelopment (executive function, impulse control). These are not character traits — they are neurological adaptations to an environment of chronic unpredictability.

**Spoken close:** "Tommy arrived home with a stress-sensitised brain primed to perceive threat. What he needed next was a relationship consistent enough to begin recalibrating that system. What he got was more unpredictability."

**References this slide:** Shonkoff / Harvard Centre on the Developing Child (toxic stress model); [M4 W2 P32 — latent vulnerability, sensitive periods, HPA axis]; Felitti et al. (1998) — FASD risk

## Slide 3 — The Predictable Child

**Slide title:** *The Predictable Child*

**Central argument:** Tommy's school-age behaviours are not failures of character. They are exactly what a stress-sensitised brain, learning from a disorganised attachment figure, looks like — and they follow a clear theoretical logic.

### Visual
**Centrepiece:** Tommy's Internal Working Model — displayed as a quote block:

> *"Adults are unpredictable. Safety is fragile. When I feel overwhelmed, I must react with immediate intensity — because I cannot rely on anyone else to regulate for me."*
> — Tommy's operational model of the world (Bowlby, 1969)

**Spoken content, beat by beat:**

1. **Attachment:** Mary's caregiving was inconsistent — sometimes responsive, often overwhelmed, at times frightening. Research on disorganised attachment (Main & Hesse, 1990) identifies this pattern — the caregiver as simultaneously the source of fear and the source of comfort — as producing the most disrupted attachment strategy. There is no coherent approach Tommy can take; hyperactivation is his only option.

2. **Internal working model:** Bowlby's concept of the IWM as a relational script that generalises from the primary caregiver to all relationships. Tommy's script: adults cannot be trusted to regulate; self-protection through aggression is necessary. He applies this script to teachers, peers, and the classroom.

3. **Mentalizing (Fonagy):** Caregivers teach children to understand minds — their own and others'. Mary's inconsistency deprived Tommy of this. He cannot reliably interpret why others do what they do, or predict how his behaviour will affect them. A neutral glance reads as hostility. A peer bumping him in the corridor registers as a deliberate attack. His explosive reactions are not disproportionate given what his nervous system believes is happening.

4. **At school:** Amygdala hijack overrides PFC function — survival processing dominates rational processing. Focus difficulties, impulsivity, "over the top" emotional reactions are the output of a system doing exactly what it was trained to do: prioritise threat detection over reflection.

**Spoken close:** "Tommy is not choosing aggression. He is deploying the only relational strategy his experience has taught him. The question is not why he behaves this way — the theory tells us precisely why. The question is what can change it."

**References this slide:** Bowlby (1969); Main & Hesse (1990); Fonagy (mentalizing); [M4 W2 P32 — amygdala/PFC]; [M4 W1 P02 — IWM and insecure attachment]

## Slide 4 — Repair Begins in Relationship

**Slide title:** *Repair Begins in Relationship*

**Central argument:** Every protective factor in Tommy's life works through the same mechanism that disrupted him: relationship. The school system, almost without designing it this way, began to give Tommy what his early ecology could not.

### Visual
Continuum of Need table (IHEED, M4 W3):

| Level | Tommy's intervention | Mechanism of repair |
|---|---|---|
| Universal | Free school meals + vitamins; calm, predictable school environment | Addresses physiological stability; creates a safe baseline — the precondition for any relational repair |
| Selective | Mary's warm moments (Peek-A-Boo, babbling) | Serve-and-return interactions (Shonkoff) — each exchange builds a neural connection; partial repair within an inconsistent relationship |
| Indicated | LSA emotion-naming sessions | Co-regulation: the LSA names Tommy's internal state, activating PFC language pathways; over time, shifts from amygdala-dominant to PFC-mediated processing |

**Key analytical move:** The mechanism of all three levels is the same — a consistent, calm adult presence that offers what Tommy's early ecology denied: *felt safety* (Siegel, 2012). When the LSA remains regulated while Tommy is dysregulated, she is functioning as the external co-regulator his HPA axis was never given.

**Critical point:** Protective factors do not erase ACEs. They do not rewrite Tommy's biological history. What they do is create windows — repeated, predictable moments of safety — that allow neuroplasticity to begin reversing the sensitisation. The ordinary magic (Masten, 2001) is not Tommy's resilience — it is the relational consistency of the adults around him.

**Spoken close:** "The shift from physical aggression to verbal communication that we see in Tommy is not incidental. It is evidence of neuroplasticity in action. The question is whether the relational conditions that enabled it can be sustained."

**References this slide:** IHEED Continuum of Need (M4 W3); Siegel (2012); Shonkoff / Harvard CoDC (serve-and-return); Masten (2001)

## Slide 5 — Not Determined

**Slide title:** *Not Determined*

**Central argument:** Tommy's trajectory has shifted — but it is not fixed. ACEs leave a biological mark; they do not write the ending. The relational environment still determines outcome.

### Visual
**ACE pyramid** (Felitti & Anda): adversity → disrupted neurodevelopment → social/emotional/cognitive impairment → risk behaviours → disease/early death.

**Mark Tommy's current position:** mid-pyramid. Disrupted neurodevelopment is evident. Social and emotional impairment is present. He has not yet reached the base.

**Spoken content, beat by beat:**

1. **What the pyramid predicts without intervention:** Elevated risk of internalising disorders (depression, anxiety) and externalising disorders consistent with ODD criteria — children with 4+ ACEs face dramatically elevated lifetime risk. This is not a diagnosis of Tommy; it is what the dose-response evidence says about children with his profile.

2. **But dose-response is not destiny:** The same research that identifies ACE-outcome relationships demonstrates that the mediating variable — the one that determines whether adversity translates into disorder — is the presence or absence of a consistent, buffering relational environment. Tommy already has evidence of this working: verbal communication has replaced hitting.

3. **What neuroplasticity requires:** Continuity. The rewiring of the HPA axis is not an event — it is a process, dependent on repeated experiences of predictability replacing unpredictability. Removing the LSA, changing schools, returning to home chaos, reverses it.

4. **The three-level implication:** Individual (continued indicated support), family (sustained support for Mary's own mental health and substance use — her capacity for warm moments is the selective intervention), community (stable housing, economic support — the macro-level conditions that determine whether Mary can be a buffer at all).

**Spoken close:** "Tommy's early years were not his fault. The biological disruption is real and it is documented. But the evidence — in the neuroscience, in the attachment literature, and in Tommy's own behaviour — shows that the story is still being written. And it is written, as it always was, in relationships."

**References this slide:** Felitti & Anda (ACE pyramid); neuroplasticity literature; [M4 W4 — internalising/externalising disorder risk]; [M4 W2 P32 — latent vulnerability as ongoing risk]
