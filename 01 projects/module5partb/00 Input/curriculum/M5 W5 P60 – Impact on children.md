### Neglect

Neglect may take many different forms and children may experience any combination of these. Possible neglect includes:

- chaotic lives with a lack of effective, consistent support—for example, children not being helped to have breakfast and get to school in the morning
- poor home and personal hygiene
- parents failing to recognize and respond to children's needs
- economic challenges when income is spent on substances—for example, children not being bought new shoes when necessary
- the presence of harmful substances and equipment in the home, which create safety hazards
- the absence of a responsible adult if a parent loses consciousness through substance use
- a parentification-child having to act as the responsible adult by caring for themselves, their parent, and often younger siblings

### Emotional abuse

The NSPCC (National Security for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children) defines emotional abuse as, "any type of abuse that involves the continual emotional mistreatment of a child. It's sometimes called psychological abuse. Emotional abuse can involve deliberately trying to scare, humiliate, isolate or ignore a child."

\[Source: National Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children\]

Emotional abuse may include passive actions, such as the parent or responsible adult being less:

- responsive to a child or children
- willing to engage in play
- able to encourage further interaction

Other, more active forms of emotional abuse include:

- behaving in irrational, unpredictable ways or being withdrawn, which can frighten children
- parents having trouble controlling emotions, which may result in physical harm to the child
- putting a child at higher risk of sexual abuse, both directly from the parents and as a result of parental behavior, making the child more vulnerable to abuse by others

Children who have been abused are then more likely to use substances as a coping mechanism. They have an increased risk of addiction compared to the general population.

### Safeguarding

Parental substance use may create safety issues for children. These can include:

- exposure of the child to criminal activity or unsafe people
- the coming together of risk and vulnerability factors such as a disabled child (vulnerability) who has a parent with substance use disorder (risk)

### Psychological damage

Parental substance use may affect the child's psychological development. Effects can include:

- preoccupation with parent’s substance use
- self-blaming for parental behavior
- irregular school attendance and/or poor attainment
- difficulty establishing and maintaining healthy relationships
- developing behavioral, emotional, or cognitive problems (children often feel shame and try to cover up their parent’s substance use)

## Sophie

Sophie's father was addicted to alcohol and other substances. Her mother left him but did not take the children. As the eldest, Sophie took on a parental role, completing household tasks and caring for her siblings. She tried to keep them clean and fed, but this was challenging due to limited resources. She often missed school and struggled to play like other children her age.

**Select the diary button to read how this situation impacted Sophie.**

My dad said if social services knew about us, they'd take us away and split us up. I couldn't tell anyone. Because I was looking after the my brothers and sisters, I couldn't go to friends' houses after school or invite them to mine.

I had to try and work out what Dad was thinking and feeling then predict his behavior to keep my brothers and sisters safe. When he passed out, I thought he might die if I didn't look after him.

I often felt sad, panicky, ashamed, and angry about the situation.

![Locked diary](https://learn.iheed.org/static/media/diary.fb1e7e33754711047e6c.png) ![Open diary](https://learn.iheed.org/static/media/diary-revealed.ded02d4ef43af1b0967c.png) 

The school nurse became involved because Sophie's teacher had concerns about her peer relationships, emotional and physical health, and ability to focus and concentrate. She built up a trusted relationship with Sophie which enabled her to learn about her situation and child protection services to become involved.

Children's services enabled Sophie's father to receive support for addiction, and the low mood that preceded it. Sophie began to attend a young carers respite group where she could play and act her age. As her father spent increasing amounts of time sober he became increasingly more emotionally attuned and responsive to the children, and Sophie was able to behave in a more developmentally appropriate way with less responsibility.

 

Sophie's experience of seeing her father use substances led her to see that as a coping mechanism for emotions. However Sophie engaged with children’s mental health services and learnt how to understand and manage strong emotions without the need to use substances. Her school attendance and grades improved and she built good relationships with peers over time.
