## Harm reduction: frameworks and initiatives

In the early days of harm reduction initiatives and grassroots advocacy, many of the strategies designed to mitigate harm were focused on the negative health outcomes of substances involving the use of needles (i.e., heroin, predominantly).

Since then, approaches have evolved to address a broader range of substances and risks, with synthetic opioid use of particular concern in recent years.

The age-adjusted rate of drug overdose deaths in the United States (US) increased from 8.2 per 100,000 population in 2002 to 32.6 per 100,000 in 2022.

\[Source: CDC\]

This nearly fourfold increase reflects the significant escalation of the opioid crisis over that period. Countries like the US and Canada have faced devastating poisoning crises due to increasingly toxic synthetic opioid supplies.

In response to the crisis, Canada introduced a harm reduction initiative, a **safer supply** **pilot project**, which offered prescribed medications like opioids, stimulants, and benzodiazepines as safer alternatives to the increasingly dangerous illegal drug supply.

This strategy has led to improved health and quality of life among people who use substances. Overdoses have reduced, as has dependency on street drugs. While Europe has been less affected by the drug poisoning crisis compared to North America, the growing threat of poisonous synthetic opioids has been linked to many deaths in the UK and Baltic states.

\[Source: Busz et al.\]

The **Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA)** is a US-based organization that has partnered with public health agencies, community members, harm reductionists, people with lived experience, and others, to collate expertise on harm reduction. The SAMHSA Framework describes the core components of community-based harm reduction programs. This framework is based on 6 pillars, 12 principles, and 6 core practice areas that apply to harm reduction approaches, initiatives, programs, and services.

SAMHSA outlines that the 6 pillars are essential building blocks for effective harm reduction.

### Community-based harm reduction programs (CHRPs)

SAMHSA speaks to the importance of CHRPs. These programs encompass harm reduction strategies whereby people with lived experience of substance use lead the planning, oversight, program development, program evaluation, and resource/funding allocation for an organization’s harm reduction initiatives.

An important facet of harm reduction is the provision of a meaningful connection between people who use substances and their communities (especially to the most marginalized communities). CHRPs offer the lowest-barrier harm reduction practices, as permitted by law. Harm reduction activities may be integrated into a comprehensive, person-centered program of care that includes treatment services meeting the specific needs of the community in which the program is implemented.

SAMHSA advises that in addition to CHRPs being consistent with the organization's principles and pillars, they should include people with lived experience as coinvestigators in any research project; and boards, staff, and at least 51% of team members should have lived experience of substance use.

\[Source: SAMHSA\]

Founded in 1993, the National Harm Reduction Coalition is a US-based harm reduction organization launched at a grassroots level by "... a handful of individual activists, educators, and people who used drugs" to support members of vulnerable communities who were dying from transmissible diseases.

The coalition sets forward a set of eight principles central to harm reduction practice.

### acceptance
Accept, for better or worse, that licit and illicit drug use is part of our world, and choose to work to minimize its harmful effects rather than simply ignore or condemn them.

### understanding
Understand substance use as a complex, multifaceted phenomenon that encompasses a continuum of behaviors from severe use to total abstinence, and acknowledge that some ways of using drugs are clearly safer than others.
### quality of life
Establish quality of life and well-being (for the person and their community), and not necessarily cessation of all drug use, as the criteria for successful interventions and policies.
### nonjudgmental
Call for the nonjudgmental, noncoercive provision of services and resources to people who use drugs and the communities in which they live in order to assist them in reducing attendant harm.
### real voice
Ensure that people who use drugs and those with a history of drug use routinely have a real voice in the creation of programs and policies designed to serve them.
### empower
Affirm that people who use drugs are themselves the primary agents of reducing the harms of their drug use. Seek to empower these people to share information and support each other in strategies that meet their actual conditions of use.
### Recognition
Recognize that the realities of poverty, class, racism, social isolation, past trauma, sex-based discrimination, and other social inequalities affect both people’s vulnerability to and capacity for effectively dealing with drug-related harm.
### harm achnowledgement
Do not attempt to minimize or ignore the real and tragic harm and danger that can be associated with illicit drug use.
