## Challenges in implementing harm reduction strategies

**Read this excerpt about intervention for harm reduction:**

"...a single harm reduction intervention will not effectively assist people who use drugs. Instead, there needs to be informed coordination among related stakeholders for a comprehensive, structured design of mixed actions.

Interventions should cover multiple dimensions, including psychiatric interventions, case management, administrative support, medication, counseling, linkages to clinical clinics, and other referrals. Other activities should also be implemented to educate the public and create a more supportive environment for drug users to integrate into society."

\[Source: Tran, Luong\]

It is estimated that around 15 million people globally inject drugs, with evidence of this type of drug use reported in 190 countries.

Harm reduction service coverage is crucial for such people. However, data from 2022 showed that 73% of people who inject drugs lived in countries with low levels of coverage, 5% lived in countries with moderate coverage, and only 2% lived in countries with high coverage.

\[Source: Nuh\]

> [!-info] -info
> **Reminder:** Opioid agonist therapy (OAT) is a harm reduction approach that includes providing users of an illegal drug (such as heroin) with a less harmful replacement drug (such as methadone or buprenorphine) under medical supervision. This helps the person reduce the frequency of injections and their dependency on the more harmful drugs.

The results of a systematic review meta-analysis from authors Santo et al. (2021) suggest that OAT is an important intervention for people with opioid dependence, with the capacity to reduce multiple causes of death.

While evidence for the efficacy of harm reduction interventions like OAT is strong, a significant challenge is that coverage is still low in most countries globally.

\[Source: Santo et al.\]

It is important to be aware that healthcare professionals, policymakers, and/or community members are not always in favor of harm reduction strategies. Critics argue that harm reduction approaches enable and encourage substance use despite evidence disputing this claim. Overdose prevention sites, for example, have been impacted by discourses around recovery and abstinence—often viewed as imperative, compared to the "at one's own pace" principles of harm reduction. Critics also believe that such sites undermine public safety and increase or encourage substance use.

\[Source: Owczarzak et al.\]

## In-clinic discussion

Margo and colleagues Faye (a psychologist), Richard (a psychiatrist), and Steven (a nurse) are discussing the remit of mental healthcare services toward applying the principles of harm reduction in their practice and professional spheres.

**Margo:** Harm reduction, over complete abstinence, should be central to how we approach substance use in the clinic. We know that meeting people where they are, without judgment, has led to better outcomes. This is important not just in terms of reducing substance use, but improving overall mental health. Pragmatism is key here.

**Faye:** Absolutely. It's about trust. People are more likely to engage with services when they don't feel like they're being judged or forced into something like abstinence. Programs like needle exchanges or supervised use are critical. They minimize harm in the moment, and over time, people feel safer coming to us for help with both their substance use and their mental health.

**Richard:** Harm reduction might address some immediate issues, but it’s enabling in the long run in my opinion. I don't think it's okay to send the message that illicit substances can be used alongside medications. The interactions between these substances and psychiatric medications can be unpredictable and harmful.

**Steven:** Our priority should be immediate interventions—things like naloxone for overdose prevention or opioid agonist therapy when necessary. Harm reduction can’t become the main focus here. We’re not equipped to manage every aspect of substance use, and frankly, some of it falls outside our scope.

**Margo:** But isn’t the mental health of some of our service users intertwined with their substance use? If we don’t address both, we’re just treating one half of the problem. Harm reduction isn’t about turning a blind eye to addiction—it’s about creating safer environments for our service users while they’re on the path to recovery.

**Faye:** Right, and harm reduction doesn't mean we don’t encourage recovery or abstinence. It's about giving people the tools to reduce harm in the meantime, like reducing the risk of infections from needle sharing or preventing overdoses. Most importantly, it keeps people connected to services. If we push abstinence too hard, we risk pushing them away.

**Richard:** I get that, but harm reduction can send mixed messages. If someone with a severe mental health condition like schizophrenia is regularly using drugs, it complicates their treatment. How can I, as a psychiatrist, effectively manage their medications if I don’t know what substances they’re also using? There’s a risk that they’re undermining their own progress. Abstinence gives us a clear, controlled environment for treatment.

**Margo:** Harm reduction isn’t about enabling—it’s about keeping people alive and functional long enough to seek the help they need. We’re not turning the clinic into a needle exchange, but we *can* partner with community services or recommend safe practices without compromising the care we give.

**Faye:** And the fact is, people are going to use substances whether we support harm reduction or not. If we don’t offer a nonjudgmental, pragmatic approach, they’ll turn to less safe options, and we’ll be dealing with the consequences—more frequent crises, more emergency room visits, and more overdoses.

**Margo:** Harm reduction is not a matter of choosing one option over another. We can integrate these approaches into our broader care model. It doesn’t mean we stop supporting people toward recovery or abstinence; it means we recognize that people have different journeys, and we meet them where they are.

**Richard:** Maybe it’s about finding a middle ground, ensuring that we are not compromising anyone’s mental health by observing use without providing helpful resources or facts, but also ensuring that we do not alienate people.

**Steven:** As long as we keep the clinic's role clear and stay focused on the most critical needs, I think we can make this work. Let’s be practical about what we can realistically offer and what’s best for our service users.

**Margo:** Pragmatism and compassion—that’s what it’s all about. Thanks everyone for participating today.
