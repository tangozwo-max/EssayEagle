## M5 W1 P08 – Biological risk factors for substance use

Imagine four siblings who are from wealthy socioeconomic backgrounds, have professional jobs, and a good social support network.

Of the four, 50% have a particular gene variant that has been strongly associated with a person's likelihood to develop an addiction.

All four siblings take heroin every day for a month.

Can you estimate what percentage of the siblings will go on to develop an addiction to heroin?

The answer in this case is... you simply have no way of knowing how many people will develop an addiction to a substance, even if their genes are associated with addictive tendencies.

Theoretically, 50% of the siblings could develop an addiction if all other psychosocial factors are equal, but the mechanisms of inheritance are very complex. Studies of families with addiction suggest that a person’s genetics (with multiple risk genes involved) account for 40% to 60% of their risk.

Even if there was certainty that the cause of addiction for a specific person was 60% (i.e., at the highest range of potential cause), this still means that another 40% of their risk for addiction stems from other factors.

In the example of the siblings, the nongenetic risk factors (socioeconomic background, social network) reduce their risk of addiction significantly. Theoretically, therefore, if they were to develop an addiction, the cause **could be** almost entirely genetic.

In someone with a genetic risk of 20% who experienced an adverse childhood, trauma, stress, loneliness, and homelessness, again the tendency to develop an addiction **could be** almost completely genetic, or the other factors may be considered the causes of an addiction. There is really no way to measure how these different factors impact.

\[Source: Hari; Learn Genetics\]

### Some insights from research on genes

Though research is ongoing, scientists have identified variants of **19 genes** that are commonly inherited across addiction disorders, regardless of the substance being used. When examining the individual genes, they found that they are involved in the regulation of dopamine signaling.

\[Source: National Institute on Drug Abuse\]

A research study involving people who inject drugs found that they are two to three times more likely to have the integration of a gene called HK2 within RASGRF2 (a gene involved in regulating dopaminergic activity in the brain).

\[Source: University of Oxford\]

People with two copies of a certain form of the gene CHRNA5 are twice as likely to become nicotine-dependent, compared to people with two copies of a different form of CHRNA5.

\[Source: Hartz et al.\]

Cannabis users who carry a particular variant in the AKT1 gene have a twofold increased probability of a psychotic disorder, and this increases to up to sevenfold if they use cannabis daily.

\[Source: Di Forti et al.\]

### Cannabis and genetics

Only a very small minority of those who use cannabis will develop psychotic symptoms. However, gene studies suggest that cannabis use is possibly the **most modifiable environmental risk factor for schizophrenia**.

Researchers studied the AKT1 gene (involved in dopamine signaling) in 442 healthy young cannabis users, who were tested while intoxicated with their own cannabis.

They found that cannabis users who carry a particular variant in the AKT1 gene had a twofold increased probability of a psychotic disorder and this increased up to sevenfold if they used cannabis daily.

This chart shows that people with one or more copies of the AKT1 risk variant had a higher psychotic symptoms score when they smoked cannabis. Researchers suggest that these findings demonstrate that AKT1 mediates the acute response to cannabis in otherwise healthy people, and implicates the AKT1 pathway as a possible target for prevention and treatment of cannabis psychosis.

\[Adapted from: Zagorski; Morgan et al.; University of Oxford\]

**Naltrexone** is a type of medication approved by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) to treat both opioid use disorder (OUD) and alcohol use disorder (AUD). Naltrexone binds and blocks opioid receptors and reduces and suppresses opioid cravings. The receptors that produce euphoric feelings when someone drinks alcohol or takes an opioid are blocked so that there is no feeling of pleasure from these substances.

There is no misuse potential with naltrexone according to experts.

\[Source: SAMHSA\]

Researchers discovered that a variant in the sequence of nucleotide bases on a gene called **OPRM1** can predict naltrexone treatment response in people who use alcohol.

Like with evidence on the AKT1 gene, cannabis use, and the development of psychotic symptoms, studies on OPRM1 among people who use alcohol might be useful to assist in selecting treatment options—some people may respond very well to naltrexone depending on their genotype, and others may not respond at all. For the latter group, psychosocial interventions may be a better fit than medication-based treatment.

\[Source: Anton et al.\]
