## The neuroscience of recovery and interventions

Recovery-orientated treatment for addiction and dependency leverages insights from neuroscience to support long-term recovery. Understanding the neuroscience behind neuroplasticity, the reward system, stress and self-regulation, and craving and impulse control may help the teams treating people for addiction and dependency to adjust treatment for the individual.

This applies whether a person is experiencing a substance use disorder or a behavioral disorder such as gambling disorder or internet gaming disorder. Addiction negatively affects several regions of the brain, but functioning can improve with the appropriate therapy.

## Neuroplasticity

The brain's ability to reorganize itself, known as neuroplasticity, plays a crucial role in both the development of addiction and recovery. Addiction can alter neural pathways, but recovery-oriented treatments aim to rewire these pathways to support healthier behaviors.

To be in recovery means that the brain is learning new skills and habits in order to change behaviors. Through the use of a combination of medication, therapy, and counseling, a person can be helped to adopt new behaviors as a result of changes in neuroplasticity.

Here are some examples of how therapies may alter brain functioning during recovery.

## Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)

Cognitive behavioral therapy affects several regions of the brain and has been shown to have positive effects on recovery. It also improves connectivity between regions of the brain, resulting in enhanced integration and functioning.

### Prefrontal cortex

CBT generates increased activity in the prefrontal cortex. This may result in better executive functions such as decision-making, planning, and the regulation of emotions. The result for the person undergoing the therapy is that they have better control over their thoughts and behaviors. They are less likely to engage in compulsive behaviors.

### Extended amygdala

CBT has been shown to reduce activity in the extended amygdala and may result in reduced anxiety and negative thoughts around a person's self-assessment of their ability to remain in recovery.

### Anterior cingulate cortex

CBT increases activity in the anterior cingulate cortex, which may have positive effects for the person undergoing therapy. They may have better reframing of thoughts, reduction of fear and anxiety, better problem-solving, and improved regulation of attention and emotional responses.
### Mindfulness-based interventions (MBIs)

MBIs are being used to support the recovery of people with substance use disorders. Lorenzetti et al. reviewed the evidence generated by functional MRI (fMRI) studies of brain function. They found that MBIs improved brain function in regions associated with substance-related stress, drug quantity, and cravings, mainly in the anterior cingulate cortex and the prefrontal cortex.
![[Pasted image 20260615115357.png]]
They also found some evidence that MBIs were related to behavioral measures of stress, mindfulness, and impulsiveness. These changes were associated with functional changes in the insula, ventral striatum, and inferior frontal gyrus.

The number of studies reviewed was small and Lorenzetti et al. recommended that more research was needed in this area.

## Pharmacological interventions and recovery

Pharmacological interventions for substance use disorders are closely linked to neuroplasticity, which is the brain's ability to reorganize and adapt its structure and function in response to experiences, including drug use and treatment. These interventions facilitate recovery by promoting positive neuroplastic changes, helping the brain to heal and adapt away from the patterns established by substance use.

Select each heading to learn more about how pharmacological interventions can support recovery.
### Reversing maladaptive changes

Chronic substance use can lead to maladaptive neuroplastic changes in the brain, particularly in areas involved in reward, motivation, and decision-making. Medications like naltrexone and buprenorphine help to normalize these changes by modulating neurotransmitter systems and reducing the reinforcing effects of drugs.
### Supporting synaptic plasticity

Some medications can enhance synaptic plasticity, which is crucial for learning and memory. For example, acamprosate helps to stabilize glutamatergic neurotransmission, which can support the brain's ability to form new, healthier neural connections.
### Reducing cravings and withdrawal symptoms

By alleviating withdrawal symptoms and reducing cravings, medications like methadone and nicotine replacement therapy help individuals avoid relapse. This support allows the brain to gradually adapt to the absence of the substance, promoting healthier neural pathways.
### Targeting specific neurotransmitters
Different substances affect various brain regions differently. For instance, opioids primarily impact the brain's reward system, while stimulants affect areas related to executive function. Pharmacological treatments are designed to target specific neurotransmitters active in these regions that are related to addiction and dependency. This aids in the recovery of normal brain function.
