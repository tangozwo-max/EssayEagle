## M5 W1 P09 – Social and environmental risk factors for substance use

**Read this excerpt about discrimination and substance use:**

"A study conducted among 2,315 African-American and white college students in the United States showed that African-American women who reported having experienced discrimination in the past were more than three times more likely than those who did not to report recent cannabis use.

In parallel, the rate of cannabis use was also elevated among black women who reported being homosexual, suggesting that discrimination associated with different types of minority status identities can accumulate and potentially synergize, consistent with the intersectional framework of health."

\[Source: United Nations\]

Substance misuse can occur at any stage of a person's life. However, studies show that use typically begins in adolescence.

Research suggests that adolescents with substance use disorders also have high rates of co-occurring mental health conditions.

Social and environmental factors influencing the development of substance use disorders include **the home and family environment**, especially during childhood and in cases of adverse childhood experiences (ACEs). Additionally, parents or older family members who use drugs or misuse alcohol can increase children's risk of future drug problems.

\[Source: National Institute on Drug Abuse\]

Studies show that friends and other peers at school or in other social environments who use drugs can sway even those without risk factors to try drugs for the first time.

Having difficulties at school academically or socially can put a child at further risk for using or misusing substances later on in life.

As risk factors, socioeconomic and environmental inequalities may be thought of within the context of a vicious circle of substance misuse.

Poverty, limited education, and ACEs drive substance misuse and by the same token, substance misuse drives poverty, adverse experiences, and limitations in pursuing education and employment.

\[Source: United Nations\]

Social exclusion caused by the vicious circle of socioeconomic factors leading to substance use can cause homelessness.

Treatment and recovery outcomes tend to be worse for homeless people who use substances because of the following factors:

- poor access to healthcare
- inadequate living conditions and sanitation
- poor personal hygiene
- needle-sharing
- multiple comorbidities including mental health conditions
- immune dysfunction due to stress, alcohol misuse, and/or substance misuse
- poor adherence to medication regimes and follow-up appointments
- poor follow-up or loss to follow-up in services

Experiences of violent conflict is another risk factor for substance use. A systematic review and meta-analysis examining the findings of six studies showed an increase in opioid use before and after situations of armed conflict, as well as high levels of opioid use among persons displaced as a result of armed conflict.

It is thought that the factors influencing these findings include lack of economic opportunities, changes in social norms (e.g., drug use becoming normalized as a coping strategy rather than stigmatized), and increases in the availability of drugs as consequences of upheaval.

In qualitative research conducted in Libya involving a study of 31 people—including 16 who used drugs—increases in drug availability, the disruption of healthy recreational activities, and stress and trauma resulting from armed conflict and political unrest were the most frequently cited reasons for cannabis or opiate use.

\[Source: United Nations\]

## Country economic status and substance use disorders

Socioeconomic inequalities in relation to drug use disorders have mostly been studied in high-income settings, where it has been shown that the socioeconomic conditions of people and their communities are associated with patterns of drug use disorders. However, the rare studies conducted in middle-income and low-income countries suggest that associations between socioeconomic disadvantage and the risk of drug use disorders in these settings are weaker.
