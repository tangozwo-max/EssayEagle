## Engagement with interventions

Substance misuse is a significant public health concern. People experiencing substance misuse disorder have an increased risk for mortality and morbidity. They may also lose family, social support, financial stability, and the ability to make economic contributions; become involved with the criminal justice system; and become marginalized by society.

Therefore, it is in society’s interest to help people engage with treatment. Greater engagement has been shown to be linked to better outcomes.

Services need to recognize that there is no "one size fits all" approach to treating people who are experiencing difficulties with substance misuse, because people may be using substances for different reasons and because of different life experiences.

Efforts to improve engagement include:

- diversified treatment settings (hospitals, clinics, outpatient programs, integrated service models)
- use of ad hoc opportunities for screening and brief intervention outside substance treatment programs
- problem-to-service matching designs to align a person's needs with services offered

### Behavioral addictive disorders

As you learned earlier in this module, there are other addictive disorders that are separate to, and are often comorbid with, substance use disorders. Examples of these are gambling disorder, shopping disorder, or gaming disorder.

Generally, interventions for these conditions are nonpharmacological, although research is looking at pharmacological interventions such as antidepressants as well.

## Barriers and facilitators to engagement with care

To be effective, people should be fully engaged with the therapies and treatments suggested by the multidisciplinary team. However, there are a number of barriers to this, which can be seen at the individual, social, and structural levels.

\[Source: Farhoudian et al.\]

| Level | Barriers | Facilitators |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Individual | - incorrect belief about treatment - perceived fears - personal traits - psychiatric comorbidities | - personal motivation |
| Social | - stigma and lack of social support - family factors - friends network - problems with therapeutic team | - supportive family - influential, safe peers - supportive, trusting relationship with treatment team |
| Structural | - problems with care - provider services - legal barriers - policy barriers | - setting of care provider services - logistics of care programs - policy and other organization |

### Psycologists
As an initial therapy, José could have taken part in motivational interviewing (MI) to help him work out the pros and cons of changing his life and recovering from substance use disorder. MI could also help José to have the motivation to engage with his care plan. The advantage of MI is that it can be used by many different qualified professionals, including, for example, social workers.

When he was ready to change, cognitive behavioral therapy could help him change his thought patterns around the cues and behaviors that lead him to use substances.

Therapy could be delivered either while he was in prison or in a community setting on release.

### Psychiatrists

The psychiatrist could help José explore any underlying mental health conditions and recommend any appropriate therapies or pharmacological interventions for these.

The psychiatrist could also supervise medically assisted withdrawal for José to reduce or end his substance use. They would prescribe the necessary medications to support him through the withdrawal symptoms. They would also meet regularly with José to monitor his progress and adjust treatment plans as necessary.

### Mental healthcare nurses

The nurse could monitor José's physical health and manage any medical issues that arise from his substance use or withdrawal. They could also monitor his medication and report on his response to the interventions.

They could provide education on the effects of substance use on his body and the importance of maintaining his physical health.

Nurses can offer day-to-day compassionate support to encourage José to engage with and adhere to his care plan.

### Counselors and other therapists

José needs a counselor with whom he has a trusting, supportive relationship. The counselor and the psychologist take on similar roles and build on the work of each other, although not at the same time. They also provide ongoing support with regular check-ins. The support they could offer could include:

- developing a relapse prevention plan with José
- helping José to develop coping strategies that do not involve substance use
- putting in place crisis intervention to help José when he feels at risk of relapse or has significant stress
- encouraging José to engage with a support network such as Narcotics Anonymous
- helping José to set realistic short- and long-term goals
- providing positive reinforcement to keep José on his recovery path

### Peer support workers

A peer support worker who has experienced substance use disorder and recovery could relate to José's experiences, which would create a sense of trust and understanding that might be hard to achieve with other professionals. They could act as a role model, demonstrating that recovery is possible.

By offering nonjudgmental and empathetic support, they could help José feel understood and less isolated. The motivation and encouragement could help José stay engaged with his care plan and make positive changes.

Peer support workers can facilitate support groups where individuals can share their experiences and support each other. They can help with building coping skills and preventing relapses.

On José's release, a peer support worker can help his transition back into society, advocating for his needs and helping him build a support network.
