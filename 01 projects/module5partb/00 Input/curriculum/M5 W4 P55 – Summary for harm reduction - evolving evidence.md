## Summary for harm reduction: evolving evidence

## Key learning points

You have completed this section. Review each key learning point before you progress.

- Evidence on effective harm reduction strategies for the use of **synthetic drugs** is currently lacking.
- Evidence from Canada demonstrates that take-home naloxone and fentanyl programs are found to reduce overdose mortality among program participants and in the community.
- A harm reduction initiative called DRIVE in Vietnam involved peer advocates identifying people in the community who needed mental health support. Rates of depression, psychotic disorder, and suicide risks among substance users with mental health disorders reduced significantly among participants of this initiative.
- Culturally sensitive programs that respect cultural values and practices are more likely to engage people from diverse backgrounds. When these programs incorporate cultural values, trust is built with participants, their families, and communities.
- Harm reduction initiatives have been viewed as radical by some, with stiff opposition on a community level to such services as supervised injection sites. Critics believe that harm reduction gives the wrong message to children and that drug use is a law enforcement issue (rather than sociocultural).
- Policymakers, academics, and people who use(d) drugs come together at an international conference on harm reduction every 2 years to discuss strategies for the responsible regulation of substance use within a post-prohibitionist context.
