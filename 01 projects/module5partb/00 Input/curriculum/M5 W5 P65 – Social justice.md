## Social justice

"Nothing has contributed more to the systematic mass incarceration of people of color in the United States than the War on Drugs."

Michelle Alexander

American writer, attorney, and civil rights activist

In June 1971, President Nixon of the United States, officially declared a War on Drugs, stating that drug abuse was **public enemy number one**.

The impact that the War on Drugs campaign has had on black communities is generating conversations about social justice. There have been huge imprisonment rates for low-level drug-related crimes. In the United States, this has a huge impact on people's future employment and income.

\[Source: History.com\]

Michelle Alexander has highlighted the impacts that the War on Drugs campaign has had on minority communities. These impacts include:

- incarceration policy for minor drug crimes that have a disproportionate effect on socially disadvantaged communities
- people convicted of drug crimes, particularly in the USA, being disenfranchised and subject to legal discrimination in housing, employment, and health care, which increases their difficulty in reintegrating into society upon release
- incarceration leading to increased hepatitis and HIV infections due to drug use in prisons
- the incarceration approach not resulting in decreased crime (In some cases, it has increased crime due to social breakdown.)
- children in these societies being criminalized at a young age and expected to go to prison at some point in their lives

\[Source: Alexander\]

Addressing addiction and the consequences of substance misuse poses a significant challenge for society. In the USA, the substance misuse epidemic has led to a higher rate of drug-related deaths per capita than any other country, with over 106,000 deaths reported in 2021 alone.

While global research advocates for a public health approach to substance use, the USA has been slow to shift from a criminalized policy framework. Many Western countries have adopted a more humanistic, research-informed public health model over the past two decades.

However, the National Center on Addiction and Substance Abuse in the USA highlights a gap in services in the US, noting that despite the availability of evidence-based tools and practices for screening and treatment, they are seldom utilized.
