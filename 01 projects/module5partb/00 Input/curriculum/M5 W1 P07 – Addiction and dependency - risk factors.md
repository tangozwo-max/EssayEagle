Many different factors impact a person's tendency to develop an addiction or dependency. Among these factors are genetics, socioeconomic background, psychological traits (e.g., resilience, coping skills), certain neurodivergent conditions like ADHD, and comorbid mental health conditions (e.g., PTSD).

Experts link many substance use disorders and compulsive behaviors to risk and protective factors that occur in childhood and adolescence.

\[Source: National Institute on Drug Abuse\]

When addictive behavior was first studied close to a century ago, people with addictions were considered to be morally flawed and lacking in willpower. The National Institute on Drug Abuse comments that these societal views resulted in substance misuse being treated as a moral failing rather than a health condition. Correspondingly, this attitude led to an emphasis on punishment rather than prevention and treatment.

A century later, pioneering neurobiological discoveries have revolutionized scientists' understanding of compulsive substance use. However, there is evidence that some in society have not caught up with science, and people with substance use disorders or dependencies are still treated as though their condition is a moral failing.

\[Source: National Institute on Drug Abuse\]

The Rat Park experiment is one of the most famous studies in addiction research. This experiment was designed to challenge the prevailing belief that drugs themselves (i.e., the physiological effects specifically) are the primary reason for addiction.

Before this experiment, most addiction studies were conducted using rats isolated in small, barren cages. These rats were given access to two water bottles—one containing pure water and the other laced with morphine or heroin. The rats almost always consumed the drugged water until they overdosed or died. This was interpreted as evidence that drugs are so powerfully addictive that they will override an animal's basic survival instincts.

In the late 1970s, Canadian psychologist Bruce K. Alexander hypothesized that the ***environment*** in which the rats were kept might play a significant role in their drug consumption. To test this, he created "Rat Park"—a spacious cage filled with things that would enrich a rat's life, including other rats to socialize with, plenty of food, toys, and space to exercise and play.

The rats were given access to the same two water bottles—one with plain water and the other with drug-laced water. Within this experiment, the rats consumed far less of the drug-laced water than the isolated rats did. Some of the rats even chose to avoid the drugged water altogether. Conversely, when rats from Rat Park were later transferred to isolated cages, their drug consumption increased.

Broadly, the experiment indicated that the vast majority (close to 100%) of rats overdose on drugs when they are isolated, and close to 0% overdose when they have happy and connected lives.

This experiment suggested that addiction might not be **solely** related to the type of chemical hooks present in drugs like heroin. The researchers concluded that drug misuse could also be significantly influenced by social and environmental factors like poverty, loneliness, and lack of engaging activity.

Experts do note that while the Rat Park experiment was groundbreaking, it has faced criticisms—particularly with relation to the experiment's applicability (or generalizability) to humans.
