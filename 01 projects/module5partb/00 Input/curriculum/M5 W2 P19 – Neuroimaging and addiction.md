## M5 W2 P19 – Neuroimaging and addiction

With advances in neuroimaging, it has proved possible to show changes in the brains of people who use substances such as cocaine, heroin, or alcohol.

A radioligand called [[raclopride|\[C11\]Raclopride]] is a competitive dopamine antagonist and it competes with dopamine at a sub-type of dopamine receptor sites ([[d1-d2-receptors|D2]]) and therefore gives an indication of how much dopamine is released in different brain regions. A higher Raclopride signal equates to low dopamine release, conversely, a low Raclopride signal equates to high dopamine release.

Researchers compared dopamine level changes in cocaine-addicted subjects who were watching either a neutral video (nature scenes) or a cocaine use video. It showed that less \[C11\]Raclopride was bound while watching the cocaine use video, and this reduction directly correlated with self-reported cravings. The subjects with the highest scores on measures of withdrawal and addiction severity had the largest dopamine changes in the dorsal striatum.

As you learned earlier, all substances of abuse can cause dysfunctions in the dopamine reward system. One particular effect is to reduce the number of dopamine receptors available. The loss of dopamine receptors is part of the tolerance process. This effect can be detected over long periods.

Neurological issues from cocaine use are well-known. Volkow et al. suggest that chronic cocaine use can also lead to brain dysfunction without obvious symptoms.

They examined brain glucose metabolism in 21 chronic cocaine users and 18 healthy controls. The cocaine users were tested 1–6 weeks after their last use, and seven were retested after 3 months of abstinence.

Overall brain glucose metabolism was similar between the two groups. However, cocaine users showed significantly lower metabolic activity in 16 out of 21 left frontal regions, and 8 out of 21 right frontal regions. These reductions persisted even after 3–4 months of detoxification and were associated with the amount and duration of cocaine use.

The study concludes that chronic cocaine users have reduced frontal brain metabolism, which remains even after months of not using the drug.

NB: Colors at the red end of the spectrum equate to high glucose metabolism, and those at the purple end equate to low glucose metabolism.

Schinz et al. measured cortical thickness and investigated how the natural process of cortical thickness shrinkage associated with aging—referred to as Brain Age Gap Estimation (BrainAGE)—is affected in cocaine users. They also explored whether this process occurs more rapidly and severely compared to the typical brain aging process in healthy controls.

To assess this, the researchers utilized structural MRI and machine learning techniques to analyze imaging data from 74 patients with cocaine use disorder (CUD) and 62 matched healthy controls.

The images on the left are from healthy controls—the top is the left hemisphere and the bottom is the right hemisphere. The images on the right are from people with CUD. The warmer colors indicate areas of cortical thinning.
