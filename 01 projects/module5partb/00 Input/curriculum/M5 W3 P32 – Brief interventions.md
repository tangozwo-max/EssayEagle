## Brief interventions

Brief interventions tend to take place outside of substance use treatment. They are short and low intensity with a focus on counseling and education. They generally take between 5 and 60 minutes and consist of no more than 5 sessions.

Brief interventions are designed to improve a person’s motivation to address their substance misuse. There is evidence to show that they give an impetus to a person to set about changing their behavior. These interventions are often delivered by healthcare staff who are not mental health professionals.

The availability of brief interventions for substance use varies across the world according to cultural, economic, and legal factors.

Alcohol use in Africa varies across countries ranging from high abstention to high consumption. Where high volume consumption is present, there are severe health and social consequences.

Ghosh et al. reviewed the outcomes of brief intervention programs for alcohol misuse and depression across low-middle-income countries. They found that brief interventions showed some impact on alcohol use. A single, nurse-delivered session seemed to be the most effective.

\[Source: Ghosh et al.\]

Brief interventions are delivered in nonspecialist settings that involve giving feedback on a person’s level of risk, encouraging people to talk about their reasons for change, and identifying strategies to support substance reduction goals.

NICE (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence) recommendations are that brief interventions focused on motivation should be offered whenever the opportunity arises if staff or the service user has concerns about substance use. This is usually to people who have limited contact with drug services, e.g., those using a needle exchange program or attending a primary care setting.

The interventions should normally consist of 2 sessions, each lasting 10–45 minutes, where ambivalence about drug use can be explored. They can also explore possible treatments. The aim is to increase the motivation to change behavior and to provide nonjudgmental feedback.

\[Source: UK Government; NICE clinical guidelines\]

Brief interventions in primary care are rare in most countries in the Arab world. Alcohol is less of an issue than polysubstance misuse as its use is discouraged by Islam. However, alcohol use may be underreported, particularly among women, due to cultural and legal issues.

\[Source: Alharbi et al.\]

In Thailand, substance use is a major issue in the burden of disease. Thailand has worked to develop prevention and treatment packages to combat the problem. This has included the employment and training of healthcare workers to support early intervention and engagement with treatment.

One of the approaches they have used is the World Health Organization (WHO) ASSIST program (alcohol, smoking, and substance involvement screening test) and its associated brief interventions (ASSIST-BI).

Assanagkornchai et al. worked on developing and implementing an ASSIST-BI program in parts of Thailand. The sessions took around 9 minutes per patient. They found that the program reduced the risk of substance use among participants and worked best if it was administered as soon as possible after screening.

\[Source: Assanangkornchai et al.\]
