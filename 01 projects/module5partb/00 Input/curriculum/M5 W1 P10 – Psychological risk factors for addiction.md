## M5 W1 P10 – Psychological risk factors for addiction

Addiction and compulsive behaviors are complex phenomena influenced by a range of psychological risk factors. These factors can increase vulnerability to developing such behaviors and make it harder to break free from them.

The same psychological factors that influence the development of mental health conditions or a resilience against trauma can also influence the development of a substance use disorder or dependency.

### Personality factors

Psychology researchers have observed that certain personality traits can impact a person's risk for developing a use disorder or compulsive behavior.

Traits that can buffer against the development of problematic use or behavior include:

- positive self-esteem (particularly in adolescents)
- an optimistic outlook
- coping and resilience skills

On the other hand, people who lack healthy coping strategies may turn to substances or compulsive behaviors as a way to manage stress, emotions, or life challenges. One study on the personality traits associated with internet addiction suggests that introverted adolescents show increased online activity when compared with emotionally stable users, which may enhance the risk for internet addiction.

\[Source: Alim et al.; Peris et al.\]

### Emotion regulation and impulse control

**Emotion regulation** refers to any process or action by which a person influences their emotions or their emotional expression.

Studies show that adults with substance use disorders (SUDs) display greater difficulties in emotion regulation compared to adults without SUDs. Compared to those without a SUD, individuals with a SUD had the most difficulty controlling impulsive behaviors and accessing strategies to regulate their emotions effectively.

An individual can regulate emotions at multiple points, including the situations that they seek or avoid, how they think about their experience and how they express their feelings.

\[Source: Stellern et al.\]

### Stress and trauma

A history of trauma and stress as a result of adverse childhood experiences is a major risk factor for later use of substances.

Dysfunctional family environments, lack of support, or having family members with addiction issues can elevate risk.

Stressful life events, like work-related stress, traumatic experiences, grief, divorce, living in a high-conflict region, and poor coping strategies may impact the risk of addiction. It is known that the feeling of stress relief may enhance the pleasure of the substance use, which compounds the addictive aspects of the use.

\[Source: Stellern et al.\]

### Comorbid mental health conditions

- **Depression:** Depression often co-occurs with addiction, as individuals may use substances to self-medicate or escape depressive symptoms.
- **Anxiety Disorders:** High levels of anxiety can lead to compulsive behaviors as a way to alleviate anxiety temporarily.
- **Personality Disorders:** Disorders such as borderline personality disorder or antisocial personality disorder are often associated with higher rates of addiction.

\[Source: European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction\]

The use of social networking sites and the internet has grown in popularity over the last few decades, and **internet addiction** is on the rise. It is believed that psychological risk factors like personality traits and emotion regulation play a significant role in internet addiction.

Across Europe, studies reported prevalence ranging between 4.4% to 13.5% for pathological internet use and between 14.3% and 54.9% for problematic internet use.

Adolescents who display characteristics of internet addiction feel the constant need to be online, usually on social networking sites. According to experts, in extreme cases, such behavior may produce psychological problems that traditionally correspond to substance-related addictions.

Addiction symptoms would include sleep disturbance, angry or agitated reactions when forced to disconnect, and losing track of time while online.

\[Source: Peris et al.\]
