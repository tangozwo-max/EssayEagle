## Impact on partners

"It's like being stuck on an unsafe and unpredictable rollercoaster."

From Weimand et al.

Weimand et al., working in Norway, explored the life experiences of people who shared their lives with a partner who used substances. They found that every aspect of people's lives was affected, with this falling into three categories:

1. stigma, dilemmas, and shame
2. lack of safety, security, and support
3. searching for hope and meaning

Weimand et al. concluded that these partners need support to help them deal with the challenges but they often found it difficult to ask for help.

\[Source: Weimand et al.\]

Weimand et al. broke the three main categories down into further sub-themes.

### Dilemmas and challenges
**Overwhelming dilemmas**  
Participants often faced difficult decisions alone, including:

- balancing children's understanding of the situation with protecting them from too much information
- containing children's feelings about the other parent

**Loneliness and responsibility**  
Participants felt increasingly alone, taking on all family responsibilities, as:

- they had no one to share worries or make decisions with
- having sole responsibility affected their parenting role, sometimes needing to protect children from the other parent

**Protector role**

Participants felt responsible for the well-being of all family members. Therefore, they engaged in:

- concealing the partner's addiction to maintain peace
- protecting children from threats, violence, and the influence of the addicted parent
- protecting the partner by enabling their addiction, which was challenging

**Strain related to the partner's substance use was due to the following:**

- Efforts to stop the partner's substance use failed, causing stress.
- When their actions didn't help, the partner left the relationship.

**Communication challenges** **included:**

- walking a tightrope in conversations with the partner
- trying to be supportive could lead to conflict
### Impact on oneself, children, relationships, and social life
**Stigma and shame:**

- affected self-view, children’s perception, relationships, and social life
- fueled fear of others discovering the substance use and its effect on the family

**Embarrassment and stress included:**

- embarrassment over the partner's drunken behavior
- stress over criminal actions, including arrests

**Feelings of shame and guilt emerged over**:

- the partner’s substance misuse and behavior
- feeling responsible for not being able to help, or over the SUP
- not leaving when the SUP affected the children (mainly guilt).

**Concealment and openness**:

- Initial efforts to hide the situation eventually became impossible.
- Some found relief in being open about the problem through peer support.

**Social life impact included**:

- avoiding social settings due to the partner’s behavior
- embarrassment and avoidance of socializing with the partner

**Relationship strain**:

- The substance use affected relationships emotionally, practically, and socially.
- Well-being was closely tied to the partner’s substance use patterns.
- Fear of relapse and worsening conditions appeared.

**Loss of confidence**:

- Participants lost confidence in their partner’s ability to be a partner and parent
- Trust-breaking behavior led to feelings of betrayal and distress.
### Lack of safety and security
**Strain from SUP on family safety and security included:**

- experiences of manipulation, harassment, conflict, threats, and violence
- unpredictability of substance use, and resulting behavioral changes
- feeling insecure and being unsafe

**Lack of trust in judgment:**

- Persons who used substances often argued that the situation was exaggerated by the other partner (participant).

**Detrimental effects of threats and violence included:**

- harm to both children and participants
- exposure to physical violence, sometimes witnessed by the children
- fear for their lives due to severe violence

**Threats of suicide**:

- contributed to a lack of control and predictability
### Lack of support
**Deficient support**:

- This was mostly related to health services, but also to limited support from family and friends.
- Strains included bodily stresses, sleep disturbances, concentration problems, depression, and anxiety.

**Never-ending worry occurred due to**:

- fear, grief, and stress over the impact of the partner’s SUP on the children
- worries about developing a SUP themselves

**Grief and stress occurred due to the**:

- loss of fulfillment of their own and the children's life expectations
- negative development of the partner’s life
- constant need to safeguard the children

**Need for support:**

- Insufficient treatment and follow-up from health services existed for the partner and the participant.
- Exclusion from the partner’s treatment plan induced worries about the partner’s condition and discharge.

**Hospitalization concerns**:

- The partner's hospitalization was a safe respite, but uncertainties regarding voluntary admissions occurred.

**Concealment and paradox**:

- Concealing the substance use made asking for support feel paradoxical.
- Peer support groups helped participants realize the need for support and encouraged openness.

**Openness leading to support**:

- Openness about the situation led to obtaining family and network support.
### Loss of hope
Many partners started off hoping that the substance use would stop, perhaps if they or the user were expecting a child. Often, though, this did not happen. Partners lived in constant fear of relapse and, over time, lost hope that things would change. They describe meeting a point of no return when all hope was lost.
### Gaining new meaning
**Re-establishing hope and finding meaning**:

- was achieved after reaching a point of no return
- required distance from the substance use problem (SUP), either through the partner’s recovery or ending the relationship

**Challenges of living with a partner with a SUP**:

- The partner prioritized substance use despite negative consequences.

**Positive changes from turning points included**:

- improved self-understanding and awareness of the situation’s impact
- reconciliation as part of acquiring new hope, requiring significant effort

**Gaining new insights and strength**:

- Participants found they had more strength than they believed.
- They gained a better understanding of others through their experiences.
### Feeling vulnerable
**Continued vulnerability**:

- Participants still felt weak and vulnerable despite reflecting on their challenging period.
- Others might see them as strong, but they hid their vulnerability.

**Ongoing worries and strains**:

- Continued worries and strains occurred even after ending relationships due to co-parenting responsibilities.
- Ensuring the children’s safety and happiness remained hard work, especially if the other parent still misused substances.

**Emotional and physical impact**:

- Participants felt physically and emotionally vulnerable.
- Some still experienced health issues and problems with staying in work, despite improvements in the SUP situation.

**Lack of joy and fulfillment**:

- Participants struggled to feel joy and fulfillment in life.
- Example: Gerry felt something was missing in his life despite having everything he needed.

**Trust and safety concerns**:

- Participants questioned whether they would ever feel safe or trusting again.
- Ending the relationship did not eliminate the risk of strains, stresses, and burdens on the family.

\[Source: Weimand et al.\]

> [!-info] -info
> **Further info:**
> 
> Participants who had separated from their partners felt additional strains, including:
> 
> - challenges surrounding children's visits to the parent with substance use problems
> - deciding if visits would help the ex-partner recover, and the potential cost of these to the children
> - fear of unpleasant or dangerous situations during visits
> - handling the children's disappointment when the parent with the SUP didn't show up or was under the influence
