## Motivation to change

If needed, refresh your knowledge of the [stages of change](https://learn.iheed.org/courses/758/modules/items/357212).

Relapse occurs frequently among people who are on a recovery journey. It may propel people back to any part of the change process as outlined in the **transtheoretical model**. It was initially thought to be part of the change process, but this was found to be inappropriate and it is now thought of as affecting the process but outside it.

People who relapse show varied responses. Some vow not to try again but others consider or plan their next attempt to get back into recovery. One positive way to view relapse is as a learning opportunity. People should be encouraged to study what triggered the relapse and work out strategies for coping with the situation should it arise again. In this way, as people cycle through the change process, they gradually approach sustained behavior change.

**Recovery** can be a reduction in the addictive behavior or total abstinence. Reducing further or maintaining reduction is the goal of the Action and Maintenance stages, but if the person abandons the change attempt, they revert to the pre-Action stages.

When a person relapses, they may experience feelings of failure, lose hope, and give up on the prospect of change. They may even reenter the Precontemplation stage.

The **Action** stage is considered to be the first 3–6 months of a change attempt and is very challenging for the service user. Single reoccurrences of the behavior or brief lapses to prior patterns of behavior without abandonment of the change goal are common.

In the **Maintenance** stage, slips and lapses may also occur, which is an indication of the difficulty of maintaining long-term behavioral change. People are particularly at risk during stressful or unanticipated events in their lives. Service users should be encouraged to anticipate, learn from the events, and problem-solve, and these should be included in any discussion of relapse and relapse prevention.

## The biopsychosocial model

Gordon Marlatt put forward the biopsychosocial model in 1985. He saw relapse as being **characterized as a failure to cope with cognitive distortions and cues**. People who relapse feel vulnerable and lose confidence in their ability to sustain their recovery.

This model was further developed by Marlatt and Witkiewitz to include the importance of coping activities, or lack of them, in relapse. Relapse is now seen as a multidimensional unfolding process that involves the individual and transactions with their environmental context.

\[Source: Marlatt et al.; Witkiewitz, Marlatt\]

"Clinicians need to find a balance between normalizing the fact that multiple change efforts are often needed, while also avoiding the belief that relapse is inevitable or pre-determined. Most importantly, they need to hold the hopeful perspective that change is possible over the long term."

Marlatt and Witkiewitz
