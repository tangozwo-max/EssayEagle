## How dopamine works

The three dopamine pathways in the brain involved in addiction and dependency, are the mesocortical, the nigrostriatal, and the [[mesolimbic-pathway|mesolimbic]]. The mesolimbic pathway, part of the [[reward-system|reward system]], is most relevant to addiction.
![[Pasted image 20260614153336.png]]

Dopamine neurons terminating (releasing dopamine) in the [[nucleus-accumbens|nucleus accumbens]], can stimulate the [[d1-d2-receptors|D1 or D2 families of receptors]] on the Gamma-aminobutyric acid ([[gaba|GABA]]) neurons (located in the nucleus accumbens in the [[basal-ganglia|basal ganglia]]). All receptor types are attached to an enzyme named adenylate cyclase. The D1 family of receptors activates adenylate cyclase, whereas the D2 family of receptors inhibits it.

There is a fourth dopamine pathway, the tuberoinfindibular/tuberohypophyseal pathway. Some antipsychotic drugs block dopamine in this pathway. You will learn more about the actions of antipsychotic drugs in a future module.

When dopamine binds to D1 and D2 receptors in GABAergic neurons, it triggers different intracellular signaling pathways that influence the neuron’s activity and function.

### D1 receptors

**Type:** Gs-coupled receptors

**Location:** D1 receptors are primarily found on the postsynaptic membrane of GABAergic neurons.

**Mechanism:** When dopamine binds to D1 receptors, it activates the Gs protein, which, in turn, stimulates adenylate cyclase. This enzyme increases the production of [[camp-pathway|cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP)]] from ATP (adenosine triphosphate).

**Effects:** The rise in cAMP activates protein kinase A (PKA), which can phosphorylate various target proteins, leading to increased neuronal excitability and enhanced synaptic plasticity. This process generally results in the facilitation of neurotransmitter release and an overall excitatory effect on the neuron.

### D2 Receptors

**Type:** Gi-coupled receptors

**Location:** D2 receptors are found both pre- and postsynaptically on GABAergic neurons.

**Mechanism:** When dopamine binds to D2 receptors, it activates the Gi protein, which inhibits adenylate cyclase, leading to a decrease in cAMP levels.

**Effects:** The reduction in cAMP results in decreased PKA activity, which can lead to reduced phosphorylation of target proteins. This generally results in decreased neuronal excitability and inhibition of neurotransmitter release. Presynaptically, D2 receptors can inhibit the release of dopamine itself, providing a feedback mechanism to regulate dopamine levels.

### Combined effects in GABAergic neurons

**Balance of excitation and inhibition:** The interaction between D1 and D2 receptor activation plays an important role in balancing excitation and inhibition in GABAergic neurons. This balance is crucial for the proper functioning of neural circuits involved in motor control, reward processing, and cognitive functions.

**Impact on behavior and cognition:** Dysregulation of these pathways can lead to various neurological and psychiatric conditions. For example, overactivation of D2 receptors is associated with the negative symptoms of schizophrenia, while underactivation of D1 receptors can impair working memory and cognitive flexibility.

### reward
When the reward circuit in the brain is activated by a pleasurable experience, the brain produces a burst of dopamine. This signals that what has happened is beneficial and should be remembered.
### habit
Dopamine creates changes in the connections between neurons that make it easier to repeat an experience without thinking about it. A habit has been formed.

### surges
Addictive substances produce large surges of [[dopamine]], which reinforces the connection between the substance, the pleasure it produces, and the external factors that were involved in the substance use experience.

Therefore, the act of taking the substance becomes easier to repeat, and a habit is formed. Large surges of dopamine teach the brain to seek the substance at the expense of other, healthier goals and activities.
### cravings
Dopamine helps the brain connect the external cues present during substance use, with the pleasure or reward the substance creates. Repeating the cues causes the brain to crave a new dose of the substance, even if it is not available. Cravings can last for a long time, even if the person has not been taking the substance.

> **Further info:** Dopamine pathways play an important role in addiction and dependency. However, other neurotransmitters and circuits are also involved. An understanding of these systems is still being developed.

## Why are some drugs so addictive?

Drugs cause a much larger surge of dopamine than natural rewards such as exercise. As a result, the brain loses receptors and, therefore, sensitivity to dopamine. Natural rewards are no longer perceived as pleasurable and the person has to use increasingly higher doses of the drug to get the same reward. The mechanism of action may be different between different substances and you will learn more about this later on.
