## M5 W1 P11 – An introduction to substance use care and services

Steven, a nurse at Margo's clinic, asks Margo and Faye for advice on a recent referral:

Steven

Hi Margo and Faye. Our clinic has a recent referral from the hospital’s hepatology department. A woman named Audrey was treated for liver cirrhosis as a result of alcohol misuse. Her doctors hope that she can access supports through our service that will help her. Do you have any advice for an initial meeting with Audrey?

Margo

The most important step is to build a trusting relationship and to provide information that will support Audrey’s decision around evidence-based treatments going forward. Your discussion with her should take place in a setting wherein confidentiality and privacy are respected.

Faye

I agree with Margo. Due to stigma and discrimination, people who misuse alcohol may minimize the issue, so it’s important to build a relationship in an empathetic and non-judgmental way.

Steven

I see that Audrey’s family wish to be involved with support. Can I involve them in any way at this stage?

Margo

The family’s needs and concerns must be considered as alcohol misuse can have far-reaching effects on the lives of so many people. However, Audrey’s confidentiality and privacy must be respected as a first priority. I advise liaising with her as an initial step and asking if she is OK with her family being provided with self-guided information leaflets on alcohol misuse.

Faye

Family meetings can be helpful if the kind of self-guided support Margo mentioned is unlikely to be beneficial. These meetings should provide information, help to identify sources of stress related to alcohol misuse, and explore coping strategies.

Steven

I understand from her doctors that Audrey has what is considered to be a moderate dependence on alcohol but has completed a “readiness to change” questionnaire also, which indicated that she is prepared to take action to stop drinking. What is an appropriate treatment goal at this juncture?

Faye

Abstinence is the appropriate goal for most people with alcohol dependence, and people who misuse alcohol and have significant psychiatric or physical comorbidity—for example, depression or alcohol-related liver disease. Some people may not be able to fully abstain yet.

Margo

Once Audrey has had an initial meeting, we will organize case management to develop an individualized care plan. We’ll monitor the impact of different interventions and revise the care plan if appropriate to do so.

Steven

What kind of interventions might be appropriate for Audrey?

Faye

We would likely look to psychological interventions such as cognitive behavioral therapy as an initial step. In more severe cases, pharmacological interventions are sometimes combined with psychological interventions.

Steven

Thank you both for the information. I'll liaise with you at a later point once we have had an initial meeting with Audrey.

**Rehabilitation** is a type of substance use care service that includes pharmacological, psychological, social, and potentially alternative therapeutic interventions. A holistic approach is adopted for rehabilitation, which may involve in-patient and/or out-patient care.

You will explore more on interventions in weeks to come.

Types of services and treatment facilities for people with substance use disorders (SUDs):

- Detoxification services may involve supervised withdrawal in a hospital or clinic, or withdrawal under the supervision of trained professionals outside of a hospital setting.
- Intensive inpatient or residential treatment is available in many regions. However, it is very expensive and therefore inaccessible to many people.
- Behavioral therapies and counseling can enhance motivation to change substance use behavior.
- Group therapy (e.g., Alcoholics Anonymous or Narcotics Anonymous) provides support from peers.
- Family therapy may help someone to address dysfunctional dynamics and improve relationships.
- Harm reduction services such as needle exchange programs can be helpful to prevent the spread of disease.
- Holistic and complementary therapies can be helpful for people in a position to access them. Such therapies may include mindfulness, meditation, exercise, and art or music therapy.

\[Source: European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction; SAMHSA\]

## The global context

The team spearheaded by Margo is in a position to provide services and individualized care for people with SUDs. However, that is not the situation in every healthcare setting.

According to the World Health Organization's (WHO) global status report on alcohol and SUDs, about 20% of reporting countries do not have any screening and brief intervention programs for substance use, and 20% of the countries that do have the programs report their availability only in some leading national institutions.

Although most countries (about 80%) provide specialized services for people with substance use disorders, in about 10% of these countries, specialized services are available only for the treatment of **acute** conditions, and about 5% of countries have no specialized services for treatment of alcohol or drug use disorders.

The proportion of people with substance use disorders in contact with treatment services varies from less than 1% to no more than 35% in all countries where such data are available. Additionally, more than 40% of responding countries do not collect data on service provision and service utilization for treatment of substance use disorders.

\[Source: WHO\]

### Depression and substance use

People with major depression show a higher vulnerability to developing substance use disorder, and substance users are at higher risk of developing major depression. Such people often have poor treatment outcomes and are at a higher risk of suicide.

**Healthcare professionals should be mindful of the following:**

- Treatment be provided at the same time for both depression and substance use disorder, and ideally in an integrated system.
- Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) are not efficacious in treating mental health conditions where comorbid substance use disorders are also present.
- Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) may help in some cases.
- Antidepressants have little effect on the maintenance of abstinence.
- When an antidepressant is effective in treating acute depression, there is only a relative reduction in the use of the psychoactive substance.
### Anxiety and substance use
There are complex associations between anxiety and substance use:

- Substances may be used for self-medication in case of anxiety.
- Anxiety can be the **result** of substance intoxication or withdrawal.

**Healthcare professionals should be mindful of the following:**

- Little research exists on effective treatment in the case of this comorbidity.
- Psychological interventions like CBT have shown a positive effect, though are insufficient if used alone.
- Combining CBT with antidepressants was found in a comprehensive review to be effective in improving the outcomes for comorbid opioid use disorder and anxiety.
### personality disorder and substance use
People with a personality disorder have more problematic symptoms of substance use disorders than those without a personality disorder.

Antisocial and borderline are the more frequent personality disorders in illicit drug users.

People with this comorbidity are more likely to participate in risky behaviors (e.g., sharing injecting equipment, risky sexual practices) and have more difficulties in complying with treatment.

**Healthcare professionals should be mindful of the following:**

- People with this comorbidity should be offered the same range of treatment options as other people.
- If high-risk behavior persists, people should also be given treatment aimed at reducing the impact of the personality disorder.
- A comprehensive review found that there is no evidence that any pharmacotherapy is particularly beneficial in the comorbidity of personality disorders with substance use disorders.

### eating disorder and substance use
Strong evidence demonstrates that eating disorders and substance use disorders tend to co-occur.

**Healthcare professionals should be mindful of the following:**

- Psychoeducation is recommended on the risks and challenging symptoms of concurrent eating disorder behaviors and substance use.
- It is important to build a collaborative therapeutic relationship.
- Behavioral therapy for eating and substance use disorders is recommended.
- **Dialectical behavioral therapy** was found in a comprehensive review effective in reducing both eating disorders and substance use disorders.

> [!-info] -info
> **Info**: Dialectical behavioral therapy is a type of talking therapy based on CBT but is specially adapted for people who feel emotions very intensely.

### psychosis and substance use
Some people may be at risk (e.g., genetically) of developing psychotic symptoms as a result of substance use.

People can experience an acute psychotic episode in response to substance intoxication or withdrawal, and the most common types of psychosis in substance users are schizophrenia and bipolar disorder.

Antipsychotic medications are typically prescribed, but certain considerations relating to the comorbidity must be taken into account:

- The level and type of substance misuse should be recorded.
- The person who uses substances should be warned about potential interactions with prescribed medication.
- Integrated treatment for psychosis and substance use disorders (i.e., combining psychological and antipsychotic treatments) was found in a comprehensive review to be effective in significantly improving psychotic symptoms and substance use.

\[Source: European Monitoring Centre for Drugs and Drug Addiction\]
