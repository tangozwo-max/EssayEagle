## Impacts of addiction and dependency

Substance use and other addiction harms span health, psychological, relationship, financial, cultural, work, and crime-related issues. Harms form a spectrum in terms of severity and temporality. Harm can occur at different levels:

- individual
- family (partners, children, and wider family)
- community and local society
- wider society

The impacts of addiction and dependency can occur in many areas of life and across all levels.

### Individual and family

Impacts on the individual and their family include the following:

**Financial** impacts include loss of employment, reduced income, erosion of savings, financial insecurity, debt, poor credit ratings, homelessness, and not enough funds to cover basic needs.

**Relationship** impacts include family disruption and disengagement, conflict, domestic violence, neglect, exploitation of relationships, and being victims of crime.

**Emotional and psychological** impacts include distress, mental health disorders, loss of control, insecurity, lack of safety, shame and stigma, and reduced well-being and happiness.

**Health** impacts include physical symptoms, risky behaviors, disrupted sleep patterns, nonadherence to treatment, comorbidities, violence, self-harm or suicide, and poor self-care.

**Cultural** impacts include breaking cultural norms, reduced engagement with culture, exclusion, and stigma.

**Work and study** impacts include presenteeism and absenteeism, loss of employment, unstable employment, breakdown of work and educational relationships, and loss of education.

**Crime** impacts include criminal activity, entry into the criminal justice system, anti-social behavior, and incarceration.

### Community and society

Impacts of addiction and dependency on the community and wider society can occur in the following areas:

**Financial** impacts include strain on welfare resources, debt, business closures, impact on other industries, loss of productivity, degeneration of residential areas, and increased living costs.

**Relationship** impacts include the breakdown of communities and society, social isolation and exclusion, and increased living costs.

**Emotional and psychological** impacts include increased disease burden, social care, and prevention costs.

**Health** impacts include increased burden of disease, regulation and research costs, prevention costs, and long-term disability costs.

**Cultural** impacts include cultural community and identity degeneration, socio-cultural costs, disconnection of young people, and normalization of addiction and dependency.

**Work and education** impacts include the economic costs of reduced productivity, unemployment, crime, and loss of skills in the workforce.

**Crime** impacts include the costs of criminal activity, incarceration, and the criminal justice system.

\[Source: Langham et al.\]

## Learning outcomes

On successful completion of this week, you will be able to:

- analyze the role of biopsychosocial factors influencing individuals, couples, and families affected by addiction, linking to typical health and statutory care interventions such as mental health, social work, and physical health services
- assess the impact of addictions on broader systems, including local communities, connecting to community psychology and third-sector interventions, as well as local health initiatives
- examine the effects of addictions on national healthcare, financial, and criminal justice systems, including at national policy and public health intervention levels
- investigate the implications of addictions on a global scale, considering international policy, global healthcare initiatives, human rights, and the legal and ethical issues involved at the highest level
