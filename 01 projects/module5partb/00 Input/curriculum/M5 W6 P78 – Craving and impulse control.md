## Craving and impulse control

Neuroscience has shown that cravings and impulsive behaviors are linked to specific brain circuits. Recovery-oriented treatments often include strategies to manage cravings and improve impulse control, which can involve strengthening the prefrontal cortex's role in decision-making.

**Cue-induced cravings:** Environmental cues associated with substance use can trigger cravings even after achieving sobriety. Recovery strategies focus on avoiding or managing these triggers to prevent relapse.

The brain forms strong associations between environmental cues and substance use through a process called classical conditioning. These cues can trigger the release of dopamine, leading to cravings.

**Neuroplasticity:** Changing the environment helps the brain form new neural pathways, weakening the old pathways associated with substance use. This process, known as neuroplasticity, is crucial for recovery.

**Stress reduction:** Stress is a significant trigger for cravings. By creating a less stressful environment, individuals can reduce the activation of the brain's stress response systems, which are closely linked to craving and relapse.

## Cue exposure therapy (CET)

Cue exposure therapy seeks to expose people who use substances to the environmental cues that trigger their substance use behaviors. By using repeated exposure, people may reduce their reactivity to the cues.

Up until now, CET has been done in clinical settings that are not so akin to real life. However, trials of virtual reality are now taking place, which put people in a more realistic setting that brings more environmental cues to bear.

CET is based on Pavlovian conditioning principles. In classical conditioning, a neutral stimulus (conditioned stimulus, CS) becomes associated with an unconditioned stimulus (UCS) that naturally elicits a response (unconditioned response, UCR). Over time, the CS alone can evoke a conditioned response (CR). CET aims to break this association through repeated exposure to the CS without the UCS, leading to extinction, where the CR diminishes over time.

For example, in Yang's study, people were exposed to smoking cues in the form of a cigarette and an ashtray or to neutral cues such as a pen or a notebook. The people who were repeatedly exposed to the smoking cues over time without actually smoking eventually showed a reduced response to them.

CET has been shown to be efficacious for short-term reduction of cue cravings, but it is difficult to replicate all the environments in which cues may occur. This may result in people responding to cues by lapsing into using substances.

\[Source: Yang et al.\]

## What is the neuroscience behind CET?

Select each marker to find out.

### Cognitive reappraisal and emotional regulation

CET also involves cognitive processes where individuals learn to reinterpret and manage their emotional responses to triggers. This involves the prefrontal cortex, which is responsible for higher-order cognitive functions.

###  Neural pathways

The amygdala, a brain region involved in fear and emotional processing, plays a crucial role in the formation and extinction of conditioned responses. During CET, changes in the amygdala and prefrontal cortex help reduce the emotional impact of the conditioned stimulus.

### Emotional processing theory

This theory suggests that exposure therapy works by activating the fear memory and allowing new, non-fearful information to be encoded. This process involves the medial prefrontal cortex, which helps regulate the amygdala's response to fear.

This theory is applied in CET by replacing a fear with the cues that trigger craving or unwanted behaviors.
