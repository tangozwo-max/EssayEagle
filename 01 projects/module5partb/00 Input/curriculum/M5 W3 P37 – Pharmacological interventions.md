## Pharmacological interventions

Psychosocial interventions are useful tools to help people with substance use disorders, but sometimes pharmacological interventions are required or preferred by individuals. At present, regulatory guidelines for pharmacological interventions only exist for people with opioid or alcohol misuse disorder.

There are no recommended pharmacological interventions for stimulant (cocaine or methamphetamine) or cannabis use disorders, although research could indicate interventions in the future. The evidence for pharmacological interventions for behavioral disorders such as gambling disorder indicates that nonpharmacological interventions may be more appropriate.

Principles of pharmacological intervention

- Substance use disorder is a long-term, chronic disorder. A treatment plan will need continuous monitoring, evaluation, and adjustment to meet changing needs.
- Individualized treatment plans are tailored to meet the specific needs of each person. They should consider the type of substance use and the severity of the disorder, be trauma-informed, and take into account any comorbidities including mental health conditions.
- Medications should be used with care to manage withdrawal symptoms, reduce cravings, and treat comorbidities. Consider whether this will be on an inpatient or outpatient basis according to the severity of a person's symptoms.
- Pharmacological interventions should be part of a holistic care plan.
- Treatment should be accessible, acceptable, and affordable for those in need. Early interventions increase the chance of successful outcomes.
- Efforts should be made to increase the person’s engagement with family, community, and friends to provide additional motivation and assistance.

> [!-info] -info
> **Further info:** Withdrawal from benzodiazepine use is a significant issue in several countries. Notably, the United States has a high prevalence of benzodiazepine prescriptions and associated withdrawal problems.
> 
> Managing benzodiazepine withdrawal can be challenging due to the potential for severe symptoms, including anxiety, insomnia, and seizures. It's important for individuals to seek medical guidance when discontinuing these medications to ensure a safe and effective tapering process.
