## Defining harm reduction through lived experience

A study by Owczarzak et al. (2024) explores how peer outreach workers in a street-based naloxone distribution program in the US define and practice harm reduction. This study involved 15 outreach workers in Baltimore, Maryland—an urban center characterized by an enduring opioid epidemic.

Participant 6

"It’s not just the Narcan \[naloxone\]. We also can refer people to drug treatment if they want to get treatment and... set people up for HIV tests; any kind of care pertaining to wellness.

... part of harm reduction is just education, people just lacking knowledge. Ignorance, you know, plays a big part in a lot. Just trying to educate the community about where they can go to get help and just encourage them to get help."

Participant 8

"Eventually they \[substance users\] are going to get tired of what they’re doing and they’re going to come ask me, 'Well, how did you get clean,' and then I can tell them. But until they come to me I can’t say anything.

That’s the best way to get somebody to come off drugs. Don’t even mention it: 'Hey, have fun but you be safe.' Eventually you’re going to get tired of shooting that garbage and then you’re going to come ask me, 'How did you get off of drugs? How did you do this or how did you do that?'”

Participant 9

"\[Harm reduction is\]... basically meeting people at their needs. That’s all it is. But the main thing is to help them turn their life around from the negative to the positive. 'You can do it.' Giving them that motivation and incentive.

It’s not gonna happen instantly. I’m telling you, if you’re one of those type of people that want instant gratification, no, it’s not gonna happen like that. But you’ve got to do some work, too, though.”

\[Source: Owczarzak et al.\]

Appreciating that abstinence was not necessarily the goal of harm reduction, participants in the Owczarzak et al. study almost universally placed harm reduction within a broader **recovery** framework. They suggested that recovery could be used to help people undergo a process of decreased substance use or abstinence and therefore become active participants in society.

At the same time, the authors observed that participants did not embrace an overly simplistic idea of recovery (e.g., one that assumed people who use substances have control over their use).

Informed by their experiences in reducing the harm associated with their own substance use, these outreach workers understood that the path to recovery was complicated, nonlinear, and beset with barriers or the potential for relapse.

Participants were also acutely aware of the social determinants of health on substance use, and their associated proximal and distal harms.

By recognizing the heavily influential racial and social factors underlying substance use that produce harms, these outreach workers were able to provide continued support for those actively using substances with a nonjudgmental and non-stigmatizing mindset as well as a deeper understanding of people’s environments.

It is from this position that outreach workers were waiting for the people they encountered during their harm reduction initiatives to indicate their readiness for recovery.

Providing care and support for people who use substances (often from socially excluded populations) is complex. The best approach is trauma-informed, multidisciplinary, person-centered, and holistic. Care should aim to reduce the sources of harm, as well as treat immediate physical and mental health conditions. Care for socially excluded people should include:

- coordinated flexible services
- no stigma
- addressing other chronic diseases, malnutrition, or addiction, with a focus on harm reduction
- addressing social determinants of health, such as housing
- a pragmatic approach to treatment (reduce harm rather than eliminate entirely)
