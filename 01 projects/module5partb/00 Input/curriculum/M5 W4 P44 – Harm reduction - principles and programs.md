## Harm reduction: principles and programs

**Harm reduction** refers to policies, programs, services, and practices that aim to minimize the negative personal and societal impact associated with substance use.

Two people living with substance use disorders have been attending the same alcohol recovery program for 6 months. Person A has not used alcohol for 4 months. Person B has significantly reduced their alcohol intake and their life has improved, but they do not yet feel in a position to stop drinking completely.

**Does the fact that Person B has not stopped drinking alcohol fully mean that this recovery program has been more successful in helping Person A?**

Note your thoughts before continuing.

The principles of harm reduction suggest that the program **has** been effective for Person B.

Harm reduction approaches **aim to reinforce positive change in a person's life, no matter how small or incremental that change may be**.

\[Source: Harm Reduction International\]

Harm reduction focuses on positive change and on supporting people who use substances. Of importance, the harm reduction approach should be free of judgmental attitudes, coercion, stigma, discrimination, or requiring complete abstinence as a precondition of support.

The types of harms that result from substance use include physical harms (e.g., drug-related mortality or injury), psychological harms (e.g., dependence, co-occurring mental health conditions), and social harms (e.g., loss of employment, damaged social relationships, crime).

Harm reduction strategies have been proven to positively impact individual and community health, and over a hundred countries worldwide already have harm reduction policies and/or practices in place.

Evidence shows that harm reduction strategies that are tailored to the unique needs of a person are most effective at reducing the risks associated with substance use. These strategies are effective in reducing the spread of infectious diseases, decreasing overdose deaths, and improving the overall health and well-being of people who use substances.

\[Source: Harm Reduction International; Nutt et al.; Busz et al.\]
