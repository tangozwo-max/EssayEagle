## The range of interventions

There are many different types of care frameworks, but they should all emphasize respect for personal diversity, personal empowerment, and understanding of the impact of trauma and attachment on treatment expectations and experiences. Trauma-informed care is important even if the person is not seeking care for trauma or post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). These frameworks have some overlap, and many services combine elements of them.

Select each heading to learn more about care frameworks.
### Trauma-informed care
Trauma-informed care is an approach that recognizes the widespread impact of trauma and integrates this understanding into all aspects of service delivery. The aim is to create an environment that promotes healing and recovery while minimizing the risk of retraumatization.

The key features of trauma-informed care are:

**Safety:** ensuring physical and emotional safety for both service users and staff.

**Trustworthiness and transparency:** building trust through clear, consistent, and transparent communication.

**Peer support:** incorporating the voices and experiences of people who have lived through trauma.

**Collaboration and mutuality:** emphasizing partnership and collaboration between service users and providers, and recognizing that recovery happens in relationships with shared decision-making and power.

**Empowerment, voice, and choice:** prioritizing service user empowerment and ensuring they have choices and a voice in their treatment and that their autonomy is respected.

**Cultural, historical, and gender issues:** having sensitivity to these issues and responding to the unique needs of different populations.
### Culturally competent care
Culturally competent care is essential for providing effective and respectful health care to people from diverse backgrounds. Key features include:

**Cultural awareness:** recognizing and understanding one’s own cultural beliefs and biases, and how they may affect interactions with people.

**Cultural knowledge:** gaining knowledge about different cultural practices and worldviews to better understand people's perspectives.

**Cultural skills:** developing the ability to assess and address the cultural needs of people effectively.

**Cultural encounters:** engaging in direct interactions with people from diverse backgrounds to improve cultural competence.

**Cultural desire:** demonstrating a genuine interest and commitment to becoming culturally competent.

There is an ongoing debate about how it is not possible to be culturally competent completely (for example, through mandatory training) as everyone's cultural experiences are unique to them.

### Responsive care 
Responsive care ensures that care is tailored to meet a person's individual needs, promoting better outcomes. The key features are:

**Individualized care:** care is personalized based on the unique needs, preferences, and circumstances of each person.

**Timely and appropriate responses:** caregivers respond promptly and appropriately to a person's cues and needs, ensuring that care is both effective and compassionate.

**Patient involvement:** people are actively involved in planning and decision-making processes regarding their care.

**Accessibility:** care is easily accessible, considering physical, social, and economic factors that might affect a person’s ability to receive care.

**Cultural sensitivity:** care is provided in a manner that respects and acknowledges the cultural backgrounds and practices of people.

**Continuous improvement:** the framework emphasizes ongoing assessment and improvement of care practices to better meet the evolving needs of service users.

### Appropriate care
The appropriate care framework focuses on delivering health care that is effective, efficient, and equitable. The key features are:

**Evidence-based care:** utilizing the best available research and clinical expertise to make informed decisions about patient care.

**Patient-centeredness:** prioritizing the needs, preferences, and values of service users in all aspects of care.

**Clinical expertise:** leveraging the skills and knowledge of healthcare professionals to provide high-quality care.

**Resource use:** ensuring that healthcare resources are used efficiently to maximize benefits and minimize waste.

**Equity:** providing fair and impartial care to everyone, regardless of their background or circumstances.

### Person- or patient-centered care
Person- or patient-centered care focuses on providing care that is respectful of, and responsive to, individual preferences, needs, and values. The key features are:

**Respect for individuals**: involving service users in decision-making and treating them with dignity and respect.

**Coordination and integration of care**: care is coordinated across different services, which improves satisfaction and efficiency, and prevents duplication or missed needs.

**Information and education**: providing people with information about their condition, treatment options, and prognosis to help informed decision-making.

**Physical comfort**: addressing pain management, assistance with daily activities, and ensuring a comfortable environment.

**Emotional support**: recognizing and addressing the emotional and psychological needs of service users and their families.

**Social support**: including family and friends in the care process as appropriate, recognizing their role in a person's support system.

**Continuity and transition**: ensuring smooth, supported transitions between different care settings and providers.

**Access to care**: ensuring care is accessible, including timely appointments and easy access to healthcare services.

A person who is trying to recover from a substance use disorder is going through a process of change. This is a journey through a series of stages, and they will need support at various levels during that journey. The model discussed here is the transtheoretical model of change which was created by Prochaska, DiClemente, and Norcross.

Select each step for more information about the stages of the change journey.
### Precontemplation
Precontemplation is the earliest stage of the change journey. People in this stage have not yet reached the point of considering change, and they are sometimes said to be **in denial**.

They do not see their behavior or substance use as a problem and may not see the damage it is causing to themselves or others.

People in the precontemplation stage may also be lacking in the information they need to understand the consequences of their actions.

**At this stage, a multidisciplinary team could:**

**Raise awareness:** educate the person about the risks and consequences of substance misuse.

**Build trust:** establish a nonjudgmental relationship to encourage openness.
### contemplation
During the contemplation stage, a person becomes aware of the benefits of making a change but may feel ambivalent toward the change because of the perceived costs. This uncertainty means that the contemplation stage can last for months or years and many people never get past this stage.

**At this stage, a multidisciplinary team could:**

**Provide information:** offer detailed information about treatment options and the benefits of change.

**Utilize motivational interviewing:** use techniques to enhance the person’s motivation to change.
### preparation
In the preparation stage, a person might begin to make small changes to prepare for a larger change. This could include things such as reducing substance use or direct action such as consulting a therapist.

**At this stage, a multidisciplinary team could:**

**Develop a plan:** help the person create a personalized care plan that includes goals and strategies.

**Provide resource coordination:** connect the person with necessary resources, such as detox programs or support groups.

### action
This is the stage where a person takes direct action in order to accomplish their goals. It takes preparation to be successful at this point, and a person should try to work through the previous steps before embarking on the action stage. They will need support in the form of positive reinforcement and recognition of any achievements.

**At this stage, a multidisciplinary team could:**

**Offer therapeutic support:** provide counseling, behavioral therapies, and medication-assisted treatment if needed.

**Monitor progress:** regularly check in to assess progress and make adjustments to the care plan.

### maintenance
In the maintenance stage, the focus is on avoiding old behaviors and maintaining new ones. People need to seek to avoid the cues that triggered their behaviors in the past and replace them with more positive actions. They should be rewarded by themselves or others when they succeed.

During this stage, people should become more assured that they will be able to continue their change.

**At this stage, a multidisciplinary team could:**

**Provide ongoing support:** continue providing therapy and support groups to prevent relapse.

**Foster relapse prevention:** teach coping strategies and help the person develop a strong support network, perhaps through the peer support worker.

### relapse
Relapses are common in any change journey. They can lead to a person feeling failure, disappointment, and frustration. People should be helped not to let relapses undermine their self-confidence. They should examine what happened, what triggered the relapse, and what they can do to avoid this trigger in the future.

**A multidisciplinary team could:**

**Provide nonjudgmental support:** offer understanding and encouragement to get back on track.

**Facilitate reevaluation:** help a person to reassess their care plan and make necessary changes to address the reasons for relapse.

\[Source: Prochaska, DiClemente\]

> - health behaviors may be too multifaceted to fit within a single stage of readiness
> - individuals can be placed in the same stage whilst having different perspectives on the health behavior
> - people may be on a continuum rather than moving sequentially between stages

## The matched care model

The matched care model is a model used in Scotland. It is based on the Psychological Therapies Matrix, which outlines evidence-based psychological interventions for alcohol and substance use problems. Like the continuum of need, it offers different levels of care with each level stepping up from the one below and offering more intensive support. The matrix also recognizes that different interventions may be appropriate at different stages of a person's recovery journey.

The basis for all service delivery should be **psychologically informed care** that is provided by all clinical and care staff. This links into the recommendation that treatments in substance misuse services should be psychologically informed and trauma-informed.

> [!-info] -info
> **Further info:** The matched care model and the stepped care model look the same. However, in a stepped care model you start at the least intensive intervention, and only when this is unsuccessful do you move up to higher and more resource-intensive interventions. In matched care, the goal is to allocate the right level and intensity of intervention from the beginning.

When creating a care plan with a person, it is important to distinguish between three elements:

- intervention: the work that is being delivered
- practitioner: who will be delivering the work
- situation: the setting and context
![[Pasted image 20260615104739.png]]
As you learned earlier, this type of work is usually delivered by a member of a multidisciplinary team that contains a number of different types of practitioners and laypeople. It may also be delivered by third sector organizations (e.g., charities) or by peer support organizations.

The setting in which support is delivered may vary as well. It can include:

- inpatient units
- outpatient services
- community services
- peer support groups
- third sector organizations
