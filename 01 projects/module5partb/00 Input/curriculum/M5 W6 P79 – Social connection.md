## Social connection

Lower social integration is linked to higher engagement in unhealthy behaviors, such as heavy alcohol and drug use. Conversely, higher social integration is associated with a reduced risk of relapse among drug users. This suggests that fostering strong social connections can play a crucial role in promoting healthier lifestyles and supporting recovery from addiction.

Positive social interactions can provide healthy alternatives to drug use and protect individuals from social stressors. These interactions can offer emotional support, companionship, and a sense of belonging, which are essential for mental well-being. On the other hand, negative social interactions can be stressful and may provoke relapse, highlighting the importance of nurturing positive relationships.

### Insula

Social exclusion activates the insula, another brain region involved in drug craving. Insula activity correlates with the intensity of cravings for addictive drugs. Lesions or targeted stimulation of the insula can reduce cravings, suggesting its significant role in addiction. This highlights the importance of the insula in both social exclusion and addiction processes.

Alcohol use disorder is associated with decreased insula perfusion and gray matter, including a loss of von Economo neurons, which are crucial for social behaviors. Von Economo neurons (VENs) are large, two-branched neurons found in the fronto-insular cortex and anterior limbic area of great apes and humans, but not in other primates. Low insula activity predicts higher relapse risk in methamphetamine users. These findings suggest that the insula plays a critical role in addiction and relapse, particularly in the context of alcohol use.

### Striatal dopamine function

Low availability of striatal dopamine D2/3 receptors is correlated with predisposition for drug use and low social status. This decrease in receptor availability may be caused by drug use and social exclusion, contributing to addiction. Understanding the role of striatal dopamine function is crucial for addressing the neural basis of addiction.

### Connectivity

The insula and striatum are connected, with the anterior insula projecting to the ventral striatum. This bidirectional connectivity influences risk preferences, with stronger connections linked to less risky choices. The anterior insula also connects with the amygdala, which is involved in stress-induced drug-seeking behavior. Reduced connectivity between the insula and amygdala is observed in alcohol addiction.

### Endogenous opioids

Endogenous opioids, through actions on μ-opioid receptors (MORs), play a crucial role in affiliative behaviors and counteracting social stress. MORs are highly expressed in the insula and are involved in social processes. Genetic variations in MORs affect responses to social exclusion and stress. Social acceptance activates MORs in the insula and amygdala, linking social behavior and addiction.


### social support
Social support is a critical component of recovery. Neuroscience research indicates that positive social interactions can enhance brain function and resilience, which is why recovery-oriented treatments emphasize building strong support networks.

Social connection plays a crucial role in supporting recovery from substance use disorder (SUD) through several psychological and neurological mechanisms.

### Emotional support and stress reduction

Social connections provide emotional support, which can reduce stress and improve mental health. This support helps individuals feel understood and less isolated, which is vital for recovery.
### Accountability and motivation

Being part of a supportive social network can increase accountability and motivation. Friends and family can encourage adherence to treatment plans and healthy behaviors, reducing the risk of relapse.
### Neurobiological mechanisms

The brain's reward system, particularly the release of neurotransmitters like dopamine and the hormone and neuropeptide oxytocin (which acts like a neurotransmitter), is influenced by social interactions. Positive social interactions can stimulate these pathways, promoting feelings of well-being and reducing the craving for substances.
### Reduction of social isolation

Social isolation is a significant risk factor for substance use and relapse. Strong social bonds can mitigate this risk by providing a sense of belonging and purpose.
### Cognitive and emotional regulation

Social connections can help individuals develop better coping strategies and emotional regulation. This involves the prefrontal cortex, which is responsible for decision-making and impulse control.
### Therapeutic engagement
Social support can enhance engagement in therapeutic activities, such as group therapy or support groups. These settings provide a platform for sharing experiences and learning from others, which can be incredibly beneficial for recovery.
