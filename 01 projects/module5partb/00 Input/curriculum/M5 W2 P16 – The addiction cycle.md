The symptoms of substance use disorder listed in the DSM-5 fall into four categories. To be diagnosed as having substance use disorder, a person needs to have symptoms from at least two of them. The number of symptoms indicates the severity of the disorder:

- 2–3 symptoms = mild disorder
- 4–5 symptoms = moderate disorder
- more than six symptoms = severe disorder, also known as addiction

### Impaired control

The symptoms indicating impaired control include:

- using more of a substance or more often than intended
- wanting to cut down or stop using but not being able to

### Social problems
Symptoms listed under social problems are:

- neglecting relationships and responsibilities
    
- giving up activities a person used to care about because of their substance use
    
- inability to complete tasks at home, school, or work

### Risky use

The signs of risky use include:

- using in risky settings
- continued use despite known problems

### Physical dependence

The signs of physical dependence are:

- needing more of a substance to get the same effect (tolerance)
- having withdrawal symptoms when a substance is not used

If a person has severe substance use disorder, they are addicted to one or more substances. They move through the [[addiction-cycle|addiction cycle]], though the speed at which they do so, can range from a few hours to weeks. The addiction cycle is a proposed model based on animal studies by Koob et al. It was later confirmed in human studies led by [[nora-volkow|Volkow]].

### Preoccupation/anticipation

In this stage of the cycle, a person has a preoccupation with obtaining the substance they are using. They may have unpleasant physical or psychological problems and will seek the substance to alleviate these. The prefrontal cortex may become preoccupied with finding the substance, to the detriment of daily tasks.

### Binge/Intoxication

During the binge/intoxication stage, a person uses an intoxicating substance and experiences the euphoric, rewarding, or pleasurable effects of that substance. The binge/intoxication stage primarily affects the basal ganglia, producing a surge of dopamine that has pleasurable effects.

### Withdrawal/negative effect

The withdrawal/negative affect stage signifies a person experiencing unpleasant physical and/or psychological symptoms, as the effects of the substance wear off. The person often seeks a new dose of the substance to avoid this. The unpleasant feelings stem from the extended amygdala.


## Allostasis

If needed, refresh your knowledge of [homeostasis](https://learn.iheed.org/courses/758/modules/items/357048) and [physiological regulation](https://learn.iheed.org/courses/758/modules/items/357047).

**[[homeostasis|Homeostasis]]** is a term that describes how the body maintains a stable internal environment. Stability is maintained through feedback loops to keep the brain functioning normally.

**[[allostasis|Allostasis]]** signifies the brain seeking stability through making alterations to its systems. It describes the brain's adaptive changes in the reward-and-stress systems, especially in addiction.

Allostasis uses a feed-forward mechanism to help the brain adapt. It is continuously adjusting to new set points. It anticipates the needs of the body before these arise, and forms part of a broader predictive processing framework.

One advantage of allostasis is that it allows quick responses to changing situations. In this, it is safer than reactive systems, which only change when an error has been made—an error that could be dangerous.

 

Substance misuse, especially addiction, induces an allostatic state in the brain. This is a long-term shift of the regulatory system away from its normal functioning. Thus, addiction can be said to be the product of an allostatic process involving adaptations of the reward system in an attempt to restore stability.

Allostasis has a long-term cost to the body, which can lead to potentially pathological states.

## Behaviors

Particular behaviors are associated with the addiction cycle.

Select each heading to learn more about these behaviors.
### Impulsivity
Impulsivity is defined as acting without foresight or regard for consequences. People who are addicted find it hard to resist urges or delay the pleasure offered by substance use.

### Positive reinforcement
Substance misuse is positively reinforced through the pleasure it brings. This makes it more likely that people will want to repeat the experience.

### Negative reinforcement
People who are addicted find they have negative feelings or emotions when the addictive substance is withdrawn. Taking the substance again reduces these negative feelings and emotions, and increases the probability of repeating the behavior.
### Compulsivity
When driven by compulsion, people with substance addiction are more likely to repeat behaviors, even in the face of adverse consequences.

## Anti-reward systems

Anti-reward systems counteract the positive effects of the reward pathways and switch off these behaviors.

As a person progresses deeper into the addiction cycle, anti-reward systems develop. These systems include the following:

- **[[corticotropin-releasing-factor|Corticotropin-releasing factor (CRF)]]** controls hormonal, sympathetic, and behavioral responses to stressors.
- **[[noradrenaline|Norepinephrine]]** increases alertness, arousal, and attention; constricts blood vessels to maintain blood pressure during stress, and affects the sleep-wake cycle, mood, and memory.
- **[[dynorphin|Dynorphin]]** is a powerful modulator of pain, stress, mood, and addiction, primarily through its interaction with kappa-opioid receptors in the brain.

These systems develop during excessive substance use, and their activation appears when the substance is removed. CRF antagonists can block excessive drug taking during dependence. This is an example of a key player in the plasticity of the extended amygdala during the development of addiction.
