## M5 W1 P05 – Mental health features of addiction and compulsive behavior

**Read this excerpt about substance use:**

"Substance use severely harms individual health, increasing the risk of chronic diseases, and mental health conditions, and tragically resulting in millions of preventable deaths every year. It places a heavy burden on families and communities, increasing exposure to accidents, injuries, and violence."

\[Source: WHO Media Team\]

A strong link has been established between addiction and mental health conditions, and studies provide evidence that about half of the people who experience a mental health condition will also experience a substance use disorder, and vice versa.

Data shows high rates of comorbid substance use disorders and the following mental health conditions:

- anxiety disorders (including generalized anxiety disorder, panic disorder, and post-traumatic stress disorder)
- depression
- bipolar disorder
- psychosis
- borderline personality disorder
- antisocial personality disorder

\[Source: Cleveland Clinic; National Institute on Drug Abuse\]

When two or more disorders or illnesses occur in the same person, the conditions are described as ***comorbid***.

There is a high prevalence of comorbidity between substance use disorders and other mental health conditions, but this **does not necessarily mean that one caused the other**, even if one appeared first.

When someone develops a mental health condition, changes in brain activity occurring as a result of this condition may increase the person's vulnerability for problematic use of substances. It is believed that this occurs when the rewarding effects of substances are enhanced and negative effects reduced. Substances may alleviate the unpleasant symptoms of the mental health condition or the side effects of the medication used to treat it.

\[Source: National Institute on Drug Abuse\]

Intoxication (e.g., being drunk or "high") produces temporary effects, but frequent intoxication and substance misuse can be significant factors in the development of a comorbid mental health condition and substance use disorder.

For example, cannabis intoxication is associated with multiple manifestations of temporary impairment of mental health functions.

Weekly cannabis use is associated with a 35% increase in risk of developing a psychotic disorder in comparison with non-users, and such risk is even higher for people using cannabis daily. The potency of cannabis used can also increase the risk of psychosis and of cannabis use disorder.

\[Source: WHO\]

The WHO has tabulated common mental health features of acute substance **intoxication** for selected classes of the following substances:  

| Substances | Mental health features |
| --- | --- |
| Alcohol | Impaired attention, inappropriate or aggressive behavior, unpredictable mood and emotions, impaired judgment, poor coordination, unsteady gait, slurred speech |
| Cannabis or synthetic cannabinoids | Euphoria, impaired attention, perceptual alterations, changes in sociability, increased appetite, anxiety, intensification of ordinary experiences, impaired short-term memory, sluggishness, potential for delirium or acute psychosis |
| Opioids | Somnolence, stupor, mood changes, impaired judgment, respiratory depression, slurred speech, impairment of memory and attention |
| Cocaine | Inappropriate euphoria, anxiety, anger, impaired attention, hypervigilance, agitation, paranoid ideation (sometimes of delusional intensity), auditory hallucinations, confusion, changes in sociability |
| Amphetamine-type stimulants | Anxiety, anger, impaired attention, hypervigilance, agitation, paranoid ideation (possibly of delusional intensity), transient auditory hallucinations, transient confusion, changes in sociability |
| Hallucinogens | Hallucinations, illusions, perceptual changes, anxiety, depressed or dysphoric mood, paranoid ideation, impaired judgment |
| MDMA or related drugs | Increased or inappropriate sexual interest and activity, anxiety, restlessness, agitation |
| Dissociative drugs including ketamine | Aggression, impulsiveness, unpredictable behavior, anxiety, agitation, impaired judgment, numbness or diminished responsiveness to pain, slurred speech |

\[Adapted from: WHO\]

Many people misuse more than one type of substance and are therefore at higher risk of developing comorbid substance use disorders and mental health conditions.

For example, one study showed that among people with a use disorder involving heroin, over 66% were dependent on nicotine, nearly 25% had an alcohol use disorder, and over 20% had a cocaine use disorder.

Among people diagnosed with a cocaine use disorder, nearly 60% also had an alcohol use disorder, 48% were dependent on nicotine, and 21% had a marijuana use disorder.

\[Source: National Institute on Drug Abuse\]

## Case study

### The comorbidity between mental health conditions and tobacco use

Evidence demonstrates that there is a strong association between mental health conditions and tobacco use. Based on survey data from a 2016 study, 30.5% of respondents who had a diagnosed mental health condition smoked cigarettes in the previous month, which is about 66% higher than the rate among those with no mental health conditions.

This prevalence is particularly high among people with schizophrenia (70% to 80%)—with rates up to five times higher than the general population.

It is believed that smoking may reduce symptoms of mental health conditions such as poor concentration, low mood, and stress.

High rates of smoking and difficulty quitting among people with schizophrenia may contribute to greater risk of cardiovascular disease, lung disease, and shorter life expectancy.

Neuroimaging research has shown the presence of abnormalities in particular circuits of the brain among people with schizophrenia, which may predispose them to tobacco addiction and increase the rewarding effects of drugs like nicotine.

A neurobiological marker of schizophrenia is a lower level of nicotinic acetylcholine receptors. These receptors are involved in cognition and memory and are naturally activated by the neurotransmitter acetylcholine—but they can also be activated by nicotine. Understanding how and why people with schizophrenia use nicotine may help inform the development of new treatments for both schizophrenia and nicotine dependence.

\[Source: National Institute on Drug Abuse\]

### Compulsive behavior and mental health

Compulsive behavior like gambling or gaming often coexists with other mental health disorders, such as anxiety, obsessive-compulsive disorder, and substance misuse. Symptoms typically begin between the ages of 20 and 30 and can lead to significant psychosocial consequences over time.

\[Source: Alavi et al.\]

Authors Pinna et al. (2015) comment that one of the important ways to prevent internet addiction is to treat risk factors such as loneliness, stress, depression, and anxiety. Mental health professionals should encourage people who overuse the internet to seek treatment when problems emerge and help them identify ways they may be using the internet to escape from concerns in their day-to-day lives.

\[Source: Pinna et al.\]

According to the American Psychological Association (APA), compulsive behaviors may be more likely to be experienced by people dealing with chronic stress—either due to a life event or because of a mental health condition.

Clinicians suggest that risk factors for the development of compulsive behaviors include:

- genetics (having a close family member with obsessive-compulsive disorder, for example)
- a more reserved temperament
- a tendency toward low mood
- experience of childhood trauma

Some psychologists believe that compulsive behaviors can develop as a result of an unconscious perceived lack of control, which can lead to anxiety and hopelessness. The compulsive behavior may serve as a tool for regaining what the person believes is agency over the experience driving the compulsiveness.

\[Source: UK Rehab\]
