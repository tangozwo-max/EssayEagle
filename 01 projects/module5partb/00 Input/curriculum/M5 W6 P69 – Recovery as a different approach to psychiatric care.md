## Recovery as a different approach to psychiatric care

You may find it useful to refresh on the following topics before further developing your knowledge:

- [The recovery movement](https://learn.iheed.org/courses/758/modules/items/356905)
- [The recovery model](https://learn.iheed.org/courses/758/modules/items/356823)

**Recovery movement:** a shift away from crisis-oriented, deficit-focused, and professionally directed models of care to a vision of care that is directed by people in recovery, emphasizes the reality and hope of long-term recovery, and recognizes the many pathways to healing for people with addiction and mental health challenges.

\[Source: Kelly, White\]

**Recovery:** A process of change through which individuals improve their health and wellness, live a self-directed life, and strive to reach their full potential.

\[Source: SAMHSA\]

Recovery is a deeply personal, unique process of changing one's attitudes, values, feelings, goals, skills, and/or roles. It is a way of living a satisfying, hopeful, and contributing life even within the limitations caused by illness.

Recovery covers four aspects of life:

- health: managing or overcoming a disease or symptoms of the disease
- home: having a safe and stable place to live
- purpose: having a meaningful daily life, which might include school, work, volunteering, family caretaking, or creative endeavors, and also having the independence, income, and resources to participate in society
- community: having relationships and social networks that provide support, friendship, love, and hope

\[Source: SAMHSA; Slade et al.\]

## The guiding principles of recovery

Select each heading to learn more.
### Hope

To be motivated to change, people need to believe that they can overcome internal and external challenges, barriers, and obstacles. They need to have **hope**. Hope can be supported by family, friends, peers, providers, and allies, but it is an internal catalyst of the recovery process.
### Driven by the person
Recovery is based on self-determination and self-direction. People define their own life goals and their path toward achieving them. Autonomy and independence are key to empowerment. People should be able to lead their journey, be in control, and be able to exercise choice over the services and supports they use.

Recovery-orientated services need to give people the resources they need to make informed decisions, initiate recovery, build on their strengths, and gain control over their lives.
### Pathways
Recovery pathways are individual and need to take into account strengths, needs, preferences, goals, culture, and background. A person's experiences and any trauma will affect and determine their pathway. Components of a personalized recovery pathway may include:

- professional clinical treatment
- use of medications
- support from families and in schools
- faith-based approaches
- peer support
- a wide range of other approaches

Recovery is characterized by growth and improved functioning, but it is not linear. There may be periods of accelerated growth and improvement, but there will also be setbacks. These are a natural part of the recovery process. People and their friends and families will need to build resilience to deal with these setbacks.

Abstinence from the use of substances such as tobacco, illicit substances, alcohol, and prescribed or nonprescribed medicines is the ultimate goal of recovery.

For some people, a supportive environment such as a recovery home may be needed. This can be especially true for children and young people, who may lack the capacity to determine their own course.

### Holistic
A person's whole life needs to be considered when working on a recovery pathway. This includes their mind, body, spirit, and community. Pathways will need to address:

- self-care
- family
- housing
- employment or education
- transport
- clinical treatment for both mental and substance use disorders
- primary healthcare
- dental care
- complementary and alternative services
- faith or spirituality
- creativity
- social networks
- community participation

The services and supports used should be integrated and coordinated.

### Peers and allies

One key aspect of recovery is support from peers and allies, whether this is one-to-one or in a group format. This type of support enables the sharing of experiences, social learning, and the knowledge and skills that have been gained.

Peers and allies can:

- encourage and engage other peers, providing a sense of belonging, supportive relationships, valued roles, and community
- provide resources to assist people on their recovery journey
- support families of children or young people who are in recovery

Professionals can also provide clinical treatment and other services to support people on their journey.

By helping other people on their journey and giving back to a community, people in recovery can gain a feeling of self-worth and help themselves.
### Relationships and social networks

Relationships and social networks provide crucial support for people on their recovery journey. They can give hope, encouragement, and support through believing in a person's ability to recover. They may also suggest strategies and resources for change, allowing people to shed unhealthy or unfulfilling life roles and engage in new, meaningful roles. These roles can give a feeling of belonging, personhood, empowerment, autonomy, social inclusion, and community participation. The members of these relationships and social networks may include:

- family
- friends
- peers
- providers
- faith groups
- community members
### Culture

A person's journey and unique path to recovery are significantly influenced by their culture and cultural background, encompassing diverse values, traditions, and beliefs. Services should be culturally grounded, sensitive, attuned, congruent, and competent, and they must be personalized to address each individual's unique needs.
### Trauma-informed

Experiencing trauma, such as physical or sexual abuse, domestic violence, war, or disaster, is often linked to alcohol and drug use, mental health issues, and related problems. Services and supports should be trauma-informed to ensure physical and emotional safety, build trust, and promote choice, empowerment, and collaboration.
### Strengths and resources
Individuals, families, and communities possess strengths and resources that form the foundation for recovery. Each person has a personal responsibility for their self-care and recovery journey. Individuals should be empowered to advocate for themselves.

Families and significant others play a crucial role in supporting their loved ones, particularly children and youth in recovery. Communities are responsible for providing opportunities and resources to combat discrimination and promote social inclusion and recovery. Those in recovery also have a social responsibility and should be able to join with peers to collectively express their strengths, needs, desires, and aspirations.
### respect
Acceptance and appreciation from the community, systems, and society for individuals affected by mental health and substance use issues are essential for recovery. This includes protecting their rights and eliminating discrimination. It is important to recognize that taking steps toward recovery often requires significant courage. Self-acceptance, developing a positive and meaningful sense of identity, and regaining self-belief are particularly crucial.

\[Source: SAMHSA\]

Sam has been learning about the recovery movement and has a question for Margo.

Sam

Margo, I came across this quote the other day, it was, "But recovery is not about getting better or ceasing to need support – it is about recovering a life, the right to participate in all facets of civic and economic life as an equal citizen." What does that mean for the services we provide?

Margo

Well, there's a set of values and beliefs that need to be embraced by the families, treatment professionals, and entire healthcare systems supporting people with substance use disorders.

Sam

That's interesting, what are they?

Margo

The first one is that individuals with a substance use disorder or behavioral disorder, whether in recovery or not, possess inherent worth and dignity. We need to respect that in them.

Sam

I can see that. I guess there's something in there about challenging stigma and discrimination as well?

Margo

That's right, stigma and discrimination can deter people from seeking help. The next value is that recovery pathways are all different but they should be celebrated. If someone has had the courage to start or think about recovery then that is a positive step and we should celebrate it.

Sam

I know recovery is holistic but some people still need clinical treatment. Do the values say anything about that?

Margo

They say that access to high-quality treatment is a fundamental right, although recovery encompasses more than just treatment. The last value is that people in recovery and their families have valuable experiences and encouragement to offer to those struggling with substance use.

Sam

Thanks Margo, I can see how those values embrace the principles of recovery.
