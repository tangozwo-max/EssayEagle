## Other therapies

## Exercise and yoga

Select each heading to learn more the evidence for exercise and yoga as treatments for addiction.

Patterson et al. studied how exercise could support and influence addiction treatment. They found, particularly in conjunction with inpatient or outpatient treatment, that a significant change in addiction-related outcomes occurred—for example, more abstinent days and reduced cravings.

\[Source: Patterson et al.\]

Brooks et al. reviewed the evidence for using yoga as an adjunct to treatment as usual for women with substance misuse disorder. They found that several small research projects suggested that yoga, particularly Hatha Yoga, could support substance use disorder treatment and give favorable outcomes. They suggest that larger research projects should look into this.

\[Source: Brooks et al.\]

## Music and art therapy

Select each image to learn more about these therapies.

### Music therapy

Music therapy is a psychological clinical intervention that is delivered by registered music therapists. It supports communication between service user and therapists through the use of music instead of words. It is therefore useful with people who are nonverbal, who find it hard to communicate via language, or who are struggling to articulate particular thoughts and memories.

A 2022 Cochrane report found that music therapy added to as-normal care reduced substance craving in people with substance misuse disorders.

\[Source: Cochrane\]

### Art therapy

Art therapy has been used with people experiencing substance use disorders since the 1950s. It enables a person to express themselves through a nonverbal, imaginative, and creative exercise. The therapy might include:

- drawing an incident that occurred while using substances
- drawing or painting emotions
- stress painting (a way to express and manage stress)
- creating an art journal
- creating sculptures

Research suggests that art therapy helps to:

- decrease denial
- support communication
- facilitate group discussions
- motivate change
- move patients through the change journey from contemplation into action

\[Source: Aletraris et al.\]

> [!-info] -info
> **Further info:** There are other forms of creative arts therapy such as dance, theatre, and the wider arts.

The evidence base for the use of complementary therapies such as acupuncture in addiction and dependency is still developing.

Shin et al. reviewed the evidence for the use of acupuncture with alcohol use disorder. They found that acupuncture interventions had a stronger effect on reducing alcohol-related symptoms and behaviors than was found in a control group. This effect lasted into follow-up, although it was weaker. Their conclusion was that this is promising but requires further research.

\[Source: Shin et al.\]

Lee et al. looked at the use of acupuncture to treat drug addiction. They found evidence that acupuncture modulates the mesolimbic dopamine system and drug-seeking behaviors by activating the brain pathway via somatosensory stimulation. They recommend more research into the extra-somatosensory effects of somatosensory stimulation.

\[Source: Lee et al.\]
