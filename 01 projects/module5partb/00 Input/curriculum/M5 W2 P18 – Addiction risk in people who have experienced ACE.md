## M5 W2 P18 – Addiction risk in people who have experienced ACE

Adverse childhood events (ACE) have been linked to changes in brain structure and function, and to stress-responsive neurobiological symptoms. ACE can render people vulnerable to poor physical and mental health and affect their emotional well-being.

## Addiction and attachment

Three important signaling molecules link [[adverse-childhood-experiences|ACEs]] to addiction—[[oxytocin]], [[dopamine]], and glucocorticoids ([[cortisol]]).

Select each tab to learn more about these substances.

### Oxytocin

Oxytocin is a hormone and neuropeptide produced in the hypothalamus and released by the pituitary gland. It plays an important role in social bonding and attachment in that it functions as a neuroregulator of social behavior. It is also important in stress regulation, and dysregulated oxytocin systems have been associated with psychosocial disorders.

Early life experiences impact long-term oxytocin system functioning, and dysregulation can lead to social impairment and aggressive behavior, together with novelty- and reward-seeking behaviors.

### Dopamine

You have already learned about the importance of the dopamine-related reward system regarding addiction and dependence. Research has established the role of early-life experiences in shaping dopamine-related systems. Early adverse experiences may result in:

- reduced dopamine transporter binding
- increased baseline dopamine levels
- exaggerated dopamine release in response to stress

These changes can cause susceptibility to novelty- or sensation-seeking behaviors associated with impulsive risk-taking and the active pursuit of rewards. A person may then seek stimulant drugs that activate dopamine-related pathways.

### Glucocorticoids

Deficits in early caregiving have been linked to prolonged or exaggerated increases in cortisol levels in response to stress. This may reverse with the occurrence of psychiatric disorders. Increased cortisol due to early caregiving deficits, may contribute to the development of anxiety and trauma-related disorders later in life.

Stress dysregulation and [[hpa-axis|hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis (HPA)]] dysfunction are linked to substance addiction. Stress exposure may cause the onset of substance misuse, diminish the motivation to abstain, and heighten the risk of relapse.

## The effects of ACE on the brain

Adverse childhood events have been associated with structural alterations of the brain and its functioning, and with stress-responsive neurobiological systems. These changes persist into adulthood and can have long-term effects on people's lives.

![[Pasted image 20260614154238.png]]
Repeated stress and adverse childhood experiences cause dysfunction in the hippocampus, amygdala, and medial prefrontal cortex. These areas mediate anxiety and mood problems, resulting in increased incidences of anxiety, depressed affect, hallucinations, and substance misuse.
![[Pasted image 20260614154245.png]]
ACEs and repeated stress cause increased activity in the locus coeruleus and greater norepinephrine activity. These increases cause unpleasant feelings that are mitigated by alcohol and opioid use, which results in greater use of these substances, as well as tobacco.
![[Pasted image 20260614154255.png]]
Defects in the amygdala, brought on by stress and adverse childhood events, play a role in sexual and aggressive behavior. Defects in oxytocin systems result in impaired pair bonding, which may result in the following behaviors in certain individuals:

- early intercourse
- promiscuity
- sexual dissatisfaction
- perpetration of intimate partner violence
![[Pasted image 20260614154303.png]]
Adverse childhood events cause a reduction in the volume of the hippocampus and amygdala. This leads to deficits in memory functions, especially memory storage and retrieval.
![[Pasted image 20260614154313.png]]
Adverse childhood events and prolonged stress disrupt the glucocorticoid pathways in the body. This can cause increased intra-abdominal and other fat deposits, and obesity.

\[Source: Anda et al.\]

Modifications to dopamine-related, oxytocin-related, and glucocorticoid-related systems can cause addiction susceptibility in people with ACEs. These modifications, together with insecure attachment, can lead to destructive behavior and substance misuse. A parent's addiction can furthermore impair their caregiving capacity, potentially creating an intergenerational cycle that perpetuates the addiction pattern.
