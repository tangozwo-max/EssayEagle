## Summary for harm reduction: principles and programs

## Key learning points

You have completed this section. Review each key learning point before you progress.

- The term *harm* within the context of substance use can reflect many different types of injury, damage, loss, trauma, and health deterioration.
- Harms include drug-specific and drug-related mortality, damage to physical health, impairment of mental functioning, loss of tangibles, criminal activity, domestic violence, and environmental harm.
- Evidence shows that the risk of harm due to substance use can be mitigated with such approaches as needle exchange programs, medication for opioid substitution, recovery peer support, cognitive behavioral therapy, education in schools, and drug safety checking.
- The history of harm reduction reflects different top-down approaches through public health initiatives and bottom-up approaches through grassroots movements.
- Organizations such as the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) partner with public health agencies, community members, harm reductionists, people with lived experience, and others, to collate expertise on harm reduction. Key components of SAMHSA's harm reduction principles include respecting the autonomy of the person, cultivating relationships, prioritizing listening, connecting with community, and working toward systems change.
- The National Harm Reduction Coalition also sets principles based around acceptance, understanding, nonjudgmentalism, and empowerment of the people with lived experience of substance use.
- Practitioners should aim to function as sources of information and guidance and provide a realistic range of support options aimed at improving people's lives while reducing harm. To encourage engagement with harm reduction services, healthcare professionals should develop plans that are not directive or forceful, but deliver a realistic range of support options aimed at improving the person's life.
- Of importance, harm reduction involves meeting people "where they are at" in terms of their substance use, even if this juncture involves their continued use of the substance.
- At the organizational level, healthcare professionals should look to developing a stigma-free culture that is respectful, trusting, understanding of service users, and directly influenced by lived experience and expertise.
