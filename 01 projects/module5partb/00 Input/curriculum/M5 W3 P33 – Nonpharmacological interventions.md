## Nonpharmacological interventions

Nonpharmacological interventions for addiction and dependency can be grouped into three types:

- information dissemination (psychoeducation)
- nonspecialized face-to-face interventions
- qualified therapeutic interventions

Evidence for the efficacy of these interventions varies, with the best evidence base being for CBT, MI, mindfulness, and contingency management. However, a wide range of interventions are being used, even if there is a lack of evidence for that use.

## Types of nonpharmacological interventions

There are several types of nonpharmacological interventions that may be used to support people recovering from a substance use disorder.

### Cognitive behavioral therapy

Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) uses the learning processes that led to substance dependence to help people unlearn those behaviors. CBT views addiction as a learned behavior, emerging in an environmental context, developed and maintained by thought patterns and processes. There are two parts to CBT: functional analysis and skills training.

**Functional analysis:** This is where the therapist and service user look at the causes and consequences of a behavior, identify thoughts and feelings, and analyze the circumstances that led them to both begin and continue using substances. This helps people to understand the difficulties that led them to use substances.

**Skills training:** This takes the form of helping people to learn, or relearn, coping skills to deal with situations that might trigger substance use. It also helps them to unlearn old habits and develop healthier new ones. Skills training also teaches people how to change how they think about substance use.

\[Source: Magill et al.\]

### Motivational interviewing (MI)

MI helps people to find the motivation to create a positive behavior change. It is particularly effective for those with mixed feelings (ambivalence) about changing. Resolving ambivalence can increase motivation. The ethos of MI is collaborative and evocative, and honors service user autonomy.

**Collaboration:** This allows the service user to develop a trusting relationship with their therapist/counselor/support worker.

**Evocation:** This allows a service user to draw on their own ideas. MI says that motivation comes from within, so a person must be able to bring forward ideas for change from themselves.

**Autonomy:** The power to change rests with the service user. They need to take the actions necessary to change their behavior and to take personal responsibility for it.

\[Source: DiClemente et al.\]

### Family therapy

Family therapy is a form of talking therapy that focuses on the improvement of relationships among family members. This is something that is often damaged by substance use. The strengthening of relationships may also support people on their change journey. It can involve any combination of family members. There are several different types of family therapy:

**Functional family therapy (FFT):** The therapist assesses the family dynamics that have led to problems. It improves family communication and supports positive reinforcement.

**Couples therapy:** Supports couples to identify problems and work on solutions.

**Structural family therapy:** Looks at the relationships, boundaries, and hierarchies within a family (its structure). Allows families to find their own alternatives to problematic behaviors with the support of the therapist.

**Strategic family therapy:** Short-term family therapy focusing on making positive structural and behavioral changes within the family.

**Systemic family therapy:** Considers family issues in the contexts in which they live, as this is regarded as most significant for psychological development and emotional well-being. This was covered in the page on [Systemic family therapy.](https://learn.iheed.org/courses/758/modules/items/357148)

\[Source: Hogue et al.\]

### Contingency management

Contingency management provides people experiencing substance use disorder with incentives for abstinence or adherence to treatment, usually in the form of vouchers or cash. There is a significant evidence base for its efficacy across a range of populations. It tends to confer more benefits than talking therapies or mindfulness at least in the short term. Less certain is its long-term effects after rewards cease.

\[Source: Rains et al.\]

## Self-help groups

Self-help groups such as Alcoholics Anonymous (AA) and Narcotics Anonymous (NA) and the clinically related Twelve-Step Facilitation (TSF) programs are popular treatments for substance use disorders. TSF is designed to encourage engagement with AA or NA programs.

The 2020 Cochrane Report found that the combination of AA and TSF could lead to higher rates of continuous abstinence in people over months or years.

\[Source: Cochrane\]

There has been some criticism of AA and NA in that the programs reference a higher power and place emphasis on spirituality, including prayer and meditation. Whilst group members are encouraged to interpret this for themselves, it makes the ethos culturally unacceptable for some people.

AA's stance on alcoholism is not in keeping with other models of substance use disorder. It promotes a disease model of alcoholism where the only management option is abstinence and people should continue to regard themselves as alcoholics long after they stop meeting the clinical criteria for alcohol use disorder.

\[Source: Cochrane\]
