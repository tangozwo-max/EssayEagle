## Does criminalization of substance use reduce harm?

"Drug-related mortality is now the leading cause of death among men and women between 20 and 50 years old in North America... Criminalization is just a way to institutionalize stigma. Making drugs illegal does nothing to stop people from using them."

Dr. Mark Tyndall

As you have explored, a significant factor in the explosion of the opioid crisis was the over-prescription of addictive opioids for pain. For example, approximately 80% of Americans who use heroin started down the path of substance use with prescription opioids.

\[Source: Lapinski\]

Despite perceptions that the root cause of this crisis was the aggressive marketing of prescription opioids, the governmental response has been to "build more jails" to incarcerate people who use substances. According to epidemiologist, physician, and public health expert Dr. Mark Tyndall, millions of people are caught up in a hopeless cycle of incarceration, violence, and poverty that has been created by the laws around substance use and not by the substances themselves.

Harm reduction initiatives have been viewed as radical by some, with stiff opposition on a community level to such services as supervised injection sites. Critics believe that harm reduction gives the wrong message to children and that drug use is a law enforcement issue (rather than sociocultural).

This type of thinking stigmatized entire communities and criminalized addiction and dependency.

\[Source: Tyndall\]

In the US, penalties for possession of substances such as fentanyl, heroin, cocaine, and methamphetamine may include up to a year in prison and a fine for a first offense, with harsher penalties for subsequent offenses. Trafficking or "intending to distribute" incurs much harsher penalties (e.g., no less than 10 years in prison).

## Case study

### Substance use legislation in Portugal

In the year 2000, Portugal had one of the worst drug misuse records among European populations.

Around 1% of the population was addicted to heroin, and the societal response to this issue was very much based on stigmatization, criminalization, and punishment.

Still, the high rate of substance misuse and addiction did not abate.

The Portuguese government set up a panel of experts, which included physician Dr. João Goulã, who played a crucial role in the development and implementation of Portugal's groundbreaking drug decriminalization policy.

The panel advocated not only for the decriminalization of all drugs but for the expenditure previously used on drug crime enforcement to be applied to a massive program of job creation and microloan set-up facilities for people with substance use disorders.

Once someone who had previously been a mechanic, for example, experienced a period of recovery and psychological rehabilitation through appropriate services, the government would arrange with employers for a significant percentage of the mechanic’s wages to be paid through the program.

People who had previously misused substances rediscovered a sense of purpose in themselves and in relationships with the wider society.

Overdose, HIV, and substance use disorders were reduced significantly, and injectable drug use reduced by more than 50%.

\[Source: Hari\]

Criminal penalties for drug possession differ globally. In some countries, like the Netherlands and Portugal, benefits have been observed in terms of the adoption of a pragmatic harm reduction approach.

In contrast, other countries impose severe penalties for substance possession (including capital punishment). In the Philippines, Malaysia, Singapore, and some countries in the Middle East, strict drug laws focus on deterrence over harm reduction, with moral and cultural beliefs often informing policy.

Research that evaluates the efficacy of harm reduction programs can be highly controversial. Government agencies funding such programs risk alienating the support of the public depending on the nature and scope of the specific programs and where/how they are rolled out.

Research also indicates that prohibitive and punitive drug policies have had counterproductive effects by **contributing to** HIV and hepatitis C transmission, fatal overdose, mass incarceration, and other human rights violations and drug market violence.

\[Source: Scheim et al.\]

## Case study

### The toleration rule in the Netherlands

Amsterdam developed a reputation over many decades as a city for drug tourism—somewhere people could go to smoke cannabis freely in coffee shops. The Dutch had a rule of toleration, by which drug use was not officially legal but was tolerated as long as public order was not disturbed. The rule of toleration involved coffee shops **not permitted to**:

- cause any nuisance (e.g., public disorder)
- sell hard drugs (e.g., cocaine)
- sell cannabis to minors
- advertise drugs
- sell large quantities (over 5 grams of cannabis) in a single transaction

In 2013, new legislation was introduced that specified only residents of the Netherlands were permitted to visit coffee shops and purchase cannabis there. However, this new rule was rarely implemented and coffee shops continue to sell cannabis (per the rule of toleration) to nonresidents.

The pragmatic approach to harm reduction adopted in the Netherlands has produced some interesting outcomes:

- Just 14% of cannabis users in the Netherlands report that other drugs are available from their usual cannabis source, compared to 52% in Sweden.
- Rates of cannabis use in the Netherlands are equivalent to or lower than those of many nearby countries and are substantially lower than those of the US.
- The coffee shops generate an estimated 400 million Euro in tax revenue—this money would otherwise have gone to criminal traffickers.
- Public opinion support for the coffee shops has increased throughout their existence.

\[Source: Veiligheid; Transform\]

It is important to acknowledge that despite the perceived benefits of the toleration rule, challenges attendant with drug tourism do arise. Also problematic is the potency of some of the cannabis products sold.

Opponents of harm reduction strategies often argue that approaches and interventions enable people to continue using substances and increase crime and public disorder. They also argue that harm reduction policies signal government acceptance of substance use.

However, the Dutch approach to harm reduction means that compared to other countries with stricter regulations, there are considerably fewer arrests for minor drug offenses. This means that costs to the criminal justice system are reduced and that long-term consequences to the arrestee are mitigated—e.g., they retain opportunities for employment, housing, and education, and there is no negative impact on their mental, physical, and financial well-being.
