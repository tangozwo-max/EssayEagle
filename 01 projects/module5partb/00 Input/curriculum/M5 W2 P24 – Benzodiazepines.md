## M5 W2 P24 – Benzodiazepines

Benzodiazepines (BZD) are used in a range of clinical conditions. They are among the most commonly prescribed medicines across the world. They are mainly used for the short-term treatment of anxiety and insomnia, and for managing alcohol withdrawal. The risk of dependency is low compared to some other drugs, but when combined with substances such as opioids, the risk increases and is related to considerable numbers of drug-related deaths.
![[Pasted image 20260614155303.png]]
Drug misuse varies according to population. Benzodiazepine misuse is a concern among elderly people and women, but the risk of death is highest when taken as a secondary drug by opioid users.

Benzodiazepines increase [[gaba|GABA-A (gamma-aminobutyric acid–type A)]] channel opening in the central nervous system leading to increased hyperpolarization. GABA is an inhibitory neurotransmitter that slows down activity in the brain, producing a calming and sedative effect.
![[Pasted image 20260614155311.png]]
GABA works by binding to post-synaptic receptors and modulating chlorine ion channels to hyperpolarize the neuron. This inhibits the action potential of the neuron and reduces the signals it can send.

## Benzodiazepines and opioids

High-risk opioid users often misuse BZD for self-medication or to increase the effect of the opioids.

### Self-medication

People who misuse benzodiazepines often self-medicate to reduce unpleasant feelings. These feelings can be due to:

- psychiatric disorders
- negative emotional states
- opioid withdrawal symptoms
- side effects of cocaine or alcohol use

### Enhancing the opioid effect

Benzodiazepine can increase the effects of opioids, particularly when injected, as they prolong the intensity and duration of the opioid effects. BZD use by people taking opioid substitutions may be due to their being under-dosed and experiencing withdrawal symptoms.

The combination of BZD and opioids puts users at high risk of drug-related death due to suppression of basic life support functions in the brain stem. A range of different BZD are misused, but those implicated in drug-related deaths are the rapid-action onset, highly lipophilic BZD such as diazepam and alprazolam.

Benzodiazepines rapidly induct users into the addiction cycle, as users quickly develop tolerance, dependence, and withdrawal symptoms.

Withdrawal symptoms can cause anxiety, confusion, panic attacks, and acute psychosis. Abrupt withdrawal can cause potentially fatal convulsions that are difficult to control.
