### Prefrontal cortex

The prefrontal cortex plays a key role in thinking, planning, problem-solving, decision-making, and self-control. As the last part of the brain to mature in humans, it explains why adolescents often engage in risk-taking and novelty-seeking behaviors until their self-control abilities are fully developed.

Changes in the balance between this circuit, and the basal ganglia and extended amygdala circuits make a person with a substance use disorder seek the substance compulsively and result in reduced impulse control.


### Extended amygdala

The extended amygdala is involved in stressful feelings such as anxiety, irritability, and unease.

With continued drug use, the extended amygdala’s response to stress and negative emotions becomes more pronounced. Its heightened sensitivity contributes to intense feelings of anxiety, irritability, and unease during withdrawal, motivating the person to seek drugs not for pleasure, but to alleviate these uncomfortable feelings and sensations.

### Basal ganglia

The basal ganglia have several important roles, including:

- creating positive forms of motivation, such as the pleasurable effects of healthy activities
    
- forming habits and routines
    
- being a key node of the reward circuit
    

Substances such as drugs over-activate the reward circuit, causing a high. With repeated drug use, the brain reduces in sensitivity to pleasurable activities such as sex, eating, or being with friends, making it hard for the person to feel pleasure from anything other than the drug.

### Brain stem

The brain stem controls basic bodily functions such as breathing, heart rate, and sleeping. It is influenced by opioids, which can slow down and depress these functions. If these functions are depressed too far, death can occur.
