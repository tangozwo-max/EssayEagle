Note your thoughts before continuing.

The answer is all of the substances listed are considered psychoactive. If a drug or other substance is psychoactive (sometimes known as *psychotropic*), it affects how the brain works and causes changes in mood.

\[Source: National Cancer Institute\]

Psychoactive substances are defined by the World Health Organization (WHO) as substances that, "when consumed or administered, have the ability to change consciousness, mood or thinking processes."

Many psychoactive substances can produce toxic effects on human organs and tissues, and interfere with regulatory functions. Such substances can lead to increased risk of developing different physical and mental health conditions.

According to the WHO, there are three main categories of psychoactive substance use in the sociolegal context:

1. medical use when psychoactive substances are used as medications (e.g., sedatives or anxiolytic medicines)
2. illegal or illicit use when such use is prohibited under the international drug conventions or national legislation
3. legal **nonmedical use** of psychoactive substances (i.e., using sedatives or anxiolytics for a type of "buzz" rather than for their intended medical purpose)

\[Source: WHO\]

## Types of substances and the sociolegal context

The National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA) provides information on the most commonly used substances that can result in use disorders and dependency:

- alcohol
- caffeine
- cannabis
- synthetic cannabinoids
- hallucinogens (e.g., psilocybin, LSD)
- inhalants (e.g., glue, paint thinners)
- khat
- mescaline (peyote)

- opioids (e.g., heroin, morphine, prescription painkillers)
- synthetic opioids
- sedatives, hypnotics, and anxiolytics
- stimulants (e.g., cocaine, methamphetamine)
- tobacco, nicotine (including vaping)
- other (or unknown substances, which may include anabolic steroids, for example)

The sociolegal context differs based on cultural values and interpretations related to the use of psychoactive substances in specific communities.

Cultural terms used to describe a psychoactive substance and its effects vary greatly. *The International Classification of Diseases, 11th Revision* (ICD-11) advises that knowledge of specific terms and interpretations improves communication with people seeking support for substance use and for the determination of possible disorder.

For example, some traditional Native Americans use the naturally occurring hallucinogen **mescaline** (also known as peyote) during worship ceremonies and they consider this psychoactive substance to be a sacrament rather than a drug.

\[Source: WHO\]

While mescaline is illegal across the US for the general population, it is legally protected under legislation for specific religious purposes among the Native American community.

**Khat** (also known as *qat* or *chat*) comprises the leaves and fresh shoots of a flowering evergreen shrub cultivated in East Africa and the Southwest Arabian Peninsula. While khat is illegal in most Western countries, it is legal and widely used (by chewing the plant) in countries like Somalia, Ethiopia, and Yemen.

Khat consumption leads to effects that are similar to those of amphetamine (i.e., increased blood pressure and a state of euphoria and elation with feelings of increased alertness and arousal). This may be followed by depression, irritability, anorexia, and difficulty in sleeping. Frequent use of khat at high doses may lead to the development of psychotic reactions.

\[Source: Griffiths et al.\]

**Benzodiazepines** such as Valium (diazepam), Xanax (alprazolam), and Klonapin (clonazepam) are known to be most effective when given for a short period of time. However, there is evidence that these drugs may lead to substance use disorders and dependence. In the US, benzodiazepines are prescribed frequently to older adults as chronic anxiety and sleep problems are more common in this age cohort. Some people find themselves using benzodiazepines long term and becoming dependent.

Recall that dependency is a state resulting from prolonged use of a substance where the body adapts to its presence, leading to tolerance and withdrawal symptoms when use is reduced or stopped.

Review some of the statistics around benzodiazepine use in the United States in the year 2019:

- Of 290 visits to a hospital emergency department by older adults, 40% were for benzodiazepine emergencies.
- Between 20% and 50% of women over age 60 are prescribed benzodiazepines.
- Somewhere between 9% to 54% of older adults took benzodiazepines in the previous year.
- Old age and gender were the greatest factors in benzodiazepine use, with women twice as likely as men to use benzodiazepines.

**Nitrous oxide** (also known as *laughing gas*) is a colorless gas that is inhaled and is commonly used as a painkiller in medicine and dentistry, and as an aerosol propellant. It has been used globally as a recreational drug for centuries, but since the COVID-19 pandemic in particular, it has become one of the most misused substances (especially by 16- to 24-year-olds).

People seek a sense of euphoria from using nitrous oxide recreationally, but such use causes a significant increased risk of long-term health conditions that include depression, psychosis, and memory loss.

It has been difficult for governments to form legislation around nitrous oxide as it is used so commonly in aerosols. As part of plans to tackle anti-social behavior, laws have been enacted in some parts of the UK whereby the possession of nitrous oxide for recreational use is now a criminal offense.

In the US, laws differ across states. In some, the sale of nitrous oxide to minors is prohibited, the consumption of nitrous oxide for recreational purposes is illegal, and/or "the possession of nitrous oxide with the intent to inhale the chemical" is a crime.

\[Source: BBC; Cleveland Clinic; LegalMatch Law Library\]

## Diagnostic criteria

The diagnosis of a substance use disorder (SUD) and its severity is made by a clinical interview between the healthcare professional and the person presenting with substance use symptoms. Diagnosis is based on the *Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Illnesses, Fifth Edition, Text Revision* (DSM-5-TR) diagnostic criteria or the criteria of the *International Classification of Diseases, 11th Edition* (ICD-11).

If people present with symptoms consistent with both an SUD and a mental health condition, clinicians should assess for both types of disorder and refer for specialty behavioral healthcare when indicated.  

| No. | DSM-5 Criteria for substance use disorder diagnosis |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | Taking the substance in larger amounts or for longer than you should |
| 2 | Wanting to cut down or stop using the substance but not managing to |
| 3 | Spending a lot of time getting, using, or recovering from use of the substance |
| 4 | Cravings and urges to use the substance |
| 5 | Not managing to do what you should at work, home, or school because of substance use |
| 6 | Continuing to use, even when it causes problems in relationships |
| 7 | Giving up important social, occupational, or recreational activities because of substance use |
| 8 | Using substances again and again, even when it puts you in danger |
| 9 | Continuing to use, even when you know you have a physical or psychological problem that could have been caused or made worse by the substance |
| 10 | Needing more of the substance to get the effect you want (tolerance) |
| 11 | Development of withdrawal symptoms, which can be relieved by taking more of the substance |

These criteria can be grouped into four main categories: physical dependence (impaired control), social impairment, risk use, and pharmacologic (i.e., a physical dependence).

\[Source: Hartney\]

Select each category to learn more.

This criterion is met if the person:
### impaired control over substnce use
- consumes the substance in larger amounts and for a longer amount of time than intended
- has a persistent desire to cut down or regulate use (they may have unsuccessfully tried to stop in the past)
- spends a great deal of time obtaining, using, or recovering from the effects of substance use
- experiences craving, a pressing desire to use the substance
### Social impairment
- Impairment in the ability to fulfill major obligations at work, school, or home due to substance use.
- Continued use of the substance despite it causing significant social or interpersonal problems.
- Reduction or discontinuation of recreational, social, or occupational activities because of substance use.
### Risky use
- recurrent substance use in physically unsafe environments
- persistent substance use despite knowledge that it may cause or exacerbate physical or psychological problems
### Pharmacologic physical dependence
**Tolerance**: Individual requires increasingly higher doses of the substance to achieve the desired effect, or the usual dose has a reduced effect; individuals may build tolerance to specific symptoms at different rates.

**Withdrawal:** A collection of signs and symptoms that occurs when blood and tissue levels of the substance decrease. Individuals are likely to seek the substance to relieve symptoms. No documented withdrawal symptoms from hallucinogens, PCP (phencyclidine, also known as its street drug name *angel dust*), or inhalants.

**Note**: Tolerance and withdrawal in the context of appropriate medical treatment (i.e., pain medication used as prescribed) **do not count** as criteria for an SUD.

\[Source: McNeely, MD, MS et al.\]

> [!-info] -info
> **Info**: Of the 11 DSM-5 criteria, severity is determined by the number of criteria met: 2–3 (mild), 4–5 (moderate), 6 or more (severe).

### ICD-11 criteria

The ICD was developed by the World Health Organization (WHO) and is intended for global use. The ICD is designed to incorporate more culturally diverse perspectives than that of the DSM (which, though used globally also, is designed primarily for US clinicians within the context of American cultural norms).

The ICD-11 aims for broader applicability across different health systems for substance use. It defines ***dependence syndrome*** with six criteria, and at least three need to be met for a diagnosis. Another classification is ***harmful use***, which is diagnosed when there is clear evidence that substance use is causing physical or psychological harm, without meeting the full criteria for dependence.

While the DSM-5 categorizes substance use disorder severity as mild, moderate, or severe, the ICD-11 does not categorize severity levels for dependence syndrome. Instead, dependence syndrome is either present or absent.

ICD-11 criteria for harmful use and dependence syndrome:

- a strong desire or compulsion to use the substance
- difficulties controlling substance use behavior (e.g., onset, termination, or level of use)
- experiencing a state of physiological withdrawal when substance use is reduced or ceased
- developing tolerance (needing more of the substance to achieve the same effect)
- neglecting of alternative pleasures or interests in favor of substance use
- persistent use despite harmful consequences

\[Source: WHO\]
