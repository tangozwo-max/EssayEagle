## Culture and harm reduction strategies

In recent decades, there has been a troubling normalization of substance use among adolescents in the US, and the need for harm reduction strategies that recognize the diverse cultural contexts of affected communities has become increasingly urgent.

At the same time, a culture of over-prescribing of opioids for pain management has set the stage for a public health crisis that has necessitated culturally tailored interventions to reduce harm.

Cultural disparities (e.g., racial, ethnic) occur in adolescent substance use prevalence, trajectories, and consequences.

For example, according to a 2019 survey in the US, Native American, multiracial, Black, and Hispanic adolescents reported higher rates of initiating cigarette smoking, alcohol use, and marijuana use before age 13 than White and Asian-American adolescents.

Data also indicated that Native American and multiracial adolescents had higher rates of past-year marijuana use and illicit drug use than Hispanic, Black, White, and Asian-American adolescents.

In the US:

- Black youth are disproportionately arrested for substance use and are subject to more severe and prolonged legal ramifications than White youth (e.g., prison incarceration).
- Hispanic adolescents charged with drug offenses received longer custody in prisons than Black and White youth.
- Black and Hispanic youth with substance use disorders are less likely to receive adequate treatment compared to White adolescents.
- Native Americans aged 12 years or older had the highest rates of past-year alcohol and substance use disorders among monoracial groups.

These disparities and inequities continue into adulthood.

\[Source: Bo et al.\]

Harm reduction programs that respect cultural values and practices are more likely to engage people from diverse backgrounds. When these programs incorporate cultural values, trust is built with participants, their families, and communities.

**Culturally sensitive programs** for young people might be considered a foundational component of harm reduction in that existing substance use may be reduced or ceased, or indeed substance use may be prevented to begin with.

Such programs address the unique cultural and social factors that influence young people's behaviors, beliefs, and attitudes toward substances.

A systematic review and meta-analysis by Bo et al. (2023) analyzed 30 randomized controlled trials and found supportive evidence for the efficacy of culturally sensitive prevention programs in preventing or reducing substance use among Black, Hispanic, and Native American adolescents.

Culturally sensitive substance use prevention programs usually address a combination of positive youth development factors (e.g., future orientation, problem-solving, communication, decision-making, emotional regulation), substance use specific factors (e.g., substance use knowledge, expectancies, peer norms, resistant skills), and racially/ethnically specific factors (e.g., racial/ethnic socialization, racial/ethnic identity, acculturation) that are related to adolescent substance use.

Findings in this study indicated that culturally sensitive prevention programs that address substance-specific risks and protective factors, positive youth development factors, and that reflect adolescents’ cultural values, norms, practices, and worldviews are beneficial for preventing or reducing substance use among Hispanic, Black, and Native American adolescents.

\[Source: Bo et al.\]

"There’s also a brutal paradox with opioids: pills that were legally prescribed as medicine—sanctioned by doctors and government-funded healthcare providers—this medicine has led to hundreds of thousands of deaths, whether directly or via an addiction to prescription drugs that leads to using heroin or fentanyl."

Jeanmarie Perrone

Addiction researcher and practitioner

Sociocultural factors have had a significant impact on the opioid crisis. In the 1990s, opioid prescriptions exploded for the treatment of pain in the US.

Stephen J. Dubner, American journalist, hosted a podcast on the topic of the opioid crisis in North America.

One of his contributors, Alicia Sasser Modestino (health economist at Northeastern University in Boston), spoke about a working paper by three economists that found that American military personnel have “an overdose rate twice that of civilians.” Deployed military personnel are disproportionately and regularly exposed to injury-related chronic pain, psychological trauma, and cheap opium supplies. Dubner asked Modestino where else the crisis has hit particularly hard.

Select the question to review her answer.

**Modestino:** Low-income communities as well \[as military personnel\]. And what’s been kind of interesting is there’s been a reverse trend in terms of racial differences. So it’s something that’s affecting the white community more than the African-American or Hispanic community.

**Dubner:** Any idea why?

**Modestino:** You know, oddly enough, it seems that discrimination perhaps, in terms of prescribing for pain, or the ease of giving these kinds of medications to people of different races, had a perverse outcome that actually protected African Americans.

**Dubner:** Meaning, white privilege would make one more likely to get hold of an opioid prescription in the first place, yes?

**Modestino:** Yes, yep.

Dubner also spoke to addiction researcher and practitioner Jeanmarie Perrone.

Select each question to review more of their conversation.

**Dubner:** So you’ve said that you’ve seen the opioid crisis kind of happen in real time. Can you just describe when you first saw it being the issue that it’s become?

**Perrone:** In the beginning, which I am talking between 2000 and 2008, what we saw was the results of doctor overprescribing. And then as a result, in the emergency departments, we would see patients coming in for something like a migraine headache requesting I.V. doses of an opioid, and not just that they were requesting I.V. doses but repeated doses. And we were operating under this paradigm that if they still said they had 10 out of 10 pain, we still needed to give them, you know, intravenous opioids.

**Dubner:** When we were speaking earlier, you told me that pharmaceutical companies in the 90s started telling a new story about opioids—that the new generation of these drugs were not only more versatile but also safer and less subject to addiction. How true was that new story?

**Perrone:** Very untrue. Very untrue in many different ways. So we now know that the rate of continued use of opioids depends a little bit on the dose and the exposure. But patients who get exposed in a prescribed way—so you know, not intending to misuse—may actually be on opioids six months later, after just a therapeutic exposure for a minor procedure. And there have been multiple studies that have really demonstrated that just that exposure in a benign setting leads people to chronic use. Which is not the same as addiction, but a fraction of them do end up having addiction.

\[Source: Lapinski\]
