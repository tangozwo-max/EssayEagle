## M5 W1 P04 – Definitions of behavioral addiction and compulsive behavior

According to the American Society of Addictive Medicine, **addiction** is defined as, “a treatable, chronic medical disease involving complex interactions among brain circuits, genetics, the environment, and an individual’s life experiences. People with addiction use substances or **engage in behaviors that become compulsive** and often continue despite harmful consequences.”

### Characteristics of behavioral addiction

Compulsion and addiction are not the same thing. However, compulsive behavior (whether gaming, hoarding, shopping, exercise, etc.) can play a key role in the development and maintenance of a cycle of addiction.

The American Psychological Association (APA) explains that a compulsion is: "a type of behavior \[…\] or a mental act \[…\] engaged in to reduce anxiety or distress. Typically, the individual feels driven or compelled to perform a compulsion to reduce the distress associated with an obsession or to prevent a dreaded event or situation." The APA also states that compulsion does not always provide pleasure—instead, it acts to *neutralize* difficult thoughts, feelings, or distress.

\[Source: UK Rehab\]

Similar to how people addicted to a substance might feel, those with a behavioral addiction experience intense urges to engage in a particular behavior despite the negative outcomes of this behavior.

In the field of addiction research, there is considerable debate over what constitutes an addiction. Some experts believe that only substances can lead to true addiction because substances directly affect brain chemistry while behaviors do not produce the same neurobiological changes. Other researchers believe that certain behaviors impact the brain in a very similar way to substances.

Studies show that as the compulsive behavior becomes more and more common, the brain can remap the reward system to recognize the behavior as a positive action.

\[Source: UK Rehab\]

It was not until 2013 that the DSM recognized gambling as a behavioral addiction. Prior to that year, gambling was classified as an impulse-control disorder.

Support for reclassifying gambling disorder in DSM-5 stemmed from several studies in the field of neuroimaging and pharmacological treatment. These studies documented changes to specific neurotransmitter systems known to be involved in substance addictions among people who gambled compulsively.

\[Source: Pinna et al.\]

ICD-11 (*International Classification of Diseases, 11th Revision*) recognizes ***behavioral addictions*** as a distinct category of disorders. Specifically, it includes "gambling disorder" and "gaming disorder" under the broader category of "Disorders due to addictive behaviors."

ICD-11 also classifies "compulsive sexual behavior" as a disorder characterized by a "persistent pattern of failure to control intense, repetitive sexual impulses, or urges resulting in repetitive sexual behavior."

Researchers emphasize that in order to make a diagnosis of behavioral addiction, **functional impairments** must be present at work, in social relationships, or in other social situations.

One of the foremost experts in the field of behavioral addiction is Dr. Mark Griffiths, who proposed six criteria that help define whether a behavior can be considered an addiction. Griffiths' criteria are based on the earlier conceptual framework of an expert named R.I. Brown.

The Griffiths/Brown criteria for behavioral addiction:

- **Salience**: Domination of a person's life by the activity.
- **Euphoria**: A "buzz" or a "high" is derived from the activity.
- **Tolerance**: The activity has to be undertaken to a progressively greater extent to achieve the same high.
- **Withdrawal**: Stopping the activity leads to the occurrence of unpleasant emotions or physical effects.
- **Conflict**: The activity leads to conflict with others or self-conflict.
- **Relapse and reinstatement**: Resumption of the activity with the same vigor subsequent to attempts to abstain, negative life consequences, and negligence of job, educational, or career opportunities.
