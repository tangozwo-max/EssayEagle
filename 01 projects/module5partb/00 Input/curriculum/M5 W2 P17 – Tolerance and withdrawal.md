## M5 W2 P17 – Tolerance and withdrawal

**Tolerance** is a diminished response to alcohol or other drugs through repeated or prolonged exposure.

**Withdrawal** is a constellation of aversive symptoms—ranging from anxiety, tremors, and nausea, to hallucinations and frank seizures—brought on by sudden termination or dosage drop of long-term substance use.
![[Pasted image 20260614154057.png]]
\[Source: Pietrzykowski, Treistman; Psychology Today\]

The withdrawal/negative effect stage of the addiction cycle follows the binge/intoxication stage. It sets up the person for future rounds of bingeing or intoxication.

During withdrawal, the person experiences withdrawal symptoms that can include negative emotions and, sometimes, physical symptoms such as nausea and diarrhea due to the high number of opioid receptors in the gut. The severity of symptoms varies according to the substance used and the severity of that use. The negative feelings are thought to be caused by two factors:

1. diminished activation of the reward circuitry of the basal ganglia due to dopamine dysregulation
2. activation of the brain’s stress systems in the extended amygdala

## cAMP

One of the most studied pathways involved in the development of tolerance is the [[camp-pathway|cyclic AMP (cAMP) pathway]]. cAMP is a secondary messenger molecule involved in neuron signaling pathways. It is synthesized from adenosine triphosphate (ATP) by adenylyl cyclase.

Adenylyl cyclase, an enzyme, is attached (at the intracellular side) to dopamine receptors. The receptors either activate (D1) or inhibit (D2) the action of this enzyme, impacting intracellular cAMP levels.

The numerous cAMP-dependent pathways include:

- neurotransmitter release
- opening or closing ion channels
- receptor sensitivity
- receptor internalization
- gene expression changes

The activity of neurons depends on cAMP levels. Increased cAMP activates neurons, while decreased cAMP inhibits neurons. Opioids block the production of cAMP and, therefore, inhibit neurons from transmitting signals. Chronic use of opioids no longer decreases cAMP, resulting in tolerance.
![[Pasted image 20260614154109.png]]
 

During allostasis, neurons attempt to boost cAMP production to counter the expected decrease caused by opioid use. As a result, individuals must take more opioids to offset this increase in cAMP production.
![[Pasted image 20260614154117.png]]![[Pasted image 20260614154136.png]]
Allostasis can also cause changes in gene expression. This results in changes to opioid receptors, which include:

- becoming less sensitive to opioids (receptor phosphorylation and receptor and effector uncoupling)
- reducing the number of receptors by pulling them back inside the neurons (receptor internalization)

Most people can stop taking medications such as opioids with few withdrawal symptoms, but some individuals, like Fahad, become addicted and experience severe withdrawal. His symptoms are more than unpleasant—they are unbearable.

Environmental issues, such as exposure to drug or alcohol use when young, can increase people's vulnerability to addiction.

Anticipatory cAMP production in allostasis means that if a person stops taking opioids, the cAMP levels will remain high and cause neurons to be overactive. This mainly affects neurons in three areas, causing withdrawal symptoms. Withdrawal symptoms are more extreme versions of the normal functions of these neurons. The areas affected are the:

1. **[[locus-coeruleus|Locus coeruleus (LC)]] in the brain stem**, which causes jitteriness, insomnia, panic, anxiety, stress, sweating, large pupils, and fast heart rate and breathing
2. **GABAergic interneurons in the brain stem**, which cause feelings of discomfort
3. **gut**, where overactive neurons cause feces to be pushed out too rapidly and water cannot be reabsorbed, resulting in diarrhea
