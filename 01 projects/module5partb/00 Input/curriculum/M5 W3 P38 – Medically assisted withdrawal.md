## Medically assisted withdrawal

Medically assisted withdrawal is sometimes called detoxification. It involves supporting a person through the withdrawal symptoms that occur after the cessation of a substance such as alcohol or opioids. In people experiencing severe substance misuse disorder, these symptoms, such as seizures, can sometimes be life-threatening.

Withdrawal from most substances will follow the same principles, but there will be variations according to the substance used and its effects on the body. For example, if alcohol is stopped suddenly it is potentially life-threatening, however stopping opiates suddenly will make a person very sick but is not life-threatening.

Medically assisted withdrawal for most substances will include:

- a thorough medical assessment
- gradually reducing levels of whichever substance they are using
- medication to help reduce the withdrawal symptoms
- medical supervision to watch for dangerous symptoms emerging

After going through withdrawal, there needs to be support programs immediately in place to help the person with substance-seeking behaviors and cravings.

Hi Margo, I've been learning about medically assisted withdrawal. Could I ask you about it please?

Margo

Of course Sam. It's an interesting topic. What did you want to know?

Sam

Where does medically assisted withdrawal take place? I saw someone on TV who was in a psychiatric hospital.

Margo

Well, it can take place in either an inpatient or a community setting. In a community setting the patient is supported at home although they may attend their primary care practice or a pharmacy for their medication.

Sam

So how does a multidisciplinary team decide who should be where?

Margo

Well, inpatient settings are usually in place for those with a severe substance use disorder or comorbid health problems that need close monitoring. In some cases, if they have a high level of the substance in their system, they may need to be in an intensive care or intensive therapy unit.

Sam

Should the person supervising the treatment be specially trained in medically assisted withdrawal?

Margo

Given the risks and complexity, there is some evidence for better outcomes for service users when clinicians with specialist addictions training are involved, but it can be supervised by others as long as they follow guidance.
