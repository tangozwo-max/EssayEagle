## Case study: José continues his journey

José has come to talk to Cassandra, his primary care practitioner. They have regular meetings to support his recovery.

Cassandra

Good morning José, how are things today? I was sorry to hear your stepfather passed away.

José

Hello Cassandra, I'm feeling a bit better thank you. I'm sad Joe died but I'm sad for me, not for him, you know? He was in a lot of pain.

Cassandra

I can understand that José. There's nothing wrong in feeling that way. You cared for him for a long time. What can I do for you today?

José

I'm feeling restless, caring for Joe was a big part of my life for so long. I want to do something else but I don't want to go back to being a mechanic. I want to give something back.

Cassandra

That's good José. I don't want you to rush into things, but I'll help you in any way I can. Have you had any thoughts about what you want to do?

José

I'm not sure, but I want to help people like me in some way. Cyril, my peer support worker, has been such a help. Knowing he had been through what I was going through helped me to keep going. It also made it easier to open up to him. I thought maybe I could do something like that. How would I find out how to get started? Do you know?

Cassandra

I think you'd be good in that role José, you're very empathetic and articulate. You have shown that you are a good carer as well. I'd be happy to give you a reference if you need it. As for getting started, why don't you talk to Cyril about how he did it? I'm sure he'll have some useful insights.

Benefits to José include:

- Helping others can provide a strong sense of purpose and fulfillment, which is crucial for maintaining motivation in recovery.
- By sharing their experiences and coping strategies, peer support workers reinforce their own recovery skills and knowledge.
- Engaging with others in similar situations fosters a sense of community and reduces feelings of isolation.
- Supporting others can boost self-esteem and confidence, reinforcing the belief in their own ability to stay in recovery.

\[Source: Office for Health Improvement & Disparities; Du Plessis et al.\]

As well as the benefits to becoming a peer support worker or another lived experience role, there are also challenges that should be carefully considered before embarking on that route. The challenges include:

- unclear job descriptions
- integrating into hierarchical structures
- stigma and discrimination
- high workplace demands and the risk of emotional strain and stress (a known factor in relapse)
- poor supervision
- low pay
- lack of training or clear career pathways

\[Source: Englander et al.\]

## Challenges to peer support services

As you will have learned, there are a number of challenges for peer support services and their staff. These can be mitigated, however, through measures such as education, training, and clear funding.

Select each heading to learn more about what can be done to mitigate these challenges.
### Role integrity

There are several areas of mitigation that come under the heading of role integrity.

**Peer support scope of practice:**

- clear job descriptions and career paths
- lived experience workers to be involved in program development to familiarize other people with their role

**Harmonization of clinical services with peer support services:**

- training and education for non-peer staff on how to work with, supervise, and support lived experience workers

**Peer role drift:**

- a clear code of ethics and scope of practice for lived experience workers that is in line with any regional certification requirements
- close monitoring of the roles of lived experience workers to identify drift early and to address it before it becomes normalized
### Stigma

Lived experience workers may experience stigma that results from people's perceptions of those undergoing recovery. This stigma may devalue them as workers and as people.

Organizations can mitigate this challenge by promoting and modeling respectful, person-centered, trauma-informed, and culturally competent behavior and language.
### Recruitment and retention

Lived experience workers are often low-paid and lack opportunities for career progression. These issues, when combined with burnout and workplace culture, make it difficult to recruit or retain the skilled people that are required. Mitigations that can be put in place include:

- supporting professional development and advancement opportunities
- reducing barriers to training and certification
- addressing burnout and well-being
### Sustainability and funding

Peer support services are often nongovernmental, charitable, and volunteer services. There are two areas of challenge where the risk can be mitigated:

**Funding streams and reimbursement:**

- Commissioning organizations need to examine financial models to optimize the sustainability of peer support services.

**Strengthening organizations:**

- Organizations need to build their capacity, plan strategically, and strengthen their business operations. This may require them to contract with healthcare systems through mutually beneficial business models.
### Training and qualifications
Peer certification programs can help standardize the level of expertise of peer support mentors and people in other lived experience roles. In addition to this, further training should be made available to help people extend their skills. This could include:

**Comprehensive training:** Provide ongoing training on substance use disorders, recovery strategies, and crisis intervention.

**Specialized workshops:** Offer workshops on specific topics like trauma-informed care and motivational interviewing.

\[Source: Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration\]
