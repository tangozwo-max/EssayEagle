## M5 W2 P25 – Cocaine

Cocaine is derived from the leaves of the coca plant, which is a native of South America. It is available in two forms.

**Powdered cocaine** is a white, crystalline powder that can be snorted, rubbed on the gums, or dissolved in water and injected.

**Crack cocaine** is processed cocaine that forms a rock crystal that is usually smoked.

Cocaine can cause long-term damage to a person's mental health. It usually presents as mood or emotional disturbances but can include:

- hallucinations
- restlessness
- paranoia
- psychosis

## Mechanism of action

Cocaine primarily acts on the brain’s [[limbic-system|limbic system]], producing psychoactive and addictive effects. Cocaine:

- interferes with [[dopamine-transporter|dopamine reuptake]] and causes dopamine dysregulation
- stimulates cortisol release
- stimulates the HPA axis

Cocaine mainly affects dopamine neurotransmission in the limbic system but also affects other monoamine neurotransmitters such as serotonin, adrenaline, and noradrenaline.

The nucleus accumbens is part of the basal ganglia. Cocaine causes a build-up of dopamine, which is responsible for the initial sense of euphoria, by overactivating cells in the nucleus accumbens (NAc) in the limbic system.

Usually, the brain regulates dopamine by reabsorbing some of it back into dopaminergic cells if levels increase too much. Cocaine interferes with this process by binding to the dopamine transporter which helps the dopaminergic cells to reabsorb the dopamine. This causes a significant increase in dopamine.

If levels remain too high, receptor cells become less sensitive to dopamine. This desensitization diminishes a person’s ability to derive pleasure from normal activities, such as eating, socializing, or sex.
![[Pasted image 20260614155527.png]]
The hippocampus and the extended amygdala contain memory centers that associate external cues (such as people, places, or situations) with the pleasure obtained from cocaine-induced dopamine euphoria. This contributes to addiction by people craving more cocaine when those external cues are encountered. Over time, users then develop a near compulsion to respond to the cues by seeking and using cocaine.
![[Pasted image 20260614155542.png]]
Normally, the frontal cortex is where the brain integrates information and evaluates courses of action. It acts as a break on impulsive behavior. Cocaine impairs the frontal cortex and causes a person to disregard the negative effects of taking the drug.

Cocaine dependence impacts the stress system and increases cortisol activity. High stress and cortisol are known to impact learning, memory, and executive function.

Fox et al. found that learning and memory deficits in cocaine-dependent individuals are associated with increased cortisol levels. They also found higher relapse in individuals with greater memory deficits. The findings align with addiction models, which suggest that chronic cocaine-related neuroadaptations impact learning and memory functions, subsequently influencing drug use outcomes.

\[Source: Fox et al.\]

### Cocaine and the addiction cycle

Cocaine causes changes to the physical structure of some nerve cells, particularly in the nucleus accumbens (NAc). The cells grow more dendrites and are therefore able to collect more signals from other cells, such as those in the hippocampus, amygdala, and frontal cortex. This gives these regions more influence over the NAc and drives some of the behavioral changes associated with addiction, such as cravings and reduced impulse control.

Cocaine also affects gene expression within the nucleus accumbens (part of the basal ganglia), particularly the neurotransmitter glutamate and the natural opioid-like compounds produced in the brain. This altered gene expression can have long-term effects and can become worse each time cocaine is used.
