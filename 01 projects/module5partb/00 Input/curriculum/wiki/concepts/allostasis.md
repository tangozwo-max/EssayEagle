# Allostasis

A regulatory strategy by which the brain maintains stability through *change* — continuously adjusting physiological set-points in response to anticipated demands. It is distinguished from [[homeostasis]], which maintains stability through reactive error-correction feedback loops.

## Allostasis vs. homeostasis

| Feature | Homeostasis | Allostasis |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism | Reactive (corrects errors after they occur) | Feed-forward (anticipates and adjusts before demand arises) |
| Set-point | Fixed | Continuously shifting |
| Response speed | Slower | Faster |
| Risk | May be too slow for dangerous situations | Long-term cost to body systems |

## In addiction

Allostasis is the brain's adaptive response to chronic substance use:
1. A substance (e.g., opioids) chronically suppresses cAMP production
2. The brain anticipates this suppression and upregulates cAMP production to maintain neural function
3. This sets a **new, higher baseline** for reward/stress regulation
4. The person now needs the substance just to feel "normal" (the baseline has shifted downward for natural reward)

This produces an **allostatic state**: a long-term shift of the regulatory system away from its normal functioning. The cost is that the body's stress and reward systems are now chronically dysregulated.

## Allostasis and the addiction cycle

The [[addiction-cycle]] is understood as a product of allostatic processes involving adaptations of the reward system. Each cycle of binge → withdrawal → craving drives the allostatic set-point further from normal, making recovery progressively harder.

## Allostasis and tolerance/withdrawal

In the [[camp-pathway]]:
- Allostatic cAMP upregulation = mechanism of tolerance (higher doses needed)
- When the substance stops, the upregulated cAMP causes neuronal hyperactivity = withdrawal symptoms

## Long-term allostatic cost

Pathological allostasis leads to:
- Dysphoria as the reward baseline drops (anhedonia for natural rewards)
- Heightened stress reactivity
- Increased vulnerability to relapse even after long periods of abstinence
- Gene expression changes (receptor phosphorylation, internalisation)
