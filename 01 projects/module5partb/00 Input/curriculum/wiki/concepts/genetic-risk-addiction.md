# Genetic Risk for Addiction

Genetics contribute significantly to a person's risk of developing addiction, with family studies estimating that inherited factors account for **40–60%** of addiction risk. Multiple genes are involved, not a single "addiction gene," and their effects interact with psychological, social, and environmental factors.

## Cross-addiction gene variants
Scientists have identified variants of **19 genes** that are commonly inherited across addiction disorders, regardless of the specific substance involved. These genes are all linked to the regulation of **dopamine signaling** — suggesting a shared neurobiological pathway underlying addiction vulnerability.

## Specific gene-addiction relationships

### AKT1 — cannabis and psychosis
Cannabis users carrying a variant in the AKT1 gene have:
- **2x** increased probability of a psychotic disorder
- Up to **7x** if using cannabis daily
AKT1 is involved in dopamine signaling. Findings suggest AKT1 as a potential target for cannabis psychosis prevention and treatment.

### CHRNA5 — nicotine dependence
Two copies of a specific form of CHRNA5 doubles the likelihood of becoming nicotine-dependent compared to two copies of an alternative form.

### OPRM1 — naltrexone treatment response
A variant in the OPRM1 gene sequence can predict whether a person with alcohol use disorder will respond well to [[naltrexone]] treatment. This points toward genotype-informed treatment selection: some individuals may respond excellently to naltrexone; others may not respond at all and would benefit more from psychosocial interventions.

### HK2/RASGRF2 — injection drug use
People who inject drugs are 2–3 times more likely to have integration of the gene HK2 within RASGRF2, a gene involved in regulating dopaminergic brain activity.

## Important caveat
Despite advances, more than **95% of the genetic variance** in addiction remains unexplained — most genetic risk factors are yet to be discovered.
