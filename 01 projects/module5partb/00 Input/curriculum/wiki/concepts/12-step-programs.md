# 12-Step Programs (AA/NA/TSF)

A family of peer-led mutual aid programs for recovery from substance use disorders, originating with Alcoholics Anonymous (1935) and later extended to Narcotics Anonymous (NA) and Gamblers Anonymous. Twelve-Step Facilitation (TSF) is a clinical programme designed to encourage engagement with AA or NA.

## Evidence
The 2020 Cochrane review found that AA combined with TSF led to higher rates of **continuous abstinence** over months to years compared to other interventions — a strong evidence base for their effectiveness.

## Core assumptions
- Addiction is a disease (not a moral failing)
- Complete abstinence is the only management option
- Recovery requires ongoing engagement with the programme and community
- Higher power / spiritual dimension guides the steps (member-defined interpretation encouraged)
- Members continue to identify as alcoholics/addicts long after meeting clinical remission criteria

## Critiques
- **Spiritual emphasis** — references to a "higher power" and prayer make the ethos culturally or personally unacceptable to some
- **Disease model limitation** — the disease + abstinence-only framing conflicts with harm reduction and moderation-based recovery models
- **Lack of professional oversight** — peer-led format may not suit people with complex comorbidities
- **Cultural variation** — NA adaptations in Iran show some innovative changes making the approach more locally applicable
