# Anti-Reward System

Neurochemical systems that counteract and switch off the positive effects of the brain's reward pathways. These systems normally provide a brake on reward-seeking; in addiction, they become chronically activated as a counter-adaptation to excessive reward stimulation.

## Development in addiction

As a person progresses deeper into the [[addiction-cycle]], anti-reward systems develop and intensify. They remain dormant during active substance use but emerge strongly when the substance is removed, driving the **withdrawal/negative affect** stage.

## Key components

Three neurochemical players constitute the anti-reward system:

### Corticotropin-Releasing Factor (CRF)
- Controls hormonal, sympathetic, and behavioural responses to stressors
- Activates the HPA axis
- **CRF antagonists can block excessive drug-taking during dependence** — a key therapeutic target
- Central to plasticity of the [[extended-amygdala]] during addiction development

### Norepinephrine (Noradrenaline)
- Increases alertness, arousal, and attention
- Constricts blood vessels (maintains blood pressure during stress)
- Affects sleep-wake cycle, mood, and memory
- Hyperactivity during ACE-related stress is mitigated by alcohol and opioid use, driving use

### Dynorphin
- A powerful endogenous modulator of pain, stress, mood, and addiction
- Acts primarily through **kappa-opioid receptors** in the brain
- Produces dysphoria (the opposite of euphoria)
- Counterbalances the dopamine reward signal; elevated in deep addiction

## Clinical significance

The anti-reward system explains why:
- Withdrawal is more than just the absence of pleasure — it is actively aversive
- Substance use becomes motivated by **relief from negative states** rather than seeking pleasure (negative reinforcement)
- CRF antagonists are being researched as addiction treatments
