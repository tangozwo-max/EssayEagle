# Neurodivergence

A framework for describing **variation in neurological processing** — a determinant of mental health that may increase or decrease risk depending on fit with environment and support.

## Three related terms

### Neurotypical
A person who thinks and processes information in an expected way for their culture and setting. Typical developmental milestones, manageable sensory tolerance, adaptability, sustained focus, age-typical varied interests.

### Neurodivergent
People who process information and behave in ways that differ from actual or perceived cultural norms. Includes:
- Autism
- ADHD
- Tourette's syndrome
- Dyslexia, dyspraxia, and some other learning differences
- (Formerly Asperger's syndrome — now part of the autism spectrum)

### Neurodiversity
A **movement / conceptual framework** promoting acceptance, inclusion, and support for neurodivergent individuals. Challenges stigma and discrimination; advocates equal opportunity.

## Two models for viewing neurodivergence
- **Medical / disability model** — frames differences as *disorders*. Common still in many countries.
- **Neuroaffirmative model** — frames differences as variation requiring support and accommodation, not cure.

## Challenges faced by neurodivergent individuals
- Social interaction and communication difficulties → isolation, anxiety
- Stigma and discrimination
- Sensory sensitivities
- Executive-function challenges (time management, overload)
- Diagnostic overshadowing — providers attributing mental-health symptoms to the neurodivergence diagnosis itself
