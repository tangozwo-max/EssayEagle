# Intergenerational Trauma

Trauma effects that pass across generations without direct exposure to the original traumatic event. Children can be affected by trauma experienced by their parents or ancestors through various biological, psychological, and social mechanisms.

## Mechanisms of transmission

### Biological mechanisms
- Epigenetic changes (gene expression alterations without DNA sequence changes)
- Altered stress response physiology (HPA axis dysregulation)
- Inflammatory changes and immune system impacts

### Psychological mechanisms
- Parental trauma affecting parenting capacity and emotional availability
- Modeling of trauma responses and coping patterns
- Transmission of fear, hypervigilance, and anxiety

### Social mechanisms
- Family and community narratives about trauma
- Disrupted family relationships and support systems
- Marginalization and systemic disadvantage continuing across generations

## Impact on subsequent generations

Children of trauma survivors may experience:
- Heightened anxiety and stress responsiveness
- Attachment difficulties
- Emotional regulation challenges
- Increased vulnerability to mental health conditions
- Intergenerational cycle of trauma

## Prevention and intervention

Healing and support for parents and families can interrupt intergenerational trauma cycles. Understanding this concept emphasizes that traumatized individuals benefit whole families and communities.
