# Dynorphin

An endogenous opioid peptide that is a powerful modulator of pain, stress, mood, and addiction. Unlike endorphins (which produce euphoria via mu-opioid receptors), dynorphin primarily acts on **kappa-opioid receptors (KOR)** and produces **dysphoria** — an unpleasant, aversive state.

## Role in the anti-reward system

Dynorphin is a key component of the brain's [[anti-reward-system]]. As addiction progresses:
- Dopamine-driven reward surges trigger dynorphin release as a counter-regulation
- Elevated dynorphin produces dysphoria, blunting the reward effect
- This contributes to **tolerance** (diminished reward despite same dose) and **anhedonia** (inability to feel pleasure from normal activities)
- During withdrawal, dynorphin activity is prominent, reinforcing aversive states

## Kappa-opioid receptors

Kappa-opioid receptors are found in the striatum, limbic system, and cortex. Activation produces:
- Pain modulation (analgesic at high doses)
- Dysphoria and sedation
- Hallucinations at high doses
- Appetite suppression

## Therapeutic relevance

KOR antagonists are being investigated as potential treatments for depression and addiction, as blocking dynorphin's aversive effects may reduce the negative reinforcement driving drug-seeking.
