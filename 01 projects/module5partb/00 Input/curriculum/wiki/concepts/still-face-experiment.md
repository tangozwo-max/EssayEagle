# Still Face Experiment

The Still Face Experiment, developed by Dr. Edward Tronick in 1978, is a landmark paradigm demonstrating the critical importance of caregiver responsiveness to infant emotional regulation and attachment security. The protocol consists of three phases that vividly illustrate infants' dependence on active caregiver engagement and their inability to self-soothe during distress.

## Protocol and Observations

The experiment unfolds across three sequential phases:

**Phase 1: Normal Interaction**
The mother engages naturally with the infant through talking, smiling, eye contact, and responsive interaction. The infant responds positively with smiling, laughing, and engaged play.

**Phase 2: Still Face**
The mother presents an emotionally unresponsive, immobile face, ceasing all verbal and physical engagement. The infant attempts to reengage the mother through increasingly distressed vocalisations and behaviours but receives no reciprocal response. The infant's distress escalates markedly.

**Phase 3: Re-engagement**
The mother resumes normal, responsive interaction. The securely attached infant gradually calms and returns to engaged, positive affect.

## Key Findings and Implications

The experiment reveals several critical insights about infant development:

- **Dependence on co-regulation**: Infants lack the neurological capacity to self-soothe; they require active adult support to modulate emotional arousal.
- **Disruption of learning**: During periods of caregiver unresponsiveness, infants cannot feel safe enough to explore or learn, highlighting the inseparability of emotional security and cognitive engagement.
- **Speed of recovery**: Securely attached infants recover quickly upon caregiver re-engagement, demonstrating the protective effect of secure attachment.
- **Impact of inconsistent responsiveness**: Infants who regularly experience caregiver unresponsiveness or inconsistency may develop more extreme distress responses and slower recovery trajectories.

## Clinical and Service Implications

The Still Face Experiment provides a powerful metaphor for understanding how service user engagement patterns may reflect early attachment experiences. People whose caregivers were frequently unresponsive or inconsistent may anticipate similar unresponsiveness from healthcare and social services, leading them to withdraw engagement or escalate distress behaviours as a bid for attention and reassurance.
