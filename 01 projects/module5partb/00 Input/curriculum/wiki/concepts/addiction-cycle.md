# Addiction Cycle

A three-stage neurobiological model describing how addiction is maintained and escalates over time. Each stage is driven by distinct brain circuits and reinforces the next. Originally developed from animal studies by **Koob et al.**, later confirmed in human studies by **Volkow**.

## The three stages

| Stage | Primary brain region | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Binge / Intoxication** | Basal ganglia (nucleus accumbens) | Dopamine surge → euphoria, positive reinforcement |
| **Withdrawal / Negative affect** | Extended amygdala | Dopamine drops below baseline + stress system activation → dysphoria, negative reinforcement |
| **Preoccupation / Anticipation** | Prefrontal cortex | Cue-triggered craving, impaired impulse control → compulsive drug-seeking |

### 1. Binge / Intoxication
The substance over-activates the reward circuit (nucleus accumbens, basal ganglia), producing a dopamine-mediated high. Positive reinforcement drives repeated use and habit formation.

### 2. Withdrawal / Negative affect
As the substance clears, dopamine activity in the basal ganglia drops below baseline and the [[extended-amygdala]] stress system activates. The dysphoria, anxiety, and physical symptoms constitute **negative reinforcement**: the person uses again to escape discomfort rather than to seek pleasure.

### 3. Preoccupation / Anticipation (craving)
The [[prefrontal-cortex]] and hippocampus drive compulsive drug-seeking through cue-triggered craving and impaired impulse control. This is the "wanting" stage that reconnects back to binge/intoxication.

## Allostasis and cycle escalation

[[Tolerance-withdrawal|Tolerance]] means each binge produces less reward; withdrawal becomes more severe. The brain adapts via **[[allostasis]]** — continuously shifting the reward set-point downward. The person uses increasingly not to get high but just to reach baseline. Each cycle deepens the allostatic state.

## Key behaviours in the addiction cycle

- **Impulsivity** — acting without foresight; difficulty resisting urges or delaying pleasure
- **Positive reinforcement** — pleasure from substance use drives repetition
- **Negative reinforcement** — substance use relieves withdrawal discomfort, increasing probability of use
- **Compulsivity** — repeating behaviours despite adverse consequences

## Anti-reward systems

As addiction deepens, **[[anti-reward-system|anti-reward systems]]** develop in the extended amygdala:
- **CRF (Corticotropin-releasing factor)** — controls stress responses; CRF antagonists can block excessive drug-taking
- **Norepinephrine** — increases arousal and unease during withdrawal
- **Dynorphin** — kappa-opioid modulator producing dysphoria

## Substance-specific patterns

The addiction cycle applies across all major substances:
- **Alcohol:** Basal ganglia rewards (euphoria, anxiety reduction) → withdrawal anxiety/dysphoria → craving
- **Cannabis:** Nucleus accumbens dopamine → extended amygdala withdrawal → PFC craving/executive dysfunction
- **Cocaine:** Limbic dopamine surge → dopamine depletion and cortisol elevation → compulsive craving
- **Methamphetamine:** Massive dopamine release (6–12h) → near-psychotic withdrawal crash → negative reinforcement cycle

## DSM-5 SUD severity

The symptoms of SUD fall into four categories (impaired control, social problems, risky use, physical dependence):
- 2–3 symptoms = mild
- 4–5 symptoms = moderate
- 6+ symptoms = severe (addiction)

## Why cycles escalate

[[Tolerance-withdrawal|Tolerance]] means each binge produces less reward; [[tolerance-withdrawal|withdrawal]] becomes more severe. The set-point for "normal" mood shifts downward (allostasis), so the person uses increasingly to reach baseline — not just to get high.
