# cAMP Pathway (Cyclic AMP Signalling)

The cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) pathway is a key intracellular second messenger system in neurons, central to the development of drug tolerance and the neurobiological basis of withdrawal. It is one of the most studied mechanisms in addiction pharmacology.

## Components

- **cAMP** — cyclic adenosine monophosphate; synthesised from ATP by adenylyl cyclase
- **Adenylyl cyclase (adenylate cyclase)** — the enzyme that produces cAMP, attached to dopamine receptors on the intracellular side
- **PKA (Protein Kinase A)** — activated by elevated cAMP; phosphorylates target proteins → changes in neuronal excitability and gene expression
- **D1 receptors** — activate adenylyl cyclase → cAMP rises → excitatory effect
- **D2 receptors** — inhibit adenylyl cyclase → cAMP falls → inhibitory effect

## Mechanism of opioid tolerance

1. Opioids bind to opioid receptors → inhibit adenylyl cyclase → **cAMP decreases** → neurons stop firing
2. With chronic opioid use, neurons counter-adapt (**allostasis**): they upregulate adenylyl cyclase to restore normal cAMP levels
3. Higher doses are now needed to suppress cAMP back down — this is **tolerance**

## Mechanism of opioid withdrawal

1. Opioids are stopped → the up-regulated adenylyl cyclase is no longer inhibited
2. **cAMP rises sharply** → neurons become hyperactive
3. Hyperactive neurons in three areas generate withdrawal symptoms:
   - **Locus coeruleus** → jitteriness, panic, sweating, tachycardia, insomnia
   - **GABAergic interneurons (brainstem)** → generalised discomfort
   - **Gut neurons** → diarrhoea (rapid transit, poor water reabsorption)

## Gene expression changes

Allostasis-driven changes in gene expression alter opioid receptor properties:
- **Receptor phosphorylation** — reduced sensitivity to opioid ligands
- **Receptor internalisation** — receptors withdrawn inside neurons; fewer available

## Cannabis and cAMP

THC modulates G-protein-coupled inhibition of cAMP via CB1 receptors — a similar mechanism to opioid-induced cAMP suppression, contributing to cannabis tolerance.
