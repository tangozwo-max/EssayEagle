# Baclofen

A muscle relaxant and antispastic agent that also has emerging evidence for use in alcohol use disorder (AUD). Baclofen works via a distinct mechanism from other AUD medications — targeting GABA-B receptors rather than opioid receptors.

## Mechanism of action
- **Selective GABA-B receptor agonist** — activates gamma-aminobutyric acid type B receptors
- Primary mechanism in AUD: **suppresses alcohol-stimulated dopamine release** in the [[mesolimbic-pathway]] (nucleus accumbens), reducing the rewarding effects of drinking
- May also influence neurotransmitter systems involved in memory, motivation, and anxiety
- Can induce a state of **indifference toward alcohol** — a distinct effect compared to aversion (disulfiram) or reward-blocking (naltrexone)

## Clinical role
- Used off-label or under guidance for AUD, particularly for patients with:
  - High craving levels
  - Significant anxiety comorbidity (GABA-B activation has anxiolytic effects)
  - Prior failure of other AUD medications
- Some evidence for benefit in people who find total abstinence challenging

## Limitations
- Evidence base is mixed; some high-dose trials show efficacy, others do not
- Risk of sedation, fatigue, and dizziness
- Dependency and withdrawal risk with abrupt discontinuation
