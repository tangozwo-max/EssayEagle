# Executive Function

Executive function encompasses the set of mental skills underlying planning, prioritisation, impulse control, sustained attention, and working memory. Often described using the NSPCC metaphor of "air traffic control," executive function manages the flow of information through the brain, enabling individuals to organise behaviour toward goal achievement whilst flexibly adapting to changing circumstances. These skills develop gradually from infancy through the mid-twenties, with critical foundation-building occurring during early childhood through attachment relationships and play-based learning.

## Core Executive Functions

The primary components include:

- **Planning ahead**: The ability to consider future consequences and formulate sequences of actions
- **Prioritisation**: Selecting which goals or tasks to address given multiple demands
- **Impulse control**: Inhibiting immediate responses in favour of more adaptive, goal-directed behaviour
- **Focus and attention**: Maintaining concentration despite distractions or competing stimuli
- **Cognitive flexibility**: Shifting between mental sets or strategies when circumstances change

## Developmental Trajectory

Executive function emerges gradually. Infants as young as 6-12 months begin developing foundational attentional control through serve-and-return interactions. Early childhood games like peek-a-boo build working memory and self-control. By early school years, children can follow multi-step instructions and inhibit impulses for brief periods. Executive function continues developing through adolescence and into the mid-twenties, with the prefrontal cortex—the brain region most critical for executive control—not reaching maturity until approximately age 25.

## Role of Attachment and Play

Secure attachment relationships provide the emotional safety and attentional scaffolding necessary for executive function development. Caregivers who help children regulate attention, break tasks into manageable steps, and model problem-solving facilitate executive function growth. Play, particularly imaginative and rule-based play, is essential for building these capacities.

## Developmental Disorders

Children with ADHD, executive function disorders, or other neurodevelopmental conditions often struggle with planning, impulse control, and sustained attention. These difficulties frequently lead to academic underachievement and social-emotional challenges. Teachers and parents who understand these underlying deficits rather than interpreting them as laziness or wilfulness can provide more effective support.
