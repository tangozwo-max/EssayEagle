# Attachment Styles

Attachment styles describe the characteristic patterns of relating to caregivers that develop through early experiences. Identified through systematic observation (notably the Strange Situation Procedure), these patterns reflect fundamental differences in expectations about caregiver availability, responsiveness, and the safety of proximity-seeking. Whilst secure attachment provides a template for healthy relationships, insecure patterns can persist throughout life unless addressed through intervention.

## Secure Attachment

Children with secure attachment are comfortable using their caregiver as a safe haven during distress and a secure base for exploration. They experience age-appropriate separation anxiety and are readily soothed upon reunion. Secure attachment develops when caregivers provide consistent, responsive, and predictable care.

## Insecure Anxious-Ambivalent Attachment

Children with this style show considerable distress upon separation but do not become calm upon reunion. They may reject or resist caregiver attention, sometimes displaying aggression. This pattern typically emerges when caregivers are inconsistently responsive to the child's needs.

## Insecure Avoidant Attachment

Children with avoidant attachment tend to avoid or ignore their caregiver, especially after periods of absence. They do not necessarily reject caregiver attention but fail to seek it out or show preference for the caregiver over strangers. This pattern often develops when caregivers are emotionally unavailable or dismissive of the child's bids for attention.

## Insecure Disorganised Attachment

Children with disorganised attachment display inconsistent and contradictory responses to their caregiver—sometimes avoiding, sometimes seeking proximity; occasionally displaying aggression or fear. This pattern emerges when the caregiver is simultaneously the source of both fear and comfort, as occurs in contexts of maltreatment, substance abuse, or untreated parental mental illness.

## Behavioural Manifestations and Developmental Consequences

Children with insecure attachments may employ disruptive behaviours to maintain connection with their caregiver, creating a maladaptive cycle wherein the child's behaviour elicits attention through conflict rather than cooperation. If used consistently, such strategies can entrench negative relational patterns and complicate future relationships. Insecure attachment is associated with difficulties in emotional self-regulation and increased vulnerability to internalising and externalising difficulties.
