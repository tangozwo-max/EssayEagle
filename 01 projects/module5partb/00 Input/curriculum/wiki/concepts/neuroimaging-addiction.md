# Neuroimaging in Addiction Research

Neuroimaging techniques have enabled researchers to directly observe structural and functional brain changes in people with addiction, providing objective evidence for addiction as a brain disease. The most studied substance in addiction neuroimaging is cocaine.

## Key neuroimaging modalities in addiction

### PET (Positron Emission Tomography)
Used with radioligands to measure receptor density and neurotransmitter release. The radioligand **[C11]Raclopride** is a D2 receptor competitive antagonist: less binding = more dopamine present; more binding = less dopamine.

Key finding (Volkow et al.): Cocaine users watching cocaine cue videos showed reduced raclopride binding in the **dorsal striatum**, directly correlating with self-reported craving severity. Subjects with highest withdrawal/addiction severity scores showed the greatest dopamine changes.

### fMRI (Functional MRI)
Measures brain activity via the BOLD signal (Blood Oxygen Level Dependent).

Key finding: Cocaine users show **lower D2 receptor density** in the striatum, persisting for at least 4 months after cessation — indicating that tolerance-related receptor loss is long-lasting.

Key finding (Volkow et al.): Cocaine users showed significantly reduced **frontal glucose metabolism** in 16/21 left and 8/21 right frontal regions, persisting 3–4 months after abstinence. Reductions correlated with duration and amount of cocaine use.

### sMRI (Structural MRI)
Measures brain volume, cortical thickness, and white matter integrity.

Key finding (Schinz et al.): Cocaine use disorder patients showed **accelerated cortical thinning** (cortical thickness reduction) compared to healthy controls, analysed using the **BrainAGE** framework. Machine learning on 74 CUD patients vs. 62 controls revealed significantly accelerated brain aging in CUD.

## BrainAGE (Brain Age Gap Estimation)

A machine learning measure comparing predicted brain age (from sMRI) to chronological age. A positive BrainAGE gap indicates the brain appears older than it is — found in cocaine use disorder, indicating accelerated aging of cortical tissue.

## Clinical implications

- Neuroimaging provides biomarkers for addiction severity and recovery monitoring
- Reduced D2 receptors and frontal hypometabolism explain impaired decision-making and impulse control
- Cortical thinning suggests long-term neurotoxic effects even after abstinence
