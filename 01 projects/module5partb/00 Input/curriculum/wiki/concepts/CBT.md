# CBT — Cognitive Behavioural Therapy

Psychotherapy aiming to change patterns of thinking and behaviour. Based on the idea that **thoughts, feelings, and behaviours are interconnected** — changing negative thought patterns → changes in feelings and behaviours.

## Neurobiological effects
- Promotes **neuroplasticity** — creates new healthy neural pathways.
- Strengthens existing positive neuron networks.
- Regulates stress response — reduces cortisol.
- Reduces pro-inflammatory cytokines; boosts serotonin and dopamine.
- Reduces amygdala activity; enhances PFC activity.
- Improves sleep quality.

## Evidence
Shields et al. (2020) meta-analysis of 56 RCTs: psychosocial interventions (especially CBT) linked to positive immune-system changes persisting ≥6 months.

## CBT in addiction (M5 W3)
CBT views addiction as a **learned behaviour** emerging in an environmental context, maintained by thought patterns. It is one of the interventions with the strongest evidence base for SUD (Magill et al.).

### Two components specific to addiction
1. **Functional analysis** — therapist and service user examine the causes and consequences of substance use; identify thoughts, feelings, and circumstances that led to both starting and continuing use. Builds self-understanding.
2. **Skills training** — learning (or relearning) coping skills for situations that trigger substance use; unlearning old habits; developing healthier patterns; changing cognitions about substance use.

### In practice (Tim's case — gambling disorder)
- Identified automatic gambling thoughts and cognitive distortions (e.g., belief that wins reflect personal skill)
- Used a tally of losses vs. wins to challenge distorted thinking
- Guided imagery to rehearse walking away from gambling situations
- Embedded [[motivational-interviewing]] to address ambivalence, shame, and stigma
