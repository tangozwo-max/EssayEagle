# Mental Health Screening

Mental health screening is a crucial step in identifying children and young people who may need further psychological support or intervention. It uses standardised sets of questions to explore children's and young people's experiences of symptoms related to potential mental health conditions, including their thoughts, emotions, and behaviours.

## Key characteristics

- **Standardised tools**: uses validated questionnaires with established cut-off scores
- **Symptom exploration**: covers areas such as school, friendships, home life, and potential risks (substance use, self-harm)
- **Conversational approach**: best conducted as part of a structured conversation rather than as a stand-alone measure
- **Population-level**: can be administered at group or population level, with referral for those exceeding clinical cut-off scores
- **Accessibility**: some tools require qualified mental health professionals; others can be used by trained lay people

## Administration methods

Screening can be:
- Self-reported by children and young people themselves
- Completed by parents or teachers
- Administered by mental health professionals or trained staff

## Clinical cut-off

A predetermined score threshold indicating need for further individual or group assessment and potential intervention.

## Ethical considerations

Screening should consider:
- Cultural appropriateness and validation in local contexts
- Availability of follow-up services for identified needs
- Impact of diagnostic labelling and potential stigma
- Informed consent and data management
- Community trust and acceptability
