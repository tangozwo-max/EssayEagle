# Separation Anxiety

Separation anxiety refers to the emotional distress experienced by infants and young children upon separation from their primary caregiver. Whilst age-appropriate separation anxiety represents a healthy developmental milestone indicating secure attachment, clinically significant separation anxiety reflects excessive distress that exceeds developmental expectations and substantially interferes with functioning.

## Developmental Manifestation

Separation anxiety emerges gradually during infancy and reaches its peak around 12 months of age, coinciding with the clear-cut attachment stage. Its appearance indicates that the child has formed a specific attachment and understands object permanence sufficiently to recognise the caregiver's absence. Age-appropriate separation anxiety typically diminishes as children develop language, understanding of time, and confidence in the caregiver's return.

## Normative Development

Healthy separation anxiety involves:
- Distress upon caregiver departure but not panic
- Ability to accept comfort from other familiar adults
- Rapid settling when caregiver returns
- Increasing tolerance of separation as the child ages
- Gradual decrease by age 3-4 years for brief separations

The secure infant protests separation but can be comforted by other adults and trusts the caregiver will return.

## Clinical Presentations

Clinically significant separation anxiety may present as:
- Excessive distress disproportionate to age and circumstance
- Refusal of school attendance or other necessary separations
- Persistent fear of caregiver's death or abandonment
- Physical symptoms (headaches, stomach pain) anticipating separation
- Significant functional impairment in academic, social, or family domains

## Contextual Factors

Post-COVID-19, some older children who had experienced prolonged isolation and disrupted caregiving showed new or exacerbated separation anxiety, illustrating how major environmental disruptions can alter attachment security and increase vulnerability.
