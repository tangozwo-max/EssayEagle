# Methadone

A synthetic opioid medication used as a first-line pharmacological treatment for opioid use disorder (OUD). It is also used for chronic pain management. In addiction medicine, methadone is the most widely used [[opioid-substitution-therapy]] agent globally.

## Mechanism of action
- **Full mu opioid receptor agonist** — activates mu receptors similarly to natural opioids (endorphins, enkephalins), producing analgesia and reducing opioid cravings without the sharp euphoric peak of short-acting opioids
- **NMDA receptor antagonist** — blocks N-methyl-D-aspartate receptors involved in pain transmission and tolerance development, providing additional analgesic benefit
- **Long half-life** (24–36 hours) — maintains stable blood drug levels, reduces the frequency of dosing, and smooths withdrawal symptoms

## Clinical advantages
- Reduces illicit opioid use and associated harms (infections, overdose, crime)
- Allows once-daily supervised dosing in clinical settings
- Can be taken orally (tablet or liquid), reducing injecting risk

## Key risks
- **Accumulation risk** — the long half-life means methadone can build up in the body; careful dose monitoring is required, especially in the early phase of treatment
- **QT prolongation** — cardiac risk at higher doses
- **Respiratory depression** — risk increases if combined with benzodiazepines or alcohol
- **Overdose** if diverted or taken without tolerance

## Regulatory position
Methadone or [[buprenorphine]] are the accepted first-line treatments for opioid detoxification under NICE guidelines.
