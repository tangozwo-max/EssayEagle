# Brief Interventions

Short, low-intensity therapeutic contacts designed to improve a person's motivation to address their substance misuse. They typically occur outside specialist substance use services, in settings such as primary care, emergency departments, or needle exchange programs.

## Characteristics
- **Duration:** 5–60 minutes per session
- **Number of sessions:** No more than 5 (NICE recommends 2 sessions of 10–45 minutes)
- **Delivery:** Usually by non-specialist healthcare staff (nurses, GPs, pharmacists)
- **Approach:** Non-judgmental, motivational, focused on exploring ambivalence rather than giving advice

## Evidence and approach
- NICE guidance: brief interventions should be offered whenever there is concern about substance use, aiming to increase motivation to change and provide nonjudgmental feedback
- Effective for reducing alcohol use in low-middle-income countries (Ghosh et al.); single nurse-delivered sessions appeared most effective in African settings
- WHO ASSIST-BI program (Alcohol, Smoking and Substance Involvement Screening Test + brief intervention): demonstrated effectiveness in Thailand when delivered immediately after screening

## Cultural considerations
Availability and acceptability of brief interventions vary widely by region. In Arab-majority countries, polysubstance misuse is more prevalent than alcohol, and stigma and legal factors affect reporting and engagement.

## Relationship to motivational interviewing
Brief interventions often draw on [[motivational-interviewing]] principles (OARS, exploring ambivalence, supporting autonomy) without constituting full MI therapy.
