# Behavioral Addiction

A pattern of behavior — rather than substance use — that becomes compulsive, persists despite negative consequences, and disrupts functioning. The ASAM definition of addiction explicitly includes behaviors: "People with addiction use substances or **engage in behaviors** that become compulsive and often continue despite harmful consequences."

## DSM-5 and ICD-11 recognition
- **DSM-5:** Only [[gambling-disorder]] is recognized as a standalone behavioral addiction (reclassified from impulse-control disorder in 2013 based on neuroimaging and pharmacological evidence)
- **ICD-11:** Recognizes both gambling disorder and gaming disorder under "Disorders due to addictive behaviors"; also classifies "compulsive sexual behavior disorder"
- Behaviors such as compulsive buying, internet use, exercise, or stealing were NOT included in DSM-5, pending more research

## Neurobiological basis
Debate exists about whether behavioral addictions truly involve the same neurobiological changes as substance addictions. Evidence from compulsive gambling showed changes to neurotransmitter systems (including dopamine) identical to those found in substance addictions — the basis for its DSM-5 reclassification.

## [[griffiths-brown-criteria]] — defining a behavioral addiction
Mark Griffiths (building on R.I. Brown) proposed six diagnostic criteria:
1. **Salience** — domination of a person's life by the activity
2. **Euphoria** — a "buzz" or "high" derived from the activity
3. **Tolerance** — needing to do more to achieve the same high
4. **Withdrawal** — unpleasant emotions or physical effects when stopping
5. **Conflict** — conflict with others or internal self-conflict
6. **Relapse and reinstatement** — resumption with same vigor after attempts to abstain

Functional impairment at work, in social relationships, or in other situations must be present for a behavioral addiction diagnosis.

## Co-occurrence with mental health conditions
Compulsive behaviors like gambling and gaming frequently co-occur with anxiety, OCD, and substance misuse. Symptoms typically begin between ages 20 and 30.
