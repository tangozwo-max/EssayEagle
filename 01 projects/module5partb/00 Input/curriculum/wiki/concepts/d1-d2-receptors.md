# D1 and D2 Dopamine Receptors

Two major families of dopamine receptors located on GABAergic neurons in the nucleus accumbens (and elsewhere). They have opposing effects on intracellular signalling via the cAMP second messenger pathway.

## D1 Receptors

- **Type:** Gs-coupled (stimulatory G-protein)
- **Location:** Primarily postsynaptic membranes of GABAergic neurons
- **Mechanism:** Dopamine binding → Gs activation → adenylate cyclase stimulated → **cAMP rises** → PKA activation → phosphorylation of target proteins
- **Effect:** Increased neuronal excitability; enhanced synaptic plasticity; facilitates neurotransmitter release (excitatory)
- **In addiction:** Underactivation of D1 receptors impairs working memory and cognitive flexibility

## D2 Receptors

- **Type:** Gi-coupled (inhibitory G-protein)
- **Location:** Pre- and postsynaptic membranes of GABAergic neurons
- **Mechanism:** Dopamine binding → Gi activation → adenylate cyclase inhibited → **cAMP falls** → reduced PKA activity
- **Effect:** Decreased neuronal excitability; inhibits neurotransmitter release; presynaptically acts as autoreceptor to regulate dopamine release (inhibitory)
- **In schizophrenia:** D2 overactivation is associated with positive symptoms; antipsychotics block D2
- **In addiction:** D2 receptor density decreases with chronic substance use (detectable by PET); lower D2 density is associated with greater addiction severity

## Role in the cAMP pathway and tolerance

The cAMP pathway mediates tolerance to opioids:
- Opioids → suppress cAMP → inhibit neuronal firing
- Chronic use → neurons adapt (allostasis) → boost cAMP production to compensate
- Tolerance: higher doses needed; withdrawal: cAMP overproduction when drug stops

## In neuroimaging

[C11]Raclopride PET scanning measures D2 receptor availability. Reduced raclopride binding = higher dopamine; increased binding = lower dopamine. Cocaine users show reduced D2 density in the striatum.
