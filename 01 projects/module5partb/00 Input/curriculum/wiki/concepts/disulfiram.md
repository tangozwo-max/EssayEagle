# Disulfiram

An aversion-based pharmacological treatment for alcohol use disorder (AUD). Disulfiram does not reduce cravings or block the reward of alcohol — instead, it makes drinking acutely unpleasant by causing a toxic reaction when alcohol is consumed.

## Mechanism of action
1. **Inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH)** — this enzyme is crucial for metabolising acetaldehyde (the first breakdown product of alcohol in the liver)
2. **Acetaldehyde accumulates** in the blood — without ALDH, acetaldehyde cannot be broken down normally
3. **Disulfiram-ethanol reaction (DER)** — the acetaldehyde buildup causes a highly unpleasant cluster of symptoms:
   - Facial flushing and sweating
   - Severe headache
   - Nausea and vomiting
   - Palpitations and tachycardia
   - Dyspnoea

## Intended effect
The DER is intended to deter alcohol consumption through learned aversion — the person knows that drinking will cause this reaction and chooses to abstain.

## Clinical considerations
- Requires **committed motivation to abstinence** — the person must actively choose not to drink
- **Supervised ingestion** may improve effectiveness (i.e., taken in front of a healthcare worker or family member)
- **Contraindications** include cardiovascular disease, psychosis, liver disease, pregnancy
- Can be combined with psychosocial support; less effective as a standalone intervention
