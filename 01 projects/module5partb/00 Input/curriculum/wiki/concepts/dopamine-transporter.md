# Dopamine Transporter (DAT)

A membrane-spanning protein responsible for the **reuptake of dopamine** from the synaptic cleft back into the presynaptic neuron. This reuptake is the primary mechanism by which synaptic dopamine levels are regulated and terminated.

## Normal function

After dopamine is released into the synapse and binds to postsynaptic receptors, the DAT transports dopamine back into the presynaptic terminal, recycling it. This terminates the dopamine signal and prevents excessive receptor stimulation.

## Cocaine's effect on DAT

Cocaine **binds to and blocks** the DAT, preventing dopamine reuptake. This causes:
- Massive accumulation of dopamine in the synapse (particularly in the nucleus accumbens)
- Sustained, intense receptor stimulation
- Euphoria and the high characteristic of cocaine use
- Over time, receptor desensitisation and anhedonia

## Methamphetamine's effect on DAT

Methamphetamine **also blocks** the DAT (reuptake inhibition), but additionally **reverses** its function — causing the transporter to pump dopamine **out** of the presynaptic terminal rather than in. This results in:
- Even larger dopamine surges than cocaine
- More prolonged effects (6–12 hours vs. cocaine's 30–90 minutes)
- Greater neurotoxicity

## In tolerance

With chronic cocaine or methamphetamine use, downregulation of the DAT (and D2 receptors) occurs. PET neuroimaging shows reduced DAT and D2 density in the striatum of long-term users — measurable even months after cessation.
