# Resilience in Children

The capacity to adapt to, recover from, and grow despite adversity, stress, or trauma. In children, resilience is not a fixed trait but emerges from the complex interaction of individual qualities, relationship quality, and environmental systems.

## Components

Resilience develops through:

### Individual qualities
- Temperament and personality
- Cognitive abilities
- Self-regulation and coping skills
- Sense of agency and mastery

### Relationship quality
- Secure, nurturing attachments
- Supportive family relationships
- Positive mentoring relationships
- Social belonging and peer support

### Environmental systems
- Access to resources (housing, nutrition, education)
- Stable, safe environment
- Community support networks
- Cultural and spiritual resources

## Important considerations

### Resilience vs responsibility
Resilience should not place burden on children to "cope" with situations that adults or institutions could remediate. Children have limited agency compared to adults.

### Context matters
In high-adversity contexts, those with more resources demonstrate greater resilience than those without. "More resources" includes social support, financial stability, education, and health.

### Systemic approach
Building a child's environment to reduce the need for resilience is more effective than focusing solely on resilience-building interventions.
