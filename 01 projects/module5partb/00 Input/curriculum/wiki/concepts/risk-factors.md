# Risk Factors

Characteristics, conditions, or experiences that increase the likelihood of negative mental health outcomes. Risk factors can operate directly (biological, genetic) or indirectly through complex pathways (environmental, relational, systemic).

## Categorization

Risk factors in child and adolescent mental health can be organized by:

### Level of influence
- **Individual** — temperament, genetic background, physical health, emotional regulation
- **Family** — parental mental health, attachment quality, resources, family dynamics
- **Community** — peer relationships, school environment, social support networks, neighbourhood conditions
- **Societal** — poverty, discrimination, policy, access to services

### Timing
- **Prenatal** — maternal health, genetic risks, substance exposure
- **Perinatal** — birth complications, early health concerns
- **Early childhood** — attachment disruption, maltreatment, developmental delays
- **School age** — peer rejection, academic failure, trauma exposure
- **Adolescence** — substance use, risk-taking behaviour, identity conflicts
- **Lifelong** — chronic illness, genetic predisposition, ongoing adversity

### Nature of effect
- **Distal factors** — non-specific factors that increase likelihood of subsequent risks (e.g., poverty increases risk of multiple adverse conditions)
- **Proximal factors** — directly affect the individual (e.g., abuse, loss)
