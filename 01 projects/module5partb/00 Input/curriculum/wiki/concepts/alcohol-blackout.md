# Alcohol Blackout

A temporary amnesia episode caused by alcohol intoxication in which the person remains conscious and capable of activity, but memory of events is not transferred from short-term to long-term storage. Blackouts differ from "passing out" (loss of consciousness).

## Mechanism

Memory transfer from short-term to long-term storage requires:
1. Neuronal communication using energy (ATP from mitochondria)
2. Intact **NMDA glutamate receptor** function
3. Minimal neuroinflammation and oxidative stress

Alcohol impairs all three:
- **NMDA receptor impairment** — alcohol inhibits glutamate activity at NMDA receptors, blocking the signalling cascade required for memory consolidation (long-term potentiation)
- **Mitochondrial disruption** — reduces energy available for memory transfer
- **Neuroinflammation and oxidative stress** — disrupt hippocampal function

## Two types of blackouts

### Fragmentary blackout (greyout)
- Memory of events is **fragmented** — islands of recall separated by gaps
- The person retains some memories but cannot fill in the blanks
- More common; occurs at lower blood alcohol concentrations

### En bloc blackout
- **Complete amnesia** for an entire period — no memories form at all
- The event "did not happen" from the person's subjective perspective
- More severe; requires higher blood alcohol concentrations
- Memories cannot be recovered; they were never formed

## Distinction from passing out

Blackouts occur while the person is **awake and potentially active** (walking, talking, driving). This makes them especially dangerous — the person may engage in risky behaviour with no memory afterwards.

## Overdose risk

Alcohol overdose depresses the brainstem, risking:
- Breathing difficulties
- Slow heart rate
- Vomiting (with aspiration risk due to suppressed gag reflex)
- Hypothermia
- Death

Risk increases when alcohol is combined with sedatives (benzodiazepines, opioids).
