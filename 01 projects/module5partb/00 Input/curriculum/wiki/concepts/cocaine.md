# Cocaine

A potent stimulant drug derived from the leaves of the South American coca plant. Available in two primary forms:
- **Powdered cocaine:** white crystalline powder; snorted, rubbed on gums, or dissolved and injected
- **Crack cocaine:** processed into a rock crystal; smoked; produces a very rapid, intense but shorter high

## Mechanism of action

Cocaine primarily acts on the brain's **limbic system**, producing psychoactive and addictive effects.

**Primary mechanism:** Cocaine blocks the **dopamine transporter (DAT)**, preventing reuptake of dopamine from the synapse. This causes a massive build-up of dopamine, particularly in the **nucleus accumbens**, producing euphoria.

Additionally cocaine:
- Affects other monoamine transporters (serotonin, noradrenaline reuptake)
- Stimulates **cortisol release** and activates the **HPA axis**
- Causes high stress and cortisol → learning/memory deficits, increased relapse risk (Fox et al.)

## Brain region effects

| Brain region | Cocaine effect |
|---|---|
| Nucleus accumbens | Dopamine build-up → euphoria; receptor desensitisation → anhedonia |
| Hippocampus + extended amygdala | Cue-memory formation → compulsive drug-seeking |
| Frontal cortex | Impaired → reduced impulse control; disregards negative consequences |

## Structural and genetic changes

Cocaine causes:
- **Dendrite growth** in the nucleus accumbens → hippocampus, amygdala, and frontal cortex gain more influence over NAc
- **Altered gene expression** in the NAc: glutamate and natural opioid-like compounds; accumulates with each use
- These changes persist and worsen with continued use

## Neuroimaging findings

PET studies (Volkow et al.) show:
- Reduced D2 receptor density in the striatum (persists 4 months post-cessation)
- Cue-induced dopamine release in dorsal striatum correlates with craving
- Reduced frontal glucose metabolism (persistent, correlates with duration/amount of use)

Structural MRI (Schinz et al.):
- Accelerated cortical thinning (BrainAGE gap) — premature brain aging

## Mental health effects

Long-term cocaine use causes mood/emotional disturbances: hallucinations, restlessness, paranoia, psychosis.
