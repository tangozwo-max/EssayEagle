# Internet Addiction

A pattern of compulsive internet use that produces psychological and behavioral consequences resembling those of substance-related addictions. Internet addiction is increasingly prevalent but is not currently classified in DSM-5 as a standalone disorder (unlike gaming disorder in ICD-11). It is considered an emerging area of behavioral addiction research.

## Prevalence (European data)
- Pathological internet use: 4.4–13.5%
- Problematic internet use: 14.3–54.9%

## Characteristics
Adolescents with internet addiction feel a constant need to be online (often on social networking sites). In extreme cases, this can produce:
- Sleep disturbance
- Angry or agitated reactions when forced to disconnect
- Losing track of time while online

## Psychological risk factors
- Introverted personality (increased online activity vs. emotionally stable users)
- Poor emotion regulation ([[emotion-regulation]])
- Loneliness, stress, depression, and anxiety
- Internet may function as an escape from day-to-day concerns

## Prevention note (Pinna et al.)
One of the most effective ways to prevent internet addiction is to treat underlying risk factors such as loneliness, stress, depression, and anxiety early — before internet use becomes a problematic coping mechanism.
