# Compulsive Behavior

A behavior or mental act performed to reduce anxiety or distress, rather than for pleasure. The APA defines compulsion as "a type of behavior or a mental act engaged in to reduce anxiety or distress... [it] does not always provide pleasure — instead, it acts to *neutralize* difficult thoughts, feelings, or distress."

## Compulsion vs. addiction
Compulsion and addiction are not the same thing, though compulsive behavior can play a key role in the development and maintenance of addiction cycles:
- Compulsion is *driven by* anxiety reduction and neutralization of distress
- Addiction is driven by cravings, reward, and loss of control
- As a compulsive behavior becomes more ingrained, the brain can remap its reward system to recognize that behavior as positive — moving it toward addiction

## Risk factors for compulsive behaviors
- Genetics (e.g., family history of OCD)
- More reserved temperament
- Tendency toward low mood
- History of childhood trauma
- Chronic stress (from life events or a mental health condition)

## Psychological mechanism
Some psychologists propose that compulsive behaviors develop from an unconscious perceived lack of control, producing anxiety and hopelessness. The compulsive behavior becomes a tool for regaining a sense of agency.

## Common forms
Gaming, gambling, hoarding, shopping, exercise, sexual behavior, internet use — all can take on compulsive qualities.
