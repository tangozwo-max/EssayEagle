# Nalmefene

An opioid receptor antagonist used to reduce alcohol consumption in adults with alcohol use disorder (AUD) who have high drinking risk. Unlike [[disulfiram]], which creates aversion, nalmefene reduces the reward of drinking by blocking opioid pathways.

## Mechanism of action
- **Pan-opioid receptor antagonist** — blocks mu, kappa, and delta opioid receptors
- This prevents endogenous opioids (endorphins) released during alcohol consumption from stimulating the reward pathway
- Consequently, **dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens is inhibited**, reducing the pleasurable and reinforcing effects of alcohol
- Reduced reward → reduced craving and alcohol consumption

## Comparison with naltrexone
Both nalmefene and [[naltrexone]] are opioid antagonists used for AUD. Key difference:
- Naltrexone primarily targets mu receptors
- Nalmefene blocks mu, kappa, and delta receptors — broader receptor coverage
- Nalmefene is taken as-needed (before drinking situations) vs naltrexone typically as daily dose

## Clinical role
Used when the treatment goal is **reduction in drinking** rather than full abstinence; suited for people who are not yet ready for complete abstinence.
