# Motivational Interviewing (MI)

Person-centred, collaborative communication style to elicit and strengthen motivation for change. Core skills summarised by **OARS**: Open questions, Affirmations, Reflections, Summarising.

Widely used in substance use, health behaviour change, medication adherence, mental-health engagement.

## Three core principles in addiction (DiClemente et al.)
1. **Collaboration** — the therapist and service user develop a trusting relationship; MI is done *with* the person, not *to* them
2. **Evocation** — motivation is drawn from the person's own ideas and values; MI assumes motivation comes from within, not from external pressure
3. **Autonomy** — the power to change rests with the service user; they take personal responsibility for their behaviour change

## Clinical uses in addiction (M5 W3)
- **Initial engagement** — can be used in [[brief-interventions]] and by non-specialist staff (e.g., social workers, GPs, nurses)
- **Addressing ambivalence** — particularly effective for people in the contemplation stage ([[transtheoretical-model]])
- **Within CBT** — a more detailed MI approach can be embedded in broader CBT to address shame, stigma, and ambivalence (as in Tim's gambling disorder case)
- **Relapse prevention** — MI helps sustain motivation during the maintenance stage
