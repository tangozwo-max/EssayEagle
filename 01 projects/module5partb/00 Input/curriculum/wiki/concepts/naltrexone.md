# Naltrexone

An FDA-approved medication used to treat both opioid use disorder (OUD) and alcohol use disorder (AUD). Naltrexone works by binding to and blocking opioid receptors, thereby preventing the euphoric and rewarding effects of alcohol and opioids. Because these reinforcing effects are blocked, cravings are reduced.

## Mechanism
- Binds and blocks opioid receptors
- Prevents euphoric feelings produced when drinking alcohol or taking opioids
- No misuse potential (does not activate opioid receptors; not addictive)

## Genotype and treatment response
Research on the **OPRM1 gene** shows that a specific nucleotide variant can predict whether a person will respond to naltrexone treatment for alcohol use disorder. This is a significant finding for personalized medicine:
- People with the risk variant may respond strongly to naltrexone
- People without the variant may gain little benefit from medication and would be better served by psychosocial interventions

## Significance
Naltrexone represents the intersection of genetics and pharmacological treatment for addiction — an example of how genetic research can guide individualized care planning.

## Mechanism in alcohol use disorder (M5 W3)
In AUD specifically, naltrexone:
- Blocks mu opioid receptors → prevents endogenous opioids (endorphins released during drinking) from binding
- This stops the dopamine surge in the [[reward-system]] (nucleus accumbens) that makes alcohol rewarding
- Result: reduced euphoria → reduced cravings and overall alcohol consumption
