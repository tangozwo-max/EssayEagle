# Tolerance and Withdrawal

The two pharmacological indicators of physical dependence on a substance, and criteria 10 and 11 in the DSM-5 diagnostic framework for [[substance-use-disorder]].

## Tolerance
An individual requires increasingly higher doses of a substance to achieve the desired effect, OR the usual dose produces a progressively diminished effect. Tolerance can develop at different rates for different symptoms within the same substance.

## Withdrawal
A collection of signs and symptoms that occurs when blood and tissue concentrations of a substance decrease following prolonged use. The person is typically motivated to seek more of the substance to relieve withdrawal symptoms.

Notable: **No documented withdrawal symptoms** for hallucinogens, PCP (phencyclidine/"angel dust"), or inhalants.

## Clinical caveat — medically supervised use
DSM-5 explicitly states that tolerance and withdrawal occurring in the context of **appropriate medical treatment** (e.g., opioids prescribed for pain, benzodiazepines for anxiety) do **NOT** count as criteria for SUD. Physical dependence on a prescribed medication is a normal physiological response and does not imply addiction.

## In ICD-11
Tolerance and withdrawal are two of the six criteria for [[dependence-syndrome]]; at least three criteria total must be met for diagnosis.

## In the Griffiths/Brown model
Tolerance and withdrawal also appear in the [[griffiths-brown-criteria]] for behavioral addictions, adapted from pharmacological definitions to behavioral contexts.

## Neurobiology — cAMP pathway and allostasis (M5 W2)

The **cyclic AMP (cAMP) pathway** is central to how tolerance develops. cAMP is a secondary messenger synthesised from ATP by adenylyl cyclase, an enzyme linked to dopamine receptors. D1 receptors activate adenylyl cyclase; D2 receptors inhibit it. Opioids suppress cAMP production, thereby inhibiting neuronal signalling. With chronic use, neurons adapt via **allostasis** — boosting cAMP production to counteract the expected suppression. This means ever-higher doses are needed to produce the same effect (tolerance), and if the substance is stopped abruptly, cAMP levels remain elevated and neurons become overactive, causing withdrawal.

Allostasis also produces lasting changes in gene expression, leading to receptor phosphorylation (reduced sensitivity) and receptor internalisation (fewer available receptors).

### Withdrawal — three neuroanatomical sites
Overactive neurons during opioid withdrawal mainly affect:
1. **Locus coeruleus (brainstem)** — jitteriness, insomnia, panic, anxiety, sweating, tachycardia
2. **GABAergic interneurons (brainstem)** — generalised discomfort
3. **Gut** — diarrhoea (overactive enteric neurons prevent water reabsorption)

The withdrawal/negative-affect stage follows the binge/intoxication stage in the [[addiction-cycle]], driven by reduced dopamine in the basal ganglia reward circuitry and activation of the extended amygdala stress system.
