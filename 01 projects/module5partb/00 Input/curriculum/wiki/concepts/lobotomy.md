# Lobotomy — Cautionary Case in Evidence-Based Practice

Widely practiced procedure through the 1930s–1950s despite **limited scientific evidence**. Initial evidence came from Portuguese neurologist **Egas Moniz**, who hypothesised that disrupting certain brain pathways would relieve mental illness.

- **Technique (transorbital)**: needle through thin bone behind the eye → frontal lobe → cut PFC connections.
- **~50,000–60,000 people** underwent the procedure globally.
- Common outcomes: flat personality, memory loss, lifelong difficulty relating to others.
- Fell out of favour by the 1950s–60s with the rise of RCTs and new therapies.

## Lesson
Illustrates the risks of practice unsupported by [[rct]]-level evidence. Foundational cautionary tale for [[evidence-based-practice]].
