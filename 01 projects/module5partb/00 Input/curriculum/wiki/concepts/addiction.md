# Addiction

A state in which a person develops a persistent, abnormally high motivation to engage in a behavior — whether substance use or a non-substance activity — resulting in compulsive use or engagement that continues despite significant harmful consequences. The term captures the loss of control that distinguishes addiction from simple dependence.

## Definitions in use

**ASAM (American Society of Addictive Medicine):** A treatable, chronic medical disease involving complex interactions among brain circuits, genetics, the environment, and an individual's life experiences. People with addiction use substances or engage in behaviors that become compulsive and often continue despite harmful consequences.

**West et al. (2024) ontology:** A mental *disposition* that results in repeated episodes of abnormally high motivation to engage in a behavior, acquired from engaging in that behavior, where the behavior results in risk or occurrence of serious net harm.

## DSM-5 and terminology
DSM-5 (2013) does not use the term *addiction*. What is colloquially called addiction would be classified as a **severe substance use disorder** ([[substance-use-disorder]]). The APA moved away from the term to reduce stigma and create a severity continuum (mild, moderate, severe).

## Addiction vs. dependency
The key distinction:
- **Dependency** ([[dependency]]) = physical tolerance and withdrawal from prolonged use; a normal physiological response, does not require compulsive behavior
- **Addiction** = mental and physical dependence *combined* with **uncontrollable behavior** in obtaining and using a substance

## Historical framing
Until roughly a century ago, addiction was viewed as a moral failing requiring punishment. Neurobiological research — including [[nora-volkow]]'s work at NIDA — reframed addiction as a medical condition shaped by genetics, environment, and brain chemistry. Some societal stigma around this framing persists.
