# Theory of Mind

Theory of mind (ToM) is the capacity to understand that other people have thoughts, beliefs, desires, and perspectives that may differ from one's own. This metacognitive ability represents a critical transition in social and cognitive development, enabling children to move beyond egocentric thinking and develop sophisticated understanding of how minds work. Whilst early forms of perspective-taking emerge in preschool years, robust theory of mind typically develops between ages 10 and 11.

## Developmental Progression

Early forms of perspective-taking begin to emerge around age 3-4 years when children start to recognise that others may have different wants or preferences. Around age 4-5 years, children develop the ability to attribute false beliefs to others—the key milestone in false belief understanding. By ages 6-7 years, children can consider others' perspectives, though their understanding remains relatively simple. By ages 10-11 years, children develop more sophisticated theory of mind, understanding that people's beliefs are based on the information available to them, that beliefs may be false, and that people's behaviour is guided by their (sometimes incorrect) beliefs.

## False Belief Understanding

The classic test of theory of mind is the false belief test. A person watches a scene where an object is placed in location A, the person leaves, the object is moved to location B, then the person returns. The question is: where will the person look for the object? Children with robust theory of mind understand that the person will look in location A because that is where they believe the object to be, even though the child knows the object is in location B. This ability represents a major cognitive achievement.

## Social and Clinical Significance

Theory of mind is foundational for effective social interaction, as understanding others' mental states enables accurate prediction and interpretation of behaviour. Difficulties with theory of mind are associated with autism spectrum disorder, where individuals may struggle to understand social conventions based on unstated mental states. Impaired theory of mind can also accompany intellectual disabilities, social anxiety, and other neurodevelopmental conditions.
