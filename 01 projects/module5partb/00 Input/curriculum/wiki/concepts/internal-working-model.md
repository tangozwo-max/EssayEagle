# Internal Working Model

The internal working model is a mental representation framework constructed through early attachment experiences that organises and guides expectations about the self, others, and relationships throughout life. These cognitive templates, formed implicitly during infancy and toddlerhood, exert profound influence on thoughts, emotions, and behaviours in all future relational contexts. The IWM acts as a predictive model, enabling individuals to interpret others' behaviour and anticipate relational outcomes.

## Formation and Structure

According to Bowlby, early experiences with caregivers serve as the basis for internal working models. Through repeated interactions, infants develop mental scripts about whether caregivers are reliable and responsive, whether the self is worthy of care, and whether the world is safe or threatening. These representations become largely automatised and unconscious, operating below explicit awareness.

## Relational Templates

The internal working model functions as a prototype for all subsequent relationships. A child whose primary caregiver consistently responded to distress develops expectations of adult responsiveness and a sense of personal worthiness. Conversely, a child whose needs were unpredictably met—or met inconsistently through maltreatment—develops scripts in which adults are unpredictable or unsafe.

## Malleability and Change

Research demonstrates that internal working models, whilst established early, are not immutable. Children and adults who have experienced maltreatment can develop new scripts when provided access to safer, more predictable, and responsive adults. This finding has significant implications for therapeutic intervention and service design: it suggests that providing consistently safe and attuned relationships can gradually reshape deeply held expectations about human connection.

## Clinical Applications

Understanding a client's internal working model enables mental health practitioners to anticipate relational patterns in the therapeutic relationship and broader engagement with services. Service users whose working models encode unpredictability may struggle with inconsistent staffing, frequent provider changes, or gaps in communication, replicating earlier patterns of abandonment or unreliability.
