# Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs)

Defined by the **CDC** as *"potentially traumatic events that occur in childhood (0–17 years)."* One of the strongest documented distal determinants of adult mental and physical health.

## Categories

### Direct events
- Experience of violence, abuse, or neglect
- Witnessing violence in home or community
- Family member attempting or dying by suicide

### Environmental factors undermining safety, stability, bonding
- Substance use problems in the home
- Mental health disorders in the home
- Parental separation, incarceration of a household member

## Clinical implications
Even without adult high-risk behaviours (smoking, alcohol), ACE exposure significantly elevates risk of heart disease, cancer, and chronic depression. Experts can now **screen** for ACEs and integrate findings into routine care.

## In M5 context — Substance Use Risk
M5 W1 identifies ACEs as a major social/environmental risk factor for substance use disorders:
- Parental or family member substance use is a direct ACE and elevates children's future drug use risk
- The "vicious circle": ACEs → substance misuse → poverty/new adversity → more ACEs
- Substance misuse typically begins in adolescence; adolescents with SUD have high rates of co-occurring ACEs

## In M5 W2 — Neurobiological pathway (src-m5w2-p18-ace-addiction-risk)

Three signalling systems link ACEs to addiction vulnerability (Kim et al.; Strathearn et al.):

1. **Oxytocin** — early adversity dysregulates the oxytocin system → social impairment, reward-seeking → susceptibility to substance use
2. **Dopamine** — ACEs alter dopamine transporter binding, increase baseline dopamine, exaggerate dopamine stress responses → sensation-seeking, impulsive risk-taking, pursuit of stimulants
3. **Glucocorticoids (cortisol)** — early caregiving deficits → prolonged cortisol elevation → HPA axis dysfunction → stress exposure triggers substance onset, diminishes abstinence motivation, heightens relapse risk

**Structural brain changes from ACE** (Anda et al.):
- Hippocampal/amygdala volume reduction → memory deficits
- Locus coeruleus hyperactivity + norepinephrine excess → unpleasant arousal states (mitigated by alcohol/opioids)
- Amygdala/oxytocin defects → impaired pair bonding
- Glucocorticoid pathway disruption → increased abdominal fat

**Intergenerational cycle:** Parental addiction impairs caregiving → creates new ACEs for children → perpetuates the pattern.
