# Developmental Coordination Disorder

Developmental Coordination Disorder (DCD), formerly known as dyspraxia, is a neurodevelopmental condition characterised by persistent and significant difficulties in sequencing and executing coordinated movements. These motor planning and execution difficulties often co-occur with challenges in attention, planning, and executive function, reflecting underlying difficulties in how the brain integrates sensory information and coordinates motor output. DCD substantially affects self-esteem, academic achievement, and social participation.

## Neurobiological Basis

DCD is associated with dysfunction in the parietal lobe, the brain region responsible for integrating sensory information (proprioception, touch, vestibular input) and coordinating motor responses. The parietal cortex translates sensory information into appropriate motor commands. Dysfunction in this area results in poor body awareness, difficulty planning movement sequences, and reduced motor automaticity.

## Core Features

Individuals with DCD typically show:
- Difficulty sequencing complex movements
- Reduced speed and accuracy of motor execution
- Poor planning of movement (e.g., difficulty organising multi-step motor tasks)
- Impaired automatic motor skills (handwriting, sports, self-care)
- Difficulties with attention and sustained focus during motor tasks
- Reduced motor automaticity (movements do not become smooth and automatic with practice)

## Associated Sensory and Executive Function Difficulties

DCD frequently co-occurs with sensory modulation difficulties. Individuals may be hypersensitive to tactile input (avoiding certain clothing textures), struggle with proprioceptive awareness (clumsiness, running into objects), or have vestibular difficulties (poor balance, fear of movement). Executive function difficulties are also common, including planning, attention, and impulse control challenges.

## Developmental and Social Impacts

During childhood, DCD can impair academic performance (handwriting, sports participation), self-care abilities (dressing, eating), and social integration (exclusion from physical games, reduced peer acceptance). Repeated experiences of failure and exclusion can substantially impact self-esteem and emotional wellbeing. Early identification and targeted motor skill training, alongside emotional support, are critical for optimal outcomes.
