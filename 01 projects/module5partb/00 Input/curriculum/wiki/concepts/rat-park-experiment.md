# Rat Park Experiment

A landmark addiction research experiment conducted in the late 1970s by Canadian psychologist Bruce K. Alexander, designed to challenge the prevailing view that the pharmacological properties of drugs — rather than environmental factors — are the primary cause of addiction.

## Background
Previous addiction studies used rats in small, isolated cages with access to plain water and drug-laced water. These rats almost always consumed the drugged water until overdose or death, interpreted as evidence of drugs' overwhelming addictive power.

## The experiment
Alexander hypothesized that the barren, isolated environment — not just the drugs — was driving consumption. He created "Rat Park": a spacious, enriched environment with other rats to socialize with, food, toys, and room to exercise.

**Key findings:**
- Rats in Rat Park consumed far less drug-laced water than isolated rats
- Some Rat Park rats avoided the drugged water entirely
- When Rat Park rats were moved to isolated cages, drug consumption increased
- Approximately 0% of rats in enriched, connected environments overdosed; close to 100% of isolated rats did

## Conclusions
Social and environmental factors — poverty, loneliness, lack of meaningful activity — are significant drivers of drug misuse. Addiction is not solely determined by a substance's pharmacological "chemical hooks."

## Limitations and critique
- Generalizability to humans is questioned (species differences, complexity of human social environments)
- Methodological criticisms have been raised about experimental controls
- The broad conclusions should be understood as indicative, not definitive

## Significance for M5
The experiment underpins the risk factor framework in M5 W1, supporting a biopsychosocial view of addiction where social/environmental factors can amplify or buffer biological and psychological vulnerability.
