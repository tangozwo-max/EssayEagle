# Acamprosate

A pharmacological treatment for alcohol use disorder (AUD) aimed at maintaining abstinence. It works by restoring the neurotransmitter imbalance that chronic alcohol use creates, rather than creating aversion or blocking reward.

## Mechanism of action
Chronic heavy alcohol use disrupts the balance between excitatory (glutamate/NMDA) and inhibitory (GABA) neurotransmitter systems. When alcohol is removed, the excitatory system becomes hyperactive, driving cravings and withdrawal distress. Acamprosate counteracts this by:

1. **Modulating glutamate at NMDA receptors** — reduces excessive NMDA receptor activity (excitatory system downregulated)
2. **Enhancing GABA activity** — supports the inhibitory system
3. **Stabilising neurotransmitter balance** — this reduces cravings and alleviates the discomfort of early abstinence

## Clinical role
- Indicated for **abstinence maintenance** after detoxification (not for use during active drinking)
- Often the first-choice pharmacological agent after medically assisted withdrawal for AUD (e.g., Grant's care plan)
- Should be combined with psychosocial support

## Key features
- No misuse potential (not a controlled substance)
- Well-tolerated; common side effects include diarrhoea
- Does not reduce the effects of alcohol if the person drinks — unlike [[disulfiram]]
