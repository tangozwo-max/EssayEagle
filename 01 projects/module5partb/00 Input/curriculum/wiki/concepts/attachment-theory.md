# Attachment Theory

Attachment theory, developed by John Bowlby, proposes that the bond between an infant and their primary caregiver profoundly shapes the development of internal working models—mental templates that guide all future relationships. These early experiences are not merely formative but constitute the foundation for lifelong patterns of intimacy, trust, and emotional regulation. Secure attachment relationships provide infants with a sense of safety and predictability essential for healthy neurodevelopment and social functioning.

## Core Principles

Bowlby defined attachment as "lasting psychological connectedness between human beings," emphasizing that the capacity to form strong, specific bonds is intrinsic to human nature and necessary for survival. The theory integrates insights from ethology and object relations theory, proposing that infants' innate behaviours (crying, smiling, seeking proximity) evolved to attract and maintain caregiver attention, thereby ensuring protection and survival.

## Four Stages of Attachment Development

Bowlby identified four progressive stages across the first two years of life:

### Pre-attachment (Birth to 6 weeks)
Infants display innate attachment behaviours (crying, smiling) that elicit caregiver attention but show no preference for a specific caregiver.

### Attachment-in-the-making (6 weeks to 7 months)
A sense of trust in familiar caregivers begins to develop; infants show preference for primary and secondary caregivers whilst still accepting care from strangers.

### Clear-cut Attachment (7 months to 2 years)
Specific attachment to primary caregivers emerges; children seek comfort and security from them and develop separation anxiety.

### Reciprocal Relationships (2 years and older)
Language acquisition enables children to understand that caregivers return after separation, facilitating more mutual, reciprocal relationships.

## Secure Attachment

Caregivers nurture secure attachment through responsive, predictable, and consistent care. Securely attached infants exhibit four core characteristics:

- **Safe haven**: seeking comfort and protection from the caregiver during distress
- **Proximity maintenance**: desire to remain physically close to the caregiver
- **Secure base**: confidence to explore the environment, knowing they can return for reassurance
- **Separation anxiety**: age-appropriate distress upon separation, indicating the attachment bond's strength

## Clinical Significance

The quality of attachment relationships directly influences emotional development and self-regulatory capacity. Secure attachment is associated with positive therapeutic alliance in mental health services and is considered the strongest predictor of treatment outcomes.
