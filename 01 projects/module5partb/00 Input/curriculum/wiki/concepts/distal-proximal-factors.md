# Distal and Proximal Factors in Risk

A distinction within risk factor frameworks describing how factors operate in a chain of probabilities, shaped by frequency, context, and timing.

## Distal factors

Non-specific factors that increase the likelihood of subsequent risks. They operate indirectly by creating conditions that make subsequent adverse events more probable.

**Examples:**
- Poverty (increases likelihood of multiple risks: malnutrition, housing instability, parental stress)
- Neighbourhood deprivation (increases exposure to violence, substance use opportunities, limited services)
- Family history of mental health conditions (genetic vulnerability)

## Proximal factors

Factors that directly affect the individual. They operate immediately and specifically to affect outcomes.

**Examples:**
- Child abuse or neglect (direct harm)
- Parental death or major loss (direct emotional impact)
- Diagnosis of chronic illness (direct health effect)
- Bullying by peers (direct social harm)

## Chain of probabilities

Distal and proximal factors interact in chains where:
- Distal factors increase probability of exposure to proximal factors
- Frequency of occurrence influences cumulative risk
- Context and timing determine magnitude of effect
- Protective factors may interrupt the chain
