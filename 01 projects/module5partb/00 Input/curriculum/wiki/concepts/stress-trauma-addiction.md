# Stress and Trauma as Addiction Risk Factors

A history of stress and trauma — especially from adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) — is among the most significant risk factors for developing a substance use disorder. Stress operates both as a direct driver of substance use initiation and as a mechanism that enhances the pleasurable effects of substances.

## Mechanisms
- Dysfunctional family environments, lack of support, and family members with addiction elevate risk
- Stressful life events (work stress, grief, divorce, conflict, displacement) reduce coping capacity
- Stress relief enhances the perceived pleasure of substance use, compounding addictive potential
- The same psychological factors that determine resilience vs. vulnerability to trauma also determine SUD risk

## ACEs and lifetime substance use risk
[[Adverse-childhood-experiences]] are a major distal risk factor:
- Parental substance use in the home is itself an ACE and directly elevates children's later drug use risk
- Academic and social difficulties associated with ACEs further compound risk
- The "vicious circle": ACEs drive substance misuse, which in turn creates new adverse experiences (poverty, job loss, relationship breakdown)

## Comorbid conditions that amplify risk
- Depression — individuals use substances to self-medicate or escape depressive symptoms
- Anxiety disorders — high anxiety drives compulsive behavior as temporary relief
- Personality disorders (BPD, ASPD) — associated with higher rates of addiction
