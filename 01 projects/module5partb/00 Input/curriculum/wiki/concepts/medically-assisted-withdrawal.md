# Medically Assisted Withdrawal (MAW)

A structured, medically supervised process that supports a person through the physiological and psychological withdrawal symptoms that occur when they reduce or cease using a substance. Also known as detoxification or detox.

## Severity differs by substance
- **Alcohol withdrawal** — potentially life-threatening; risks include tonic-clonic seizures and delirium tremens; requires close medical supervision
- **Opioid withdrawal** — highly distressing (flu-like symptoms, pain, dysphoria) but not directly fatal; still requires medical support
- **Benzodiazepine withdrawal** — can cause seizures and severe anxiety; requires gradual tapering

## Standard components
1. Thorough medical assessment (including substance use history, comorbidities)
2. Gradual dose reduction of the substance (or substitution medication)
3. Medication to manage withdrawal symptoms (e.g., [[methadone]] or [[buprenorphine]] for opioids; benzodiazepines for alcohol; [[lofexidine]] for sympathetic symptoms)
4. Medical supervision and monitoring for dangerous symptoms

## Setting
| Setting | Indication |
|---|---|
| **Inpatient** | Severe SUD, comorbid health problems, high-risk withdrawal; ICU if very high substance levels |
| **Outpatient/community** | Lower-risk; medication dispensed via primary care or pharmacy with regular check-ins |

## Specialist involvement
Evidence supports better outcomes when clinicians with specialist addictions training supervise MAW, though non-specialists following guidance can also deliver it safely.

## After MAW
Immediate psychosocial support is essential after completing withdrawal to address substance-seeking behaviours and cravings; without this, relapse rates are high.
