# Benzodiazepines

A class of psychoactive substances classified as sedatives, hypnotics, and anxiolytics. Common examples include diazepam (Valium), alprazolam (Xanax), and clonazepam (Klonopin). Benzodiazepines are most effective when prescribed for short periods; long-term use is associated with dependence and, in a minority of cases, substance use disorder.

## Patterns of use — US data (2019)
- 12.5% of US adults used benzodiazepines
- 2.1% misused them; 0.2% had a benzodiazepine use disorder
- 35% of people taking benzodiazepines for more than 4 weeks became physically dependent
- 16% of opioid overdose deaths also involved benzodiazepines
- 9,711 benzodiazepine-related overdose deaths

## Older adult vulnerability
Benzodiazepines are frequently prescribed to older adults for chronic anxiety and insomnia, making this group particularly vulnerable to dependence:
- 20–50% of women over 60 are prescribed benzodiazepines
- 9–54% of older adults used benzodiazepines in the previous year
- Women are twice as likely as men to use benzodiazepines
- 40% of hospital emergency department visits by older adults are for benzodiazepine emergencies (of 290-visit sample)

## Dependency note
Physical dependence on benzodiazepines is common with long-term use; only 1.5% of benzodiazepine users develop a SUD. This illustrates the important distinction between [[dependency]] and [[addiction]].

## Mechanism of action (M5 W2)

Benzodiazepines increase **GABA-A channel opening** in the CNS, enhancing hyperpolarisation — the inhibitory slowing of brain activity. GABA works by binding to post-synaptic receptors and modulating **chloride ion channels** to hyperpolarise the neuron, inhibiting action potentials.

## BZD + opioid combination (high-risk)

High-risk opioid users often misuse BZD for two reasons:
1. **Self-medication:** To reduce psychiatric symptoms, negative emotional states, or opioid withdrawal discomfort
2. **Enhanced effect:** BZD prolongs and intensifies opioid effects (especially when injected)

The combination is particularly dangerous: BZD + opioids together depress brainstem functions → risk of respiratory failure and drug-related death. Rapid-onset, highly lipophilic BZD (diazepam, alprazolam) are most implicated in overdose deaths.

## Misuse risk profile (Schmitz data, USA)

- Highest risk group: non-Hispanic white young adults aged 18–35
- Strong association with comorbid psychiatric disorders and history of SUD
- Antisocial personality disorder and alcohol misuse history = particular risk

## Withdrawal

Benzodiazepine tolerance and dependence develop rapidly. Abrupt withdrawal can cause:
- Anxiety, confusion, panic attacks, acute psychosis
- Potentially **fatal convulsions** that are difficult to control
