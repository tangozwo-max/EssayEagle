# Raclopride ([C11]Raclopride)

A competitive dopamine D2 receptor antagonist used as a **radioligand** in PET (positron emission tomography) scanning to measure dopamine release and receptor availability in the living brain.

## How it works

- Raclopride competes with endogenous dopamine for binding at D2 receptor sites
- **High raclopride signal** = low endogenous dopamine (more receptor sites available)
- **Low raclopride signal** = high endogenous dopamine (dopamine is outcompeting the radioligand)

This inverse relationship makes raclopride an indirect measure of dopamine release in specific brain regions.

## Use in addiction research

Volkow et al. used [C11]Raclopride PET to study cue-induced dopamine release in cocaine-addicted individuals:
- Subjects watching cocaine cue videos showed significantly **lower raclopride binding** in the dorsal striatum
- This reduction correlated directly with self-reported **craving severity**
- Subjects with highest addiction/withdrawal severity scores showed the greatest dopamine signal changes

Separately, PET with raclopride-based methods showed that chronic cocaine users have **reduced D2 receptor density** in the striatum — the neurobiological signature of tolerance.

## Significance

Raclopride PET is a key tool for:
- Measuring dopamine dysregulation in addiction
- Assessing receptor loss (D2 downregulation) as a marker of tolerance
- Evaluating treatment effects on dopamine systems
