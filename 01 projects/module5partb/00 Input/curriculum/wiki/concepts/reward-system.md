# Brain Reward System

The brain's reward system is a set of interconnected neural circuits that identify and reinforce behaviours that promote survival and wellbeing. Hijacking of this system by addictive substances is the central mechanism of addiction.

## Core components

- **Ventral tegmental area (VTA):** Source of dopamine in the reward circuit
- **Nucleus accumbens:** Primary site of dopamine release; encodes reward salience
- **Basal ganglia:** Broader structure including nucleus accumbens; habit formation and reward processing
- **Prefrontal cortex:** Evaluates reward value; executive control over impulse to seek rewards
- **Hippocampus:** Encodes memories of reward-associated cues
- **Extended amygdala:** Encodes negative emotional states that motivate escape-from-withdrawal behaviour

## Normal function

When a naturally rewarding behaviour occurs (eating, sex, social bonding):
1. The VTA releases dopamine into the nucleus accumbens
2. Dopamine signals "this was beneficial — remember it"
3. A habit is formed: the behaviour becomes easier to repeat automatically
4. Cues associated with the behaviour trigger anticipatory dopamine release

## How substances hijack the reward system

1. Substances produce dopamine surges **far exceeding** natural rewards
2. The brain forms strong habits around substance use at the expense of natural rewards
3. Natural rewards become insufficient to produce adequate dopamine (anhedonia)
4. External cues associated with substance use trigger powerful cravings
5. The prefrontal cortex's inhibitory control is weakened, reducing capacity to resist

## Why it matters for gambling and behavioural addictions

Gambling and other behavioural addictions activate the same reward circuits as substances, with similar effects on dopamine, habit formation, and impulse control — explaining why they share neurobiological features with SUD.
