# Gambling Disorder

The only [[behavioral-addiction]] recognized as a standalone disorder in DSM-5. Prior to 2013, gambling was classified as an impulse-control disorder; it was reclassified as a behavioral addiction in DSM-5 based on neuroimaging and pharmacological research showing that compulsive gambling produces the same changes to neurotransmitter systems (particularly dopamine) as substance addictions.

## Classification
- **DSM-5:** Listed under "Substance-Related and Addictive Disorders" (the only behavioral addiction in this section)
- **ICD-11:** Listed under "Disorders due to addictive behaviors" (alongside [[gaming-disorder]])

## Significance
Gambling disorder's reclassification provided the empirical model for understanding other potential behavioral addictions. Its recognition opened the scientific debate about whether other repetitive behaviors (internet use, sexual behavior, shopping, etc.) might also meet addiction criteria.

## Risk traits (M5 W2 — Pérez)

People at risk of gambling disorder demonstrate certain traits — even before the disorder develops, including in children:
- Increased **impulsivity**
- Preference for games of chance
- Increased incidence of co-occurring substance use disorder

## Neuroscience of gambling (M5 W2)

Gambling activates the same dopamine reward systems as substances. Multiple systems are affected:
- Memory and learning
- Motivation and drive
- Inhibitory control
- Reward and salience

**Brain effects of gambling:**
- Sleep deprivation
- Increased stress hormones
- Chronic activation of neurochemical systems → neurocognitive impairment, emotional pain, reduced flexibility

## DSM-5 diagnostic criteria

| Criterion | Description |
|---|---|
| Preoccupation | Constant thoughts about gambling |
| Lying | Covering up extent of habit |
| Tolerance | Needing bigger and longer gambling fixes |
| Withdrawal | Craving gambling when unable to; affects relationships/work |
| Focus on lost opportunities | Overestimates chances of winning; ignores logic |
| Chasing losses | Always believes losses can be recovered |
| Not knowing when to stop | Persists despite warnings |
| Inability to stop | Gambling seen as solution to problems |
| Gambling to escape feelings | Avoidance of unpleasant emotions |
