# Double Jeopardy: Neurodevelopmental Conditions and Adverse Childhood Experiences

The concept that children with both neurodevelopmental conditions (NDCs) and adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) face compounded risk for poor mental health and functional outcomes. Maltreated children are approximately 10 times more likely than non-maltreated peers to have three or more NDCs.

## Mechanism of compounded risk

The "double jeopardy" occurs through:

1. **ACEs independently increase mental health risk** — through trauma effects and toxic stress
2. **NDCs independently increase mental health risk** — through neurobiological differences
3. **Combined effects are cumulative** — exposures interact to amplify overall risk

## Key findings

- Maltreated children are nearly 10 times more likely to have multiple NDCs
- Maltreatment does not directly cause NDCs, but increases co-occurrence and detection complexity
- Adolescents with both maltreatment and NDC symptoms at age 9 were twice as likely to develop severe psychiatric symptoms (e.g., mania) by later adolescence

## Clinical implications

- NDCs may be overlooked if child is experiencing adversity
- ACEs can delay diagnosis of conditions like autism and ADHD (masking or trauma symptoms obscuring NDC signs)
- Clinicians should conduct full neurodevelopmental assessment in maltreated children
- Children with multiple NDCs are at higher risk of maltreatment (vulnerability)
