# Alcohol Use Disorder (AUD)

A substance use disorder in which problematic alcohol consumption leads to clinically significant impairment or distress. Alcohol is recognized as the most harmful substance both to individual users and to society, as demonstrated by Nutt et al.'s drug harm ranking (score: 72/100, highest of 20 substances assessed).

## Global burden
According to the WHO Global Status Report (2024):
- An estimated **400 million people** (7% of adults aged 15+) live with alcohol use disorders globally
- An estimated **209 million people** (3.7% of adults) live with alcohol dependence
- Significant regional variation exists

## Socioeconomic link
Lower education is a particularly strong predictor of alcohol-attributable mortality risk. Individuals with medium or low education have a 3–5x higher alcohol-attributable mortality risk compared to those with high education (Probst et al.).

## Benzodiazepine overlap
In older adults, alcohol use disorder and benzodiazepine dependence frequently co-occur, as both substances are often used to manage anxiety and sleep problems — the two most prevalent concerns in this age group.

## Treatment
- [[naltrexone]] — FDA-approved medication that blocks opioid receptors, reducing alcohol's rewarding effects; treatment response predicted by OPRM1 gene variant
- CBT and abstinence-focused counseling
- Abstinence is the appropriate goal for most people with alcohol dependence, especially those with significant psychiatric or physical comorbidity (e.g., liver disease)

## Mechanism of action (M5 W2 — NIAA)

Alcohol's primary CNS mechanism:
- Enhances **GABA** (inhibitory) → slows brain function → the "depressant" effect
- Disrupts **NMDA glutamate receptor** function → impairs memory consolidation → blackouts
- Long-term: physically changes brain appearance and function, visible on neuroimaging

## Risk factors for AUD (NIAA)

- **Consumption pattern:** Quantity, speed, and frequency; binge drinking and heavy use
- **Early age onset:** Starting before age 15 = significantly higher lifetime AUD risk (women more than men)
- **Genetics:** ~60% of AUD risk; family drinking patterns compound genetic risk
- **Mental health:** Depression, PTSD, ADHD, ACE all increase risk

## Addiction cycle — alcohol

Alcohol fits the standard [[addiction-cycle]] model:
- **Binge/intoxication:** Basal ganglia activation → euphoria, anxiety reduction, social rewards
- **Withdrawal/negative affect:** Sleep disturbance, physical pain, dysphoria, irritability
  - Caused by (1) reduced basal ganglia reward system activation and (2) extended amygdala stress activation
- **Preoccupation:** Craving to escape negative withdrawal states (negative reinforcement dominates)

Note: Short-term alcohol use reduces negative feelings; long-term it enhances negative emotional states between bouts, deepening the cycle.

## Effects on the brain

- **Adolescent brain:** Last to mature (prefrontal cortex development into 20s); alcohol during adolescence causes long-lasting structural and functional brain changes (heightened plasticity = heightened vulnerability)
- **Memory:** Alcohol impairs NMDA receptor function + disrupts mitochondrial energy → prevents memory transfer to long-term storage → [[alcohol-blackout|blackouts]] (fragmentary and en bloc)
- **Overdose:** Brainstem suppression → breathing failure, cardiac arrest; danger multiplied with BZD or opioids
- **Prenatal:** Any stage of pregnancy → [[fetal-alcohol-spectrum-disorder|FASD]]; behavioural, developmental, cognitive effects
- **Moderate use:** Still associated with hippocampal shrinkage, blood vessel damage, increased stroke/cancer risk, thiamine deficiency → [[wernicke-korsakoff|Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome]]
