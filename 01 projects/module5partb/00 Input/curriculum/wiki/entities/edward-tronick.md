# Edward Tronick

Developmental psychologist renowned for his landmark Still Face Experiment (1978), which elegantly demonstrated the critical importance of caregiver responsiveness for infant emotional regulation and neurodevelopment.

## The Still Face Experiment

Tronick's 1978 Still Face Experiment remains one of the most influential studies in developmental psychology. The paradigm consists of three phases: (1) normal caregiver-infant interaction wherein the caregiver engages responsively; (2) the still face phase wherein the caregiver becomes emotionally unresponsive, presenting a blank face; (3) re-engagement wherein the caregiver returns to responsive interaction.

The experiment revealed profound insights: infants became visibly distressed during the still face phase, engaged in increasingly desperate attempts to reengage the unresponsive caregiver, and showed marked changes in affect and behaviour. Upon re-engagement, securely attached infants quickly recovered and returned to engaged play.

## Implications for Development and Service Engagement

Tronick's work illuminated that infants cannot self-soothe; they require active, responsive caregiving for emotional regulation. The experiment's findings extend beyond infancy to understand service user engagement. People whose early experiences included caregiver unresponsiveness or inconsistency may respond to service gaps or staff changes with patterns mirroring those observed in the Still Face Experiment—withdrawal, escalation of distress signals, or reduced engagement.

## Understanding Co-Regulation

Tronick emphasised that emotion regulation is not an innate capacity present from birth but rather develops through repeated experiences of caregiver-child co-regulation. The caregiver's responses literally teach the infant how to manage emotions.
