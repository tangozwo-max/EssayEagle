# Jack Shonkoff

Professor of Child Health and Development at Harvard University's T.H. Chan School of Public Health. Leading scientist in early childhood development and the neuroscience of how early experiences shape lifelong health, learning, and behaviour.

## Core Message

Shonkoff's work synthesises neuroscience research into an accessible but scientifically grounded message: "The early years are important for brain development because the experiences that young children have and the relationships they have with important people in their lives literally shape the development of their brain and that early foundation affects all the learning and behaviour and physical and mental health that follows through a lifetime."

This statement captures a fundamental shift in understanding—from viewing early childhood as merely setting the stage for later development to recognising it as literally building the brain.

## Key Concepts

**Brain Architecture**: Shonkoff popularised the metaphor of brain development as house construction, emphasising that strong foundations in early years are essential for all subsequent development.

**Sensitive Periods**: He emphasises that whilst early childhood (0-5 years) and adolescence (12-25 years) are particularly sensitive periods wherein experience exerts maximal influence, brain plasticity persists across the lifespan.

**Stress and Development**: His work on toxic stress articulates how excessive, prolonged stress without protective relationships disrupts neural development, affecting memory, emotional regulation, and executive function.

**Serve-and-Return**: He champions the serve-and-return metaphor for understanding how reciprocal caregiver-child interactions build neural circuits.

## Policy and Translation

Shonkoff is renowned not only as a scientist but as an effective translator of neuroscience into policy and practice. His work has influenced education policy, child protection frameworks, and early childhood interventions across the developed world.
