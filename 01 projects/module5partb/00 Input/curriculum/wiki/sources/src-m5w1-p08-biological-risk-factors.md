# M5 W1 P08 — Biological Risk Factors for Substance Use

This page examines the genetic contributions to addiction risk. Family studies estimate that genetics account for 40–60% of addiction risk, with multiple genes involved. Scientists have identified 19 gene variants commonly inherited across addiction disorders, all involved in dopamine signaling regulation. Key gene-addiction relationships covered include: HK2/RASGRF2 (injection drug use risk), CHRNA5 (nicotine dependence), AKT1 (cannabis-induced psychosis risk, twofold to sevenfold), and OPRM1 (predicts naltrexone treatment response in alcohol use disorder). Despite advances, over 95% of genetic variance in addiction remains unexplained.

## Key concepts covered
- [[genetic-risk-addiction]] — 40-60% heritability; 19 cross-addiction gene variants; all linked to dopamine signaling
- [[dopamine-signaling]] — central neurobiological pathway in addiction risk genes
- [[naltrexone]] — FDA-approved medication for OUD and AUD; blocked by OPRM1 gene variant predictor
- [[akt1-gene]] — cannabis + AKT1 variant: 2x psychosis risk; 7x if daily use
- [[oprm1-gene]] — predicts naltrexone response; informs personalized treatment selection
- [[chrna5-gene]] — two copies of risk variant: 2x nicotine dependence likelihood
