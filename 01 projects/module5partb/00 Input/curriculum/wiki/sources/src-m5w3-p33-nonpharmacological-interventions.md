# M5 W3 P33 – Nonpharmacological interventions

- Nonpharmacological interventions fall into three categories: psychoeducation (information dissemination), nonspecialized face-to-face interventions, and qualified therapeutic interventions; the strongest evidence base is for CBT, MI, mindfulness, and [[contingency-management]].
- [[cognitive-behavioural-therapy]] in addiction involves two components: functional analysis (identifying causes, consequences, thoughts, and feelings around substance use) and skills training (learning new coping skills and unlearning substance-related habits).
- [[motivational-interviewing]] in addiction contexts rests on three principles: collaboration (building trust), evocation (drawing on the person's own ideas — motivation comes from within), and autonomy (the person holds the power and responsibility for change).
- Family therapy approaches used in addiction include functional family therapy, couples therapy, structural, strategic, and systemic forms; all address relationships damaged by substance use.
- [[contingency-management]] provides material incentives (vouchers, cash) for abstinence or treatment adherence; it shows stronger short-term benefits than talking therapies or mindfulness, but long-term effects after cessation of rewards are less certain.
- [[12-step-programs]] (AA, NA, TSF): a 2020 Cochrane review found AA/TSF produces higher rates of continuous abstinence over months/years; critiques include spiritual/higher-power emphasis and a disease model that mandates permanent self-identification as an addict.
