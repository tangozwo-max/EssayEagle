# M5 W1 P09 — Social and Environmental Risk Factors for Substance Use

This page examines how social and environmental inequality drives substance use disorders through a vicious circle: poverty, limited education, and adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) drive substance misuse, which in turn drives poverty, adverse experiences, and reduced opportunities. Key risk domains include family environment and ACEs, peer influence, academic and social difficulties, discrimination (intersectional minority stress), homelessness, and armed conflict/displacement. The page also highlights that socioeconomic determinants of substance use show stronger associations in high-income countries than in low/middle-income settings.

## Key concepts covered
- [[adverse-childhood-experiences]] — family environment, parental substance use, and ACEs as strongest early risk factors
- [[social-exclusion]] — vicious circle linking poverty, substance misuse, homelessness, and limited education
- [[intersectionality]] — discrimination across multiple minority identities amplifies cannabis/substance use risk
- [[homelessness]] — poor treatment outcomes due to access barriers, comorbidities, needle-sharing
- [[socioeconomic-status]] — education as particularly strong SES indicator for alcohol-attributable mortality
