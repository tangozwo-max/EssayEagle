# M5 W3 P40 – Interventions for alcohol misuse

- WHO recommends combining psychological and pharmacological interventions for alcohol use disorder (AUD); five main medications are used: [[acamprosate]], [[naltrexone]], [[disulfiram]], [[nalmefene]], and [[baclofen]].
- [[acamprosate]] restores glutamate/GABA neurotransmitter balance disrupted by chronic alcohol use (decreases NMDA activity, enhances GABA), reducing cravings and withdrawal symptoms to maintain abstinence.
- [[disulfiram]] inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase, causing toxic acetaldehyde accumulation when alcohol is consumed, producing flushing, nausea, and palpitations — a pharmacological aversion strategy.
- [[nalmefene]] blocks mu, kappa, and delta opioid receptors, suppressing dopamine reward pathways; [[baclofen]] is a GABA-B receptor agonist that suppresses alcohol-stimulated dopamine release and induces indifference toward alcohol.
- Grant's case study illustrates multifaceted AUD care: medication review, possibly switching to mirtazapine, medically supervised outpatient withdrawal with acamprosate, followed by CBT, mindfulness, AA or SMART Recovery, peer support, and family therapy.
