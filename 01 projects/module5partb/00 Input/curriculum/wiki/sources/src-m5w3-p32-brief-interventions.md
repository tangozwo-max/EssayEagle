# M5 W3 P32 – Brief interventions

- [[brief-interventions]] are short, low-intensity contacts (5–60 minutes, ≤5 sessions) delivered outside specialist substance use services, typically in primary care or other opportunistic settings.
- NICE recommends 2 sessions of 10–45 minutes to explore ambivalence about substance use and increase motivation to change; the approach is nonjudgmental and focused on motivation rather than advice-giving.
- Evidence from low-middle-income countries (Ghosh et al.) shows brief interventions reduce alcohol use, with single nurse-delivered sessions appearing most effective.
- The WHO ASSIST-BI program (Alcohol, Smoking and Substance Involvement Screening Test + brief intervention) demonstrated effectiveness in Thailand, reducing substance use risk — particularly when delivered immediately after screening.
- Cultural and legal factors strongly influence brief intervention availability and acceptability; in Arab-majority countries, polysubstance misuse is more common than alcohol use, and underreporting is likely due to stigma and legal risks.
