## Practice implications for harm reduction

You have previously explored the topic of interventions for substance use, including medically assisted withdrawal, interventions for opioid use (e.g., methadone and buprenorphine), and interventions for alcohol use. Although harm reduction includes pragmatic practices such as distributing naloxone and educating people about safe substance use, the principles of harm reduction also necessitate **a shift in the philosophy toward the care** of people who use substances and who are seeking access to support services (whether mental health care or other types of care).

Treatments for people with substance use disorders require a holistic approach, with an emphasis on strategies that will reduce harm in the long term.

**Margo has sent an email to the clinic team**:

Dear all,

In developing a substance use harm reduction plan for clients, we must aim to function as sources of information and guidance, and provide a realistic range of support options aimed at improving people's lives while reducing harm.

We must not be directive or forceful in reducing harm, but instead must focus on pragmatic measures of success and collaborative care.

While providing healthcare advice, we must also respect and acknowledge the person's autonomy to make their own decisions with regard to the resources we enable them to access.

The expectation is not that the person must be compliant to treatment for substance use, but for the provider to be responsive to the person’s needs and help people to practice safer behaviors.

Of importance, harm reduction involves meeting people "where they are at" in terms of their substance use, even if this juncture involves their continued use of the substance.

\[Source: SAMHSA\]

In allowing the person who uses substances to set their own goals, harm reduction in a healthcare setting shifts the locus of control from the medical personnel to the person seeking support.

The spectrum of roles that exist within a harm reduction setting includes those that exist in direct care provision (primary care physicians, psychologists, patient navigators, care coordinators, social workers, community health workers, and nurse practitioners) and those that are involved in establishing clinic policies and in practice administration (medical directors, directors of operations, directors of finance, project directors, and operations coordinators).

 

SAMHSA six core recommendations for practices:

- safer practices
- safer settings
- safer access to healthcare
- safer transitions within healthcare
- sustainable workforce and field
- sustainable infrastructure

### safer practices
Examples of safer practices include:

- needs-based syringe services (also referred to as needle or syringe exchange programs)
- safer smoking supplies
- overdose education, overdose detection services, naloxone distribution
- drug-checking education, fentanyl test strips, other drug-checking technology at community drug-checking sites
- integrated reproductive health education, services, and supplies with sexually transmitted infection screening, prevention, and treatment
- onsite access or immediate accessible referral to basic wound care supplies and services in the community
### safer settings 
Examples of safer settings include:

- day centers and social spaces that offer harm reduction services and are led by the communities they serve
- access to safe and secure housing
- public health programs as alternatives to criminal proceedings
- hybrid recovery community organizations providing peer-delivered harm reduction services
### safer access
Examples of safer access to healthcare include:

- low-barrier treatment services offering a whole-person approach (i.e., not a medical intervention only)
- flexible provision of services that offer choice of medications and individualized dosages
- healthcare settings and providers receiving training infused with the principles and pillars of harm reduction
- nonpunitive care offered in a non-stigmatizing, nonjudgmental manner
- mobile access and take-home methadone medication
### Safer transitions
Safer transitions to care refer to connections and access to harm reduction services and trauma-informed care. Examples of practices in this sphere include:

- health hubs for integrated HIV, viral hepatitis, and other healthcare services for people who use substances
- expanded telehealth (with due regard to addressing low technology literacy needs, and enhancing access in languages other than English)
- medication access and treatment on-demand (abstinence not required)
- onsite or immediate referral to accessible nutritional assistance, clothing, temporary shelter, and housing
- coordination of care for people leaving incarceration, institutional, or treatment settings that do not offer medications, as people are at greatly heightened risk for overdose fatality when back in the community
### Sustainable workforce
Sustainable workforce and field strategies refer to resources for managing a skilled, well-supported, and appropriately managed workforce, and for sustaining community-based programs. Examples of practices in this sphere include:

- organizational leadership from people with lived experience
- living wages and essential benefits for harm reduction workers
- wellness services and support for harm reduction staff and volunteers without mandated abstinence
- training and technical assistance for community-based providers
- the inclusion of harm reduction expertise and lived experience in the selection process for harm reduction grants and other competitive processes
### Sustainable infrastructure 
Sustainable infrastructure refers to resources for building and maintaining a revitalized and community-led infrastructure to support harm reduction best practices and the needs of people who use substances. Examples of practices in this sphere include:

- hiring and appropriately compensating people who use substances to inform policy
- co-leadership of people who use substances in organizational partnership in research
- promoting education on the value of harm reduction services

## A model of person-centered care for harm reduction

Authors Chang et al. (2023) described a model of integrated harm reduction and healthcare to mitigate some of the fear and uncertainty experienced by people who use substances when they access healthcare systems. ### Beyond clinical practice

Care providers discussed the importance of reaching beyond their own clinic to disseminate practices and elements of harm reduction strategies to other clinics and healthcare providers.

**Shifting perceptions of stigma** around people who use substances is a key role of care providers beyond the clinic. In the Chang et al. study, some providers who taught at healthcare training programs spoke to the importance of infusing harm reduction philosophy into the next generation of care providers, emphasizing that care provision does not end in the clinic, and recognizing the person who uses substances as autonomous in the treatment process.

Creating referral networks for someone can help to make the transition from clinical care to external services easier (e.g., hiring peer navigators to help people attend appointments, providing people with prepaid transport cards, making appointments for the person with other services.)
### Person-provider relationship

The role of care providers within the person-provider relationship is to both learn from service users and to inform them of potential options to improve their health and well-being.

The service user should be considered the expert in their own substance use, and therefore it is the person, not the care providers, who drives the conversation and identifies their own health priorities.

Care providers should aim to develop a plan that is not directive or forceful, but that functions as a source of information and guidance, delivering a realistic range of support options aimed at **improving** the person's life.

Within clinical care, members of interdisciplinary care teams should adopt a collaborative and communicative approach with their colleagues. A wide range of providers and specialists may be needed to meet the varied needs of people with co-occurring mental and physical health conditions.

### Organizational care

Of importance at the organizational level is the development of a stigma-free culture that is respectful, trusting, and understanding of service users. A strong leadership team should promote the principles of harm reduction and hire clinical providers and staff who are willing to learn about and apply harm reduction strategies to their work.

Initial and ongoing training and education on harm reduction principles is key, as is using stigma-free language rooted in trauma-informed care.

A physical space that is comfortable and welcoming to service users is also important. This could be achieved by remembering the person's name as they walked in the door; ensuring that signage around the clinic is not stigmatizing to substance users; making wait times less onerous by handing out snacks and drinks; and allowing people to bring bags, carts, and even pets into the clinic.

A walk-in system for appointment times rather than scheduled appointments can go some way to removing the barriers put in place by the healthcare system within other medical models of care.
