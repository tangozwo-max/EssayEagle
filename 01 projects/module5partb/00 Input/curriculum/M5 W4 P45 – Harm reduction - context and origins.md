## Harm reduction: context and origins

"At its core, harm reduction supports any steps in the right direction."

DE Logan and GA Marlett

The term ***harm*** within the context of substance use can reflect many different types of injury, damage, loss, trauma, and health deterioration that occur to the person using substances and by proxy to their families, friends, communities, and wider society.

### types of harm
**Drug-specific** mortality is a type of harm that occurs when a person dies as a direct result of using a substance (e.g., overdosing). **Drug** - **related** mortality reflects the extent to which life is shortened by the use of drugs (e.g., HIV, lung cancer, suicide).

**Drug-specific** damage is a type of harm referring to physical health (e.g., liver disease, seizures, stomach ulcers). **Drug-related** damage includes the indirect consequences of substances as might occur with physical health (e.g., sexually transmitted diseases, self-harm, blood-borne viruses).

**Drug-specific** impairment of mental functioning is a type of harm that occurs as a direct result of using a substance (e.g., amphetamine-induced psychosis, ketamine intoxication). **Drug-related** impairment of mental functioning is a secondary harm (e.g., mood disorders).

Harms occurring outside the physiological and neurological domains include **loss of tangibles** (e.g., loss of income, jobs, housing, educational achievements). **Loss of relationships** is another common harm, where the substance user has fractured relationships with family and friends.

Injury is a type of harm that reflects the extent to which a substance increases the chance of injuries to other people both directly and indirectly. Such injury may include domestic violence, traffic accidents, fetal harm, and transmission of viruses.

Harm is caused by the type of criminal activity that is involved in procuring illicit substances (i.e., if a user can be prosecuted for possession), or in attaining the money to fund a drug habit through crime.


**Environmental harm** is caused by substance use—whether by discarded needles and other drug paraphernalia in the local environment or by toxic waste generated by amphetamine factories. Additional environmental damage is caused by deforestation due to drug-crop cultivation.

Harm occurs to communities when substances create decline in social cohesion, and to the wider population when the economic cost of substance use is considered in terms of government administration (e.g., healthcare, prison, social services, police).

\[Adapted from: Nutt et al.\]

There are many different facets to harm reduction, and it is important to recognize that rarely would one isolated element be sufficient to promote a whole-person approach toward recovery. Rather, complementary elements of harm reduction can aim to reduce the risk of harm and to improve quality of life.

Evidence shows that the risk of harm due to substance use can be mitigated with such approaches as needle exchange programs, medication for opioid substitution, recovery peer support, encouraging the practice of safer sex, education in schools around alcohol consumption, and drug-checking technologies.

### Supervised use

Supervised drug consumption services are designated sites where people can use substances under the safety and supervision of trained personnel. There are over 100 such sites in countries in North America and Europe. These sites have been demonstrated to prevent:

- overdose
    
- HIV and hepatitis C transmission
    
- injection-related infection
    
- public disposal of syringes
    

These services promote engagement and referrals to other support services such as employment facilitation, housing placement, and medical treatment.

[Source: National Harm Reduction Coalition]

### Medication-assisted treatment

Medication-assisted treatment can be used to provide a whole-person approach to harm reduction.

This harm reduction approach combines medications like methadone, buprenorphine, or naltrexone with counseling and behavioral therapies to address both the physical and psychological aspects of addiction. Medication-assisted treatment helps reduce withdrawal symptoms, mortality, and the risk of relapse while supporting long-term recovery.

[Source: SAMHSA]

### Psychosocial approaches

Psychosocial approaches to harm reduction might include counseling and cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT). The aim is to enhance people's psychological and social well-being by addressing the factors contributing to their substance use, such as trauma, mental health issues, or social isolation.

Emphasis is placed on peer support programs and community engagement within harm reduction frameworks. Such an approach helps to foster a nonjudgmental environment that encourages safer practices, reduced harm, and healthier coping mechanisms without making abstinence compulsory.

[Source: Marlatt et al.]

### Public health education

Public health education increases awareness around pathways toward recovery and provides practical information that minimizes the negative consequences of substance use.

Campaigns can also challenge stigma around substance use, promote access to health services, and empower people to make informed choices about their health.

[Source: Hawk et al.]

Public health education can also intersect with harm reduction in the context of advertising campaigns and legislative measures that target the marketing of substances like alcohol and tobacco (e.g., graphic warnings on packaging, health campaigns in schools, drink-driving campaigns, banning advertisements for substances in certain media, etc.).

### Peer support programs

Peer support can take many forms. Some of the more well-known programs include 12-step groups like Alcoholics Anonymous or Narcotics Anonymous. Other programs involve recovery coaching, peer-led support groups, or integrated peer support services within treatment programs.

Peer support offers a person-centered and nonjudgmental approach to substance use recovery by leveraging shared experiences to help people on their recovery journey.

## The history of harm reduction: an overview

The history of harm reduction reflects different top-down approaches through public health initiatives and bottom-up approaches through grassroots movements.

The Rolleston Report was a groundbreaking report on substance use published in 1926 in the UK. The report committee was established by the British government to investigate the medical use of morphine and heroin. The Rolleston Report remains a foundational document in the history of harm reduction and its findings played a significant role in shaping the UK's drug policies.

One of the most significant recommendations of the report was that doctors could continue to prescribe morphine or heroin to people who were addicted to these substances under certain circumstances (for example, if a person could not lead a normal life without the drug or had been unable to overcome their addiction through other treatments).

The report viewed addiction to substances primarily as a medical (rather than criminal) issue. This perspective allowed clinicians in the UK to treat substance dependence like any other chronic illness, which contrasted with the punitive criminalization approaches adopted by other countries (including the US).

By treating addiction as a medical condition rather than a moral failing or criminal activity, the Rolleston Report influenced the development of harm reduction approaches that focused on care and treatment rather than punishment.

\[[Rolleston Report](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Sir_Rolleston_%22committee_on_morphine...%22;_beginning_of_report_Wellcome_L0016501.jpg) image by Wellcome Trust / CC-By-4.0\]

In the early 1970s, Dutch legislative committees issued recommendations around the decriminalization of personal drug use (versus drug dealing). The legislation further differentiated between cannabis-derived products and harder drugs (e.g., heroin) based on the perceived harm that could result from their use.

Committees recommended that prevention should take priority over legal sanctions and noted that certain risks associated with substance use may be more acceptable than others.

This first harm reduction national policy was enacted in legislation within the 1976 Dutch Opium Act, which effectively decriminalized the use of so-called *soft* *drugs* like cannabis.

Along with first legislative enactments of harm reduction policies, grassroots harm reduction movements first arose in the Netherlands also. By the mid-1980s, there were some 35 *Junkiebond* (activist groups of substance users) operating in 28 Dutch cities.

These groups were successful in influencing government policy, local action, and public acceptance of harm reduction approaches.

One particularly important harm reduction approach advanced by the *Junkiebond* was the introduction of the **world’s first government-backed needle exchange program in 1984**.

\[[Junkiebond poster](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Urn-gvn-NAGO02-IISG-30051002852421-large.jpg), " *Never go out without a clean needle*," by draughtsman; Asselt, René van; Paulussen, Joep / CC-By-4.0\]

The **Mersey Model of Harm Reduction** refers to a pioneering approach to drug policy and harm reduction that emerged in Liverpool, England, during the 1980s. This model was developed in response to the rising HIV/AIDS epidemic and the public health crisis linked to intravenous drug use. Of importance, the model focused on **minimizing** the negative health and social consequences of substance use **rather than eliminating** substance use entirely.

Key aspects of the Mersey Model include:

- needle exchange programs
- substitute prescribing (e.g., methadone as a substitute for heroin)
- nonjudgmental approach (no expectation of substance use cessation/abstinence)
- public health focus (endeavor to protect the wider community from the spread of infectious diseases)
- community involvement (collaboration between health professionals, social services, and law enforcement)

With its emphasis on the pragmatic management of substance use and prioritization of individual and community health and well-being over punishment, the Mersey Model of Harm Reduction is seen as a major milestone in drug policy reform in the UK.

Since the early days of legislation around substance use, the United States has tended to adopt a zero-tolerance stance.

Not only was substance use criminalized (resulting in the mass incarceration of substance users), but many states implemented laws that prohibited the distribution of clean syringes. This led to the arrest of harm reduction advocates and the restriction of access to clean needles, which fueled the spread of HIV/AIDS and hepatitis.

The HIV/Aids epidemic spurred on early harm reduction efforts in the US. Needle exchange programs were organized at a grassroots level in more liberal cities like New York and San Francisco in the 1980s. However, in other areas of the country, people who used substances faced stigma and harsh penalties.

The prohibition and criminalization approach spurred on more unified grassroots and public health efforts on a community level. The Harm Reduction Coalition was founded in 1993 to promote policies and practices that reduce the negative consequences of drug use.

Needle exchange programs expanded in the US (despite legal and political challenges), which led to community-based harm reduction approaches.

With the intensification of the opioid epidemic in the 2000s, the Center for Disease Control (CDC) officially recommended needle exchange programs as a harm reduction strategy to reduce disease transmission. State governments evolved legislation to allow for medication-assisted treatment, the distribution of fentanyl test strips (to test for contamination), and naloxone (opioid overdose reversal medication).
