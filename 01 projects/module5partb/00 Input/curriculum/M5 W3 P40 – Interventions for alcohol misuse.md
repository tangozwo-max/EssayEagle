## Interventions for alcohol misuse

The World Health Organization (WHO) recommends a combination of psychological and pharmacological interventions for alcohol use disorder (AUD). The main medications for pharmacological interventions are:

- acamprosate
- naltrexone
- disulfiram
- nalmefene
- baclofen

Before deciding to use one of these interventions, practitioners should consider:

- medical history and comorbidities
- severity of AUD
- the person's preferences and goals
- potential medication interactions
- stage of recovery
- side effects and contraindications
- monitoring and follow-up
- evidence-based guidelines according to jurisdiction

### Acamprosate
Acamprosate is used to help maintain abstinence in people experiencing AUD (Alcohol use disorder). Its mechanism of action involves:

- restoring neurotransmitter balance—rebalancing excitatory and inhibitory neurotransmitters, which are disrupted by chronic alcohol consumption
- modulating glutamate and GABA systems—decreases the activity of glutamate, particularly at the NMDA receptors, and enhances activity of GABA
- reducing alcohol cravings—stabilization of neurotransmitters results in reduction of cravings and withdrawal symptoms associated with alcohol dependence
### Naltrexone
Naltrexone is used in both opioid use disorder and alcohol use disorder. Its mechanism of action for alcohol use disorder involves:

- blocking endogenous opioids—it is an opioid receptor antagonist. It primarily targets mu opioid receptors. It prevents the endogenous opioids such as endorphins that are released during alcohol consumption from binding to the opioid receptors.
- reducing the reward effects of alcohol—by blocking endogenous opioids, naltrexone prevents increases in dopamine release in the reward pathways, particularly in the nucleus accumbens.
- decreasing cravings and consumption—blocking the euphoric effects of alcohol reduces cravings and overall consumption of alcohol.
### Disulfiram
Disulfiram causes an acute sensitivity to alcohol, thus creating an aversion to alcohol in the person taking it. It does this by:

- inhibiting aldehyde dehydrogenase—this is crucial for metabolizing alcohol in the liver. By inhibiting it, acetaldehyde builds up in the blood.
- causing unpleasant symptoms—the build-up of acetaldehyde causes flushing, headache, nausea, vomiting, sweating, and palpitations. They are intended to deter people from consuming alcohol.
### Nalmefene
Nalmefene is an opioid receptor antagonist like naltrexone. It works by blocking mu, kappa, and delta receptors, thus inhibiting the dopamine reward pathways in the nucleus accumbens.
### Baclofen
Baclofen is a medication that acts as a selective agonist of gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) B receptors. Its primary mechanism of action in treating alcohol use disorder (AUD) involves the suppression of alcohol-stimulated dopamine release. This helps reduce the rewarding effects of alcohol, making it easier for individuals to abstain from drinking.

Baclofen may influence other neurotransmitter systems and pathways involved in addiction, such as those related to memory, motivation, and anxiety. By activating GABA-B receptors, baclofen can induce a state of indifference toward alcohol, which can be particularly beneficial for individuals struggling with cravings and relapse.

### Goals
Alcohol use disorder (AUD) is a long-term, chronic condition, so treatment goals should be personalized and change over time as a person's circumstances change.

The goals should be discussed with the person experiencing AUD and with the multidisciplinary team.

A traditional goal of AUD is cessation of alcohol consumption, but this is not realistic for many people. However, a recovery approach looks at AUD goals as a cessation from heavy drinking, and recovery of life functions such as fulfilling basic needs, socialization, or improvements in mental and physical health.
### Harm reduction
Harm reduction is a topic you will learn more about in a future week.

However, AUD harm reduction goals may include:

- maintaining engagement with care
- reducing alcohol use
- reducing high-risk behaviors
- improving quality of life
- improving mental health
### pharmacological interventions

### nonpharmacological interventions
You learned about these earlier, and it is recommended that pharmacological interventions should be combined with nonpharmacological interventions after discussion with the person experiencing AUD.
### withdrawal
People experiencing AUD who wish to go through withdrawal should be assessed to see what their risk of severe symptoms is. Those with a high risk should go through medically assisted withdrawal in an inpatient setting. Those with lower risk could be treated within an outpatient setting.
### follow-u
People experiencing AUD will need frequent follow-up to provide treatment monitoring, support, and encouragement. Initially, this should be within 2 weeks but could move to monthly or at least quarterly once the person has stabilized.
