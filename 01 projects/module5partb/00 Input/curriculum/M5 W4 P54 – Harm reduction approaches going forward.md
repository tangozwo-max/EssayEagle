## Harm reduction approaches going forward

**Read this excerpt about harm reduction in the Netherlands:**

"Since its inception, and particularly in the Dutch context, harm reduction is in its essence, a rights-affirming movement. Harm reduction services provide essential care, encompassing a wide array of strategies to improve individuals' health, socio-economic, and legal circumstances.

Similar to the momentum seen in the 1970s and 1980s, there is now a renewed push for change. Starting at the grassroots, local level, the time has come to drive systemic transformation and ensure substantial reinvestment in the harm reduction infrastructure."

\[Source: van Santen et al.\]

The **Amsterdam Conference on Harm Reduction** first brought together international policymakers, academics, people who use drugs, and civil society representatives in 1990 to discuss alternatives to punitive drug policies globally. The event focused on **how** legal regulation could replace prohibition, rather than debating whether it should.

The conference convenes every 2 years in a different city around the world and attracts over 1,000 international delegates. Serious debates around **responsible regulation of substance use** as a harm reduction strategy are gaining momentum, with delegates encouraged to generate narratives reframing harm reduction in post-prohibitionist policies.

\[Source: Busz et al.; Harm Reduction International\]

In the countries where pragmatism is a key component of policy, there is increasing support among proponents of harm reduction for the implementation of **drug quality testing** (e.g., strips that test for the presence of fentanyl in other drugs) as a public health measure.

Though not without its flaws in terms of potentially providing false negatives, this type of testing serves as a means of pharmacovigilance and an early warning system of potentially lethal drugs.

However, opponents believe that if adopted as a public health measure, such testing facilitates and supports illicit substance use.

\[Source: van Santen et al.\]
