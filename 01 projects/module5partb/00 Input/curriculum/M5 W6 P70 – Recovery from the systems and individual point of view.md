## Recovery from the systems and individual point of view

## Individual recovery

A person's recovery journey is a subjective experience. People may share experiences, but no two journeys will be exactly the same. A person's understanding of their recovery may also change over time.

A substance use or behavioral disorder can go into remission either through stopping the substance or behavior or by reducing them to a safer level, i.e., to a point where they no longer meet the criteria for substance use disorder. They are chronic conditions and can go through cycles of remission and relapse over years or decades—it can be a lifetime challenge.

To sustain remission, people need a personalized program of sustained recovery management. For some people, recovery is part of a broader change in their behavior, outlook, and identity and becomes part of how they think of themselves. These people may describe themselves as being in recovery.

Kelly and Hoeppner described recovery with a biaxial structure. One axis is about remission, the other axis is about the positive outcomes of remission. The emphasis that is placed on each axis depends on the perspective of recovery that is taken by the individual, their family and community, or services.

There are two aspects to the remission axis:

- voluntary control of problem substance use or behavior
- remission from the diagnostic symptoms of the disorder

People with lived experience of these disorders tend to focus more on the positive consequences of remission. They consider recovery to be a highly individualized experience and a process rather than a state. As you have already learned, it involves achievements in a range of different life areas.

### Treatment services

Treatment services give support to people to stabilize them, reduce their use or behavior, and promote recovery. This includes continuing care. They will work with lived experience recovery organizations to support outreach, engagement, and harm reduction.

### Recovery support services

Recovery support services provide continuing care to sustain recovery. They will interact with the lived experience recovery organizations and help to provide:

- peer-based recovery support services
    
- recovery community centers
    
- recovery housing
    
- recovery support services in educational settings
    

They will also interact with treatment services to support outreach, engagement, and harm reduction.

### Lived experience recovery organizations

Lived experience recovery organizations provide support to promote and sustain recovery. They will interact with the recovery support services and help to provide:

- peer-based recovery support services
    
- recovery community centers
    
- recovery housing

## Systems

Recovery-orientated systems of care (ROSC) are holistic, multi-agency, integrated, and coordinated.

To be effective, ROSC services need to be embedded within the community they serve. In this way, ROSC can integrate and coordinate with other public services such as:

- employment, education, training, and volunteering
- housing support
- mental and physical health services including primary care
- criminal justice services
- social care and welfare support services
