## Harm reduction: evolving evidence

Many obstacles exist to implementing harm reduction strategies in recent years. This is primarily due to the increasing use of illicit **synthetic drugs**, which have become a significant concern for many countries.

> [!-info] -info
> **Info:** Synthetic illegal drugs are made in laboratories and can be especially dangerous because the ingredients and the dosages are unknown. Examples of these drugs include synthetic cannabinoids, MDMA (also known as *ecstasy* or *Molly*), and methamphetamine.

In Southeast Asia, for example, synthetic drugs account for 80% of substance misuse. While the application of a single harm reduction intervention, such as methadone, has been proven effective in reducing harm to users of heroin, it is much more difficult to provide effective harm reduction strategies for synthetic drugs.

## Emergency harm reduction

About nine Americans per hour lose their lives to a fentanyl overdose, which is more than the number who die from car accidents, cancer, or cardiovascular disease. Fentanyl is a synthetic opioid with a lethal dose of only 2 mg.

Opioid overdose accounts for the majority of deaths among injection drug users worldwide. These deaths are preventable through the timely administration of **naloxone** (an opiate antagonist that rapidly reverses opiate‐induced respiratory depression).

In 2014, the World Health Organization (WHO) launched guidelines on the community management of opioid overdose, recommending that "people likely to witness an opioid overdose should have access to naloxone and be instructed in its administration."

Evidence demonstrates that take-home naloxone programs are found to reduce overdose mortality among program participants and in the community. Additionally, evidence shows that these programs have a low rate of adverse events.

\[Source: McDonald, Strang\]

## Case study

### Naloxone and the inner-city youth experience

The aim of a qualitative study by authors Mitchell et al. (2017) was to explore whether a take-home naloxone (THN) program impacted street-involved young people in Vancouver, Canada.

**What was studied?** The researchers studied the experience of THN training among young adults at an inner-city youth program.

**Who participated?** 11 youths between the ages of 19 and 25 with a history of opioid use.

**When was the study conducted?** July and August 2015.

**What did the researchers conclude?** Study findings suggest improved safety, altruism, empowerment, and self-esteem because of training. Other benefits include increased young adult engagement with the healthcare staff who provide and the incorporation of participant recommendations into local and provincial THN initiatives.

\[Source: Mitchell et al.\]

Also in Vancouver, Dr. Christy Sutherland, the medical director of a take-home fentanyl program, advised that the street supply of fentanyl was tainted with other substances that can be up to 100 times more potent (and lethal) than the prescription strengths.

> [!-info] -info
> **Info:** The take-home fentanyl program enables participants to purchase a safe supply prescription of either injectable or smokable fentanyl (as well as other medications like oral methadone). People purchase their prescription from the service's onsite pharmacy, with the fentanyl at the equivalent price to its street value.

## Case study

### Community-based harm reduction in Vietnam

There is a high prevalence of mental health conditions among people who inject drugs in Vietnam, and peer support is crucial to improving mental health and reducing substance use disorders.

Authors Tran and Luong (2022) provide harm reduction evidence from a community-based project called *DRIVE,* which supports people who inject drugs with mental disorders in Hai Phong, Vietnam.

The DRIVE project provides psychological screening and support through community-based organizations (CBOs). The project team engages peer members from different CBOs and connects them with psychiatrists to detect and treat people with mental health disorders.

The unique aspect of this harm reduction program is that peer advocates identify people needing mental health support in the community (rather than healthcare professionals identifying such needs). These advocates then help people with substance use disorders to access psychiatric care outside of a hospital setting.

Over a one-year follow-up period following the implementation of DRIVE, the rates of depression, psychotic disorder, and suicide risks among substance users with mental health disorders reduced significantly from 80.6%, 44.7%, and 42.4% to 15.9%, 21.8%, and 22.9%, respectively.

This project has proven feasible through improving the mental health of people who use substances and leveraging the human resources of CBOs.

\[Source: Tran, Luong\]

## Evidence around gambling and tobacco

Along with substance use, evidence-based harm reduction initiatives have been applied across a variety of domains, including gambling and other harmful behaviors such as tobacco use.

For example, in the context of vaping, harm reduction efforts often focus on promoting e-cigarettes as a safer alternative to traditional smoking. At the same time, healthcare policy and practice workers are expected to balance the risks of physical harm or dependency from e-cigarettes with similar risks that arise from smoking tobacco. This can be a difficult path for professionals to navigate, with ever-evolving guidance and legislation around harm reduction strategies.

### Tobacco harm reduction

The Royal College of Physicians (RCP) published a report that looked at the part e-cigarettes (also known as *vapes*) can play in preventing death and disability from tobacco use.

The report looks at several themes, including how e-cigarettes can be used to support more people to quit tobacco while discouraging young people and never-smokers from taking up e-cigarette use. Also examined by the RCP are trends in tobacco and vaping use and the effectiveness of e-cigarettes to treat tobacco addiction. The report concluded that there was a role for alternative nicotine products to support people to stop smoking tobacco and that regulation for those products should be formalized. 

\[Source: RCP\]

 

### Gambling harm reduction

Gambling harm reduction initiatives aim to mitigate the risks associated with problem gambling through measures like self-exclusion programs, financial limits, and public awareness campaigns.

Experts outline that the digitalization of the gambling industry poses new challenges for harm prevention and harm reduction. The digital environment (i.e., online gambling) differs from traditional, land-based gambling environments such as casinos and booking shops (known as *bookies*) where people who gamble attend in person.

The online world of gambling increases many risk factors for addiction. These include availability, ease of access, and game characteristics such as speed and intensity. Data collected on people who gamble in digital environments enables gambling platforms to offer increasingly personalized and targeted access.

Artificial intelligence (AI) can help to identify problematic game playing within online environments by using algorithmic solutions that recognize play or spending patterns.

Evolving evidence around AI indicates that it can be used to predict problem gambling or be employed to limit spending.

\[Source: Marionneau et al.\]
