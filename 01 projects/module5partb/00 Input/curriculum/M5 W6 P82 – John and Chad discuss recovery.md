John

Oh, I got clean and had even lined up a job interview because I knew I would pass the drugs test. I was really excited about it. Then I didn't get the job, that really put a downer on things. I thought, if things are going to be bad anyway, I might as well have something that makes me feel better.

John and Chad are both struggling to maintain their recovery. It is important that their support team finds a balance between letting them know that change often requires multiple efforts and avoiding the expectation that relapse is inevitable or predetermined.

The team needs to give John and Chad hope that they can make change happen over the long term. John, Chad, and their support team need persistence and patience.

One often quoted definition of insanity, (misattributed to Einstein) is doing the same thing over and over while expecting different results. Thus, with change, it is important that people learn from what has happened as they recycle through the process. John and Chad need to learn from their mistakes and failures—working smarter, not just harder.

It may be useful for them to think of the change process as a spiral: Each time they pass through it, they advance upwards, even though they revisit some stages multiple times. By doing this, they may be able to turn previously unsuccessful attempts at change into successfully sustained new patterns of behavior.

## Working with people in recovery

How could therapists work with John and Chad? One useful metaphor is that of the black box, as on an aircraft. The black box is a robust instrument designed to withstand an aircraft crash. It records everything that happens in the cockpit, which can then be reviewed after the event of a crash. John and Chad can set up their own black boxes in their minds by self-observing and being mindful of their environment when they are tempted to use substances.

Another technique is called prospective hindsight. Whereas the black box technique analyzes events after they have happened, prospective hindsight considers what could go wrong in a situation before it occurs. It is used where someone is at risk of relapse but it has not yet occurred.

### John

John's therapist uses the metaphor of an airplane's black box to help him understand and analyze his relapses. Just as a black box records critical flight information to determine the cause of a crash, John is encouraged to self-observe and reflect on the circumstances leading to his relapses. This process involves exploring thoughts, feelings, and contextual factors that contributed to the relapses.

For example, John is guided to identify not just immediate triggers like disappointment from not getting a job, but also deeper thoughts and feelings such as frustration, lack of support, and negative self-talk. The concept of apparently irrelevant decisions is introduced to help John recognize subtle choices that increased his risk of relapse, like taking a route that passed by his dealer's house.

The goal of this detailed analysis is not to induce shame but to learn from the experience and develop strategies to prevent future relapses. This includes setting realistic expectations, improving frustration tolerance, and planning ways to cope with high-risk situations.

### Chad

Chad's therapist uses prospective hindsight to help people like Chad, who face high-risk situations, by imagining a scenario where they relapse. Chad is asked to visualize a situation where he resumes heavy drinking during a trip to San Francisco and to detail what led to this relapse. This helps identify triggers and vulnerabilities.

For example, Chad might realize that overhearing his partner's conversation led to fears about their relationship and social circle, which contributed to his decision to drink. By understanding these factors, Chad can develop strategies to manage them, such as having open conversations with his partner and planning nondrinking activities.

The goal is to prevent relapse by addressing potential risk factors and strengthening his change plan before the high-risk situation occurs.
