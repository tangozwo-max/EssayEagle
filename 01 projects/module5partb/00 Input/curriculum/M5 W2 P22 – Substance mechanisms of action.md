## Alcohol

Alcohol use disorder (AUD) is a brain disorder characterized by an inability to control alcohol use despite negative consequences. It includes terms such as alcohol abuse, dependence, addiction, and alcoholism. AUD can be categorized as mild, moderate, or severe, according to the presenting signs and symptoms.

Alcohol misuse can cause lasting brain changes that perpetuate AUD and increase the risk of relapse. Evidence-based treatments, such as behavioral therapies, support groups, and medications, make recovery possible.

## Risk factors for AUD

Several different factors define a person's risk for developing AUD.

### Patterns of alcohol consumption
The risk of developing AUD can be influenced by the quantity of alcohol a person takes, how fast they drink, and how often they drink. Alcohol misuse over time, which includes binge drinking and heavy alcohol use, also increases the risk of AUD.

### Early age

A survey in the USA found that people who began drinking before age 15 were more likely to report AUD in later life than those who didn't start until they were in their twenties. Women were more at risk than men.

### Genetics and family history

Genes inherited from parents play about 60% in the risk of developing AUD. However, genes interacting with the environment also affect the risk. The drinking patterns of parents influence the chances of a child developing AUD in the future.

### Mental health

As you learned earlier, people experiencing depression, post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), and neurodivergent conditions such as ADHD, are more likely to misuse alcohol and are therefore at a greater risk of developing AUD. Adverse childhood experiences (ACE) are also a risk factor for AUD.

## The addiction cycle: alcohol

Alcohol produces pleasurable feelings in the brain and blunts negative ones. This stimulation of the dopamine reward system motivates some people to keep drinking despite the risks to their health and well-being. Research has shown that while alcohol use reduces negative feelings in the short term, it tends to enhance negative emotional states between bouts of drinking. This, in turn, leads the user to seek more alcohol and become stuck in the addiction cycle.

### Binge/intoxication

During the binge/intoxication stage of the addiction cycle, the brain experiences rewards as the basal ganglia is activated. The rewards take the form of euphoria, anxiety reduction, and improved social interactions.

This activation reinforces the drinking behavior, increasing the likelihood of repetition and the formation of a habit, potentially leading to compulsive alcohol use.

### Withdrawal/negative affect

When a person stops drinking alcohol, it may trigger withdrawal symptoms that contrast with the rewards felt during drinking. A person undergoing withdrawal may experience:

- sleep disturbances
    
- physical pain
    
- feelings of illness
    
- emotional symptoms that include dysphoria, irritability, anxiety, and emotional pain
    

Two systems give rise to these negative experiences:

1. Reduced activation in the reward system of the basal ganglia makes it difficult for the person to find pleasure in anything other than alcohol.
    
2. Increased activation of the stress systems in the extended amygdala contributes to anxiety, irritability, and unease.
    

At this point in the cycle, a person will continue drinking to escape negative experiences, not to experience the rewards.

### Acute effects

Alcohol can have several acute effects on the body, even in the absence of addiction. This is commonly known as a hangover. The effects include:

- dehydration
- disrupted sleep
- gastrointestinal irritation
- inflammation
- acetaldehyde exposure
- mini-withdrawal

> [!-info] -info
> **Info:** Acetaldehyde is a toxic metabolite of alcohol that can damage DNA and proteins.

## The effects of alcohol on the brain

Alcohol interacts with the [[gaba|GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid)]] neurotransmitter systems in the brain and that leads to an inhibitory or depressant effect which slows down brain function (GABA has an inhibitory effect on the brain).
![[Pasted image 20260614154907.png]]
Alcohol interferes with the communication pathways in the brain. It changes the brain's appearance and functioning. The physical effects of long-term alcohol use can be seen on neuroimaging scans. The changes make it more difficult for the brain areas that control balance, memory, speech, and judgment to work, which can result in injuries and other negative outcomes.
![[Pasted image 20260614154916.png]]
Adolescent brains are more vulnerable to the effects of alcohol than adult brains. As adolescents mature, their brains undergo complex changes, leading to widespread shifts in brain organization and function. The development of the social, emotional, and cognitive skills necessary for adulthood continues into their twenties.

Misuse of alcohol during adolescence can alter brain development, resulting in long-lasting changes to brain structure and function. The adolescent brain has plasticity, it changes easily in response to experiences. This makes it more vulnerable to the negative effects of alcohol.

In adolescents, the prefrontal cortex, which manages planning and decision-making, is the last area to mature. Adolescents already struggle to think before acting, and alcohol makes this even more difficult. Alcohol can lead to poor decision-making and harmful behaviors such as drinking and driving, unsafe sexual behaviors, and using other substances.
![[Pasted image 20260614154927.png]]
Alcohol interferes with the transfer of memories from short-term to long-term memory. The transfer requires neurons to communicate with each other, which needs energy. The mitochondria are impacted by disruptions in several processes caused by alcohol, including:

- impairment of glutamate receptor (NMDAR) activity
- neuroinflammatory events
- oxidative stress

Blackouts differ from passing out or being unconscious because the person experiencing the blackouts remains awake. Whatever they experience at this point does not pass into their long-term memory, meaning memories are not created.

There are two types of blackouts:

1. **Fragmentary:** The memory of events is fragmented, with islands of recall separated by periods of no recall.
2. **En bloc:** This is a severe form of blackout where memories of the entire event do not form and cannot be recovered. To the person experiencing the blackout, the event did not happen.
![[Pasted image 20260614154952.png]]
An alcohol overdose shuts down activity in the brain stem, which is responsible for controlling basic life functions such as breathing, heart rate, and temperature control. An alcohol overdose can lead to permanent brain damage or death.

The symptoms of alcohol overdose include:

- mental confusion
- difficulty remaining conscious
- vomiting
- seizures
- breathing difficulties
- slow heart rate
- clammy skin
- poor reflexes, including the gag reflex, which poses a risk of choking
- low body temperature

When alcohol is combined with sedatives such as benzodiazepines or opioids, the risk of overdose is increased.

People who consume large quantities of alcohol too quickly are at a higher risk of overdose, as the body does not have time to metabolize it. This causes alcohol levels to rise more rapidly and reach higher levels than when a person drinks more slowly over time.
![[Pasted image 20260614155000.png]]
Prenatal alcohol exposure can cause brain damage to the unborn. It can lead to behavioral, developmental, and cognitive problems, which can appear at any stage during childhood. Alcohol can disrupt fetal development at any stage of pregnancy, including the very early stages before pregnancy is known.

### Moderate use

Alcohol consumption, even at moderate levels, is associated with adverse brain outcomes, including hippocampal shrinkage causing memory and cognitive problems. Long-term effects can range from mild cognitive impairment (MCI) to alcohol-related brain damage (ARBD, Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome). Other effects, apart from hippocampal atrophy, include:

- damage to blood vessels, high blood pressure, increased risk of stroke
- low levels of thiamine (vitamin B1), poor diet, and higher risk of malnutrition, which increase the risk of brain damage
- increased risk of head injuries due to accidents and violence, with associated risk of brain damage
- increased risk of developing cancer

\[Source: Topiwala et al.; World Health Organization\]
