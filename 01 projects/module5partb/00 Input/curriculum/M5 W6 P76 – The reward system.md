## The reward system

Addiction often hijacks the brain's reward system, leading to compulsive substance use. Recovery-oriented treatments work to restore balance in this system, helping individuals find pleasure and reward in healthier activities.

As you learned earlier, dopamine is a key part of the brain’s reward system. Psychoactive substances and certain activities cause a flood of dopamine in the brain, which brings pleasure and euphoria. This reinforces the behaviors that caused the dopamine release, resulting in the person wanting to repeat them until a habit is formed.

During recovery, changes occur to the reward pathways.

Chronic substance use often leads to reduced sensitivity to dopamine, requiring more of the substance to achieve the same pleasurable effects. In recovery, the brain gradually restores its sensitivity to dopamine, although this process can take time.

## Mindfulness-based interventions

Addiction is often marked by automatic, habitual responses without conscious control. Mindfulness-based therapies help people to develop nonreactivity to distressing thoughts, observing experiences, emotional discrimination, and awareness of automaticity. This is called *trait mindfulness* and can help people self-regulate automatic addictive behaviors, serving as a buffer against addiction.

MBIs work by:

- **Strengthening cognitive control** networks in the prefrontal cortex that are weakened by chronic drug use and hijacked by drug-related cues.
- **Restoring adaptive cognitive control** and increase functional connectivity between prefrontal networks and limbic-striatal brain circuitry involved in reward processing and motivated behavior.
- Enhancing connectivity between top-down (prefrontal) and bottom-up (limbic-striatal) brain networks helps **reduce automatic addictive behaviors**.
- Augmenting the prefrontal cortex's capacity to regulate subcortical brain networks, **strengthening neurocognitive resources** to modulate addiction-related mechanisms like reward processing, cue-reactivity, and stress reactivity.

Garland and Howard put forward the restructuring reward hypothesis, which suggests mindfulness shifts focus from drug-related rewards to natural rewards, reducing addictive behaviors. Key points are:

- Attention regulation and positive affect: mindfulness training enhances attention regulation and positive emotions, increasing pleasure from natural experiences (savoring).
- Reward system: studies show MBIs increase natural reward processing, reduce cravings, and decrease addictive behaviors.
- MORE: Mindfulness-Oriented Recovery Enhancement (MORE) increases physiological responses to natural rewards, reduces opioid cravings, and enhances positive affect.
- fMRI: MORE reduces brain responses to drug cues and increases responses to natural rewards, improving functional connectivity in reward circuits.
- Integrated training: MORE combines mindfulness, reappraisal, and savoring skills, which may not be the case for all MBIs.
