## M5 W1 P01 – Addiction and dependency - definitions and concepts

This is Suzanne. She led a fulfilling, fun, and active life before a car accident caused a severe injury to her back.

Suzanne had surgery and was prescribed strong painkillers. Her active lifestyle was over. Suzanne struggled to deal with the memories of the car accident and began to use these painkillers less for pain relief and more for the sense of escape and calmness they provided. She was facilitated with a work-from-home arrangement initially, but the impact of the painkillers meant that she could no longer sustain her employment. Friends and family were worried, but Suzanne pushed people away as she did not want them to know that her life was falling apart. As far as Suzanne was concerned, only the painkillers allowed her to cope with the physical and emotional pain she experienced.

This is Laura. She experienced postnatal depression after the birth of her first child 5 years ago. Laura was prescribed antidepressants by her physician and proceeded to take these for around 8 months. However, Laura's relationship with her child's father broke down at this time, and she had little social support to help her with the childcare demands of full-time employment.

Laura felt more and more overwhelmed by her situation and returned to speak to her physician. Again, she was prescribed antidepressants, and she continued to take these for several years. More recently, Laura started to feel that the antidepressants were not working. Her physician switched her medication to a different type of treatment. Laura began to experience severe symptoms like headaches and nausea.

In the scenarios described, Suzanne has developed an addiction to painkillers, and Laura has developed a dependency on a specific type of medication.

**Addiction** refers to harmful behaviors such as loss of control, with active cravings and dysfunctional recognition of negative consequences.

When people use the term ***dependence***, they are usually referring to a physical dependence on a substance. Dependency is a state resulting from prolonged use of a substance where the body adapts to its presence, leading to tolerance and withdrawal symptoms when use is reduced or stopped.

\[Source: Addiction Center\]

**Read this excerpt from the transcript of an interview with Dr. Nora Volkow:**

"Addiction is not a choice. Nobody is born with the idea that they want to choose to become addicted and lose control over their desire, intense desire to take drugs. It's something that you may have (the vulnerability) because actually you have genetic risk, family history, or because you have a very stressful environmental upbringing. The choice starts, and again it's relative, when you are exposed for the first time to the drug. And then some people become addicted and others do not. And that's where a lot of the research is going. To try to understand what are the differences in that transition."

\[Source: National Institute on Drug Abuse\]

## Learning outcomes

On successful completion of this week, you will be able to:

- define addiction, dependency, behavioral addiction, and compulsive behaviors
- describe the differences between addiction and dependency, and critically evaluate the terminology around the definitions
- evaluate the reasons that different types of addiction may be more common in different age or cultural groups
- identify a number of reasons why addiction often co-occurs with mental health conditions
- weigh the impact of stigma and discrimination on people who use substances
- evaluate the role of genetic, environmental, socioeconomic, and psychological factors in addiction and dependency
