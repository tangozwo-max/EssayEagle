## M5 W1 P02 – What is meant by addiction and dependency

Globally, over 90% of people ingest the world's most widely used drug—caffeine.

But what if, suddenly, there were no more coffee beans in the world?

Would you struggle without your favorite drink?

Many people consider themselves "addicted" to coffee, but in reality, they have developed a **dependence** on caffeine.

If you stopped drinking coffee suddenly, you may (or may not, depending on your physiology and the amount of caffeine you consume) experience withdrawal symptoms such as headaches, anxiety, sleeplessness, and increased blood pressure. Such physical symptoms occur because people develop a **dependency** on a drug.

An **addiction** to coffee would mean that you use caffeine or engage in behaviors around caffeine in ways that become compulsive and often continue despite harmful consequences to yourself and those around you. Though rare, it is feasible that someone could develop an addiction to caffeine (this would be most commonly observed with co-occurring mental health conditions like eating disorders).

\[Source: Addiction Center; Hartney\]

You have probably heard many different terms around the topics of addiction and dependency.

Stigmatizing terms like *addict* or *drug abuser* are considered unacceptable among healthcare agencies and, for the most part, in society generally.

At the same time, terms like *dependency* and *substance use* or *misuse* can cause confusion.

This confusion was compounded by frequently changing terminology among those tasked with framing the diagnostic criteria.

In 2013, the American Psychiatric Association (APA) released the fifth edition of the *Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders* (DSM-5) and clarified that the correct diagnostic term for what had previously been classified as *addiction* is now ***substance use disorder***.

In the decades prior to the release of DSM-5, *abuse* was considered to be a mild form of addiction, and *dependence* was considered a moderate or severe form of addiction.

Since the APA released the first edition of the DSM in 1952, the various terms used included:

- *addiction* (with subcategories of *alcoholism* and *drug addiction*)
- *abuse*
- *habitual*, *episodic*, or *excessive drinking*
- *out-of-control use* (classified as *dependence* in editions prior to DSM-5)
- *harmful use* (classified as *abuse* in editions prior to DSM-5)
- *substance abuse*
- *substance dependence*

For many reasons, these terms proved problematic. With the release of the fifth edition, the APA removed both *substance abuse* and *substance dependence*, and put forward ***substance use disorder (SUD)***, which measures substance use on a continuum from mild to severe. Part of the reason for this change in terminology was the hope that defining an addiction as a substance use disorder was **a more inclusive way to identify people who need help but who may not have a debilitating addiction**.

\[Source: Addiction Center\]

According to the American Society of Addictive Medicine, **addiction** is defined as, “a treatable, chronic medical disease involving complex interactions among brain circuits, genetics, the environment, and an individual’s life experiences. People with addiction use substances or engage in behaviors that become compulsive and often continue despite harmful consequences.”

\[Source: ASAM\]

Substance misuse

**Substance misuse** occurs when someone uses certain substances in a way that harms themselves and/or people around them.

Misuse is often defined in medical and public health contexts as the use of a substance in a way that deviates from the accepted medical or social norms (e.g., using more opioids than a person has been prescribed, or frequently drinking alcohol to excess). Such misuse can **potentially lead** to the development of substance use disorder.

\[Source: National Institute on Drug Abuse\]

Substance use disorder

The DSM-5 defines ***substance use disorder (SUD)*** as a condition in which the use of one or more substances leads to clinically significant impairment or distress.

The disorder is diagnosed based on a set of 11 criteria, which you will explore in greater detail this week in content to come.

SUD is complex and involves different factors such as uncontrolled use, social impairment, risky use, harmful consequences, and pharmacological indicators (tolerance and withdrawal).

DSM-5 does not use the term *addiction*. What might be commonly referred to as addiction would be categorized as a **severe** SUD.

\[Source: American Psychiatric Association\]

Dependence

**Dependence** on a substance refers to the physical symptoms of withdrawal and tolerance that a person develops with prolonged use of the substance.

**Physical dependence is much more common than addiction**.

Symptoms of dependence can occur regardless of whether the substances are taken as prescribed or are misused.

The DSM-5 commented that the term *dependence* has been often confused with the term *addiction* when, in fact, the tolerance and withdrawal symptoms that define dependence are actually **very normal responses** to prescribed medications and do not necessarily indicate the presence of an addiction.

For example, the vast majority of people taking medications such as opioids and benzodiazepines are doing so as prescribed by their clinicians, with only 1.5% of people taking benzodiazepines classified as having a substance use disorder.

\[Source: Horowitz, Taylor\]

Dependence syndrome

**Dependence syndrome** is a condition defined by the World Health Organization (WHO) as representing a cluster of behavioral, cognitive, and physiological phenomena that develop after repeated substance use.

Dependence syndrome typically involves a strong desire to use the substance, difficulties in controlling its use, persisting in its use despite harmful consequences, and the person giving a higher priority to substance use than to other activities and obligations. Dependence syndrome results in increased tolerance, and sometimes a physical withdrawal state.

Note that the DSM-5 does not carry the term *dependence syndrome* specifically and refers instead to substance use disorder to represent the broader framework of substance misuse and dependence disorders.

\[Source: ICD-10 WHO\]

**Read this excerpt about the clarity and coherence required for conceptualizing addiction:**

"... the question ‘ *what is addiction?*’ is a *non sequitur*. The meaningful question is: what are useful constructs to which we can apply labels such as ‘ *addiction* ’ or ‘ *substance dependence* ’ and ‘ *addictive behavior’*? There may be several useful constructs to which people want to attach these labels, and the key is to define each of these constructs carefully using a formulation that makes it clear how they relate to each other."

\[Source: West et al.\]

When the symptoms of mental and physical dependence are apparent, a severe substance use disorder (i.e., what people refer to as *an addiction*) is usually present.

However, the main characteristic that distinguishes addiction from dependence is the **combination of mental and physical dependence with uncontrollable behavior** in obtaining and using a substance.

\[Source: Addiction Center\]

Authors West et al. (2024) developed an ontology around addiction and substance use behavior in an effort to achieve consensus and clarity:

| Term | Definition |
| --- | --- |
| Addiction | A mental ***disposition*** that results in repeated episodes of abnormally high levels of motivation to engage in a behavior, acquired as a result of engaging in the behavior where the behavior results in risk or occurrence of serious net harm |
| Addictive disorder | A mental ***disorder*** that results in repeated episodes of abnormally high levels of motivation to engage in a behavior, acquired as a result of engaging in the behavior where the behavior results in risk or occurrence of serious net harm |
| Substance dependence | A ***bodily disposition*** that is realized as impaired functioning following reduction or termination of use of a psychoactive substance as a result of long-term physiological adaptation to that substance |
| Addictive behavior | An individual ***human behavior*** that is addictive in a significant proportion of people who engage in it |
| Addictiveness of a behavior | A ***behavioral attribute*** that is the extent to which it results in addiction in people who engage in it |

\[Source: West et al.\]
