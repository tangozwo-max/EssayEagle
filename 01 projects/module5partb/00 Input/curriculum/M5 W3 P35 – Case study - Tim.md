It started when I was feeling very stressed, at work as well as home. I thought a night out with my mates would be good for me. We went to some bars and then to a casino. I won big! It felt so good, I had a buzz for the first time in ages. When we left to go home, I felt much better about myself and was making all kinds of plans.

After that, I wanted to feel that way again, so I returned to the casino. I kept trying to persuade my mates and wife to go with me too. When they didn’t want to go, I went on my own, but my bets got bigger and bigger. I thought that winning was down to my skill in playing the games. Losses were just part of the game. I thought I was picking up on patterns in the games, even though they were random games of chance. I thought that the reason other people couldn’t see them was just lack of skill on their part.

Eventually, gambling was taking up huge amounts of my time. I was thinking about it constantly, watching poker videos online, and placing more and more online bets.

Betting small amounts no longer gave me the same thrill. I had to place large bets to get the same buzz. I was running out of money each month so I took money from our savings. Eventually, I took out loans, in my name, and in my wife’s.

My wife found out and she was furious. We had lots of arguments and I kept lying about what was going on. I felt ashamed and couldn’t see a way out. The only thing that helped me feel better was more gambling. I was sure that I’d get a big win and be able to pay everything off. I won often enough to keep hope going but eventually I started to look into remortgaging my house.

This was the last straw for my wife, she told me she wanted to end our relationship and move out. She couldn’t stand it anymore. Then work noticed my poor performance and lack of focus and placed me on review. At this point, I turned to alcohol to dull all the bad feelings.

## Phases of gambling disorder

The Illinois Institute for Addiction Recovery has identified the following four phases in gambling addiction.

Select each heading to learn more.

### winning
This phase starts with a big win, giving the person excitement and a positive view of gambling. They develop a cognitive distortion and believe that the winning is due to a special talent for gambling and not just chance. They also believe that the winning will continue and begin to spend more and more money on it.
### Losing
A person experiencing gambling addiction becomes preoccupied with gambling. It may have started as a social activity, but they will begin to gamble alone. They borrow money, are less productive at work, lie to their family and friends about their gambling, and may default on debts.
### Desparation
This person will believe that by continuing gambling, they are sure to win again and be able to pay off their debts. They are chasing their losses.
### Hopeless 
In this phase, the person experiencing gambling addiction loses control over their gambling. They have negative feelings of shame and guilt after they gamble but these don't stop them. In order to gain funds for gambling, they may cheat or steal. They may lose their jobs or relationships and enter the criminal justice system.

In this phase, the person experiencing gambling addiction loses hope. They may feel that no one cares about them and that there is no support for their addiction. People in this phase may start to use substances to deal with their negative feelings, they may consider self-harm or suicide.

### psychoeducation
Tim received psychoeducation around cognitive biases and automatic thinking styles, learning principles of reinforcement theory, understanding the role of urges, and understanding basic neuroscience around the role of dopamine in sensation-seeking behaviors.

### CBT
A more detailed motivational interviewing approach was used as part of broader CBT to address high levels of ambivalence, shame, and stigma, and to encourage staying in treatment for longer.

CBT was used to increase awareness of automatic gambling thoughts, increase compassionate curiosity about behavior, implement behaviors to challenge automatic thoughts (e.g., a tally of losses vs wins to highlight the costs), and increase perspective around the reality of gambling odds. Guided imagery was used to imagine a gambling scene. This enables alternative responses to gambling environments to be practiced, such as both walking away relaxed and feeling good about doing so. This can decrease the arousal associated with urges.

###  mindfulness
Learning mindfulness techniques helped Tim to increase his awareness of his bodily reactions and cognitive processes related to gambling, such as thought suppression and rumination.

Tim benefitted from this support and began a debt repayment plan. He experienced several relapses, as is expected within gambling recovery, but was able to seek help and apply strategies quickly before the situation escalated again.

Gambling had taken up so much of his life that he had to actively find new activities and create new relationships that did not revolve around gambling. It took his family time to regain trust, as gambling had been secretive for so long for him, but they were able to feel more connected to him the longer he went without gambling.
