## Interventions for opioid misuse

Methadone, buprenorphine, and lofexidine are all agonists to various receptors on neurons. They are used to treat opioid use disorder by either substituting for the opioid (methadone and buprenorphine) or by reducing withdrawal symptoms (lofexidine).

Select each tab to learn more about how they work.

### Methadone

Methadone fully activates the mu opioid receptors and mimics the effects of natural opioids such as endorphins or enkephalins. The effect of this is to cause pain relief and euphoria.

Methadone is also a noncompetitive antagonist at the N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptor. These receptors are involved in pain transmission and the development of tolerance. Because methadone blocks them, it provides better pain relief.

Methadone also has a long half-life—it stays in the body for a longer time than other opioids, which helps to maintain stable drug levels, reduces frequency of dosing, and helps to manage withdrawal symptoms. This can be a cause for concern as methadone can build up in the body, giving increased risk of respiratory depression and overdose if not carefully monitored.

### Buprenorphine

Buprenorphine is a partial mu opioid receptor agonist. This means that it activates the receptors to a lesser extent than morphine or other opioids.

It also binds to kappa opioid receptors, which helps to reduce the dysphoric and hallucinogenic symptoms that occur with opioid use.

Due to its high binding affinity and long duration of action, it reduces the euphoria and respiratory depression that opioids cause while still maintaining pain relief.

### Lofexidine

Lofexidine is an alpha-2 adrenergic receptor agonist—it activates the receptors to reduce the release of norepinephrine, which is involved in the flight or fight response.

Decreased norepinephrine levels lower sympathetic tone and alleviate withdrawal symptoms such as anxiety, agitation, muscle aches, and sweating.

Methadone or buprenorphine are the accepted first-line treatments for opioid detoxification. Lofexidine, which enables withdrawal over a shorter time period, may be used in certain circumstances. When making choices between medications, practitioners should consider:

- whether the service user is already using one of these medications for maintenance treatment
- the personal preferences of the service user

Lofexidine works differently to methadone and buprenorphine in that it blocks different receptors. It is listed as an alternative to methadone or buprenorphine in the NICE guidelines in the UK. It may be considered if the person has:

- made an informed and clinically appropriate decision not to use methadone or buprenorphine for detoxification
- made an informed and clinically appropriate decision to detoxify over a short time period
- mild or uncertain dependence

> [!-info] -info
> **Further info:** Other pharmacological treatments may be used to reduce withdrawal symptoms. For example, loperamide for diarrhea and paracetamol for pain.

## Five pillars of pain management

Pain is subjective. It is influenced by physiological stimuli and emotional, cultural, environmental, and social climates.

Chronic pain that lasts for 12 weeks or longer can cause psychological distress, decreased mobility, obesity, decreased physical function, social isolation, and financial loss, and it may develop into a chronic disability. Chronic pain treated with opioids can lead to substance use disorder in some people, and so these should be used with care.

Because chronic pain may last for years, the treatment goal should be to manage pain, not to get rid of it completely. Treatment should follow five principles, or pillars.

Select each heading to learn more about each pillar.
### psychosocial engagement
Improving a person's socialization can improve their engagement with treatment and should be part of their care plan. This may take the form of participation in a peer therapy group, a 12-step program for substance use, or other activities with family and friends.

Psychological interventions such as cognitive behavioral therapy have been shown to be useful for managing pain but less useful for opioid dependence.

Social work interventions may help relieve financial or housing stress. Finding a way to feel useful and engaged will help with recovery.
### improving mobility
Increasing physical activity and mobility can improve mood and help with weight loss. This in turn can help to manage pain and reduce reliance on opioids. Physical therapy and activities such as yoga have been shown to improve pain outcomes.
### weight loss
For obese people with chronic pain, losing weight through diet and exercise can improve their pain symptoms and physical functioning. This in turn can reduce reliance on opioid pain relief.
### substance use treatment
Recovery from substance use disorder requires people to do cognitive and emotional work to help change their behaviors and to take accountability for their use. People with chronic pain and a substance use disorder may focus on the pain to the exclusion of this work. This reduces their engagement with treatment programs.
### Pharmacological interventions
Pharmacological interventions can be used to treat both pain symptoms and other conditions that occur with chronic pain such as depression. Patients should be assessed to see if they have substance use disorder, or are at risk of it. Opioids should not be used with these patients where possible. The exception is for people with a terminal condition. Opioids may be used for acute pain in an inpatient medical setting.

Opioid agonist agents may help with chronic pain.
