## International impact

"Drug production, trafficking, and use continue to exacerbate instability and inequality, while causing untold harm to people’s health, safety and well-being. We need to provide evidence-based treatment and support to all people affected by drug use, while targeting the illicit drug market and investing much more in prevention."

Ghada Waly

Executive Directory, UNODC

New synthetic opioids, such as nitazenes, and increased supply and demand of other drugs have increased the impacts of drug problems across the world. These resulted in increased substance use disorders and environmental harm. The United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) has produced a report about the harms of the world drug problem.

### Empowering organized crime groups
Drug traffickers in the Golden Triangle (a mountainous region of South-East Asia that covers several different countries) are expanding into various other illegal markets, particularly wildlife trafficking, financial fraud, and unlawful resource extraction. As a result of this instability, vulnerable communities—including displaced, impoverished, and migrant populations—are facing dire consequences. Many are compelled to resort to opium farming or illegal resource extraction for survival, often falling into cycles of debt with criminal organizations or becoming users of drugs themselves.

Additionally, these illegal activities are exacerbating environmental harm through deforestation, the disposal of toxic waste, and chemical pollution.
### Cocaine surge
In 2022, cocaine production reached a record high of 2,757 tons, marking a 20% increase from the previous year. During the same period, global coca bush cultivation grew by 12%, totaling 355,000 hectares.

This sustained rise in both cocaine supply and demand has been linked to escalating violence in regions along the supply chain, particularly in Ecuador and various Caribbean nations. Additionally, there has been a notable increase in health issues in destination countries, especially in Western and Central Europe.
### Cannabis legalization
As of January 2024, Canada, Uruguay, and 27 jurisdictions in the United States have legalized the production and sale of cannabis for recreational use, while various legislative approaches have emerged in other parts of the world.

In these regions of the Americas, this shift seems to have contributed to an increase in harmful cannabis use and a wider variety of cannabis products, many containing high levels of tetrahydrocannabinol (THC). There has been a rise in hospitalizations due to cannabis use disorders, as well as an increase in psychiatric disorders and suicide attempts linked to regular cannabis use, particularly among young adults in Canada and the United States.
### Psychedelic rennaisance
Though interest in the therapeutic use of psychedelic substances has continued to grow in the treatment of some mental health disorders, clinical research has not yet resulted in any scientific standard guidelines for medical use.

However, within the broader psychedelic renaissance, popular movements are contributing to burgeoning commercial interest and to the creation of an enabling environment that encourages broad access to the unsupervised, quasi-therapeutic, and non-medical use of psychedelics. Such movements have the potential to outpace the scientific therapeutic evidence and the development of guidelines for the medical use of psychedelics, potentially compromising public health goals and increasing the health risks associated with the unsupervised use of psychedelics.
### Afghanistan opium ban
In 2023, Afghanistan experienced a dramatic 95% decline in opium production compared to 2022, while Myanmar saw a 36% increase. As a result, global opium production dropped by 74%. This significant reduction in the Afghan opiate market has left Afghan farmers poorer, while a few traffickers have benefited financially.

The long-term effects of these changes may soon be evident in transit and destination countries for Afghan opiates. This could potentially impact heroin purity, prompt some heroin users to switch to other opioids, and increase the demand for opiate treatment services.
### Right to health
The right to health is a universally acknowledged human right that applies to everyone, irrespective of their drug use status or whether they are imprisoned, detained, or incarcerated. This right extends equally to individuals who use drugs, as well as their children, families, and other members of their communities.

The impact of drug abuse on developing countries is significant, affecting both the economy and society as a whole. Drug abuse can impair work, employment, and productivity.

Additionally, the rise in drug abuse in developing countries leads to increased costs, including higher budgets for:

- law enforcement
- justice systems and courts
- health care (illness, premature death, emergency room visits)
- reduced productivity

These impacts on developing or lower-income areas mean that if a country has to focus on tackling trafficking and addiction, they have fewer resources to focus on, for instance, reducing poverty and achieving sustainable development.

## Global drug policy

A globally coordinated and proactive approach to reducing the prevalence and impact of drug production, supply, and use continues to be required, but this needs to be implemented in a way that does not lead to further unintentional harm and systemic injustice.

The Global Drug Policy Index shows the response to drugs in individual countries. It divides responses to substance use into the following categories:

- absence of extreme responses
- proportionality and criminal justice approaches
- harm reduction
- access to medication
- considerations around development; e.g., are alternative sustainable development programs supported to provide alternatives to illegal drug production?
