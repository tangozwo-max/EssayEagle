## M5 W2 P23 – Cannabis

Here are some facts about cannabis use from the United States of America:

1. About 19% of Americans, used cannabis in 2021.
2. Approximately 3 in 10 people who use cannabis have cannabis use disorder.
3. The risk of developing cannabis use disorder is greater for people who use it before age 18.
4. Cannabis use directly affects the parts of the brain responsible for memory, learning, attention, decision-making, coordination, emotion, and reaction time.
5. Infants, children, and teens are especially susceptible to the adverse effects of cannabis.
6. Long-term or frequent cannabis use has been linked to psychosis or schizophrenia.
7. Using cannabis during pregnancy may increase the risk of pregnancy complications.

\[Source: CDC\]

Cannabis is a plant of the *Cannabaceae* family. It contains hundreds of biologically active compounds. The most potent of these are delta-9-tetrahydrocannabinol (THC) and cannabidiol (CBD).

Cannabis is one of the most commonly used drugs in the world and adolescents are the most common users. Usage declines as individuals age into adulthood due to life factors such as careers, marriage, cohabitation, and parenthood.

## Mechanism of action

Cannabis affects the brain in several different ways.

Heavy cannabis use can alter brain circuitry but the mechanisms are unclear. THC is likely responsible for the reinforcing properties of cannabis. Research suggests that, compared to other substances, a lower dopamine response may occur.

Active chemical compounds in cannabis modulate the transmission of endocannabinoids, GABA, glutamate, and serotonin, which alter the brain’s network for self-awareness. Cannabis reduces anxiety, impairs memory, and affects motivation and personal experience.
![[Pasted image 20260614155118.png]]
[[thc-cbd|THC]] primarily binds to [[cannabinoid-receptors|cannabinoid receptors CB1]] which are located throughout the central nervous system, and in the lungs, liver, and kidneys. CB2 receptors are expressed to a lesser extent to in the brain and are involved in immune responses.
![[Pasted image 20260614155110.png]]
THC modulates the G-protein-coupled inhibition of cyclic adenosine monophosphate. It influences pain, mood, appetite, nausea, and sexual activity. Microglial cells are affected more than neurons. Astrocytes may play a role in signaling by regulating endocannabinoid turnover.

Cannabis binds to lipids and can therefore remain detectable in the body for a long time after inhalation or ingestion. The higher the body fat level, the longer cannabis remains. This also makes withdrawal a slow-onset phenomenon.

## The addiction cycle: cannabis

Select each marker to learn more about the effects of cannabis in the addiction cycle.
### Binge/intoxication

During the binge/intoxication phase of the addiction cycle, cannabis affects the basal ganglia, particularly the nucleus accumbens. It activates the brain's reward system, leading to the release of dopamine. This results in feelings of euphoria and relaxation.

With chronic use, changes occur in the basal ganglia which may impair motor control and cognitive functions but this is not yet proven through research.


### Withdrawal/negative effect

When cannabis use is ceased, the extended amygdala comes into play. This area of the brain is involved in stress and emotional regulation. Users may experience withdrawal symptoms such as irritability, anxiety, and mood swings.

Chronic use may lead to structural changes in the extended amygdala, which can exacerbate negative emotional states during withdrawal.

### Preoccupation/anticipation

During the preoccupation/anticipation phase, the pre-frontal cortex is affected, showing altered activity and connectivity. This influences decision-making and impulse control and impairs executive functioning, which increases the likelihood that a person will seek more cannabis.

The person will likely be preoccupied with obtaining cannabis and experience cravings for it.
