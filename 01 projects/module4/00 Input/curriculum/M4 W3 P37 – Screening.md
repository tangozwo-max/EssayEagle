## Screening

Mental health screening is a crucial step in identifying children and young people who may need further psychological support or intervention.

Screening uses a standardized set of questions to explore children and young people's experiences of symptoms of potential mental health conditions. This includes exploring their thoughts, emotions/feelings, and behaviors.

The topics covered by a screening process will vary depending on what is being screened for. A holistic screening tool will often cover areas such as school, friendships, home life, employment for older teenagers, sexuality, and potential risks such as substance use and self-harm. The aim is to identify issues that may be affecting their mental health and well-being.

As with all aspects of individual mental health assessments, screening is best completed as part of a conversation rather than through use as a stand-alone measure. Flagged areas can then be assessed in greater detail.

At a group or population level, screening programs will administer standardized and validated questionnaires. Where a certain score is reached (a clinical cut-off), young people will be referred for further individual or group intervention. In this situation, young people typically complete questionnaires independently (self-reporting).

Screening tools will vary according to jurisdiction. Some should only be used by qualified mental health professionals and some can be used by trained lay people. Whoever is using them, they should always consider the appropriateness of the tool they are using.

There is a need to bridge school based mental health care and community psychology. Schools need to consider how to use mental health screening equitably and with contextual considerations.

Moore et al. advocate for a shift in screening practices from focusing on individual deficits to embracing a systems and holistic approach that:

- Recognizes strengths and stressors within individuals, groups, and communities
- Works to dismantle structural oppression
- Enhances mental health outcomes for minoritized youth

They propose that schools should:

- Integrate diverse perspectives
- Elevate the voices of students, families, and communities
- Foster collaborative partnerships to jointly develop a vision for equitable school mental health

\[Source: Moore et al.\]

## Common screening tools

There is a range of widely recognized tools used in mental health screening for children and young people. These are usually a standardized set of questions that are used to identify issues or concerns in a child that will then need further investigation.

Many countries have taken these tools and translated them into local languages. Care needs to be taken that the tool is suitable for the population it is being used in and that research is undertaken to validate it in its new form.

These questionnaires can be used by parents or teachers, or self-reported by the children and young people themselves. Best practice is to use them as part of a structured conversation where they can ask follow-up questions.

| Tool | Purpose | Age range | Method |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Pediatric Symptom Checklist (PSC) | To identify and assess psychosocial issues that may affect a child's functioning | 4–16 years | Parent, teacher, or self-report questionnaire |
| Strengths and Difficulties Questionnaire (SDQ) | To screen for behavioral and emotional disorders | 3–17 years | Two versions, youth self-report and parent report, that can either be completed by parents, teachers, or mental health workers, or by the children and young people themselves |
| Child Behavior Checklist (CBCL) | To assess a range of behavioral and emotional problems | 6–18 years | Parent-report questionnaire |
| Beck Youth Inventories | To evaluate children's and adolescents' emotional and social impairment | 7–18 years | Self-report inventories |
| Me and My School (M&MS) | A school-based measure of child mental health covering emotional and behavioral difficulties | 8–19 years | Mental health lead or self-report questionnaire |
| Revised Child Anxiety and Depression Scale (RCADS) | To evaluate a range of anxiety and depression disorders | 8–18 years | Two versions, youth self-report and parent report |

> [!-info] -info
> **Further info:** In addition to these generalized questionnaires, screening measures exist for specific conditions such as trauma, depression, anxiety, OCD, eating disorders, and neurodivergent conditions.

> [!-clinical-tip] -clinical-tip
> **Clinical tip:** Administration of a screening tool should always happen in the context of a wider conversation about assessment and potential results. They should always be followed up with a conversation with the child or young person and/or their family to explore any positive findings.

Salamanca-Butello et al. examined the ethical challenges around tools used in population screening for mental health conditions in lower and middle-income countries. Here are the challenges they identified:

- tools that are not designed for the population being screened and so may mislabel or pathologize behavior that is normal to the culture in question
- accessibility, availability, and acceptance of the tools
- tools that are not appropriate for the population under study and that do not accommodate their needs
- screening that may identify children and adolescents in need of mental health support when there is none available
- identifying children and adolescents with mental health conditions when they may face stigma, exclusion, or marginalization
- fear and lack of trust of both those screening, and those who are screened and their families
- complex issues around consent, autonomy, information sharing, and data management
- identifying mental health conditions in communities that may lack support to engage and deal with them

\[Source: Salamanca-Buentello et al.\]
