## Cognitive development

## Infancy and toddlerhood

Select each tab to learn about cognitive development up to the age of 2 years.

#### 4–5 months

The infant becomes interested in their environment.

#### Around 12 months:

[[symbolic-thinking]] is the ability to use symbols, words, or objects to represent something that is not physically present. The toddler points to pictures in response to verbal cues and begins to comprehend [[object-permanence]] (peek-a-boo). They also engage in trial-and-error learning.

#### 15 months

Imitating behavior is a developmental milestone where toddlers begin to copy the actions and gestures of adults or older siblings during play. This can include a variety of behaviors such as waving, clapping, or mimicking facial expressions. They may also start to use objects correctly, like pretending to talk on a phone or brushing their hair.

#### 2 years

The toddler can complete simple puzzles.

## Pre-school

The following are characteristics of cognition during this development stage:

**Egocentric thinking**: Children at this stage find it difficult to see situations from perspectives other than their own.

**Illogical reasoning**: Their thought processes are not yet logical or operational. They tend to make conclusions based on intuition or superficial appearances.

**Magical thinking**: This is where children believe that their thoughts can influence the world (e.g., thinking hard about something can make it happen). It reflects their imaginative capabilities and the beginnings of abstract thought.

**Poor understanding of time and sequencing**: Young children are still grasping the concepts of past, present, and future. Their sense of time is more immediate, and they often do not understand the sequence of events well.

**Memory and suggestibility:** While children can remember events, their memories can be reshaped by suggestions from others. This is why eyewitness testimonies from children can be unreliable without proper interviewing techniques.

**Artistic expression:** Their primitive drawings reflect their motor skills and cognitive understanding.


## Childhood

### **5–8 years**

Children develop [[concrete-operational-thinking]], which allows for more accurate perception and memory of events. They become capable of rational and logical thought and start to reflect on themselves. During this stage, they recognize that others may have different perspectives, but they cannot fully understand or accommodate those views.

### **8–10 years**

Their cognitive abilities expand to distinguish between a person’s behavior and their underlying intent.

###   
**10–11 years**

They can consider other people’s viewpoints, indicating a deeper understanding of the mental states of others and a more sophisticated [[theory-of-mind]].

## Adolescence

Adolescents develop the ability to think abstractly, reason logically, and use deductive reasoning to solve problems.

**11–13 years**: The adolescent is interested in the present and has limited thoughts about the future. They may not fully connect knowledge, action, and consequences until their mid-20s.

They can think about thought, and they can be introspective and reflective.

At this age, they have insight, take another person’s perspective, and understand multiple perspectives.
