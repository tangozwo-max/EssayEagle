## Evaluating change

![[Pasted image 20260419140802.png]]

Evaluation of interventions is the final step in the standard clinical approach to mental health care. Understanding the factors that contribute to successful implementation is essential for ensuring these interventions are effective. Gee et al. conducted a systematic review to identify factors that influence the successful implementation of psychological interventions in schools and colleges.

## Factors that affect the implementation of school-based interventions

Gee et al. arranged the factors that affect implementation into 11 themes using the ecological framework for effective implementation, which is based on Bronfenbrenner’s ecological systems theory.

This framework emphasizes the importance of multiple levels of influence on behavior and development.

### Intervention characteristics

For interventions to be successful, they must be **acceptable** and **practical**. Acceptability depends on whether the intervention is perceived as helpful, enjoyable, appropriate, and well-designed, which, in turn, influences attendance and engagement. Group delivery can enhance acceptability by fostering peer support and social connections, though it may be a barrier for some students due to behavioral issues or discomfort with sharing personal experiences. Ensuring interventions match adolescents’ needs and preferences is key to achieving high acceptability.

Successful implementation also relies on the flexibility of interventions to fit within school routines, such as limiting session lengths and scheduling around academic activities.

### Organizational capacity

**Relationships between staff and intervention facilitators**

Positive relationships between intervention facilitators and school staff are key for successful implementation. Effective communication and integration into school systems are especially important for external facilitators.

**Support of school leadership**

Support and involvement from senior school leaders are essential for the successful implementation of interventions. Their backing ensures necessary resources and wider school system support. Conversely, a lack of endorsement from senior leaders can hinder implementation and sustainability.

**School environment**

Logistical issues, such as scheduling conflicts and lack of appropriate spaces, are major barriers to implementing psychological interventions in schools. The suitability of the wider school environment for therapeutic work also impacts success. Despite these challenges, successful implementation can improve the school climate and increase teacher support for counseling services, potentially creating a positive feedback loop.

### Training and technical assistance

**Quality of training and ongoing supervision**

High-quality training and supervision from experienced experts are essential for maintaining the fidelity of intervention delivery. More intensive training is needed for less experienced staff. Informal support from peers also aids successful delivery, but protecting staff time for supervision requires sustained funding and leadership support.

**Suitability of intervention manual and other materials**

Clear and user-friendly intervention manuals, along with high-quality supporting materials, facilitate successful implementation. Well-structured, manualized interventions help novice facilitators maintain treatment fidelity. User-friendly technology also enhances acceptability and engagement.

## Provider characteristics

According to Gee et al., many interventions are delivered by diverse school-based staff, with some facilitated by external specialists. Successful implementation relies on skilled facilitation, with trained school professionals generally delivering interventions effectively.

However, these professionals, often less experienced with specific interventions, sometimes struggle compared to specialist mental health staff. While external specialists might ensure better implementation, there are concerns about the sustainability and cost-effectiveness of this approach.

Concerns were raised about the feasibility of identifying suitable students for interventions. While school-wide screenings and comprehensive recruitment strategies by research teams were effective, they were considered unsustainable outside research contexts. Recruitment through school staff referrals was often effective, but some studies highlighted issues with staff confidence and time constraints in identifying students with depression symptoms.

## Community factors

### **Stigma and mental health literacy**

Gee et al. also note the impact of stigma on intervention implementation, potentially explaining lower student participation and challenges in obtaining parental consent.

However, not all studies found stigma to be a barrier. Crisp, Gudmundsen, and Shirk reported that participants rarely viewed stigma as a problem. Additionally, several authors noted that school-based interventions were seen as less stigmatizing than conventional mental health treatments.

### **Priorities of health and education systems**

Several studies highlighted the importance of aligning health care and education system priorities to implement school-based mental health interventions successfully. A significant barrier identified was the lack of adequate resource allocation for mental health services in schools, reflecting a low prioritization of these issues.
