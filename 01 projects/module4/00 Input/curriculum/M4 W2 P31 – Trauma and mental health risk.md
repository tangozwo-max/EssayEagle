Why is experiencing trauma a risk factor for mental health?

## Terminology

The terminology around trauma varies depending on your context.

"[[trauma|Trauma]] refers to the way that some distressing events are so extreme or intense that they overwhelm a person’s ability to cope, resulting in lasting negative impact."

\[Source: UK Trauma Council\]

The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, fifth edition (DSM-5 ), defines **[[trauma|trauma]] relating to [[ptsd|PTSD]]** as exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence.

Exposure to a traumatic event includes:

- directly experiencing the traumatic event
- witnessing the events as it occurred to others
- learning that the traumatic event happened to a close family member or friend
- experiencing repeated or extreme exposure to aversive details of the traumatic event

\[Source: Lebow\]

For **post-traumatic stress (PTSD)** diagnosis, exposure to trauma must be accompanied by re-experiencing, avoidance, and hyperarousal.

## Intergenerational trauma

[[intergenerational-trauma|Intergenerational trauma]] is a form of [[trauma|trauma]] that occurs when traumatic effects are passed across generations without exposure to the original event.

\[Source: Isobel et al.\]

**Complex trauma** involves exposure to multiple, often interconnected traumatic experiences. It also encompasses survivors' challenges while adapting to or coping with these experiences. These adverse events typically start in early childhood, persist over time, and are caused by others—often within significant attachment relationships. Complex trauma can intersect with broader societal issues, affecting marginalized communities.

An example of complex trauma could be a child who experiences prolonged abuse and neglect by their caregivers. This child might face repeated physical, emotional, and possibly sexual abuse over several years, combined with neglect, where their basic needs for food, safety, and emotional support are not met.

\[Source: Complex Trauma Resources\]

Having experienced trauma affects the way a person views themselves, the world, and relationships. The person has adapted to situations with an inordinate level of stress, which might affect how they behave under more ordinary circumstances. For example, they might act in a way that looks like they are overreacting to others, or seem overly aggressive or overly passive.

If they consistently behave in ways that others see as unwarranted or inappropriate, they might not be accepted into peer groups, or teachers and other adults might withdraw support. Then they might face social isolation, or experience educational difficulties.

Without friends or supportive adults, children miss out on important social interactions, which are important for gaining confidence and social skills as they grow up. Later on, as an adult, they might find it difficult to find employment or seek support or services.
