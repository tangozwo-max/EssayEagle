**Read this excerpt about resilience.**

"The resilience of an individual child... depends on the operation and interaction of many other systems, both within the child (immune system, stress response system, etc.), in relationships or family resilience, or in the larger sociocultural and ecological systems in which that child’s life and development are embedded."

\[Source: Ungar, Theron\]

[[resilience|Resilience]] is about doing well even though you are exposed to [[risk-factors|risk]] or facing adversity. It involves adjusting to negative stress or life events, adapting to risk, and recovering from trauma.

[[resilience|Resilience]] is a reflection of the complex interactions between

- the nature of the risk or adversity affecting the child
- the qualities and experiences of the child
- the qualities of the child's relationships and environment

The quality of [[resilience|resilience]] is not innate to an individual. Strengths-based interventions that focus on [[protective-factors|protective factors]] (often called resilience-building) may help children cope with everyday stresses. However, those who care for or provide services to children should focus on improving the child’s environment so that [[resilience|resilience]] is unnecessary, rather than helping the child build [[resilience|resilience]] to manage adverse conditions.

> [!-info] -info
> **Note**: The term *resilience* is sometimes considered contentious because it suggests individuals should cope with situations that others or institutions could fix.
> 
> Children’s resilience is also complex due to their limited agency. Unlike adults, children often cannot change negative situations like abuse or neglect. However, when they can act, such as reporting bullying, it is important that they do not see this as a lack of resilience.

Traditionally, positive mental health outcomes have been associated with adaptive psychological systems (e.g., self-regulation, and coping strategies). However, resilience research shows that sustainable change requires robust social and physical systems (e.g., family support, housing, natural environment).

In high-adversity contexts, where resilience is most relevant, individuals with more resources demonstrate greater resilience than those without. In this context, “more resources” refers to having access to additional support, advantages, or assets that can help an individual cope with adversity. These can include social support networks, financial stability, education, and good physical health. Essentially, having more resources means having a stronger foundation to navigate challenges and maintain mental well-being.

\[Source: Ungar, Theron\]
