There are several ways in which [[risk-factors|risk factors]] can be conceptualized. The [[lifecycle-approach|**lifecycle approach**]] provides a model that chronologically maps relevant [[risk-factors]], from the period before the conception of one generation to the next generation.

[[distal-proximal-factors|**Distal factors** are non-specific factors that affect the likelihood of subsequent risks and **proximal factors** negatively affect the individual directly.]] Distal and proximal factors interact in a chain of probabilities, which is influenced by frequency of occurrence, context, and timing.

### Before conception

Risks to mental health that arise before conception include the circumstances around conception, short intervals between pregnancies, and consanguinity (having children with a close relative).

Genetic risks at this stage involve passing on genetic conditions that impact mental health, such as Huntington's disease.

###   Prenatal and perinatal periods

Maternal health, both physical and mental, is a key factor in an individual's mental health risk. Factors that affect maternal health during the prenatal and perinatal periods include:

- access to adequate prenatal care
    
- maternal adaptation to pregnancy and caring for a newborn
-

Lack of quality maternal care and support, high-risk pregnancy, and perinatal maternal morbidity are risk factors associated with this period.


### Infancy and early childhood

Secure attachment during infancy and early childhood is an important protective factor for mental health. Risk occurs when caregivers cannot or do not provide a nurturing and stimulating environment.

Some children with neurodevelopmental conditions, or behavior that does not reflect expectations for their age or stage of development, require support during infancy and early childhood in the form of early intervention. Lack of access to such support is another mental health risk factor.

  
### School age

Strained relationships with family members or peers can contribute to stress, anxiety, and depression. Poor educational experiences, lack of support, or ineffective teaching methods may affect students’ well-being. Challenges related to cognitive, emotional, or behavioral development can also impact mental health.

  
### Adolescence

Like younger children, adolescents need positive relationships with family, peers, and teachers, and those with neurodevelopmental conditions require ongoing support to achieve the best possible outcomes. Additional risks during this period include risk-taking behavior, early sexual activity, and substance use.


### Life-long risks

Life-long risks to mental health include:

- genetic risk factors
    
- physical and mental health conditions in the child or family
    
- deficiencies in the psychosocial or educational environment
    
- exposure to harmful substances or toxins
    
- exposure to violence, abuse, or neglect
\[Source: Kieling et al.\]
