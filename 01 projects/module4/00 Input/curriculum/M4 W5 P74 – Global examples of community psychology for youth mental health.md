## Case study

### MAC-UK

MAC-UK is an example of community psychology in action. Founded on the principle of taking mental health support out of traditional clinical settings and into the community, MAC-UK works directly with young people who are often excluded from mainstream services. This approach aligns with community psychology’s emphasis on understanding individuals within their social contexts and addressing broader societal factors that impact mental health.

MAC-UK employs a model called INTEGRATE, which involves co-producing mental health interventions with young people and local stakeholders. This model ensures that the services are relevant and accessible to those who need them most. By working with young people in their own environments, MAC-UK addresses issues such as social exclusion, inequality, and the stigma associated with mental health problems.

The organization also focuses on building the capacity of communities and professionals through training and collaborative projects. This includes employing individuals with lived experience and drawing on evidence-based psychological practices to create sustainable change. MAC-UK’s work highlights the importance of addressing the root causes of mental ill-health, such as social, economic, and health policies that contribute to exclusion and inequality.

Ireland

In the Republic of Ireland, the Jigsaw program, established in 2006, operates 13 centers that provide psychosocial services for youth aged 12–25. The program features a Youth Advisory Panel of volunteers, aged 16–25, who engage in discussions about decision-making, leadership, service quality, and meeting service-user needs. These volunteers also participate in advocacy activities in the centers and the broader community.

An evaluation study involving 2,420 participants, aged 12–25, who completed the Clinical Outcomes in Routine Evaluation (CORE-10 and YP-CORE) questionnaires, reported a significant decrease in mental distress. Additionally, a study conducted across 12 Jigsaw centers found that 510 parents of users expressed high satisfaction with the service and noted positive outcomes for their children.7

Netherlands

In the Netherlands, the Dutch National Youth Council (_Nationale Jeugdraad_, or NJR) was established in 2001 to empower youth aged 12–30 to actively participate in reducing stigma around mental health.

The NJR engages young people in political activities, encouraging them to share their experiences through videos and reports that highlight their needs within the community and schools. These efforts have led to the presentation of key documents at European Union conferences, outlining the main barriers youth face in engaging with society.

Inspired by the Australian Headspace model, the NJR’s initiatives include advocacy, collaboration with mental health professionals, and training for educators. The program also offers social skills groups, short-term therapy, and mentoring projects.

Singapore

In Singapore, the Community Health Assessment Team (CHAT) was established in 2009 to address mental health issues among youth aged 16–30. CHAT engages in various community and media initiatives to reduce stigma and raise awareness about mental health services through creative avenues like theater, arts, exhibitions, and filmmaking.

The team provides:

- peer support training workshops
    
- mental health information
    
- care coordination
    
- referrals to additional services.
    

Notably, a significant portion of referrals (73.9%) comes from the participants themselves, highlighting the program’s accessibility and trust within the community.

Since 2014, the CHAT Ambassadors Program has empowered young volunteers to lead projects and campaigns aimed at enhancing service quality, advocacy, and user participation. By 2021, 55 ambassadors had contributed to redesigning service facilities, creating a youth center, developing a website, and organizing forums for service evaluation.

Australia

Headspace National Youth Mental Health Foundation, established in 2006 by the Australian Government Department of Health, operates over 100 centers across Australia. These centers provide integrated support for adolescents and young adults aged 12–25, collaborating with schools and social services. They also host community events like concerts and gaming sessions. The program nurtures young leaders who offer feedback and support and advocates to reduce stigma. Evaluation studies show high satisfaction among users, with nearly half reporting reduced mental distress after treatment.
