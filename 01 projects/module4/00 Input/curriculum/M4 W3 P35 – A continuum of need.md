Most mental health disorders arise before adulthood, which makes early detection and intervention in childhood and adolescence important. This week we will be looking at [[mental-health-promotion|mental health promotion]] and interventions that can be used at various stages on a [[continuum-of-need|continuum of need]].

### Public mental health

[[public-mental-health|Public mental health]] refers to the organized efforts and informed choices of society, organizations (both public and private), communities, and individuals. It contributes to the well-being of populations by promoting mental health and preventing mental health disorders.

The goals of public mental health interventions are:

- preventing mental health disorders: by addressing [[risk-factors]] and promoting [[protective-factors]], public mental health aims to prevent mental health disorders from arising in the first place.
- reducing impacts and inequalities: it seeks to minimize the negative effects of mental health conditions and address disparities in access to care.
- promoting mental well-being and resilience: public mental health focuses on enhancing positive mental health and building [[resilience]].
- supporting effective interventions: it facilitates the delivery of [[evidence-based-practice|evidence-based treatments]] for existing mental health disorders.

> [!-info] -info
> **Further info:** Early intervention has been shown to be effective at preventing mental health conditions from developing.

## A continuum of need

A continuum of need is a framework that categorizes the varying levels of support or intervention required by individuals or families in different circumstances. People may move up and down the levels as their circumstances change. It is widely used in the United Kingdom by health, social care, and education services.

### Universal

[[universal-prevention|Universal]] is the base level of the continuum of need. It contains services that are available to all without any specific assessment of need. The focus is on promoting well-being and preventing escalation of needs.

An example of a universal service might be health and wellbeing lessons in schools.



### Selective and indicated

Selective and indicated services are sometimes known as **targeted** or **early help** services. They consist of support for individuals who have additional needs under the [[social-determinants|social determinants of health]] and who are at risk of developing more complex needs. A diagnosis is not required for this level of support. The focus is on timely interventions to prevent conditions from worsening.

An example of a selective service might be one that is targeted at young people who have been identified as being anxious about school.


### Specialist

[[specialist-services|Specialist]] interventions cover three phases:

1. acute phase: providing specialist interventions to alleviate symptoms before formal diagnosis
    
2. identification phase: where those in need of more specialized mental health services are identified
    
3. recovery phase: supporting people through recovery over the long term
    

Some people may require specialist services to meet their severe, enduring, and complex needs. These people need to be identified and have a diagnosis to access services. There may be multi-agency involvement in their support, and it may involve statutory interventions.

An example of a specialist service might be one targeted at young people who are self-harming.
