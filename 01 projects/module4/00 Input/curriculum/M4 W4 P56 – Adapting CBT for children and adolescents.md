As you learned in a previous module, **cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)** is a form of psychotherapy (talking therapy) that aims to change patterns of thinking and behavior. CBT is based on the idea that people's thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are interconnected and that changing negative thought patterns can lead to changes in feelings and behaviors.

 ![[Pasted image 20260419132829.png]]

CBT is a flexible, nonstandardized approach that combines various behavioral, cognitive, and problem-solving techniques tailored to an individual’s needs. It provides clinicians with a range of tools to help develop more functional cognitive, emotional, and behavioral skills, adapting techniques based on the child’s development and specific case formulation.

CBT helps children develop both behavioral and cognitive skills through problem-solving methods. The therapy considers the broader context, including the family and other factors that might contribute to the child’s difficulties. These factors are recognized and addressed during therapy.

\[Source: Graham, Reynolds\]

In cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) programs for children, the initial phase involves **psychoeducation**, where the child and their family are informed about the presenting problem, the cognitive model, and the therapy process. This phase clarifies the general relationship between cognitions, emotions, and behaviors, and develops a formulation that conceptualizes the child’s specific issues within the CBT framework.

## What is the evidence concerning using CBT with young people?

### Under 7
It was previously assumed that children under 7 could not handle the cognitive and verbal demands of CBT, but research shows that even very young children understand that people have internal mental states and can articulate their thoughts by age 5. This suggests they can engage with CBT techniques earlier than previously thought.

Scheeringa et al. found that while young children struggled with understanding rating scales and emotional graduations, they could complete most CBT tasks, especially with visual aids like cartoons and drawings. Freeman et al. and Hirshfeld-Becker et al. showed that modifying CBT to include concrete examples, stories, play, and games significantly improved young children’s engagement and understanding of CBT concepts.

\[Source: Wamser-Nanney et al.\]

When conducting CBT with young children, it’s important to use developmentally appropriate and creative methods, such as visual aids, games, and stories, while limiting complex verbal explanations. Parents play a key role, often participating in sessions or being trained to use CBT techniques at home, which helps address both the child’s and parent’s behaviors and cognitions.

### Age 7–11

Many randomized trials have shown CBT to be effective for children aged 7–11, though details on age-specific adaptations are often lacking. Despite this, a meta-analysis by Durlak et al. found significant benefits for this age group, with adolescents benefiting even more. When CBT techniques are adapted to match children’s developmental levels, they can effectively engage in and benefit from the therapy.

CBT for children should be engaging and use a mix of verbal and nonverbal techniques, including visual aids like drawings and cartoons. Children often need guidance to identify their thoughts, which can be facilitated through therapist-led discussions, visual activities, and metaphors. Parents play an important role in supporting their child’s CBT process.

Metaphors like an “anger volcano” help children understand and manage their emotions. Play and games make CBT concepts engaging and nonthreatening, using activities like problem-solving tasks, sorting games, quizzes, and role-play to teach and practice coping skills.

### Adolescents

Early studies assumed adolescents had the necessary skills for CBT, but recent research emphasizes adapting CBT to their developmental levels. Nonverbal materials and technology, such as computerized CBT programs, are effective tools. Adolescents benefit from both cognitive and behavioral techniques, with some preferring complex cognitive work and others finding behavioral experiments more helpful.

CBT with adolescents often emphasizes their autonomy and self-efficacy, typically involving minimal parental participation. However, the level of parental involvement should be agreed upon at the start and reviewed periodically. Increased parental involvement may be necessary at different stages, such as during behavioral experiments or when addressing risks like suicidal ideation.

\[Source: Stallard\]

Two key aspects of CBT with adolescents are objective empiricism and collaborative partnership.

### **Objective empiricism**

Adolescents often hold egocentric views, making it counterproductive to directly challenge their beliefs. Instead, therapists should use a Socratic dialogue to help adolescents critically appraise their views by reflecting on new or contradictory information.

### **Collaborative partnership**

Therapists promote a collaborative relationship where the adolescent takes an active role. This involves using the adolescent’s vocabulary (without sounding false) and encouraging their contributions in setting goals, planning sessions, and determining practice tasks. This highlights their strengths and promotes self-efficacy.

## Reciprocal CBT formulation

A reciprocal CBT formulation is a way to understand and address interpersonal and relational problems by looking at how more than one person's thoughts, feelings, and behaviors, influence each other in a reciprocal manner.
