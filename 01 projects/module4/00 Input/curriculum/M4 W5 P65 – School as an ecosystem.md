A child or adolescent and their teacher form a microsystem, as does the child with their class and the child with their immediate family.

![[Pasted image 20260419140020.png|502]]
These systems interact and influence each other. This can be seen directly; for example, during parent-teacher meetings, or indirectly, such as when parental separation affects school relationships. A child’s dependence on their teacher or isolation from peers illustrates these connections. These interconnected systems are **mesosystems**.


![[Pasted image 20260419140034.png]]

The **exosystem** is the system of connected social structures that influence the development of students without direct contact.

![[Pasted image 20260419140049.png]]

The **macrosystem** includes the culture and society that influences the child and school, the family's socioeconomic status, and government policies that affect school and children, such as policies regarding exams and assessments.

![[Pasted image 20260419140102.png]]

The **chronosystem** includes an individual’s timeline and the broader socio-historical context. For example, starting school impacts a child differently based on their age and the historical context. Major events like a global pandemic or technological advancements and their timing in a person’s life, also influence development.

________

Different levels of school organizations are interconnected, with classrooms, grade levels, departments, and administrative bodies influencing each other. For example, a new school policy can impact classroom practices and student experiences.

These connections are often bidirectional; schools may adapt conduct or bullying policies in response to incidents.

Additionally, schools can pose unique risks, such as bullying or academic pressure. They can also provide protection through supportive teachers, inclusive policies, and a positive school climate.

Effective practitioners, including educators, counselors, and administrators, can intervene at various levels within the school system. They can implement classroom strategies, influence school-wide policies, and engage with the community to support students’ development and well-being.

## Team in the school

![[Pasted image 20260419140118.png]]

The **team in the school** refers to all members of management and staff that have responsibilities for mental health, and pastoral care.

### Educational psychologists
are responsible for:

- consultation and advice
- assessment
- intervention
- professional development and training
- research
- strategic development

They also have statutory responsibilities to provide advice for children and young people (CYP) with special educational needs and disabilities (SEND) and contribute to education, health, and care plans (EHCPs).

### Speech and language therapists

Speech and language therapists work directly with students, their families, and other education professionals to develop personalized strategies that support individual needs.

### Senior Leadership Team

The senior leadership team (SLT) in a school typically includes key leaders responsible for various aspects of a school's operation or strategic direction. The composition of the SLT might vary depending on geography, size, and type of school, but generally includes a principal or head teacher, vice-principal, or deputy head teacher, school governor, department heads, and possibly year heads.

A designated safeguarding lead (DSL) is a member of the school's SLT responsible for overseeing and managing all aspects of safeguarding and child protection in the school. The DSL's responsibilities include:

- ensuring the school’s safeguarding policies are up-to-date and effectively implemented
- providing training and support to all staff on safeguarding issues
- acting as the main point of contact for external agencies
- maintaining detailed, accurate, and secure records of all safeguarding concerns
- providing support to students experiencing safeguarding issues

### Special educational needs and disability coordinator (SENDCO)

Special educational needs and disability coordinators (SENDCOs) coordinate provision for social, emotional, and mental health. The SENDCO might also be part of the SLT.

### Teaching and pastoral care staff

All staff should have at least basic safeguarding training.

Teaching assistants in primary schools assist with additional learning needs

In many schools, various staff members have pastoral care responsibilities, including classroom teachers, tutors, and grade-level coordinators.

### School nurses

School nurses are often the first point of contact for children experiencing emotional or psychological difficulties. By providing a safe and confidential environment, school nurses can identify early signs of mental health issues and offer immediate support or referrals to appropriate services. They work closely with teachers, parents, and mental health professionals to create a comprehensive support network for students. School nurses can also deliver health education programs that promote mental well-being, resilience, and healthy coping strategies.

## Team around the school (TAS)

A TAS refers to a multidisciplinary support team focused on promoting the well-being of students in a particular school. It unites professionals from different fields, including mental health, education, social services, and sometimes healthcare, to provide coordinated support for students' mental health and emotional needs. The main goal is to address challenges students may face holistically by creating a network of support that includes both the school environment and external services.

School outreach teams

School outreach teams provide support from specialist neurodevelopmental and behavior support teams. Linked Child and Adolescent Mental Health Services (CAMHS) practitioners offer advice on managing or referring children and adolescents, including consultation, co-working, or liaison. This may involve targeted or specialist mental health staff working directly in schools, medical services, and with voluntary sector providers, families, and carers.

  
Social services

Social workers and family liaison officers often collaborate to address social factors affecting students' mental health, like family challenges, socioeconomic concerns, or trauma.


Charities

Charities and independent organizations contribute through mentoring and third-sector counseling services.


Mental health practitioners

This can include school psychologists, therapists, or counselors who offer more specialized mental health interventions.

Schools should have dedicated contact with targeted or specialist mental health specialists or targeted services (UK).

  
Healthcare providers

When relevant, health professionals (such as pediatricians or community health workers) may be involved to offer medical insights that impact mental health, such as managing chronic health conditions that may affect school performance or attendance.
