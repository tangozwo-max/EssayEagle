A **whole school approach to mental health (WSA)** means developing and implementing policies and practices to promote the mental health and well-being of pupils, parents and caregivers, and staff.

## Principles of a whole school approach

Public Health England and the Department of Education developed eight principles of a whole school approach to promoting mental health and well-being. These principles are designed to create a supportive and inclusive environment for students.

### Ethos and environment

The physical, social, and emotional environment in schools significantly impacts the health, well-being, achievement, and behavior of staff, pupils, and students. Schools should be supported to foster a culture of respect. The guidance emphasizes individualized responses to student needs, recognizing that behavior may stem from various vulnerabilities.

A trauma-informed approach can create a safe environment for those with adverse experiences. Strong relationships between staff and students promote well-being and a sense of belonging. Resources for supporting children with disabilities and chronic illnesses should also be available.


  
### Curriculum teaching and learning

School and college programs focused on social and emotional learning can significantly enhance young people’s academic progress and overall well-being. These skills are often developed through a dedicated personal, social, health, and economic (PSHE) curriculum, which includes statutory content on relationship and sex education, and health education.

The curriculum should be delivered in a carefully sequenced manner. By the end of primary school, pupils should recognize mental health issues and know how to seek support, while secondary students should understand their emotions and where normal variations end. Complementary lessons in physical education, the arts, and citizenship can support this learning. Engaging lessons that are practically relevant to students’ lives, along with regular assessments, help ensure effective learning. Specific times during the academic year can focus on skills for transitions or exam pressures, and addressing locally relevant issues.

### Student voice

Involving students in decision-making can enhance their mental health and well-being by fostering a sense of belonging and control. Individually, it helps them to build self-belief, make healthy choices, and develop independence. Collectively, it provides opportunities to influence decisions, express views, and build strong social networks.


  
### Staff development

Schools can demonstrate commitment to staff health and well-being by assessing staff needs, supporting their well-being, and promoting work-life balance. Staff training is essential to increase knowledge of mental health, and to equip staff to identify and address student concerns.

  
### Identifying need and monitoring impact

Formally identifying what students need helps schools make better decisions about what support to provide. It also helps track how effective the support is. Schools can use different tools—from simple surveys to detailed assessments—to understand and plan for students’ mental health and well-being needs.


### Working with parents and carers

Families significantly influence children’s mental health and well-being. Effective interventions that support parenting and family life, including education on drugs, alcohol, and sex, can provide social and economic benefits. Schools should communicate their mental health support to parents and carers and work with local authorities to ensure families are aware of additional support available in their area.


  
### Targeted support

Some children and young people are at higher risk of poor mental health, such as those in care, young carers, and those living with mental health disorders or domestic violence. Delays in addressing their needs can impact their future well-being and potential.

The Department for Education provides guidance to schools so they can offer targeted support. New Mental Health Support Teams offer early intervention and help schools implement a whole school approach. School-based counseling and school nurses also support students’ mental health, provide early intervention, and are trusted adults for students.


### Leadership and management

Implementing an effective whole school or college approach to mental health and well-being requires coordinated change and collective responsibility from all staff. A senior leadership team must champion these efforts to ensure they are accepted and embedded. It is important to Identify a senior mental health lead to ensure strategic implementation. Leaders play a key role in advocating for the needs of children and young people and influencing local planning and commissioning. Commitment to mental health should be integrated into improvement plans and policies, involving the whole school community.

### Teams in schools

Establish a school mental health implementation team. Schools can form a school mental health team, as a part of the school-health system, which may include school leaders, teachers, school-based mental health staff, and special education teachers.

The school mental health team also needs to develop a network of connections within a school’s local community, which might include local health services, families, civil society organizations, and youth groups.

### Identifying needs

Profile existing needs, resources, and systems. Schools can conduct a mapping exercise to identify gaps between needs and current resources, including accessibility. This will be relevant for identifying students who may benefit from health services. It can also guide the selection of curriculum materials, such as those that help build mental health literacy and reduce stigma around people with mental health conditions. Additionally, it may encourage consideration of the school’s social and physical environments in promoting well-being.

### Partnerships

Partner with policymakers and the community. Schools are required to maintain cooperative engagement with governments and related stakeholders, as, beyond ensuring that standards and regulations are implemented, access to resources and personnel is also needed.

The UK's National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) whole schools framework is a comprehensive approach designed to support the social, emotional, and mental well-being of students and staff in primary and secondary education.

NICE recommends that schools consider the value of trauma-informed approaches and neuro-affirming practices. Trauma-informed practice is the deliberate creation of school environments that are sensitive to the neurobiological, psychological, and relational impact of adverse childhood events and developmental trauma on students.

\[Source: NICE\]

Aims of trauma-informed care include:

1. the realization of the pervasive effects of trauma
2. recognizing the signs of trauma in students, staff, and parents and caregivers
3. responding by integrating knowledge about trauma into policies, procedures, and practices
4. avoiding re-traumatizing children and young people

\[Source: Pote et al.\]

### Scope

A school health program can be comprehensive in its scope. The 1994 multi-tiered mental health model introduced a comprehensive range of interventions, from universal health promotion and prevention to targeted support for students with mental health issues, addressing diverse populations and needs.![[Pasted image 20260419140516.png]]





### Focus

A school-based health program can be comprehensive in terms of focus. WHO's health-promoting schools program views comprehensive health promotion as addressing a range of health topics, including mental health. Schools respond to community health priorities, such as nutrition, physical activity, injury prevention, and substance use.

### Approach

The third concept of comprehensive school health is a holistic framework addressing health, including mental health, to improve students’ educational outcomes. It takes a whole-school approach, promoting overall health, well-being, and educational attainment.
