This process is a structured approach that aims to ensure comprehensive care and effective outcomes.

### Assessment 
involves gathering comprehensive information about the child or adolescent’s mental health through interviews with the child, parents, and sometimes teachers. It also includes using questionnaires and standardized tests to evaluate symptoms and behaviors, as well as observations in school.  
  
Assessments consist of:

- clinical interviews
- questionnaires
- reports from significant others
- observations
- neuropsychological assessments (to gain further information about specific cognitive processes or to help understand behaviors, for example, if a person seems to be struggling with memory or cognition)
	> [!-info] -info
	> **Note**: Medical examinations are not typically standard in routine mental health care. However, they may be necessary in psychiatry appointments for prescribing medication, ruling out medical conditions, or for weighing in cases of eating disorders.

### Formulation
is the process of making sense of the assessment data by considering the young person's experience holistically. It involves understanding their context, including family dynamics, social environment, and developmental history, and creating a hypothesis about how these factors interact to affect their mental health.

### Intervention
Based on the formulation, a tailored intervention plan is developed. This plan can include therapeutic approaches like cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), play therapy, or family therapy, as well as medication if necessary, prescribed by a psychiatrist. Supportive measures like school accommodations or social skills training to develop relationship skills may also be included in the intervention.

### Evaluation
involves assessing the effectiveness of the intervention. Regularly monitoring the child’s progress is performed in several ways, including:

- questionnaires that track symptoms over time at weekly appointments
- questionnaires at the start, midpoints, and end of therapy to monitor change

Evaluation includes adjusting the intervention plan as needed based on feedback and observed changes, and using outcome measures to determine if the goals of the intervention have been met.

> [!-info] -info
> **Note**: In this week's learning, you will learn about mental health disorders that commonly affect children, and evidence-based interventions used to treat these disorders. It is important to note that formulation is an essential step that occurs between assessment and intervention.

## The 5Ps model of case formulation

The 5Ps model provides a structure for developing a formulation using information gathered from a detailed and holistic assessment. This model was developed within the context of adult cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT).

Select each marker to learn more about each of the 5Ps.
![[Pasted image 20260419130240.png]]

### Presenting difficulties

**Presenting difficulties** include thoughts, feelings, and behaviors, e.g., avoiding crowds.

What difficulties is the person experiencing and how do these difficulties affect the person's life?

### Precipitating factors

**Precipitating factors** are important events or conditions that happen just before the start of or worsening of the current issue.

What has triggered this particular reaction?

This can include significant events preceding the onset of the disorder, such as substance use, or interpersonal or school-based stressors.

### Protective/positive factors

**Protective (or positive) factors** are strengths or support systems that might mitigate the impact of the mental health disorder on the person’s life.

These can include social support, skills, interests, and some personal characteristics.

### Perpetuating factors

**Perpetuating factors** are situations or dynamics that maintain or worsen the condition.

What is keeping these difficulties going?

These can include ongoing substance use, repeating behavioral patterns (including avoidance or withdrawal), biological patterns (such as insomnia or hypersomnia), or cognitive patterns such as attentional biases, memory biases, or hypervigilance.

### Predisposing factors

**Predisposing factors** refer to internal or external historical factors likely to have increased a person’s vulnerabilities.

What has happened to the young person in the past that might be playing a role in their current difficulties?

This comprises identifying possible biological contributors (e.g., organic brain injury and birth difficulties), genetic vulnerabilities (including family history of mental health difficulties), environmental factors (such as socioeconomic status, trauma, or attachment history), and psychological or personality factors (including core beliefs or personality factors), which may put a person at risk of developing a specific mental health disorder.


\[Source: Macneil et al.; Anna Freud\]

A full psychological formulation would integrate the information from assessment with a specific psychological model (for greater validity and explanatory power).

For example:

- psychodynamic formulation would include a focus on unconscious processes within the individual and an emphasis on transference/countertransference
- a full CBT formulation would have an increased focus on learned behaviors and the role of thoughts
- systemic formulation would consider the role of social systems and the narratives and stories told within families in relation to the presenting difficulty
