## Key learning points

You have completed this section. Review each key learning point before you progress.

- Attachment refers to the emotional bond that develops between an infant and their primary caregiver.
- Attachment theory describes the importance of attachment relationships, particularly the long-term connections formed during early childhood between infants and their primary caregivers, which influence relationships throughout life. John Bowlby was a pioneer in attachment theory.
- Secure attachment is characterized by the child’s search for comfort and protection from their caregiver in times of distress (safe haven), a desire to stay close to them (proximity maintenance), using the caregiver as a base for exploration (secure base), and experiencing distress during separation (separation anxiety).
- Insecure attachment arises when caregivers fail to provide consistent comfort and security, leading to maladaptive behavior patterns and difficulties within relationships.
- Insecure attachment can lead some children to exhibit anxiety and aggression upon reunion after separation; others avoid caregivers, and some respond inconsistently, seeking and rejecting care unpredictably due to mixed signals of fear and comfort.
- The Still Face Experiment, developed by Dr. Edward Tronick in 1978, powerfully demonstrates a baby’s distress and attempts to re-engage when their mother maintains an unresponsive expression. This highlights the importance of emotional connection for a child’s development.
- When individuals face illness or mental health challenges, the stress and experience of vulnerability trigger their attachment system. Their interactions with health and social care services tend to mirror their attachment styles. Healthcare professionals can foster healing by providing a secure base and reshaping internal models for healthier attachment bonds.
