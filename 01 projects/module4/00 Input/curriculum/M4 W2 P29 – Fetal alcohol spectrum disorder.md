Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorder (FASD) refers to a range of conditions that can occur in a person whose mother drank alcohol during pregnancy. The symptoms of fetal alcohol spectrum disorder are variable and depend on which part of the brain was developing at the time it was exposed to alcohol. Other factors influencing FASD presentation include the quantity and frequency of alcohol intake, maternal nutrition, genetics, and maternal and fetal alcohol metabolism.

\[Source: Ungerer et al.\]

Fetal alcohol spectrum disorder can sometimes be diagnosed by distinct facial features, which include:

- short palpebral fissure
- thin upper lip
- smooth philtrum

In FASD, congenital abnormalities in the structure, size, growth, and function of the brain and central nervous system (CNS) can lead to a wide range of issues across multiple domains.

These include:

- general intelligence
- motor skills
- language
- academic achievement
- memory
- attention
- affect regulation
- adaptive behavior

Individuals with FASD may also have difficulties with hyperactivity, impulsivity, social cognition, and communication.

> [!-info] -info
> **Note**: The developing brain is very vulnerable to toxic substances. Teratogens, like alcohol, certain medications, recreational drugs, tobacco, and chemicals, can cause birth defects. Except for certain anti-epileptic medications, the evidence base for other teratogens is not as consistent as that of alcohol. the evidence base is hampered because many teratogens act at an early stage of brain development and likely trigger spontaneous miscarriage, often before the pregnancy is even known.

FASD is an under-diagnosed disorder for several reasons, including the:

- lack of standardized diagnostic approaches and training
- lack of confidence discussing alcohol exposure during pregnancy
- reluctance to diagnose FASD in case it is unhelpful
- overshadowing of prenatal alcohol exposure by other causes of neurodevelopmental delays, such as environmental factors or exposure to other substances
