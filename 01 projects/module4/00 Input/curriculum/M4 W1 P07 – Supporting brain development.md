Key points for supporting brain development

- When infants and children have [[attachment-styles|secure attachments]], they have more opportunities to engage in play, which enables learning and brain development.
- The most important thing parents can do to support the brain development of a young child is to get to know the child, learn their cues, and engage in [[serve-and-return]] (aka, reciprocal) interaction.
- The best games for brain development provide a sense of emotional safety that enables learning. Learning takes place in the context of play (exploration, figuring out, developing mastery) and requires a safe environment.
- Attunement to children means taking into account their individual needs. For example, an autistic baby may have different preferences for types of play and sensory experiences than a neurotypical baby.

## Encouraging brain development

In the UK, the [[nspcc|National Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children (NSPCC)]], uses a set of metaphors to help explain child brain development. These tools use simple language that can be understood by professionals, parents, carers, and children.

### Brain architecture

This metaphor ([[brain-architecture-nspcc]]) compares brain development to the construction of a house, emphasizing the importance of laying a strong foundation in the early years to support healthy development later on.

The following are key points of the brain architecture metaphor:

- Brain development is an active process.
    
- Positive early experiences lay the foundation for future health, learning, and behavior.
    
- There are two sensitive periods of development—early childhood (0–5 years) and adolescence (12–25 years).
    
- Interventions outside of the sensitive periods can support positive brain building—it is never too late.
    

### Serve and return

This metaphor is about the interactive process between children and their caregivers. Similar to a game of tennis, the serve and return of engagement help build and strengthen brain circuits.

### Stress

Managing the stress in a young person’s environment is also important for brain development. While positive stress can be manageable and promote growth, [[toxic-stress]] can be damaging to brain development. [[toxic-stress|Toxic stress]] refers to the excessive or prolonged activation of the body’s stress response systems in the absence of protective relationships.

This type of stress can profoundly impact brain development, particularly in young children. When a child experiences toxic stress, it can disrupt the formation of neural connections, leading to difficulties in learning, behavior, and health.

Later in this module, you will learn how chronic exposure to toxic stress can alter brain architecture, affecting areas responsible for memory, emotional regulation, and executive function.

### Look, say, sing, play

[[look-say-sing-play|Look, Say, Sing, Play]] is a campaign and set of resources designed to help parents and carers engage in brain-building activities with their babies and toddlers. The initiative emphasizes the importance of everyday interactions in developing a child’s brain from birth.

- **Look**: Making eye contact and observing your child helps build attention and strengthens your bond.
    
- **Say**: Talking to your child, even before they can speak, helps develop their language and communication skills.
    
- **Sing**: Singing songs or rhymes can be soothing and it also supports memory and the learning of new words.
    
- **Play**: Playing with your child stimulates their imagination and encourages problem-solving skills.
    

### Air traffic control

The brain’s [[executive-function]] helps manage the flow of information, similar to how air traffic control manages planes at an airport.

**Executive function** refers to a set of skills that underlie the capacity to:

- plan ahead
    
- prioritize
    
- control impulses
    
- follow multi-step directions even when interrupted
    
- stay focused and filter out distractions
    

Executive functions develop throughout a child's early years and into their early twenties.

[Source: Garlovsky]

\[Source: UNICEF\]

Executive function skills are developed through experience and practice.

The foundation of [[executive-function]] is laid during infancy when babies first learn to pay attention. At this age, they practice executive function skills through their attachment relationships. For example, playing peek-a-boo can build the early foundations of working memory and self-control.

Adults can help young children develop executive function skills by:

- establishing routines
- breaking big tasks into smaller chunks
- encouraging games that promote imagination, role-playing, following rules, and controlling impulses

\[Source: NSPCC Learning\]

### Working memory

[[working-memory]] is a foundational construct of [[executive-function]]. Working memory determines how a child uses, processes, and remembers information. Working memory informs tasks such as learning, reasoning, and comprehension, and acts like a mental workspace where information is stored and processed for short periods.

Children with neurodevelopmental disorders may use more of their working memory to process information. For example, children with auditory processing issues must exert extra effort to listen and remember classroom instructions. Similarly, children with attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) need to work harder to stay focused and organized. These challenges with working memory can lead to mistakes, difficulty following directions, and forgetting tasks like homework and chores. Understanding these struggles can help teachers and parents provide the necessary support and assistance.

\[Source: Child Mind Institute\]

Where parents and teachers do not understand the impact of working memory difficulties, they may misinterpret a child's behavior as lazy or as evidence that the child is not motivated to do well, which in turn can impact the child's well-being further.
