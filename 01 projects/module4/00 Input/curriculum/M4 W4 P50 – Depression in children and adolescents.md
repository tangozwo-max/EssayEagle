Depression is a major contributor to the global disease burden. Although the prevalence of depression is low in children, it increases sharply in adolescents, especially for females.

Furthermore, the prevalence of depression has increased significantly in later adolescence over the past two decades. In this group, the prevalence of depression is particularly high in people who have special educational needs, chronic physical ill health, and low socioeconomic status.

## Why is it important to treat depression in adolescents?

### Risk of recurrence

Depression in adolescence and early adulthood is a risk factor for recurrence later in life. About 50% of adolescents who experience depression will have another episode, and they are nearly three times as likely to have depression in adulthood compared to their peers without adolescent depression.

Risk factors for recurrence include:

- being female
    
- having multiple depressive episodes in adolescence
    
- parental mental health disorders
    
- family history of recurrent depression
    
- chronic interpersonal stress
    
- poor peer relationships
    
- comorbid anxiety disorder
    

### Associations with other mental health disorders

Adolescents with depression have an increased risk of developing other mental health disorders, including later anxiety disorders and increased rates of conversion from depression to bipolar affective disorder. Symptoms of depression are also associated with eating disorders.

### High-risk behaviors

Depression in young people is linked to high-risk behaviors such as substance use and unsafe sexual practices. It is also associated with self-harm and suicidality.

  

### Psychosocial functioning

Depression in adolescents has significant psychosocial impacts that extend into adulthood. Young people with depression often experience lower educational attainment and higher rates of welfare dependence and unemployment compared to their peers without depression. Additionally, depression in adolescence can lead to future interpersonal difficulties, such as challenges in marital functioning, increased loneliness, and a greater need for social support. Conversely, preventing the onset or recurrence of depression during adolescence is associated with better adult functioning, highlighting the importance of early intervention and support.

### Physical health

Physical health outcomes associated with depression in young people include increased risk of cardiovascular disease and obesity. Depression is also associated with somatic symptoms.

## What depression looks like in young people

###   

- Younger children commonly present with more somatic complaints.
    
- For children generally, depressive symptoms may present more as irritability than sadness.
    
- The impact is often seen in academic performance, social interactions, and family relationships.
    

###   

- Symptoms resemble the _Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition_ (DSM-5) criteria more closely as children become older teenagers.
    
- Symptoms typically include a depressed mood most of the day, nearly every day.
    
- Symptoms must cause significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.
    

**Note**: Clinicians need to consider developmental stages when diagnosing children and adolescents, as depressive symptoms can vary with age.

## Managing depression in adolescents

In many countries, guidelines for treating depression in children and adolescents up to age 18 suggest a stepwise approach. The following approach outlines the UK recommendations.

Mild

For mild depression in adolescents, the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) recommends:

- **lifestyle changes**, such as improving sleep and diet and reducing drug use
    
- **psychoeducation**, consisting of support and information for the child and family
    
- **"watchful waiting**," or monitoring the child for up to 4 weeks to see if symptoms improve without intervention
    

If needed, psychological therapies such as cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) and interpersonal therapy (IPT) are recommended to treat mild depression in the community.

Moderate

Specialist care is usually indicated for moderate to severe depression and for young people with comorbidities or suicide ideation. For moderate depression, treatment with evidence-based psychotherapy or combined treatment with medication is recommended first. There is little evidence to favor one psychotherapy over another.

Chronic or severe depression

For chronic or severe depression, a combination of medication and psychotherapy is recommended, after ruling out bipolar disorder in the patient or their first-degree relatives.

- If the young person does not respond within 4–6 weeks, they should be reassessed for other contributing factors. If no psychosocial contributors are found, the person should be offered combined treatment if they were on psychotherapy alone.
    
- If a person on combined treatment does not respond within 4–6 weeks, it is reasonable to try an alternative psychotherapy or increase the dose of antidepressant.
    
- If there is still no response, switching to another antidepressant is reasonable.
    
- For partial responses to combined treatment, augmenting with another drug is a reasonable next step.
