## Social development

## Infancy and toddlerhood

Select each image to learn about social development during the first 2 years.
### Infancy

Social development during infancy is dependent on attachment relationships. The infant settles when soothed by a parent or caregiver.

### Around 5 to 6 months

**Around 5 months**: The infant responds to social stimuli and shows emotion through facial expression.

**Around 4–6 months**: The infant begins to share focus on an object or event ([[joint-attention]] with caregiver).

**Around 6 months**: The infant recognizes familiar faces.

### 9 months to 2 years

**9–12 months**: The infant engages in social interaction and plays games (i.e., pat-a-cake).

**Around 12 months**: The infant experiences stranger and [[separation-anxiety]], and mostly engages in solitary play.

**2 years**: The toddler mimics adult behavior. They engage in parallel play and symbolic play. The toddler explores away from their safe base and returns to seek comfort from their caregiver.

## Preschool

During the preschool years, children engage in cooperative imaginative play, learning to take turns and share in the creation of stories and adventures. They begin to experiment with social roles, often imitating adults and exploring different identities through play. A desire to please adults emerges, and they may feel guilty when disobedient, indicating a developing awareness of "good" and "bad" behavior.

## Childhood

As they transition into childhood, their understanding of how behavior affects others deepens. They recognize that their actions can have positive or negative impacts on those around them. Friendships are situation-specific, often formed around shared activities or interests, and may change as circumstances evolve.

**6–9 years**: The child engages in play with peers who share similar interests, which fosters a sense of belonging and helps them practice social roles. As they grow, there’s a noticeable shift from fantasy play to more structured activities, reflecting their enhanced understanding of the real world. Preschoolers begin to grasp the concepts of right and wrong, which is evident in their rule-based behavior and play. They learn to adapt their behavior to different situations, demonstrating flexibility and social awareness. Additionally, their interactions are guided by a desire to avoid punishment and engage in self-interested exchanges.

**9–10 years**: The child starts to negotiate rules.

## Adolescence

**12–14 years:**

During adolescence, young people distance themselves from their parents as they seek to establish their own identity, often aligning more closely with their peer group. This realignment can lead to rejection of parental standards and increased conflict with parents, as the adolescent begins to test boundaries and challenge established rules.

Social dynamics become increasingly important, with status often being tied to group membership and social acceptance. Adolescents may feel pressure to conform to specific traits or roles within their peer group. This period is also characterized by an ambivalence about sexual relationships. Exploratory behavior is a hallmark of this stage, as adolescents seek to understand themselves and their place in the world.

**15–17 years:** Friendships are based on loyalty, understanding, and trust, and one-to-one relationships are based on intimacy rather than interests. The adolescent makes conscious choices about who to trust and may be sexually active.
