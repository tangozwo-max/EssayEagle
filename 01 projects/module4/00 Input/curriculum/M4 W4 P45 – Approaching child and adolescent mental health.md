Throughout this course, you have learned about the multifactorial determinants of mental health, and in this module, you have looked in depth at risk and protective factors that are especially relevant to children and adolescents.

Many common mental health disorders present initially in childhood or adolescence. This module focuses on the most prevalent—anxiety, depression, and behavioral disorders. Other disorders that initially present in youth, such as eating disorders and psychosis, require more specialist interventions. As you have already learned, neurodevelopmental conditions like autism and attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) are not mental health disorders, but they can cause distress and mental health needs if learning and well-being needs are not met.

Consider the following discussion between multidisciplinary team members, who have come together to discuss several cases involving child and adolescent mental health.

**Person 1:** For me, one of the things I don't like to hear is that teenagers are just being dramatic. It's important to take child and adolescent mental health seriously.

**Person 2** Yes, I agree. Teenagers can be dramatic, and we're aware of the biological and developmental changes that are going on, but it's always important to remain curious, and to ask what else is going on.

**Person 3**: Mental health is also affected by so many factors and systems. Adults are often treated individually in a way that doesn't work with children. Their well-being is so dependent on the family and school systems around them. We need to make sure we understand this impact and, wherever possible, get them involved as part of a team working towards better outcomes for the young person.

**Person 4**: Also, the adults in the child's sphere are often affected by what's going on with the child, especially if the child has a serious problem, like self-harm or suicidality. In my experience, the adult might panic in these situations, and they need to be supported. We need to ask, "What do the parents and school need to remain calm and feel confident and supported in their approach to keep the child safe?"

**Person 3**: It's also about the services. They need to be designed in ways so children can access them, which means they need to be trauma-informed, neuroaffirmative, and appropriate to the developmental stage of the child.

**Person 2:** I know what you mean. Many services see children up to 18 years old, but a 5-year-old will need a very different type of support than a 15-year-old.

## Learning outcomes

On successful completion of this week, you will be able to:

- analyze the stages of a formulation-based approach to child and adolescent mental health
- evaluate the importance of case formulation in child and adolescent mental health
- examine cultural factors that are particularly important when engaging with children and adolescents
- outline the most common mental health disorders, including depression, anxiety, and behavioral disorders
- outline potential types of psychological interventions for children and young people
