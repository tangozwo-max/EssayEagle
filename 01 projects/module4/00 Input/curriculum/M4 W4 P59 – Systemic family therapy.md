Family and systemic psychotherapy, also known as family therapy, is a form of psychotherapy that views individuals as part of interconnected systems, such as families or other close relationships. This approach focuses on understanding and improving the dynamics within these systems rather than just addressing individual issues.

These disorders often arise and are maintained within the context of problematic family relationships and can be alleviated by developing more supportive and functional family dynamics. Systemic therapy helps families create healthier relationships and effective problem-solving routines, roles, rituals, and narratives.

### Evidence for systemic family therapy

Since the 1970s, reviews and meta-analyses have consistently shown that systemic therapy is as effective as, or sometimes more effective than, individual therapy for mental health and relationship problems. Meta-analyses, which synthesize results from multiple studies, have been particularly influential in establishing this evidence base.

Systemic therapy has been shown to yield medium to large effect sizes, meaning about two out of three cases improve with this approach. This effectiveness is comparable to individual therapies for both child-focused and adult-focused problems. The accumulated evidence supports the use of systemic therapy and has important implications for service development policy. However, more specific research is needed to guide practitioners in their work with specific problems across different life stages.

In a systematic review and meta-analysis of 16 studies, Facompré, Bernard, and Waters (2018) found that various systemic interventions effectively reduced disorganized child-parent attachment in at-risk families with infants under 4 years old. The mean effect size was 0.35, indicating that the average treated case fared better than 64% of control group cases.

The interventions included:

- **child-parent psychotherapy**: weekly sessions for about a year, helping mothers resolve ambivalent feelings about their infants by linking these to their own adverse childhood experiences and current life stresses
- **attachment and bio-behavioral catch-up**: psychoeducation and coaching to improve child-parent interactions
- **video-feedback intervention to promote positive parenting**: video feedback and coaching to enhance attachment security
- **circle of security**: psychoeducation and strategies to avoid replicating problematic parenting practices

These interventions varied in intensity, with child-parent psychotherapy being the most intensive, suitable for high-risk families with histories of adversity and high stress levels. The other three programs were less intensive, involving 4–20 sessions.

Research shows that effective therapy to reduce the risk of further physical child abuse and neglect is family-based and typically lasts at least 6 months, often involving home visits. It addresses parenting skill deficits, children's post-traumatic adjustment, and family support.

Effective approaches include cognitive behavioral family therapy, parent-child interaction therapy (PCIT), and multisystemic therapy (MST).

In trials, cognitive behavioral family therapy outperformed routine services in reducing abuse risks among school-aged children. The 16-session Alternatives for Families program teaches parents and children skills for managing anger and communication through separate and joint sessions.

A meta-analysis by Kennedy et al. found that PCIT significantly reduced physical abuse and parenting stress. This therapy involves joint parent-child sessions with coaching to strengthen attachment and manage behavior, typically lasting 14–16 sessions.

MST studies indicated significant improvements in parenting skills and family functioning, along with fewer out-of-home placements, using tailored intervention plans and regular supervision for therapists with small caseloads.
![[Pasted image 20260419133438.png]]
Trauma-focused cognitive behavioral therapy (TF-CBT) is effective in reducing symptoms of post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) and improving overall adjustment in children who have experienced sexual abuse, as well as their non-abusing parents. A systematic review of 33 trials found that TF-CBT was more effective than standard care, making it the best-supported treatment for children following maltreatment.

TF-CBT involves concurrent sessions for abused children and their non-abusing parents, either in group or individual formats, with periodic joint sessions.

The therapy for children includes exposure to abuse-related memories to help them habituate, training in relaxation and coping skills, learning assertiveness and safety skills, and addressing issues related to victimization, sexual development, and identity. Concurrent sessions with non-abusing parents focus on helping them develop supportive and protective relationships with their children and build their own support networks.

A systematic review and meta-analysis of 17 randomized controlled trials on children and adolescents with disruptive behavior disorders found that systemic interventions, such as parent training and family therapy, had small but significant effects compared to alternative treatments, with effect sizes between 0.26 and 0.36. Parent training programs, particularly for preadolescents, are strongly supported, with several evidence-based models showing effectiveness, including the Oregon model of parent management training and parent-child interaction therapy.

For adolescents with conduct disorders, multiple family therapy programs have also been empirically validated. A meta-analysis of 24 studies demonstrated that brief strategic family therapy, functional family therapy, multisystemic therapy, and multidimensional family therapy were all effective, with effect sizes ranging from 0.21 to 0.26. Additionally, another meta-analysis confirmed that these family therapy models remained effective up to 2.5 years post-treatment.

![[Pasted image 20260419133452.png]]
A comprehensive review of systematic reviews and meta-analyses indicates that family therapy is highly effective for treating anorexia nervosa in adolescents. Key findings include weight restoration, with 50–66% of adolescents achieving a healthy weight after treatment. Long-term follow-ups (6 months to 6 years) show that 60–90% fully recover, with only 10–15% remaining seriously ill. The relapse rate after family therapy is low compared to moderate rates for individual therapies and high rates (25–75%) associated with inpatient treatments.

For adolescent bulimia, family therapy has also shown positive results. It is more effective than supportive therapy and leads to quicker recovery compared to CBT, although long-term outcomes for both therapies are similar.

Family therapy involves 15–20 sessions, starting with separate meetings for adolescents and parents to motivate engagement. This is followed by conjoint sessions focusing on supervising mealtimes to disrupt the binge-purge cycle while supporting healthy eating habits.
