### Child factors

Risk factors in this category are those that are internal or individual and include, for example, the child's temperament and genetic background. They can also be factors that involve the child's interaction with family, peers, and education.

### Physical health and development

The following factors increase the likelihood of a child being at risk of mental health conditions:

- complications around the birth and consequent concerns about early development
- cancelled or infrequent health care appointments
- sudden changes in physical health
- frequent presentation for medical attention
- inadequate nutrition
- speech, language, and communication difficulties
- concerns about hygiene, which might affect esteem or development, or cause isolation
- poor or disturbed sleep
- one or more incidents of self-harm

### Emotional well-being and relationships

The following factors increase the likelihood of a child being at risk of mental health conditions:

- insecure attachment relationships
- reactive temperament
- adverse experiences, such as maltreatment, abuse, or neglect
- low self-esteem
- difficulties recognizing, articulating, and managing emotions
- behaviors which impact on health, well-being, development, and relationships
- risky behaviors
- inability to manage changes commensurate with peer capabilities
- pressures at home, such as young carer responsibilities
- difficulties with relationships in the wider family
- few or no positive community links
- isolation or rejection within their peer group
- belonging to a socially disadvantaged or socially excluded group
- lack of a safe adult who can provide support in times of distress


### Education and school-related factors

The following factors increase the likelihood of a child being at risk of mental health conditions:

- frequent lateness and absence from school
    
- restlessness, inability to attend to instructions, difficulty concentrating or executing tasks
    
- significant or persistent changes in behaviors that adversely affect relationships
    
- learning differences whereby the child feels unsuccessful
    
- difficulty progressing in different areas of education

## Family factors

As you learned previously, the quality of the relationship between a young child and their caregiver is an important factor in determining the child's future development and mental health. Other family-related factors involve resources, mental health awareness and coping, a family history of mental health conditions, and the caregivers' ability to support the child's development.

As with many other risk factors, those related to family are complex and interrelated.

### Peer factors

Peer risk factors might relate to the child's feeling that they do not belong to or are not accepted into a positive peer group. Within peer groups, risks might include bullying, substance use, and risky or illegal activities.

There can be many reasons a child finds it difficult to approach or engage with peers. They might be vulnerable due to past traumatic experiences, or it could be related to a neurodevelopmental condition.

Social exclusion for any reason is a risk factor for mental health conditions because it can affect self-esteem and limit opportunities for learning and development.


### Social and community factors

Social and community risk factors often relate to resources that enable learning, growth, and positive social interaction. War, poverty, social deprivation, and lack of services contribute to social and community risk factors.

### Professional factors

These risk factors relate to the quality of care the professional staff provide to support the child. In this category, risk factors include:

- inexperienced, unqualified, or inadequately trained staff
    
- professionals working without adequate supervision
    
- crisis management rather than proactive care
    
- lack of staff availability for example due to unmanageable workloads

### Organizational factors

Systemic factors within organizations can significantly affect the care and services provided to children. For example, implicit bias within an organization, such as a healthcare provider’s bias towards people with certain demographic characteristics, can limit a child’s access to necessary services.

Additionally, high thresholds for intervention, such as requiring extensive documentation before providing mental health support, can delay or prevent children from receiving timely care. An example of an organization where these factors might be observed is a public health clinic that requires multiple referrals and extensive paperwork before a child can see a specialist.

### Government factors

Government factors influence health, including mental health, in ways you have studied throughout this course. Providing universal health care, reducing poverty and inequality, and improving access to health and educational resources all impact the long-term mental health of children.
