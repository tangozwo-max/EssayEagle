Gillick competence and Fraser guidelines are legal judgments used to assess whether a child under 16 can consent to their own medical treatment without parental permission.

#### Gillick competence
refers to a child's maturity and understanding to make informed decisions about their health care.
Gillick competence relates to a young person's ability to consent to medical treatment. Those aged 16 and over are generally presumed to have the capacity to make informed decisions about their health care. For those under 16, they can consent to their own treatment if they are assessed as having sufficient understanding of what is involved. Typically, a parent or carer consents on behalf of the young person, but if they wish to proceed without this consent, their competence must be evaluated by a trained professional.

This assessment considers the young person's age, maturity, mental capacity, understanding of the treatment and its implications, and ability to make and communicate a reasoned decision. Gillick competence helps balance respecting young people's wishes with ensuring they have the necessary understanding to make informed choices. It is primarily used in medical settings but can also apply to other areas like seeking therapeutic support or making decisions about living arrangements. If a young person is deemed Gillick competent, their parents or carers will not be informed about the treatment unless there are serious concerns about the young person's welfare.
#### **Fraser guidelines**
specifically apply to decisions regarding contraception and sexual health, ensuring that the child comprehends the implications and that the treatment is in their best interest.
The Fraser guidelines are a set of criteria that healthcare practitioners use to determine whether a young person can receive medical advice or treatment in relation to sexual health and contraception without parental consent:

1. The young person cannot be persuaded to inform their parents or carers about seeking advice or treatment, nor to allow the practitioner to inform them.
    
2. The young person understands the advice being given.
    
3. The young person's physical or mental health, or both, are likely to suffer unless they receive the advice or treatment.
    
4. It is in the young person's best interest to receive the advice or treatment without parental consent.
    
5. The young person is very likely to continue having sex with or without contraceptive treatment.

> **Note:** Practitioners should always encourage a child to inform their parents or carers about the decisions they are making. Gillick and Fraser guidelines should be considered if the young person still wishes to proceed without their parents' or caregivers' knowledge or consent.

Potential child protection concerns should always be considered when using Fraser guidelines for issues relating to sexual health:

- Underage sexual activity is a possible indicator of child sexual exploitation, and children who have been groomed may not realize they are being abused.
- In the UK, sexual activity involving a child under 13 should always result in a child protection referral as children of this age are deemed legally not to have capacity. Child protection referrals should always be considered for children aged 13–15 (as 16 is the legal age for sexual activity).
- If a young person presents repeatedly about sexually transmitted infections or the termination of pregnancy, this may be an indicator of child sexual abuse or exploitation.
