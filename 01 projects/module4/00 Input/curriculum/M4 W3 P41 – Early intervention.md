## Early intervention

Early interventions are often universal, but some are targeted at specific populations, such as children and young people who have risk factors for developing mental health conditions or children and young people who are displaying some symptoms of mental health conditions. You will learn more about therapies in the week on common mental health disorders and treatments.

### Infants

Early interventions for infants tend to be centered around improving parenting skills, nurturing attachment, and reducing toxic stress such as exposure to conflict, displacement, family discord, and substance use.

**Parenting skills:**

There is a significant link between parenting skills and later behavioral problems. Parenting programs can improve parental mental health and support children's health and well-being.

**Attachment:**

Forming secure attachments to their caregivers is vital for an infant's healthy social and emotional development. Parental sensitivity in responding to care-seeking cues from infants is important. Attachment interventions seek to improve that sensitivity through educating parents and through providing feedback on the way they interact with their infants.

**Toxic stress:**

Whilst the goal of supporting families should be to remove the cause of stressors wherever possible, where this is not possible then interventions can help to mitigate the impact of stressors. Interventions that support children to develop healthy relationships with caregivers, friends, and their community all help with this as well.

\[Source: Wang et al.; Vseteckova et al.; Ataullahjan et al.\]

### Young children

Interventions targeted at school-aged children can include:

- behavioral-based parenting support
- child-centered play therapy
- psychosocial interventions
- socioemotional interventions
- art and music therapy
- positive psychology interventions
- meditation and mindfulness-based interventions
- self-regulation interventions (mainly for ADHD)
- sensory-based interventions (mainly for ASD)

\[Source: Hudson et al.\]

### Adolescents

Interventions for adolescents tend to fall into three categories: family/individual-based, school-based, and community-based.

**School-based:**

- solution-focused brief therapy
- cognitive behavioral therapy
- interpersonal psychotherapy

**Community-based:**

- creative activities
- environmental interventions
- person plus environmental interventions
- computerized CBT interventions

**Individual/family-based:**

- exercise-based interventions for depression
- media-literacy and advocacy-based interventions for eating disorders
- cognitive behavioral therapy
- evidence-based youth psychotherapies
- home-based multisystemic therapy
- day therapy: multimodal, multidisciplinary

\[Source: Das et al.\]

## A neuroaffirmative approach

**Read this excerpt about parent-mediated early intervention for children with autism:**

"Previous evidence from trials has suggested that early intervention can result in short-term symptom reduction in young children with autism spectrum disorder. We now show that a 12 month parent-mediated preschool intervention can produce sustained improvement in child autism symptoms and social communication with parents, which remained at nearly 6 years after the end of treatment. These findings support the potential long-term effects and value of early parent-mediated interventions for autism."

\[Source: Pickles et al.\]

Consider the excerpt above. It discusses how parent-mediated interventions for autism might improve child autism symptoms.

**What are the ethical dilemmas raised by such an approach to working with neurodivergent people?**

Within the autistic community, it is considered unethical to aim to reduce someone’s symptoms of autism. This is in the context of a much broader historical and political context. Previous interventions (such as applied behavior analysis, ABA) have been criticized for aiming to extinguish autistic traits, pathologizing nonharmful behaviors, and teaching children to mask autistic traits. Any intervention that aims to identify core autistic symptoms early to lessen or reduce them is seen as at odds with a neuroaffirmative approach.

The goals of a neuroaffirmative approach therefore center on what is most important for that child’s quality of life. In early interventions, they often include changing the environment around the child and supporting relationships with key individuals such as parents, without suggesting that their autism should be cured or removed.
