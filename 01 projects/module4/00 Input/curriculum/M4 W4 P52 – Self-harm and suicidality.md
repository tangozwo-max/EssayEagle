Suicide and self-harm among young people are significant public health issues. Nonsuicidal self-injury (NSSI) is defined as deliberate, self-inflicted harm without suicidal intent.

NSSI is a strong predictor of later suicide, so its detection is important for suicide prevention. NSSI has a pooled prevalence of 17.7% among adolescents, with higher rates in females (21.4%) than males (13.7%).

\[Source: Denton, Álvarez\]

Despite a global decline in suicide rates over the past 30 years, suicide remains a leading cause of death, particularly for those aged 15–29. For every suicide, many more individuals attempt or engage in self-harm. A prior suicide attempt is the most significant risk factor for future suicide, with the risk being about 50 times higher for those who have self-harmed, whether the self-harm was suicidal or not, compared to the general population.

![[Pasted image 20260419132255.png]]

\[Source: McEvoy et al.\]

Adolescents are particularly vulnerable to self-harm, with around 30,000 adolescents in the UK receiving hospital treatment for it each year. The risk of suicide increases with the number of self-harm episodes and is higher in those aged 10–24 compared to older age groups. Non-suicidal self-injury (NSSI) is common among adolescents and young adults (AYAs), with 16–18% of teenagers reporting having engaged in NSSI at least once.

\[Source: McEvoy et al.\]

## Risk and protective factors for self-harm in adolescents

McEvoy et al. conducted an umbrella review to identify the key risk and protective factors for self-harm in adolescents and young adults, as determined by systematic reviews.

Psychiatric/psychological and adverse life events (ALE) were the most frequently associated categories. Childhood abuse/neglect was the most identified risk factor.

Psychological and psychiatric

The three main psychiatric or psychological factors most strongly linked to self-harm are conduct or behavioral issues, personality disorders, and depression or anxiety.

Substance use/misuse and poor family relationships were also commonly associated with self-harm.

Psychiatric and psychological issues are major risk factors for self-harm in young people. Among the top 20 risk factors, 9 were related to these issues, including the 3 highest.

The review suggests that designing and implementing programs to prevent or reduce self-harm (and possibly suicide) in young people should focus on identifying those with psychiatric or psychological risk factors. Effective interventions often involve psychotherapeutic treatments like cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), dialectical behavioral therapy (DBT), or problem-solving therapy, delivered individually or in groups.

### ALE

Childhood abuse or neglect, particularly sexual abuse, was the most frequently identified risk factor for self-harm in young people, found in nine systematic reviews. Bullying and **adverse life events** (ALEs) or trauma were also significant, ranking third alongside other psychiatric illnesses (excluding depression and anxiety). These factors had the highest pooled odds ratios in the ALE category.

Psychological and ALE risk factors are closely related, as adverse experiences like physical abuse, sexual abuse, neglect, and witnessing domestic violence during childhood negatively impact mental health, leading to internalizing and externalizing symptoms, and increasing the risk of self-harm and suicide attempts.

Professionals working with young people should refer those who disclose these risk factors to mental health services, especially if they also show signs of mental health disorders. There are also opportunities for interventions within school and college settings, where key risk factors include bullying, peer conflict, exposure to self-harm, social isolation, behavioral issues, poor academic performance, and school avoidance.

### Behavioral

Substance use or misuse of tobacco, cannabis, alcohol, and illicit drugs is strongly associated with self-harm in adolescents and young adults (AYAs). It is the most frequently identified risk factor from the behavioral category. Research indicates that health risk behaviors, such as smoking, drinking, drug use, sexual risk-taking, aggression, and self-harm, often co-occur. Suicidal behavior is considered the “ultimate health jeopardizing behavior.” These behaviors may be ways for young people to manage emotions during internal and external changes. Professionals should implement cessation strategies and provide interventions and referrals for AYAs engaging in substance use, especially those with concurrent mental health disorders.

### Social

Poor family relationships and lack of friends were prominent risk factors for self-harm in adolescents and young adults. Other significant factors included lower socioeconomic status (SES), having a harsh or controlling parent, and experiencing discrimination or racism. The only pooled odds ratio (pOR) over 2 in this category was for being in foster care, with a pOR of 3.89. This means that among the various risk factors related to family and social relationships, being in foster care was the only one with a pooled odds ratio (pOR) greater than 2. Specifically, the pOR of 3.89 indicates that young people in foster care are almost four times more likely to engage in self-harm compared to those not in foster care.  

### Fixed

Fixed/physical risk factors were less common compared to other categories. Both being female and being lesbian, gay, or bisexual appeared in the review.

\[Source: McEvoy et al.\]

Young people may engage in self-harm for various reasons:

- to cope with extreme emotional distress
- to relieve stress
- to feel physical pain as a distraction from emotional pain
- to express emotions, such as hurt, frustration, or anger
- as a form of escape
- to regain control over emotions or problems
- to punish themselves
- to elicit care or attention from others
- to fit in with a peer group
- as a way of attempting suicide

\[Source: Trust\]

Protective factors for self-harm were identified much less frequently than risk factors, with only 5 out of 12 systematic reviews noting any protective factors. These reviews highlighted that good social, family, and friend support were the most frequently identified protective factors. Additionally, specific reviews reported that good sleep and strong school connectedness were associated with lower rates of suicide attempts.

\[Source: McEvoy et al.\]

## Evidence-based treatments

The evidence for psychosocial interventions in children and adolescents who engage in self-harm (SH) is currently uncertain due to the moderate or very low quality of available studies and the small number of trials. Dialectical behavior therapy for adolescents (DBT-A) shows promise and warrants further evaluation. Additionally, given its benefits in adults, individual cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)-based psychotherapy should be further developed and assessed for this younger population.

\[Source: Witt et al.\]
