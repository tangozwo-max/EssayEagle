## Gender-affirming care

Healthcare aims to provide inclusive care that is accessible, appropriate, timely, and of the highest quality for all people.

\[Source: BMA\]

In particular, there is contention around some aspects of gender affirmative care, primarily in relation to longer-term interventions for younger people. This is a current example of ethical decision-making in clinical practice, as the areas of care under discussion are both highly emotive and have little established clinical guidance.

Exploration of identity is a natural part of childhood and adolescence. In the UK, over the last decade, there has been a rapid increase in the number of children referred for support regarding their gender identity. This is particularly true for **gender dysphoria**, which is the incongruence between their expressed gender and the one assigned at birth.

Exploration of identity is a natural part of childhood and adolescence. In the UK, over the last decade, there has been a rapid increase in the number of children referred for support regarding their gender identity. For instance, the number of children and young people referred to the Gender Identity Development Service (GIDS) has risen markedly, with around 50 children being referred each week. This represents a significant increase from previous years. For example, in 2009-2010, only 94 children were referred over the entire year.

\[Source: Lee; BBC\]

Unsupported gender dysphoria can severely affect people's quality of life and mental health. The reasons for increased referrals to specific services are complex, involving biological, psychological, and social factors.

It can be difficult to tell from referral rates whether referrals have increased due to, for example:

- greater incidence/prevalence in the general population
- increased accessibility and provision of services
- clinicians learning that these services exist and so referring more

Data in this area is often poor quality and interpreted differently depending on the reader. Much more high-quality and unbiased research is needed.

> [!-info] -info
> **Note:** Gender dysphoria is not classified as a mental health condition. However, **gender incongruence**, defined as a marked and persistent difference between an individual's experienced gender and the gender assigned at birth, is a specific diagnostic term in ICD-11.
> 
> 1% of the population may experience some degree of gender incongruence.

Accessing high-quality care is crucial for transgender youth, who experience disproportionately high mental health needs, including depression, anxiety, and suicidal ideation and attempts. However, there is not always consensus among healthcare professionals and society on what constitutes the best care for this group of young people.

Many trans individuals and their allies advocate for the importance and right to early transition, which involves changing gender expression to align with their gender identity.

Other groups raise concerns about providing medical interventions with lifelong and potentially irreversible consequences for children and young adults. These interventions can lead to a range of physical, psychological, social, and emotional changes, and may involve various types of medical treatments to bring a person's physical characteristics more in line with their gender identity and expression.

Medical interventions for transgender youth include puberty blockers (gonadotrophin-releasing hormone analogs), hormone therapies, and surgery.

Puberty blockers work by suppressing the release of sex hormones such as testosterone and estrogen, thereby preventing the development of permanent sex characteristics.

While these interventions are well established for adults, their use in children and young people remains highly contentious. This debate involves balancing the mental health risks associated with gender incongruence against the immediate and often unknown long-term risks of physical interventions.

The debate surrounding National Health Service (NHS) gender identity development services in the UK has been particularly prominent, focusing on whether these services provide safe, holistic, and effective care for young people.

Concerns have been raised about the quality of care, leading to a national review by consultant pediatrician Hilary Cass.

Reviewing the safety and quality of national services is a standard practice to ensure high-quality healthcare. However, this particular review has been significant due to the emotive and political nature of the discussions among different stakeholder groups.

Healthcare professionals have expressed concerns that the strong and polarized political viewpoints within society and services hinder open discussions about these issues. Additionally, there has been controversy over the topic of **detransitioning**, or returning to one's birth gender. Debates focus on its prevalence, how well it represents the trans community's needs and experiences, and the clinical lessons that can be learned from it.

The role of healthcare professionals and those interested in mental health care is to understand the perspectives of different groups regarding this issue and discuss the political, clinical, and ethical issues raised in this debate. This ensures the contribution to the best quality care experiences and outcomes. All healthcare services are influenced by the societal beliefs and time in which they are provided. This debate has highlighted the importance of discussing politics within healthcare and where they intersect with clinical and ethical issues.
