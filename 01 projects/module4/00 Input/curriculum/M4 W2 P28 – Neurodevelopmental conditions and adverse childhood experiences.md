"[[child-maltreatment|Maltreated]] children are nearly ten times as likely as their non-maltreated peers to have three or more [[neurodevelopmental-conditions|neurodevelopmental conditions]]."

\[Source: Gajwani, Minnis\]

Gajwani and Minnis argue that children with both [[neurodevelopmental-conditions|neurodevelopmental conditions (NDCs)]] and [[adverse-childhood-experiences|adverse childhood experiences (ACEs)]] are placed in so-called [[double-jeopardy|"double jeopardy"]] of poor health outcomes. This means that these children are at a higher risk for poor health outcomes due to the combined impact of both factors.


![[Pasted image 20260417164942.png|245]]
The reasoning behind this double jeopardy theory is as follows:

- ACEs increase the risk of poor child health outcomes (especially child maltreatment).
- NDCs increase the risk of poor child mental health outcomes.
- ACEs and neurodevelopmental conditions often co-exist and can have cumulative effects on a child's mental health.

\[Source: Gajwani, Minnis\]

Child maltreatment and neurodevelopmental conditions often co-occur, and maltreated children are at higher risk of having multiple NDCs. However, Dinkler et al. concluded that maltreatment does not directly cause this increased neurodevelopmental load.

Additionally, adolescents who experienced both child maltreatment and neurodevelopmental symptoms at age 9 were twice as likely to develop severe psychiatric disorder symptoms, such as mania.

\[Source: Dinkler et al.\]

The potential to overlook neurodevelopmental conditions is particularly high if the child is experiencing adversity, and ACEs can delay the diagnosis of conditions such as autism and ADHD.

\[Source: Zhou et al.\]

> [!-info] -info
> Maltreated children should receive full neurodevelopmental assessments, and clinicians should be aware that children with multiple NDCs are at higher risk of maltreatment.
> 
> \[Source: Dinkler et al.\]
