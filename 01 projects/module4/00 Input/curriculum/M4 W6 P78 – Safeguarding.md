## Safeguarding

Safeguarding refers to the measures and practices put in place to protect individuals, especially children, young people, and vulnerable adults, from abuse, harm, and neglect. It involves ensuring that their health, well-being, and human rights are upheld, allowing them to live safely and free from harm.

In the context of children, safeguarding includes:

- protecting children from abuse and maltreatment
- preventing harm to children’s health or development
- ensuring children grow up with the provision of safe and effective care
- taking action to enable all children and young people to have the best outcomes

\[Source: SCIE; NHS England\]

In the UK, many children and young people from Black, Asian, and minoritized ethnic communities face racism, bias, stereotyping, and cultural misunderstandings. These issues can occur at various levels and may be conscious or unconscious. As a result, some children are more likely to come to the attention of child protection services, while others may not receive adequate support.

To ensure these children receive the necessary help and support, adults working with them should:

- understand the challenges these children face
- build trusting relationships
- take appropriate actions to keep them safe
- use a strength-based approach to empower parents and carers to protect their children

### Unconscious bias

Unconscious bias among adults working or volunteering with children can negatively impact their ability to identify and respond to child abuse. Bias can also lead to misinterpretation of behaviors based on ethnicity, such as viewing a child's fear as cultural respect rather than a sign of abuse. Additionally, stereotypes about who experiences certain types of abuse can cause practitioners to overlook risks to children who do not fit these stereotypes.

### Adultification

Adultification is a bias where children from Black, Asian, and minoritized ethnic communities are seen as more "streetwise," mature, and less vulnerable than other children. This particularly affects Black children, who may be viewed as threats rather than needing support. Such children might be perceived as understanding their actions and consequences better. For instance, practitioners often assume Black boys involved in gangs could protect themselves, leading to a lack of action to safeguard them from exploitation, violence, and substance misuse.

### Overlooking child protection concerns

Conscious or unconscious bias can cause professionals to take child protection concerns about children from Black, Asian, and minoritized ethnic communities less seriously than those about other children. This bias can result in dismissing certain behaviors or practices as cultural, thereby neglecting necessary protective actions.

Practitioners might hesitate to raise concerns to avoid appearing culturally insensitive or racist, which can lead to further neglect of potential issues. Additionally, some practitioners may adopt a "universal" approach, avoiding acknowledging race or ethnicity. This can hinder their understanding of the child's lived experiences and prevent them from identifying concerns.

### Criminal justice system

In the UK, Black and mixed-race children are disproportionately represented in the youth justice system. This disparity can be attributed to various complex factors, including the adultification of these children, leading to criminal justice responses rather than child protection.

Higher rates of school exclusion among Black Caribbean, Gypsy, Traveller, and Roma children contribute to long-term negative outcomes such as criminal exploitation, exposure to antisocial behavior, and mental health and behavioral disorders.

\[Source: NSPCC Learning\]

Published case reviews emphasize the importance of professionals considering families' cultural and religious contexts when conducting assessments and providing support. The rights and needs of the child must always remain the central focus of any intervention, irrespective of these contexts.
