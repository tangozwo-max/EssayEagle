## Attachment theory

Attachment theory focuses on the bond between a child and their primary caregiver, typically a parent, and how this bond influences relationships throughout the person's life. Insecure or problematic attachment can occur when this bond is disrupted or lost.

[[john-bowlby|John Bowlby]] (1907–1990) was a British psychologist, psychiatrist, and psychoanalyst known for his pioneering work in [[attachment-theory]].

Bowlby defined attachment as a "lasting psychological connectedness between human beings." He believed that the need to form strong bonds with specific individuals is intrinsic to human nature and necessary for survival.

\[Source: Barbour\]

## Stages of attachment

Bowlby proposed that the first 2 years of life are the most important for forming attachments.

Select each image to learn what happens during each stage of attachment.### Pre-attachment (Birth to six weeks)

Infants show innate behaviors like crying and smiling that attract caregiver attention, but they do not show a preference for a specific caregiver.

### Attachment-in-the-making (6 weeks to 7 months)

Infants begin to develop a sense of trust in familiar people who respond to their needs. They might start to show a preference to their primary and secondary caregivers, although they still accept care from strangers.

### Clear-cut attachment (7 months to 2 years)

A specific attachment to the primary and secondary caregivers develops, and the child seeks comfort and security from them. Children start to develop separation anxiety during this time.

### Formation of reciprocal relationships (Two years and older)

As children acquire language skills, they start to understand that caregivers return after separation, and they form more mutual relationships.

\[Source: [[nspcc|NSPCC Learning]]\]

## Secure attachment

Caregivers nurture secure attachments by responding to their children's physical and emotional needs and providing care predictably and consistently. Securely attached infants and children are comfortable exploring their environment, which provides ongoing learning opportunities.

Characteristics of secure attachment

- **Safe haven**: The child seeks comfort and protection from the caregiver when they experience distress or danger.
- **Proximity maintenance:** The child has an innate desire to be physically close to the caregiver.
- **Secure base**: The caregiver provides a sense of safety and stability, so the child can explore their environment with confidence, knowing they can return to the caregiver for comfort and protection when needed.
- **[[separation-anxiety]]**: The infant experiences distress when they are separated from the caregiver, even for short periods.

\[Source: Barbour\]

## Patterns of insecure attachment

When caregivers do not or cannot provide comfort and security to children during the early years, the child's neurological, emotional, psychological, and physical development can be negatively affected.

Insecure attachments can manifest in several forms:

- Some children show considerable anxiety when they are separated from their caregivers, but they do not become calm when they are re-united. Sometimes, these children might reject the caregiver after separation or, in more complex cases, become aggressive.
- Some children avoid their caregivers, especially after a period of absence. Although they do not necessarily reject caregiver attention, they do not seek it out or prefer a caregiver to a stranger.
- Some children inconsistently respond to their caregivers, sometimes avoiding or showing aggression, and other times responding positively. Research shows that this attachment style occurs when caregivers are sources of both fear and comfort.

\[Source: [[nspcc|NSPCC]]\]

Children with [[attachment-styles|insecure attachments]] might use disruptive behaviors to maintain their connection with their caregiver. These behaviors increase the need for the caregiver to pay attention to the child. However, if the child uses this strategy regularly, it may lead to maladaptive behavior patterns and difficulties within relationships.

The quality of the attachment relationship can significantly influence a child’s emotional development, particularly their ability to self-regulate their emotions.

\[Source: Bendel-Stenzel et al.\]

## Attachment and internal working models

According to Bowlby, early experiences with caregivers serve as the basis for **[[internal-working-model|internal working models]]**, which are mental representations of the self, others, and the nature of relationships. Internal working models guide thoughts, feelings, and behaviors in attachment situations and relationships throughout a person's life. Through these internal working models, the relationship between the child and caregiver serves as a prototype for future relationships.

\[Source: Bucci et al.\]

Children who have experienced maltreatment develop internal working models, or so-called "scripts," based on their experience of adults being unpredictable or unsafe. Changing the environment for these children and providing access to safer adults can help them develop new scripts, in which adults are loving and caring.

\[Source: Anna Freud National Centre for Children and Families\]

**[[mentalizing]]** refers to the ability to understand the mental states, such as thoughts, feelings, and intentions that underlie one's own behavior and the behavior of others. Mentalizing is essential for social interaction and communication because it enables the person to predict and interpret other people's behavior.

The ability to mentalize is influenced by early experiences and attachment relationships. When caregivers show that they understand, and can name a child's thoughts, feelings, and intentions, children then learn to internalize this knowledge about themselves and apply it to others. This enables healthy and positive social relationships.
