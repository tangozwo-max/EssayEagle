Anxiety disorders are highly prevalent among children and adolescents. Globally, about 6.5% of children are affected by anxiety disorders. In the United States, nearly 1 in 12 children aged 3 to 17 years have anxiety.

> \[Source: Kowalchuk et al.\]

Anxiety disorders affect educational and social functioning and create a risk of ongoing anxiety and other mental health disorders across the lifespan. They are associated with substantial economic burden. Even so, very few children with anxiety disorders access evidence-based treatments.

In the context of child and adolescent mental health, the following additional disorders are included among the most prevalent:

**Separation anxiety disorder** occurs when someone has an inappropriate fear of separation for their developmental stage, unlike the normal anxiety young children feel at daycare drop-off.

**Selective mutism** occurs when a person is consistently unable to speak in specific social situations where there is an expectation to speak, such as school, despite speaking in other situations.

## Emotionally based school avoidance

Historically, unauthorized school absences were labeled as truancy and seen as deliberate misbehavior. However, there is now a growing recognition of the impact of emotional and mental health on these absences. This shift is especially important since school absence rates have reportedly doubled since the COVID-19 pandemic.

\[Source: Anna Freud\]

**Emotionally based school avoidance (EBSA)** is a developing concept and refers to challenges in attending school due to emotional distress, including anxiety. EBSA is not a psychiatric diagnosis but frequently co-occurs alongside established mental health conditions such as anxiety and mood disorders.

> EBSA can result in an ongoing, cyclical process that makes it more difficult for the young person to attend or engage in school.
> ![[Pasted image 20260419132135.png]]
> \[Source: South Gloucestershire Council\]

Difficulties with attending or engaging in aspects of school are associated with a complex interplay of factors. The reasons for avoiding school include:

- avoiding uncomfortable feelings brought on by attending school, such as anxiety or low mood
- avoiding situations that might be stressful, e.g., academic demands, social pressures, and aspects of the school environment
- pursuing interests outside of school
- reducing separation anxiety or gaining attention from caregivers

\[Source: South Gloucestershire Council\]

Anxiety and difficulties in managing stress are common in autistic children and often worsen during adolescence due to more complex social interactions and increased awareness of their differences from neurotypical peers. Approximately 40% of autistic individuals have at least one anxiety disorder. Although research on the prevalence of autism and emotionally based school avoidance (EBSA) is limited, evidence suggests that autistic children and young people are at higher risk of EBSA due to their anxieties.

\[Source: Anxiety and Depression Association of America\]

For this reason, it is important to have inclusive school systems to prevent additional stress for children with neurodevelopmental conditions and provide alternative options when mainstream schools are not suitable.

## Evidence-based treatments

Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) is recognized as an effective treatment for anxiety in children and young people. However, there is insufficient evidence to support the effectiveness of other psychological treatments. While selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) are effective in the short-to-medium term, their long-term efficacy has not been proven. Brief guided interventions by nonspecialists can be beneficial for preadolescents, though their effectiveness for adolescents is less certain.

\[Source: Creswell et al.\]

Anxiety disorders in children and adolescents continue to be underrecognized and undertreated.
