Formulation in schools can be considered in two contexts:

- **organizational formulation**, which addresses the needs of the school as a whole
- **individual formulation**, which focuses on the needs of specific students

This process can lead to both indirect and direct interventions.

If needed, refresh your knowledge of [formulation approaches](https://learn.iheed.org/courses/758/modules/items/357135).

In schools, the content of a formulation typically includes information about school-specific factors, such as teacher-pupil relationships, exam stress, school engagement, and peer networks.

Sharing a formulation in a school setting differs from other contexts due to the organizational environment and the nature of indirect work and consultation. Issues of consent, confidentiality, and safeguarding are particularly important in this setting. The formulation may need to be understood by several staff members to ensure effective implementation.

Psychological interventions in schools can involve direct contact with students or indirect work with staff and other adults. These interventions may be tailored to the specific needs and goals of the school, or involve the delivery of more general therapeutic approaches within the school setting.

## Indirect interventions

Indirect interventions are those that do not include the child or young person or their families participating in sessions.

Select each heading to learn about indirect interventions.

### Consultation 
refers to discussion, often between school staff and an expert (in a particular model or practice). The practitioner offering consultation must be trained in the model or practice and have adequate supervision and support.

### Facilitating
reflective practice can be an important indirect intervention. Reflective practice refers to reviewing and reflecting on actions, decisions, and experiences. Unlike consultation, which usually occurs during a single session, reflective practice typically requires multiple sessions and ongoing commitment. It is often facilitated in group sessions by a psychological practitioner.

### Reflective practice
is intended to influence staff practice. Reflective practice can also contribute to staff well-being by providing opportunities for staff to consider the emotional impact of their work.

### Professional meetings
, which often involve a psychologist, social worker, teacher, and guidance counselor, focus on planning and delivering tailored interventions for individual students or families.

By collaborating, these professionals can share insights and develop comprehensive strategies that address the unique needs of each child. This multidisciplinary approach ensures well-coordinated and effective interventions.

### Training 
for school staff is often a valued way for practitioners to support pupil well-being and contribute to the school community. Sometimes, psychological practitioners are asked to provide training on specific topics, such as self-harm or exam stress.

### Observation
, referral, and signposting are key components of indirect school mental health interventions. Observations can focus on a single student or their interactions with peers and teachers, providing valuable insights into their behavior and needs. Signposting and referring students to other services—either through the school or directly to child and youth programs,—ensures they receive the appropriate support.

## Direct interventions

Many direct interventions are delivered at the curriculum level by teaching or pastoral staff. Direct mental health interventions in schools often involve individual psychological therapies and group-based approaches.

### Social and emotional learning (SEL) programs

SEL programs promote well-being and emotional development using games, activities, and group tasks.

Large-format group activities (assemblies, and class workshops) enable practitioners to provide psychoeducation directly to students.

### Assessment and screening

School-based assessments make it easier to access information such as learning and achievement, staff impressions, school engagement, and upcoming exams.
