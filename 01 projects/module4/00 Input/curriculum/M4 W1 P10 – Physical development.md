## Infancy and toddlerhood

Select each tab to learn about physical development during the first 2 years.

#### **Newborn**

Newborns perform automatic reflexive movements and involuntary actions in response to stimuli. These movements facilitate interaction with their environment and motor skill development. Examples include the grasp reflex and the rooting reflex. The rooting reflex prompts newborns to turn their heads and open their mouths when their cheek is touched, and it aids in feeding. The grasp reflex causes babies to close their fingers when their palms are stroked,

Over time, these movements gradually become more coordinated and purposeful.

#### 1-3 months

The infant grasps objects and repeats behaviors that have pleasurable results.

At 3 months, the infant may roll over.

#### 6 months

The infant:

- lifts their head and torso
- sits
- supports their weight on their legs
- brings objects to their mouth
- starts eating solids

9-18 months

**9-12 months**: The infant crawls.

**12-15 months**: The toddler manipulates objects.

**12-18 months**: The toddler walks.

#### 2 years

The toddler can climb stairs.

### Pre-school

The child becomes more physically active.

They refine the following, more complex gross motor skills:

- hopping
- jumping
- climbing
- riding a tricycle
- running

They also improve fine motor skills, including:

- hand-eye coordination
- drawing
- cutting with scissors

The child is usually toilet-trained between 3 and 3½ years.

## Childhood

The child's motor and perceptual skills are better integrated. Between the ages of 10 and 12 years, children typically begin the onset of puberty, with girls generally starting to develop approximately 2 years earlier than boys.

## Adolescence

During this stage, individuals experience growth spurts, which can sometimes lead to clumsiness as they adjust to their changing bodies. They also experience increased appetite, which is the body’s natural response to the heightened nutritional demands of growing tissues and organs. To support this intense period of growth, adolescents often exhibit a greater need for physical rest and sleep.

Puberty triggers a series of hormonal changes that lead to the development of secondary sexual characteristics and reproductive maturity. The emergence of sexual drives accompanies these physical changes.
