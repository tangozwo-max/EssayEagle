## WHO recommendations

The World Health Organization (WHO) has reviewed the evidence around mental health support for adolescents and developed some recommendations as to what this should look like. These could usefully be applied to children of other ages too.

### Universal interventions

Universal interventions should promote positive mental health, as well as prevent and reduce:

- suicidal behavior
- mental health disorders such as depression and anxiety
- aggressive, disruptive, and oppositional behavior
- substance use

The interventions should cover aspects of social and emotional learning including:

- emotional regulation
- problem-solving
- interpersonal skills
- mindfulness
- assertiveness
- stress management

### Humanitarian emergencies

Adolescents who have experienced humanitarian emergencies will benefit from psychosocial interventions. This can help to reduce the likelihood of mental health conditions developing such as:

- depression
- anxiety
- stress-related disorders
- substance use

A wide range of interventions have been tried with adolescents who have experienced humanitarian emergencies. However, due to the heterogeneous nature of these emergencies, caution should be exercised and the evidence base checked before they are used. The interventions that have been shown to be most effective are:

- stress management techniques
- relaxation strategies
- care for well-being

For adolescents who have experienced high levels of trauma, trauma-focused cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) has shown positive effects on reducing depression, anxiety, and stress. Group-based CBT has been shown to be effective for other adolescents who have experienced stressful events.

### Pregnancy and parenting

Adolescents who are experiencing pregnancy and adolescent parents should be offered interventions to promote good mental health and improve school attendance.

There is evidence that cognitive-behavioral skills-building programs should be considered for these adolescents.

### Emotional difficulties

WHO recommends that appropriate psychosocial interventions such as group-based CBT should be provided for adolescents with emotional symptoms.

Although the certainty of the evidence is low, self-reported symptoms of depression and anxiety seem reduced by these interventions. They also seem to promote positive mental health. The effect on school attendance is uncertain.

Early intervention with adolescents who are displaying emotional symptoms has proved crucial in preventing the progression of mental health disorders and optimizing health and lifestyle trajectories. Thus the benefits of these interventions seem to outweigh the risks.

### Behavioral difficulties

Interventions for behavioral difficulties include strategies such as parenting training based on social learning approaches, and social-cognitive problem-solving and interpersonal skills training for adolescents with disruptive or oppositional behaviors. Multimodal interventions for adolescents and their families can also be effective.

The interventions should aim to reduce aggressive, disruptive, and oppositional behaviors, prevent mental health disorders such as depression and anxiety, and promote good mental health.

Interventions should be in place to help adolescents avoid, or decrease, substance use.

Universal interventions are generally about mental health promotion, education, and prevention. As you learned earlier, mental health promotion is about increasing the factors that help to prevent mental health conditions occurring.

Strategies that can be used include:

- early childhood interventions (e.g., home visits for pregnant women, pre-school psychosocial activities)
- support for children (e.g., skills-building programs, child and youth development programs)
- programs targeted at vulnerable groups, including minorities, Indigenous peoples, migrants, and people affected by conflicts and disasters (e.g., psychosocial interventions after disasters)
- mental health promotional activities in schools (e.g., programs supporting ecological changes in schools and child-friendly schools)
- violence prevention programs
- community development programs
