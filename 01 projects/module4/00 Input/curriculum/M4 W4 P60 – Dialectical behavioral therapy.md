Dialectical behavior therapy (DBT) focuses on developing awareness and acceptance of thoughts and emotions, including painful or distressing internal experiences, without judgment or attempts to change them.
![[Pasted image 20260419133509.png|417]]
The primary goals of DBT are to:

- reduce self-harm
- minimize behaviors that interfere with treatment success, such as nonadherence
- address other factors that negatively impact a person’s quality of life

The four core components covered of DBT are:

- **mindfulness**: focusing on the present
- **interpersonal effectiveness**: learning to work with and respond better to others
- **emotion regulation**: understanding and increasing awareness of, and better controlling of, emotions
- **distress tolerance**: dealing with crises without harmful behavior

DBT for adolescents (DBT‐A) was adapted from the initial conceptualization of DBT, which was developed for adults diagnosed with borderline personality disorder. DBT‐A typically includes a combination of weekly individual and family therapy sessions and telephone support as needed. The aim of DBT-A is to support young people who experience longer-term difficulties related to emotion dysregulation, self-harm, and suicidality, as well as other problematic and impulsive behaviors. As such, this treatment is intensive and longer than other psychological treatments.

\[Source: Ohlis et al.\]

> [!-warning] -warning
> **Caution:** While DBT has shown effectiveness in treating self-harming adolescents, the studied interventions were resource-intensive, involving individual and group therapy, along with staff support. Simpler DBT skills programs are not comparable. Additionally, DBT-A was designed to treat borderline personality disorder. There is a lot of debate about whether this concept can be ethically and accurately applied to adolescents, whose personalities are still developing.
