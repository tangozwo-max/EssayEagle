In addition to being a risk factor for maternal depression, [[intimate-partner-violence|domestic abuse]] is an independent risk factor for infant and child mental health.

In some cases, the first time domestic abuse is present for a woman is during pregnancy, and other women experience heightened abuse during pregnancy.

### During pregnancy

Domestic abuse can impact babies before they are born by increasing the likelihood of miscarriage, lower birth weight, and premature birth.

### Infancy and toddlerhood

Infants and caregivers who experience domestic abuse can be negatively affected by [[toxic-stress|stress]], which can impact development. Children who are always in fight or flight mode do not have time to learn and develop as they would if they were not exposed to stress. Stress can also affect the attachment relationship, so the infant might withdraw from the caregiver or the caregiver may not be able to maintain connection.

Toddlers who have been exposed to domestic abuse may struggle to manage their emotions more so than other toddlers.

### Adolescents

Adolescents who experience domestic abuse might be distracted by worry for younger siblings, which could affect school attendance.

They might:

- struggle to make and keep friends
- exhibit aggressive behaviors similar to what they have observed, or they might do the opposite by becoming overly passive and compliant

In older children, there is an increased risk of substance use and criminal activity when they have experienced domestic abuse, as well as risks of self-harm and suicidal ideation.

[[toxic-stress|**Toxic stress syndrome**]] occurs when a child has been exposed to damaging and stressful situations over a prolonged period and they do not have the support network to deal with stress healthily. Toxic stress can contribute to anxiety and depression and can affect the way an individual views relationships and handles stress throughout their lives.
