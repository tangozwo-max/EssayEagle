### Maria Montessori
![[Pasted image 20260419135810.png|354]]

Maria Montessori made significant contributions to education, particularly in mental health and child development. As the first woman to graduate in medicine from the University of Rome, she began her career working with children with disabilities, observing that a lack of stimulation was a major factor in their developmental delays. In 1909, she published her influential work on the Montessori method.

Montessori’s educational approach emphasized child-centered learning and a supportive and stimulating environment tailored to the needs of children. She introduced hands-on learning materials that allowed children to learn through direct engagement, fostering fine motor skills and problem-solving abilities. Her method focused on the holistic development of children, addressing their physical, emotional, and intellectual needs, and she argued that many issues traditionally seen as medical were educational. Montessori’s ideas quickly gained international recognition, leading to the establishment of schools and training centers worldwide.

### August Aichhorn
![[Pasted image 20260419135820.png|341]]
August Aichhorn was a pioneer in psychoanalytic education, particularly known for his work with at-risk youth. Trained as an elementary school teacher in Austria, Aichhorn was tasked in 1918 with developing educational centers for troubled adolescents in Lower Austria. His approach was revolutionary for its time, as it focused on understanding and addressing the psychological needs of these youths rather than imposing strict discipline.

Influenced by psychoanalytic principles, Aichhorn believed that delinquency often stemmed from emotional disturbances and that effective intervention required understanding these underlying issues. In his landmark book, *Wayward Youth* (1925), Aichhorn outlined his philosophy and methods, emphasizing the importance of creating a supportive and understanding environment where youths could feel valued. His core principle was reparation, or treating at-risk youths not as criminals, but as individuals who had faced significant hardships. He introduced practical measures to provide stability and belonging, such as giving them personal drawers or boxes and ensuring regular meals.

### A.S. Neill
![[Pasted image 20260419135830.png|157]]

A.S. Neill, originally from Scotland, founded the Summerhill School in Suffolk, England, in 1921. Neill’s approach emphasized freedom and self-regulation, rejecting traditional educational constraints. Inspired by Wilhelm Reich’s ideas on the psychobiological alleviation of anxiety through freedom, Neill sought to create an environment where children could learn and grow without fear.

Neill proposed that teachers should be supported to better understand and address children’s emotional needs. His philosophy of education focused on prevention rather than cure, emphasizing the importance of proper upbringing over psychoanalytic intervention. At Summerhill, Neill influenced progressive education and mental health practices by championing democratic freedom and personal liberation.

### Susan Isaacs
![[Pasted image 20260419135839.png|123]]
Susan Isaacs led the development of Malting House School in Cambridge from 1924 to 1927, blending her training as a teacher and psychoanalyst. The school catered to children from professional and academic families. Malting House provided ample play spaces and resources, such as sandpits, climbing frames, paints, and musical instruments.

Isaacs believed in treating each child as a unique individual with the capacity for social relationships. Her work at Malting House and subsequent research significantly influenced the development of nursery schools and child development policies in the UK, which lead to a substantial increase in nursery care by 1944.

### Anna Freud
![[Pasted image 20260419135848.png|229]]

Anna Freud, along with Dorothy Burlingham, established the London Nurseries between 1939 and 1945, which laid the foundation for the Hampstead Child Therapy Clinic. Her interest in applying psychoanalysis to education began in 1927 with the Hietzing School in Vienna, where she developed theories on child development and the impact of repressive parenting.

Freud’s work emphasized balancing freedom and discipline in education. After fleeing to England in 1937, she continued her work with children, including those who were evacuated or displaced during World War II. Her observations led to the formulation of developmental stages, significantly influencing child psychoanalysis and education.

Policy priorities in the UK have reflected the increasing focus on emotional well-being and mental health in schools.

### 1955

W.D. Wall’s book, *Education and Mental Health*, introduces the term **mental health** to describe emotional and psychological issues in children. Wall emphasizes cooperation between schools and families to address these challenges.

### 1990s

Educators and policymakers begin acknowledging the importance of integrating social and emotional development into schools. Daniel Goleman’s work on emotional intelligence highlights the benefits of fostering emotional intelligence for academic outcomes, behavioral issues, and resilience.

In 1998, the UK introduced the Social and Emotional Aspects of Learning (SEAL) curriculum to promote pupil well-being. SEAL programs show success in improving social engagement and academic performance, especially with a whole school approach.

### Post-2010

In the UK, under Prime Minister Theresa May, the focus shifted towards using schools to improve children’s mental health and targeted problem behaviors in socio-economically disadvantaged areas.

Some critics, like Ecclestone and Hayes, argued that the increased focus on mental health and emotions in education led to a therapeutic ethos that diminished people’s resilience and autonomy, aligning education too closely with welfare. They believed this shift interfered with the primary goal of knowledge acquisition and created an overly emotional environment filled with vague terms and numerous disorders, reflecting a broader social pessimism.

There was a rise in targeted mental health interventions in schools, driven by government policies and guidance, which led to the employment of counselors and psychotherapists.

### 2014-2019

**2014**: The *Mental Health and Behavior* report highlighted the need to consider mental health when addressing behavioral issues, viewing behavior as a potential indicator of mental health needs.

**2017**: The UK government’s report, *Transforming Children and Adolescent Mental Health Provision,* aims to extend mental health support in schools by introducing mental health first aid training and appointing mental health leads in all schools by 2025.

**2018**: The green paper *Mental Health: Failing a Generation* calls for a more cohesive approach to children’s mental health.

**2019**: The Children’s Commissioner estimates that one million children need mental health support, highlighting that around four children in every UK classroom are affected, with 120,000 children being homeless or in temporary accommodation.
