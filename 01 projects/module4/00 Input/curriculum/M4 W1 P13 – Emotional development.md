## Emotional development

## Infancy and toddlerhood

Select each tab to learn about emotional development during the first 3 years.

#### 0–16 months

During their first year, infants engage in behaviors such as rocking, feeding, and sucking to regulate their emotions. They also rely on their caregivers to provide support during times of stress.

Between **14 and 16 weeks,** the infant smiles in response to social interaction.

#### Around 6 months

The infant smiles, laughs, and displays signs of anger and sadness when their soother is taken away.

#### 9-12 months

**9 months:** The infant shows fear in facial expression following [[separation-anxiety|separation]].

**12 months:** The infant shows capacity for [[social-referencing]], which involves expressing the appropriate emotion in a given situation by responding to the expressions of caregivers. They can increasingly discriminate between positive and negative emotions expressed by others.

#### 12-36 months

**12** – **18 months:** The toddler experiences the so-called "terrible twos," a phrase often used to describe a period of willful and stubborn behavior, marked by frequent tantrums and irritability when limits are set.

**18** – **36 months:** The toddler exhibits a heightened expression of self-conscious emotions. They begin to feel pride when they accomplish tasks and may experience embarrassment or shame when they fail or do not behave as expected. They start to recognize signs of distress in others. This newfound sensitivity often leads to acts of altruism, as they learn to respond to others’ emotional states. At this stage, toddlers often form strong emotional attachments to certain toys or objects, which serve as sources of comfort and security.

Preschool

- The child increasingly uses language to regulate emotions, including both internal speech and conversations with others.
- Their self-esteem is based on what others say about them.
- They have an increased ability to control and tolerate frustration and show capacity for delayed gratification.
- At this stage, they develop an understanding of right and wrong.
- They show increased insight into the emotions experienced by others and increasingly sophisticated empathic and altruistic behavior.
- They develop an awareness that they can mislead others by falsely expressing emotions.

## Childhood

### **5–7 years**:

The child is increasingly able to self-regulate emotions. At this age, they can present a so-called "cool" emotional front to peers, meaning they can demonstrate a controlled and composed emotional response that aligns with the social expectations of their peer group. This ability to present a calm exterior, even in emotionally charged situations, reflects their growing emotional intelligence and self-control.

### **7–10 years**

- The child is acutely aware of and sensitive to what others think of them.
    
- They use emotional expression to regulate distance and closeness in relationships.
    
- When emotionally overwhelmed, they will seek distance to regain composure.
    
- They become aware that they can experience conflicting emotions about the same person.
    
- They use information and memories about self and others to help maintain friendships.
    
- Their self-esteem is based on their ability to perform and produce.
    

### **10–13 years**

The child possesses multiple strategies for autonomously regulating emotions.

They can distinguish between genuine and managed emotional expression.

## Adolescence

[[storm-and-stress|”Storm and stress”]] is a concept that refers to the period of adolescence, which is often characterized by emotional upheaval and behavioral challenges. This term was originally coined by psychologist G. Stanley Hall in 1904, and it is based on the German literary genre _Sturm und Drang_ (Storm and Stress) which depicted the emotional turmoil of teenagers.

**12–14 years**: The adolescent:

- becomes self-conscious about physical appearance
    
- worries about being normal
    
- tends to return to behaviors from an earlier age when distressed
    
- relies on peers for support
    

At this age, the adolescent may overreact to questions and criticism and may engage in risk-taking behavior for intense emotional experiences.

Questioning and exploring their emerging sexuality and sexual orientation is developmentally appropriate at this stage.

**14–18 years**: The adolescent becomes increasingly egocentric. During this stage, they often perceive themselves as omnipotent and invulnerable. They begin to form a more organized perception of their own attitudes, values, and behaviors (identity formation).

Managing self-impression becomes important to adolescents, as they strive to present themselves in a certain light to others. They begin to experience multifaceted emotional states. For example, adolescents may experience meta-emotions such as feeling guilty for feeling angry or being ashamed of their fear. Their emotions are increasingly governed by their moral principles and beliefs about right and wrong.

**Note**: While adolescence classically finishes on reaching adulthood, the brain continues to dominate behavior this way until around the mid-twenties/early adulthood.
