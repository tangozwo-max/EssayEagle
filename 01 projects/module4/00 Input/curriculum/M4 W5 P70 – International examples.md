## Case study

### Adolescent Mental Health and Suicide Prevention (AMHSP) program, Kazakhstan

The program was implemented by the Government of Kazakhstan in partnership with UNICEF and the Bilim Foundation.

A key feature of the program is a digital monitoring system that identifies at-risk adolescents and refers them to trained mental health workers. The program also focuses on building the capacity of school and public health professionals, incorporating strategies to reduce stigma, such as parent outreach, distributing awareness materials, and conducting mental health literacy workshops and campaigns within the school community.

During the COVID-19 pandemic, an online counseling platform was launched, along with online training sessions for school psychologists.

The pilot program showed significant decreases in suicidal ideation, depression, anxiety, and stress among at-risk adolescents who received treatment. Over 10,000 adolescents (4.5%) were referred to additional services, and approximately 116,000 school psychologists and school personnel were trained to identify and respond to adolescents’ mental health issues. Additionally, over 1,500 primary health care and mental health workers were trained to support adolescents with mental health issues or suicidal behavior. The online training platform for school psychologists has reached more than 25,000 users.

CASEL is a not-for-profit, multidisciplinary network for establishing high-quality, evidence-based social and emotional learning (SEL) in schools. In 2011, CASEL launched the Collaborating Districts Initiative (CDI) for the systematic implementation of SEL in large urban districts across the USA. Implementation of SEL extends beyond classroom instruction and includes the school climate, staffing, professional learning, district policies, family engagement, and increasing the voice of students.

High-quality SEL programs lead to improvements such as better academic performance and classroom behavior, less emotional distress, and a more positive outlook on themselves and others. After 10 years of implementation, districts were able to sustain their commitment to SEL, even as the people and contexts in the district changed. New schools are joining the CDI, professional development in SEL is being extended to more staff, such as security officers and bus drivers, and SEL is being embedded in district policies and hiring practices.

\[Source: WHO\]

Sustained implementation of SEL was attributed to six main factors:

- strong leadership
- core district policies for SEL that connect all stakeholders
- school resources and SEL implementation pathways that can be customized to the needs of the school community
- staff culture
- climate and learning based on SEL co-creation of SEL with students, families, and communities
- collaborative groups of educators, or communities of practice, to strengthen SEL implementation
