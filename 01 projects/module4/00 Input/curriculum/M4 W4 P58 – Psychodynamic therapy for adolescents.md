Psychodynamic therapy is a form of talk therapy that focuses on exploring how the unconscious mind influences behavior and emotions. It examines early attachments, experiences, and relationships to understand how they relate to current experiences. Psychodynamic therapy is based on the assumption that a child's behavior and feelings will improve once their inner struggles are brought to light.

The contributions of Melanie Klein and Anna Freud significantly advanced psychodynamic therapy for children and adolescents by applying psychoanalytic concepts to therapeutic practices. Their work emphasized the importance of understanding children's emotional experiences, particularly in the context of separation and loss, which gained prominence during and after the Second World War. This foundational perspective has since evolved to encompass a broader range of therapeutic approaches, including family and group therapy, parent-infant psychotherapy, and parent-couple interventions, ultimately enriching the treatment options available for young clients facing various clinical challenges.

### Play

The focus of psychodynamic therapy is on unconscious processes and the child's internal world, which is not always accessible, even to the child. Therapists use toys, games, and creative activities to help children communicate their feelings and experiences through play.

### Exploring early experiences

The therapist helps the child explore significant early experiences and relationships, particularly those with primary caregivers, to help understand how these experiences influence current behavior and emotional responses.

### Understanding Defense Mechanisms

Children, like adults, use defense mechanisms to cope with emotional distress. The therapist works to identify these mechanisms and understand how they impact the child’s behavior and interactions.

### Therapeutic alliance

The clinician builds a therapeutic alliance by respecting the child’s autonomy, developmental stage, defensive style, and specific issues. The therapist pays close attention to all forms of the child’s communication, including verbal, gestural, and symbolic (through play). A safe therapeutic environment is created by maintaining confidentiality and listening to the child’s perspective. The therapist’s sensitivity to the child’s developmental status and defense mechanisms is key to forming a strong therapeutic bond.

### Parental involvement

Parental involvement in psychodynamic therapy often includes parents or caregivers participating in sessions or receiving guidance on supporting their child’s emotional development and addressing behavioral issues at home, with it being common practice for them to attend parallel psychotherapeutic sessions.

> [!-info] -info
> **Note**: While the focus is on underlying issues, therapists also help children manage specific symptoms, such as anxiety, depression, or behavioral difficulties. This can involve teaching coping strategies and emotional regulation techniques.

The evidence base for psychodynamic child psychotherapy is limited due to the small number of randomized controlled trials (RCTs).

A 2013 meta-analysis suggested that short-term psychodynamic psychotherapy (STPP) might be effective for various conditions in children, with a “sleeper effect” where treatment benefits continue post-therapy.

An upcoming large-scale National Institute for Health and Care Research (NIHR) multicenter RCT will compare cognitive behavioral therapy, STPP, and specialist clinical care for adolescents with major depression. This will be the largest trial to include psychodynamic child psychotherapy.
