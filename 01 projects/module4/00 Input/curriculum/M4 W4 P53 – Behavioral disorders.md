## Behavioral disorders

Internationally, approaches to conceptualizing and diagnosing behavioral disorders vary. In some countries, even very young children may be readily diagnosed with disorders like oppositional defiant disorder (ODD) and conduct disorder based on psychiatric criteria. In contrast, other countries may use these diagnoses less frequently, reserving them for cases where symptoms are severely problematic, such as when the individual has interactions with law enforcement.

In other settings, the emphasis might be more on understanding and identifying the underlying causes of disruptive or problematic behavior rather than on diagnosing a disorder.

> [!-info] -info
> **Note**: The following diagnoses can often be incorrectly given, for example, when a child displays behavior that is challenging to others or risky due to mental health reasons such as trauma or neurodevelopmental conditions. For this reason, these diagnoses should not be made unless there has been a thorough and multidisciplinary assessment of the child that considers the child’s context and different diagnoses and explanations.

### DSM-5 classification

In the *Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders* (DSM), behavioral disorders fall under the classification of " **disruptive, impulse-control, and conduct disorders**." These disorders are characterized by difficulties controlling emotions and behaviors that manifest in behaviors that violate the rights of others (e.g., aggression, destruction of property) or bring the individual into significant conflict with societal norms or authority figures. The underlying causes of the problems in the self-control of emotions and behaviors can vary greatly.

\[Source: American Psychiatric Association\]

The following disorders are included in this category in the DSM-5:

- oppositional defiant disorder
- intermittent explosive disorder
- conduct disorder
- antisocial personality disorder
- pyromania (where individuals feel an irresistible urge to set fires, experiencing relief or pleasure afterward)
- kleptomania (characterized by a recurrent urge to steal items that are not needed and have little value, leading to relief or gratification during or after the theft)
- other specified disruptive, impulse-control, and conduct disorder
- unspecified disruptive, impulse-control, and conduct disorder

> [!-info] -info
> **Note**: All the disorders in this DSM category involve difficulties with emotional and behavioral regulation, but they differ in which type of self-control problem is more prominent.

## Oppositional defiant disorder

Select each question to learn about ODD.

#### What is it?
Oppositional defiant disorder (ODD) is characterized by a frequent and persistent pattern of angry/irritable mood, argumentative/defiant behavior, or vindictiveness. Individuals with ODD often lose their temper, are touchy or easily annoyed, and are frequently angry and resentful. They tend to argue with authority figures or adults, actively defy requests or rules, deliberately annoy others, and blame others for their mistakes or misbehavior. Additionally, they may exhibit spiteful or vindictive behavior at least twice within 6 months. These symptoms typically occur as part of a pattern of problematic interactions with others. Interestingly, individuals with ODD usually do not see themselves as angry, oppositional, or defiant; instead, they often justify their behavior as a response to what they perceive as unreasonable demands or circumstances.

#### Key facts
ODD is more common in children and adolescents from families with frequent caregiver changes or harsh, inconsistent, or neglectful parenting. It often co-occurs with ADHD and conduct disorder and is linked to a higher risk of suicide attempts, even when other disorders are accounted for. The first symptoms of ODD typically appear during the preschool years and rarely after early adolescence. While ODD often precedes conduct disorder, not all individuals with ODD develop conduct disorder. ODD increases the risk of anxiety and major depressive disorders, even without conduct disorder. Defiant, argumentative, and vindictive symptoms mainly lead to conduct disorder, while angry/irritable mood symptoms lead to emotional disorders. ODD symptoms remain consistent throughout development, and those affected are at higher risk for problems with adjustment as adults, which may manifest as antisocial behavior, impulse-control problems, substance use, anxiety, and depression. The disorder is linked to neurodevelopmental conditions (NDCs), attachment issues, and trauma.
#### Evidence
Behavioral interventions targeting parenting skills, teacher classroom management, and child cognitive behavioral skills have shown promise in reducing oppositional behavior across various age groups. Among these, parenting interventions have the strongest evidence and are recommended as the primary approach. Psychopharmacological treatments are not recommended for ODD.

Parenting interventions, which are skills-based programs often consisting of 8–16 sessions, have the most robust evidence base and are endorsed by clinical guidelines from organizations like NICE and WHO. These programs, effective in both group and individual formats, typically start by enhancing parent-child relationships through increased parental warmth, child-centered play, and positive reinforcement. They then focus on positive discipline, helping parents set clear rules and expectations, provide effective instructions, and apply consistent, nonviolent consequences for oppositional behaviors.

Teacher-focused interventions to address the school environment and child-focused interventions such as CBT show mixed results for ODD.

\[Source: Hawes et al.\]

## Conduct disorder

According to the DSM-5, conduct disorder is characterized by a repetitive and persistent pattern of behavior that violates the basic rights of others or major societal norms. These behaviors are grouped into four categories:

1. aggressive conduct causing or threatening harm to people or animals
2. nonaggressive conduct causing property damage
3. deceitfulness or theft
4. serious rule violations

Conduct disorder typically begins in childhood or adolescence, with symptoms rarely starting after age 16. While many individuals outgrow the disorder by adulthood, those with early onset are at higher risk for ongoing issues, including criminal behavior and substance use.

\[Source: American Psychiatric Association\]

**Read this excerpt about conduct disorder:**

"Conduct disorder diagnosis may at times be potentially misapplied to individuals in settings where patterns of disruptive behavior are viewed as near-normative (e.g., in very threatening, high-crime areas or war zones). Therefore, the context in which the undesirable behaviors have occurred should be considered."

\[Source: American Psychiatric Association\]

It can be difficult to identify behavioral disorders for the following reasons:

- Overlapping symptoms between multiple disorders, like ADHD and anxiety, make it hard to distinguish the primary issue.
- Behaviors that seem problematic might be typical for a child’s age due to developmental variability.
- Subjective assessments by different professionals, parents, or teachers can lead to inconsistent evaluations.
- Environmental influences, such as trauma or stress, may cause temporary behavior changes that mimic disorders.
- Co-occurring conditions often complicate the picture, as many children experience more than one disorder simultaneously.
