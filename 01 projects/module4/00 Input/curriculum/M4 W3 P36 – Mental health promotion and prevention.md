## What is the difference between promotion and prevention?

[[mental-health-promotion|Promoting good mental health]] and [[mental-health-prevention|preventing mental health conditions]] from developing are key elements of a public health approach to child and adolescent mental health.

Select each heading to learn more.

PROMOTION
- Promotion involves intervening to optimize [[positive-mental-health|positive mental health]] by addressing determinants of positive mental health before a specific mental health condition has been identified.
- The ultimate goal of promotion is to improve the overall [[positive-mental-health|positive mental health]] of the population.
- It emphasizes proactive measures to enhance well-being, resilience, and mental health factors (such as social support, coping skills, and lifestyle choices) even before any mental health conditions arise.

PREVENTION
- Prevention aims to minimize mental health conditions by addressing determinants of those mental health conditions before a specific issue has been identified in individuals, groups, or populations.
- The ultimate objective of prevention is to reduce the occurrence of future mental health conditions.
- It targets [[risk-factors|risk factors]], [[protective-factors|protective factors]], and early signs of mental health conditions to prevent their development or progression.

\[Source: Mi Cho, Mi Shin\]

## Promotion

As well as encouraging [[protective-factors|protective factors]] and health behaviors, [[mental-health-promotion|mental health promotion]] involves creating environments that support good mental health and allow children and young people to maintain healthy lifestyles.

Without the security and freedom that is provided by respecting people's right to live a healthy life, it is hard for people to maintain good mental health.

Good mental health can be promoted through:

- providing early childhood interventions that support families through pregnancy and early life
- supporting children to develop the skills they need
- supporting vulnerable groups such as minorities, Indigenous peoples, or refugees with programs aimed at promoting good mental health
- creating programs that promote good mental health in schools and other educational facilities
- creating programs that prevent antisocial behavior and violence
- creating programs that support the development of communities and the links people have with them

 

[[youth-development-programs|Youth development programs]] that will encourage good mental health have certain characteristics:

- They provide opportunities for young people to build the skills they need for life while fostering positive relationships.
- They recognize and build on strengths demonstrated by young people.
- They engage positively and constructively with young people from within their communities and educational facilities.

\[Source: Mi Cho, Mi Shin\]

## Prevention

Prevention efforts will vary in intensity and individuality depending on where they sit on the continuum of need and the stage of development the child or young person is in.

![[Pasted image 20260417170135.png|451]]

\[Source: Mi Cho, Mi Shin\]

Treatment

Treatment is indicated when preventative efforts have not worked and a young person has been diagnosed with a mental health disorder.

An example of this might be where prevention of abuse (possibly at a parenting level) has not worked, and a young person has subsequently developed [[ptsd|post-traumatic stress disorder]] (PTSD) due to abuse. This would then be treated with longer-term psychotherapy.

  
Indicated

Indicated prevention efforts are for high-risk young people who may already be showing some signs of emotional or behavioral disorder. They do not necessarily need a diagnosis at this point.

An example of this might be a program to support school attendance in a young person who has unauthorized absences from school or has been excluded from school due to behavioral issues.


  
Selective

Selective prevention efforts are targeted at individuals or groups who exhibit [[risk-factors|risk factors]] for developing poor mental health.

An example of this might be a support group for young carers.


Universal

Universal prevention efforts are accessible without people needing to be at risk.

An example of this might be a substance use prevention curriculum that is delivered in school to teenagers.
