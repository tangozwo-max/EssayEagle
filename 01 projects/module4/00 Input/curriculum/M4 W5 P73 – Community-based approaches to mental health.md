Community mental health is increasingly recognized as an approach to addressing mental health issues by focusing on the social determinants of health.

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), preventing common mental disorders like anxiety and depression requires action on social, cultural, economic, political, and environmental factors. This also includes national policies, social protection, living standards, working conditions, and community support. Charitable and voluntary organizations play an essential role in supporting community mental health, offering vital services and resources.

Key reasons for the current shift towards community mental health:

- Services need to consider the impact of social context and wider social forces, such as unemployment, inequality, and low income. Services must also be able to intervene at these wider, system levels to help prevent distress and not blame individuals.
- Services benefit from being more accessible, appealing, asset-focused, and non-stigmatizing. Community psychology can support this by reducing barriers to engagement through community-led and co-production approaches.
- In a setting where mental health challenges are rising alongside growing inequality—and funding for services is decreasing—community psychology can support services in becoming more proactive, preventative, and financially sustainable.

Within community mental health, there is a field known as community psychology. This discipline emerged in the United States during the 1960s as a response to the limitations of traditional clinical psychology, which often focused narrowly on individual treatment while neglecting broader social issues. Influenced by the civil rights and feminist movements, community psychology emphasizes social justice, empowerment, and cultural diversity.

By focusing on community-based approaches, practitioners can engage in relational, strength-based, and participatory practices that are culturally valid and responsive to the needs of diverse populations. This approach is particularly important for supporting Indigenous communities, refugees, and those recovering from severe mental illnesses.

### Relational practices

Relational practices emphasize the importance of building strong, supportive relationships within the community. This approach recognizes that mental health and well-being are deeply influenced by social connections and interactions.

By fostering trust, empathy, and mutual respect, relational practices aim to create a sense of belonging and support among community members. This can involve activities like group therapy, community gatherings, and peer support networks.

### Participatory practices

Participatory practices involve actively engaging community members in the processes that affect their lives, from planning and decision-making to implementation and evaluation. This approach is rooted in the belief that those who are most affected by social issues should have a say in how they are addressed.

Participatory practices can include methods like community-based participatory research (CBPR), where researchers and community members collaborate as equal partners to identify problems and develop solutions. This ensures that interventions are culturally relevant and tailored to the specific needs of the community.

### Strength-based practices

Strength-based practices focus on identifying and leveraging the inherent strengths and resources of individuals and communities, rather than concentrating on their deficits or problems. This approach encourages people to recognize their own capabilities and resilience, which can empower them to overcome challenges. For example, a strength-based intervention might involve helping a community identify its existing social networks, skills, and cultural assets to address local issues.

In community mental health, **co-production** refers to a collaborative approach where service providers and community members work together to design, deliver, and evaluate services or projects. This method emphasizes equal partnership, ensuring that the voices of those who use the services are given the same weight as those who provide them.

Co-production is grounded in principles of accessibility, diversity, equality, and reciprocity. It aims to challenge traditional power dynamics by valuing the lived experiences of community members, allowing them to influence decisions and outcomes meaningfully.

**How can you recognize a community-based service for children and adolescents?**

Community-based psychology services are:

- driven by the principles of social justice and transformational change
- proactive in their design, delivery, and evaluation
- designed to address the multiple levels that impact the health of young people
- integrated with local communities and structures to define needs and co-produce solutions
- focused on empowering practitioners and community members to create social change
- designed to share learning and approaches through networks and alliances



> [!-warning] -warning
> **Note**: It is important to differentiate between community mental health teams (CMHTs) and community-based approaches.
> 
> The key difference is that CMHTs are provided by statutory services and fit within the clinical model. Community-based approaches are much broader and can be based outside statutory services, often at the grassroots level. This provides more flexibility and freedom in their creative and collaborative approaches.
