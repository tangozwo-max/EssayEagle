Pediatric eating disorders, which include conditions such as anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, and binge-eating disorder, often emerge in adolescence, impacting both physical health and psychological well-being.

Some of the ethical challenges involved in caring for young people with eating disorders include:

- **Involuntary treatment**: Deciding when to enforce treatment against a person's will, especially in life-threatening situations.
- **Competency and consent**: Assessing the person's ability to understand their condition and make informed decisions about their treatment. This involves taking into account the young person's developmental stage.
- **Parental involvement**: Balancing the rights and wishes of parents with the best interests of the child.
- **Confidentiality**: Managing the confidentiality of the young person's health information while involving family members in treatment.

**Consider the following scenario:**

Emma, a 14-year-old girl, has been diagnosed with anorexia nervosa. She is deemed to be severely underweight for her age, which presents with clear medical risks. She insists that she is fine and refuses to eat. Her parents and doctors are extremely worried as her condition continues to deteriorate rapidly.

Emma's doctors assess her using the Gillick competence framework to determine if she can make informed decisions about her treatment. However, due to the nature of her illness, including the impact of malnourishment on cognitive decision-making, Emma cannot accurately appraise the severity of her condition and the subsequent implications of this. As such, she is ambivalent about the need for support, including renourishment. This impairs her ability to fully understand the risks and benefits of treatment.

The medical team decides that involuntary treatment is necessary to ensure Emma's safety. They work closely with her parents to explain the situation and the reasons for this decision, emphasizing the goal of stabilizing her health so that she can eventually participate more fully in her treatment decisions.

The medical team consult the NICE guidance on eating disorders, which states that:

"If a child or young person lacks capacity, their physical health is at serious risk and they do not consent to treatment, ask their parents or carers to consent on their behalf and if necessary, use an appropriate legal framework for compulsory treatment (such as the Mental Health Act 1983/2007 or the Children Act 1989)."

\[Source: NICE\]
