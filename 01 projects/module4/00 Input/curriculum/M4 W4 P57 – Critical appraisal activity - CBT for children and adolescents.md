You will now review a structured critical appraisal of a randomized control trial using the Critical Appraisal Skills Programme (CASP) checklist. You may prefer to work through the paper and checklist on your own and check your appraisal against the one provided.

## Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) CASP checklist

The following checklist is used for this critical appraisal:

### Section A: Is the basic study design valid for a randomized controlled trial?

1\. Did the study address a clearly focused research question?

2\. Was the assignment of participants to interventions randomized?

3\. Were all participants who entered the study accounted for at its conclusion?

### Section B: Was the study methodologically sound?

4\. Were participants, investigators, and analysts blinded?

5\. Were the study groups similar at the start of the randomized controlled trial?  
6\. Apart from the experimental intervention, did each study group receive the same level of care  
(that is, were they treated equally)?

### Section C: What are the results?

7\. Were the effects of the intervention reported comprehensively?  
8\. Was the precision of the treatment effect reported?  
9\. Do the benefits outweigh the harms and costs?

10\. Can the results be applied to your local population/in your context?

11\. Would the experimental intervention provide greater value to the people in your care than any of the existing interventions?

## Section 2: Was the study methodologically sound?

![[Pasted image 20260419133134.png]]
> It is typically not possible to blind psychological therapists to the intervention that they are delivering, as they require training and experience to deliver therapy to a high standard. They do not state whether participants were blinded to condition. Again, it is possible that their prior experience of mental health care means they may recognise the type of intervention they were receiving. Independent research assistants who were blind to condition conducted semi-structured diagnostic interviews.

![[Pasted image 20260419133139.png]]
The authors state that they considered randomization successful, as groups did not differ significantly on gender, age, ethnicity, history of mental healthcare use, internalizing- or externalizing problems, depressive symptoms, pretreatment number of diagnoses, suicide criteria assessment, global functioning, or severity of depression. There was no significant difference in antidepressant use across the two conditions at the beginning of the intervention.

> 6\. Apart from the experimental intervention, did each study group receive the same level of care (that is, were they treated equally)?

This is where it is really important to understand and have sufficient detail on the comparator condition. Comparing a new treatment (such as CBT) to treatment as usual is a good way of addressing whether a service should bring in a new intervention. It is best practice to use an active control group (such as people receiving a different type of therapy), rather than a passive control group (waiting list controls or people receiving no intervention) when questioning whether a new intervention should be used in routine care.

However, studies rarely provide enough information on aspects of care other than the direct intervention received. For example, did young people just see their therapist, or were they allocated a key worker? Did they just have contact with the service for a therapy appointment of a certain duration, or was there other contact during the week (e.g., a psychiatry review; liaison with school etc; a duty worker available)? Without knowing this, we cannot fully say if the two groups were treated equally. The researchers attempted to make sure participants were treated equally by providing therapists with the same information about depressive disorders and matching the frequency of therapy sessions and duration of sessions.

Also, treatment as usual can be used as a comparator as if it is one homogenous group, when we can actually see in this paper that it included many different types of therapies. This therefore means that one theoretically similar group (people receiving CBT) is being compared to a heterogenous group. This is important, both clinically and statistically.

Researchers did request that TAU did not use any elements of CBT, and in clinical practice it is common that many eclectic or multicomponent interventions would include elements of CBT.

The follow-up intervals were the same for each study group.

## Section 3: What are the results?

Select each question to learn more.
![[Pasted image 20260419133201.png]]
A power calculation was completed, but the study only included 88 adolescents when the power calculation required 140. It was therefore underpowered, a significant limitation. Outcomes were clearly stated and measured using validated tools. Missing data was imputed at item and assessment level. Statistical tests used included chi-square tests, t-tests, and analyses of covariance (ANCOVAs). Clinical significance was established by examining the percentage of adolescents falling below the clinical cutoff score for the Child Depression Inventory-II. Moderation was analyzed by multigroup analyses and linear regression analyses. P values were reported where appropriate.

The results were reported per study group pre- post and 6 month follow-up. Dropout was considered and while the difference was not statistically significant, important clinical factors were flagged, such as different reasons for discontinuation of both treatments. A potential area of bias is that for older participants, they could choose whether to include their parents in the study or not, and there may have been important reasons behind this decision (such as the nature of family relationships, conflict, etc.).
![[Pasted image 20260419133213.png]]
Confidence intervals do not seem to have been reported.
![[Pasted image 20260419133224.png]]
Moderate to large effect sizes were found for decreases in depressive symptoms as reported by adolescents, parents, and therapists within both CBT and TAU conditions. However, no significant difference was found between TAU and CBT at both post-treatment or follow-up. No significant predictors or moderators were identified.

Many studies of psychological interventions do not report adverse events, but this one does. It would be beneficial to further understand any reasons why increased suicide attempts were observed in the CBT but not the TAU group. A cost-effectiveness analysis was not reported.

> 10\. Can the results be applied to your local population/in your context?

A real strength of this study is that it is was completed within routine care with a referred clinical sample. This is different to many studies that heavily filter and select participants, and then apply the intervention in settings that do not translate to the challenges of everyday routine care. For example, looking at the sample information, there were high levels of comorbidity, suicidal ideation, and prior experience with mental health services. All of these factors are both commonly encountered in routine clinical practice, and suggest a greater degree of complexity. We can therefore be confident that these findings hold greater generalizability than those from samples that screened out based on complexity indicators such as these.

The results are transferable to other settings where treatment as usual is provided by highly qualified and experienced therapists, able to provide interventions covering multiple areas of need. The authors highlight that the effect size of TAU in the Netherlands has been found to be higher than in other countries.

The authors highlight the high number of young people with very high symptoms of depression pretreatment. Findings may therefore not apply equally to young people attending earlier in the trajectory of their development of depression.

Participants were included up to age 21, which is older than seen in some child and adolescent services internationally. For example, the UK would typically include young people up to the age of 17.5 in most generic services, whilst specialist services may go up to age 25 years to account for transitioning from child to adult services.

11\. Would the experimental intervention provide greater value to the people in your care than any of the existing interventions?

As findings suggest that both CBT and TAU are effective for this population, it may come down to clinical decision-making as to which intervention is best indicated for whom. There is no health economics data provided to share with service commissioners, which would be needed to make an argument for providing an alternative intervention routinely.

## Appraisal summary

Where services need to invest significant resources into training up their workforce in CBT, they may not immediately do so if the evidence suggests current routine practice (treatment as usual) is equally effective. However, we need to be mindful that treatment as usual in this study was provided by highly qualified and experienced therapists providing a variety of therapy modalities, which may not be the case for every service internationally.
