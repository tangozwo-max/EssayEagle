[[perinatal-depression|Perinatal depression]] encompasses both antenatal (during pregnancy) and postnatal depression. Both are common in both high and middle-and-low-income countries.

Untreated antenatal depression is associated with postnatal depression and poor infant physical and neurocognitive developmental outcomes.


Risk factors for perinatal depression include:

- somatic symptoms
    
- [[intimate-partner-violence|intimate partner violence]]
    
- unintended pregnancy
    
- low maternal educational attainment
    
- [[socioeconomic-status|low socioeconomic status]] at the time of pregnancy
    
- lack of social support
    
- history of mental health disorders

Gelaye et al. conducted a systematic review of studies that focused on the epidemiology of perinatal depression in low-income and middle-income countries and summarized the evidence for the association of perinatal depression with infant and child outcomes. Although they note that causal inferences are limited by sample size and confounding factors, they note the following associations:

- Untreated prenatal depressive disorders are associated with medical complications during pregnancy, including preterm delivery and low birth weight.
- Postnatal depression has been linked to impaired postnatal infant growth, with depressed mothers more likely to stop breastfeeding early.
- Maternal depressive symptoms are linked to lower infant social and motor development scores. Postnatal depression, in particular, has been associated with delayed language development, sleep disturbances, and behavior not expected for a child's age or stage of development.

\[Source: Gelaye et al.\]

> [!-info] -info
> **Note:** Maternal depression is a well-known risk factor, but it is important to note that these risks relate to the long-term nature of depression. All parents and caregivers experience some stress, and a few stressful moments will not have long-term effects.
> 
> Maternal depression is also not the mother's fault. It is important to routinely support parents, and identify and treat postnatal depression as early as possible.
