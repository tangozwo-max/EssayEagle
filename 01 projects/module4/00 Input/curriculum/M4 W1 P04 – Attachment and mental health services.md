When a person becomes ill or requires mental health services, the associated stress activates their [[attachment-theory|attachment system]], and their interaction with healthcare services can be reflective of their [[attachment-styles|attachment style]] and experiences.

[[john-bowlby|Bowlby]] suggested that the therapist should act as an attachment figure by:

- creating a secure base, which allows the individual to explore attachment‐related experiences
- helping individuals rebuild [[internal-working-model|internal working models]] so that they have more positive representations of attachment relationships, which should enable more secure attachments

\[Source: Berry and Danquah\]

Attachment‐informed research suggests that people often react to clinical staff and services based on their attachment history.

Consider the following clinical experiences through an attachment lens.

"The children were living with both parents, but only the mother attended the sessions. The father came in at the end to meet her but did not engage. The mother explained that her husband did not believe in therapy. She said he has had to be very strong due to having had a difficult upbringing. It takes a long time for him to 'let anyone in.'"

"Xavier has a weekly appointment with me, and he attends his regular appointments maybe half of the time. He rarely calls to cancel appointments; he just doesn't show up. Every once in a while, especially after missing several appointments, he calls to say he needs urgent support. I have always managed to accommodate him, but after one or two sessions, he falls into the same pattern of struggling to regularly attend."

When a person has difficulty engaging consistently with services, it might reflect their attachment experiences and style. People who have had difficult childhood relationships may find it difficult to engage in the therapeutic process and other types of support, such as management of drug and alcohol use.

People who experience inconsistent caregiver responsiveness during early childhood often develop attachment patterns characterized by intense emotions and behavior patterns that cause problems in interactions with other people. These patterns present in many ways within therapy and services. People may push others away, declining offered care. They may request care in a way that paradoxically limits their access. For example, their behavior might cause them to be seen as demanding by shouting, complaining, or requesting more frequent or more intensive support than people with more secure attachments.

\[Source: Barber et al.\]

This concept is related to the [[still-face-experiment]]. In that experiment, researchers observed how infants react when their caregiver’s face remains unresponsive and emotionless. Similarly, when people anticipate that their needs will go unnoticed and unmet, it can impact their level of consistent engagement with services.

If they expect that they will not be understood, heard, or supported, they may not fully engage. If they have learned that their needs are only addressed during moments of extreme distress, they may interact with services in a manner that could be misconstrued as intentionally demanding or challenging for staff.

> [!-info] -info
> **Note**: Providing and receiving health care is a relational experience. When healthcare workers understand and appreciate their own attachment styles, it can impact their communication, empathy, and overall quality of care. There is some evidence that healthcare workers' attachment style predicts the therapeutic alliance with patients and patients' health outcomes.
> 
> \[Source: Berry and Danquah\]

For example, when faced with someone who needs support, do you:

- help by trying to fix things for them or rescue them
- feel overwhelmed by them
- try to avoid them

Note your thoughts in your personal reflective journal and please remember that it is common to have different responses in different situations.

How can attachment theory inform design of services?

- Attachment theory is directly applicable to illness and healthcare.
- Attachment theory can help practitioners understand the way people use and engage with service.
- Practitioners may represent attachment figures for their patients.
- Practitioners should understand their attachment styles.
- Secure attachment is related to positive therapeutic alliance, which has consistently been shown to be the best predictor of treatment outcomes.

\[Source: Berry and Danquah; Baier et al.\]

What challenges could hinder building and maintaining positive attachment relationships in healthcare?

Some examples of issues that negatively impact attachment relationships in mental healthcare services include:

- frequent turnover of staff
- patients not being allocated a key worker who can get to know them well
- people being regularly discharged from services without a discussion
