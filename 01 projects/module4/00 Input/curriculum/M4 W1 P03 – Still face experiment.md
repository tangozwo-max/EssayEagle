The [[still-face-experiment]] is a psychological study developed by [[edward-tronick|Dr. Edward Tronick]] in 1978. The experiment provides insight into how a parent's responsiveness can affect a baby's behavior and emotional development.

In the experiment, the following happens:

1. A mother interacts with her baby through talking, smiling, and making eye contact, to which the baby responds positively by smiling and laughing.
2. Then, the mother presents a still face, where she stops engaging with the baby and reacting to her behaviors. The baby initially tries to engage the mother, but when the mother does not reciprocate, the baby shows signs of distress.
3. The mother re-engages, talking to and playing with the baby. The baby becomes calm and responds happily to the mother.

Implications of the Still Face Experiment

- The experiment highlights the role of consistent responsiveness in forming secure attachments. The infant seeks emotional connection, emphasizing attachment as a fundamental aspect of neurodevelopment.
- Babies this young do not have the capacity to self-soothe and require an adult to help them regulate strong emotions.
- The mother’s lack of response causes the baby distress. The baby uses energy and behaviors to connect with the mother and gain her attention.
- While the baby is in distress, she does not feel secure or have a safe space from which to explore, and learning is disrupted.
- The [[attachment-styles|securely attached]] baby can settle quickly after this upsetting experience and trusts the mother to engage again. A baby who regularly experiences unresponsiveness or inconsistent responsiveness might exhibit more extreme behavior or behavior that escalates more quickly.

> [!-info] -info
> **Note**: Parents often worry that they can damage their children through rare occurrences such as that shown in the video. However, [[attachment-theory|attachment]] is built over time, so it takes repeated instances to cause an [[attachment-styles|insecure attachment style]]. A multitude of risk factors can affect a parent's ability to support their child to develop secure attachments, such as depression, bereavement, stress, and adverse environments.
