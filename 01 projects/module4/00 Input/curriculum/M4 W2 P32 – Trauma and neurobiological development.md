As you have already learned, all children need care and stimulation from adults who nurture and value them. These positive experiences with caregivers influence brain growth and development. When children experience abuse or neglect, their brains adapt to help them cope.

[[latent-vulnerability|**Latent vulnerability**]] refers to the increased risk of future mental health disorders in children who have experienced abuse or neglect. This concept highlights how early adverse experiences can lead to changes in the brain’s threat, reward, and memory systems.

These changes might help a child cope in a harmful environment but can create difficulties in safer settings, such as school or foster care. For example, a child might become overly vigilant or anxious, interpreting neutral situations as threatening. This can make it harder for them to form relationships or succeed in social settings.

Latent vulnerability primarily affects three brain systems: memory, threat, and reward.

### Memory

This system, particularly the hippocampus, is involved in forming, organizing, and retrieving memories, including autobiographical memories.

Autobiographical memories are the memories of someone's personal history. Adverse experiences can alter how these memories are processed and recalled. This can lead to difficulties in managing emotions and behaviors, as past traumatic experiences might be more easily triggered, influencing current emotional states and reactions.

### Threat

This system is responsible for detecting and responding to potential dangers. It includes structures like the amygdala, which plays a key role in emotional processing and fear responses.

When this system becomes hypervigilant, individuals may experience heightened anxiety and stress. They might overreact to perceived threats, even in relatively safe situations, leading to chronic stress and anxiety disorders.

### Reward

This system involves brain regions like the nucleus accumbens and the ventral tegmental area, which are associated with experiencing pleasure and motivation. It helps regulate feelings of reward and reinforcement.

Changes in this system can diminish the ability to feel pleasure or motivation, contributing to conditions like depression. Individuals might find it harder to enjoy activities they once found rewarding, leading to a loss of interest or pleasure in most activities.

Childhood trauma, especially when experienced early and frequently, can lead to neurobiological changes that impact executive function, emotional regulation, and interoceptive awareness.

The timing of exposure to trauma, as well as its chronicity, affects its impact. You learned that humans experience sensitive periods of brain development, characterized by enhanced synaptic plasticity. Sensitive periods are characterized by high levels of neurogenesis, synaptic growth, and organization of neural circuits. Exposure to trauma during these sensitive periods can increase the risk for mental health disorders throughout the person's life.

Sensitive periods in neurobiological development can be extended if environmental input is inconsistent or unpredictable. Childhood trauma often involves irregular caregiver interactions, which can keep the window of vulnerability open for neurobiological impact and potential recovery. Unpredictability associated with trauma may contribute to heightened hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis **(**HPA) reactivity and cortisol levels during these sensitive periods, compounding neurobiological risk.

You have learned that traumatic experiences during childhood may alter sensory and motor development. The five more well-known senses—sight, hearing, smell, taste, and touch—involve detecting stimuli from the **external** environment.
![[Pasted image 20260417165309.png|298]]

**Internal senses** involve detecting stimuli from within the body and include:

- vestibular (helps you maintain balance and spatial orientation by detecting head movements and changes in position)
- proprioception (allows you to perceive the position and movement of your body parts without looking at them)
- interoception (helps you detect internal bodily states, such as hunger, thirst, and heart rate)

Long-term trauma, especially in childhood, can alter sensory processes, including the ability to notice internal body cues (interoception). This can lead to difficulties in developing or changes in interoceptive abilities.
![[Pasted image 20260417165320.png|227]]

People who have experienced trauma may become less attuned, or numb to bodily changes, making it difficult to recognize internal states like pain, hunger, and fear. This can be adaptive for individuals who regularly experience sensations difficult to tolerate, such as pain.

Alternatively, some people experience heightened sensitivity to interoceptive experiences. For example, in panic disorder, benign physical sensations can feel intense and overwhelming. While heightened emotions can be adaptive in immediate danger, in the longer term, people often turn to coping strategies such as substance use.

Neurodivergent people may have heightened sensory needs related to interoception, proprioception, and the vestibular system, similar to their increased sensitivity to the main five senses, like sound and taste.

Even neurodivergent people who have not experienced trauma may need help identifying and communicating interoceptive experiences (like hunger, thirst, fatigue, pain), naming emotions, and balancing vestibular and proprioceptive input (e.g., ensuring more movement-related activity for people with ADHD). Unsupported sensory needs (both over- and under-stimulation) can cause significant stress for neurodivergent individuals.
