Social, psychological, and physical factors can play important roles in [[autism|autistic]] mental health.

Select each image to learn more.### Psychological

All people need to feel accepted and appreciated for their authentic selves. When a person feels judged, it can affect their self-worth.

Autistic people often have intense interests that bring them joy, and in which they have considerable expertise. It is important for their mental health that they should not feel ashamed to pursue their interests and hobbies due to external pressure.

### Social

To have good mental health, people need access to positive relationships within their families and communities. Autistic people may struggle to access social connections, and they may face adversities such as stigma, discrimination, and negative life events.

If schools and workplaces are unsupportive, autistic people might struggle, or find attendance intolerable, leading to education and employment inequities.

**Masking** occurs when an autistic person finds ways to pass as non-autistic in social interactions. Masking takes considerable effort and energy and can lead to mental health disorders such as anxiety and depression.

### Psychological

### Biological

Autistic people may have sensory differences that require acceptance and accommodation. For example, they might stim, which is performing repetitive actions or movements. This helps them to express themselves and feel calm.

Autistic people may also be affected by co-occurring physical health conditions, which complicate mental health risks.

Samuel Beldie shares his experience as an autistic person living with a neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by difficulties with social interaction and communication.

"Having autism is like wearing a label. People treat me differently. Sometimes I can understand why and sometimes I don't. I struggle with things like wanting to be cool in front of my friends, not knowing when someone is making a joke or being serious, and not knowing if someone is making fun of me, I also tend to misinterpret things or make big deals out of things. Nobody likes to be excluded, made fun of, or made to feel that they don't belong."

### Barriers to supporting children with NDCs

Children with neurodevelopmental conditions often have significant clinical needs. While having autism or ADHD should not automatically lead to depression or anxiety, unmet support needs often result in these mental health disorders being common among children with NDCs.

Practitioners often prioritize specific neurodevelopmental concerns reported by referrers, sometimes overlooking potential mental health disorders. Consequently, children and families receive an NDC diagnosis without clear guidance on the next steps to take, leading to suboptimal functional outcomes.

Clinically, distinguishing between behaviors caused by mental health issues versus core NDC characteristics can be challenging, especially during initial assessments for children with complex developmental needs.

\[Source: Boulton et al.\]

You have learned that transdiagnostic approaches focus on identifying and addressing factors that are common across multiple mental health conditions, rather than treating each condition separately.

How might a transdiagnostic approach result in improved outcomes for children with neurodevelopmental conditions?

Select **Benefits** to view the potential benefits of this approach.

- Instead of focusing on specific diagnoses, a transdiagnostic approach considers shared features and overlapping difficulties across conditions.
- By adopting this holistic perspective, practitioners can address the diversity in diagnostic categories more effectively.
- Early detection during developmental assessments allows timely intervention.
- Integrating mental health evaluation with developmental assessments facilitates timely referrals to psychology, psychiatry, and family support services.
