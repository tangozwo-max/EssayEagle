Your Baby's First Years

How relationships, everyday moments and a calm-enough home build your child's brain — a plain-language guide for new families.
For parents and carers expecting or welcoming a new baby. Browse it stage by stage — you don't need to read it all at once. Built from the NPMH Module 4 curriculum on child & adolescent development.
Please read this first. This is an educational summary of what the Module 4 course teaches about how children develop — it is not medical advice. Every child is different, and the ages below are typical ranges, not deadlines. For anything about your baby's health, feeding, growth or development — or if something simply doesn't feel right — talk to your midwife, health visitor or GP. They are there to help, and asking early is always the right call.

The five ideas that matter most

If you remember nothing else, remember these.

🧠

1. The early years count — a lot

"It's impossible to overestimate how important the early years are" (Prof. Jack Shonkoff, Harvard). Early relationships literally shape the developing brain and the foundation for lifelong health and learning.

🎾

2. Relationships build the brain

The single most powerful thing you can do is serve-and-return: when your baby coos, looks or points, you respond. Like tennis, this back-and-forth "builds and strengthens brain circuits."

⚓

3. You are the safe base

A baby who trusts that you will respond explores the world with confidence and returns to you for comfort. Responding to cries does not "spoil" a baby — it builds security.

🤱

4. Babies borrow your calm

Young babies "do not have the capacity to self-soothe and require an adult to help them regulate strong emotions." Your calm presence is how they learn to settle (co-regulation).

🌱

5. Resilience is built, not born

Resilience is "not innate" — it grows from supportive relationships and a stable environment. What families and communities provide matters, and it is never too late.

💬

6. Talk, sing, read — from day one

"Talking to your child, even before they can speak, helps develop their language." Look, Say, Sing, Play — everyday moments are brain-building.

Stage by stage: what's happening & how you help

Each stage has four parts — what's happening, what you can do, good to know, and the science.

🤰 Before birthpregnancy
What's happening

Your baby's brain is forming in a uniquely sensitive window. The developing brain is "very vulnerable to toxic substances," and a parent's physical and mental health in pregnancy is a key factor in how a baby starts life.

What you can do
  • Take up antenatal (pregnancy) care — it's one of the named protective factors.
  • Avoid alcohol in pregnancy. Alcohol has the most consistent evidence of harm to the developing brain (it can cause Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorder, FASD). Check any medicines with your midwife/GP.
  • Look after the parent's mental health — low mood in pregnancy is common, treatable, and not your fault. Tell your midwife early.
  • Reduce ongoing, unsupported stress where you can, and lean on your support network.
Good to know

"A few stressful moments will not have long-term effects." It's prolonged stress without support that matters — so accepting help is part of looking after your baby.

The science

FASD & substances in pregnancy — Ungerer et al.; SIGN. Perinatal (antenatal/postnatal) depression — Gelaye et al. Prenatal care as a protective factor — Kieling et al.

👶 Newbornbirth – 6 weeks
What's happening

Your baby uses reflexes (rooting to feed, grasping your finger) and innate signals — crying and smiling — to bring you close. This is "pre-attachment": they don't yet prefer one carer, but the bond is starting. Brain connections are forming rapidly.

What you can do
  • Respond to cries and cues — predictably and consistently. This is how a secure bond begins.
  • Hold, rock, feed, talk and sing. Skin-to-skin and a warm voice are exactly what a newborn needs.
Good to know

You cannot "spoil" a newborn by comforting them — and one hard moment won't harm the bond: "attachment is built over time… it takes repeated instances" to shape a child's pattern. Newborns can't calm themselves; they need you to do it with them.

The science

Attachment & stages — Bowlby; Ainsworth; NSPCC Learning. Reflexes & early regulation — developmental-milestone framework (Kempen).

🍼 Young infant6 weeks – 6 months
What's happening

The first real social smile (~3–4 months), babbling, rolling and reaching. Your baby starts to prefer familiar carers and lights up in face-to-face "conversations" with you.

What you can do
  • Serve-and-return: copy their sounds, smile back, take turns "chatting." This is the headline brain-builder.
  • Lots of face-to-face time, eye contact and narration ("Look, Say, Sing, Play") — describe what you're doing all day long.
Good to know

The famous "still-face" studies show babies actively work to reconnect when a parent goes blank — and learning pauses when they feel unsure. Brief lapses are completely normal; what counts is coming back and re-engaging.

The science

Still-face / co-regulation — Tronick (1978). Serve-and-return & brain architecture — UNICEF; NSPCC Learning. Rapid synapse growth (peaks ~age 2) — Konkel; The Economist.

🧸 Older infant6 – 12 months
What's happening

"Clear-cut attachment": a strong, specific bond to you, crawling, first understanding of words, and the discovery that things still exist when hidden (object permanence — the magic of peek-a-boo). Separation anxiety usually begins now.

What you can do
  • Play peek-a-boo and pat-a-cake — these build the "early foundations of working memory and self-control."
  • Be the secure base: warm, reliable comfort so they feel safe to explore and come back.
Good to know

Separation anxiety is a sign of healthy attachment, not a problem. Babies also "read" your face to decide how to feel (social referencing) — your calm helps them stay calm.

The science

Attachment stages & separation anxiety — NSPCC Learning; Bowlby. Working memory through play — NSPCC Learning. Object permanence — Piaget.

🚼 Toddler1 – 3 years
What's happening

Walking, first words then a vocabulary explosion, big feelings and the "terrible twos," and exploring away from you then returning for a "refuel." They understand far more than they can say.

What you can do
  • Be a secure base for exploration — let them venture out and welcome them back.
  • Talk and read constantly (their understanding is ahead of their speech).
  • Build self-control gently: simple routines, breaking tasks into small steps, and games with turns and rules. Name their feelings for them.
Good to know

Tantrums are normal limit-testing, not defiance to fear. Playing beside other children rather than with them ("parallel play") is age-appropriate. A favourite blanket or toy is a healthy comfort object.

The science

Secure base — Barbour; Bowlby. Executive-function tips — NSPCC Learning. Milestones — Kempen. Naming feelings (mentalizing) — Fonagy.

🧒 Preschool3 – 5 years
What's happening

Imagination soars; thinking is still "magical" and self-centred (they may believe their thoughts cause events). They start using words to manage feelings, play cooperatively, and take turns. Self-esteem now depends a lot on what adults say.

What you can do
  • Co-regulate with language: name feelings and talk them through ("You're frustrated the tower fell — that's hard").
  • Encourage pretend play, turn-taking and simple rules — these grow self-control.
  • Use kind, specific words — they land hard at this age.
Good to know

Because of "magical thinking," a preschooler may blame themselves for things that aren't their fault (e.g. a parent's stress or illness). Gentle reassurance — "this is not because of you" — really matters.

The science

Cognitive stages (egocentric/magical thinking) — Piaget. Emotional development & self-esteem — developmental-milestone framework. Co-regulation via language — emotional-development curriculum.

Building your child's "protective wall"

🛡️ What protects a child

The course pictures every child as having a "wall of protective factors." "When a child has all their needs met, their protective wall is strong, and they are more likely to be able to handle and overcome adversity and develop resilience." A secure attachment in the early years is named as a key protective factor for mental health.

"The quality of resilience is not innate to an individual." Resilience grows from the child's relationships and environment — so the goal is to improve the child's world, not to toughen the child. (Ungar & Theron)

What strengthens the wall: warm, responsive relationships; a stable, supportive home; people to lean on (family, friends, services); and meeting basic needs. In hard times, families "with more resources — social support networks, financial stability… and good physical health" cope better — and those supports can be added at any time.

Gentle care: things to be aware of

⚠️ Worth knowing — without worry

These are things the course flags as risks to buffer, shared here calmly so you know what helps — not to alarm you.

  • Ordinary stress is fine; unsupported, prolonged stress is the issue. "Toxic stress" happens when stress is severe and long-lasting and a child lacks a supportive relationship to buffer it. The buffer is the point: a caring, present adult is what keeps stress tolerable.
  • A parent's mental health matters for the baby — and help works. Perinatal depression is common, treatable and "not the mother's fault." Identifying and treating it early protects both of you. (And it affects fathers/partners too.)
  • A calm-enough home. Conflict and domestic abuse are independent risks to a baby's wellbeing (and can begin in pregnancy). If home doesn't feel safe, your GP, midwife or local services can help confidentially.
  • Alcohol in pregnancy is the clearest avoidable risk to the developing brain (FASD).

A hopeful, evidence-based reminder: early adversity "does not determine a child's future. With the right support and positive experiences, children can build resilience and thrive." (Anna Freud National Centre)

When to seek help

🤝 Trust your instincts — asking early is strength, not failure

Most worries turn out to be normal variation, and the course is clear that you don't need a diagnosis to get early help. Reach out if:

  • Something about your baby's feeding, growth, movement, hearing/vision or development worries you, or they're clearly missing many milestones for their age (ranges are wide — a "late" skill often arrives in a burst).
  • You're struggling with your own mood, sleep or coping — perinatal low mood is common and treatable.
  • Home feels unsafe or overwhelming.

Who to talk to: your health visitor, GP or midwife first. Family "early-help" services (like the UK's Sure Start family centres) offer health, parenting and practical support as a "one-stop shop" — and evaluations found Sure Start reduced children's hospitalisations and improved later school outcomes. There are also simple parent-completed checklists (e.g. SDQ, PSC) your professional can use — always alongside a conversation, never on their own.

A note on nutrition & vitamins

🥦 Important: this part is not from the course

The Module 4 curriculum focuses on relationships, the developing brain and early support — it does not cover maternal or infant nutrition or vitamins. So rather than invent advice, here are the well-established UK public-health basics to discuss with your midwife, health visitor, GP or pharmacist, who can tailor them to you:

  • In pregnancy & trying to conceive: folic acid and vitamin D are routinely recommended; some families qualify for free NHS Healthy Start vitamins.
  • Breastfed babies: a daily vitamin D supplement is generally advised; formula-fed babies' needs differ.
  • Feeding support (breast or bottle) and weaning from around 6 months: your health visitor and the NHS website are reliable, free sources.

Please don't take doses from this leaflet — confirm what's right for you and your baby with a health professional. (Sources: NHS / your local health service — general public-health guidance, not Module 4.)

Key ideas, in plain words

IdeaWhat it means for you
AttachmentThe lasting bond between baby and main carer; built through everyday responsiveness. Shapes how a child relates to others later.
Secure base & safe havenYou are the safe place a child explores from and returns to for comfort.
Serve-and-returnThe back-and-forth of responding to your baby's cues — the core brain-builder.
Co-regulationYoung children calm down with you before they can do it alone. Your calm is contagious.
Internal working modelsThe "template" a child forms about whether people are reliable and safe — based on early experience.
Neuroplasticity & sensitive periodsThe young brain changes fast and is especially shapeable in early childhood — though "it's never too late."
Executive functionThe brain's "air-traffic-control" — focus, impulse control, remembering steps. Grown through play and routines.
Toxic vs tolerable stressStress buffered by a caring adult is tolerable and even helps growth; severe, prolonged, unsupported stress is the harmful kind.
ResilienceDoing well despite adversity — built by environment and relationships, not something a child simply has or lacks.
