# Morris (2019) — Child & Adolescent Anxiety and Family Accommodation: Exploring the Role of Cognitive Coping

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Morris, J.A. (2019) *Child & Adolescent Anxiety and Family Accommodation: Exploring the Role of Cognitive Coping*. Doctor of Philosophy dissertation, The University of Texas at Austin.

**Evidence tier:** Cross-sectional — Feasibility/pilot (dissertation, n=93, single site)
**Validity estimate:** 45/100
**DOI:** not found (unpublished dissertation)
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Family accommodation significantly predicted anxiety severity at baseline in a treatment-seeking sample of anxious youth (β=0.309, p=0.002), replicating prior findings and extending the accommodation–severity relationship beyond OCD to GAD, Social Anxiety Disorder, and Separation Anxiety Disorder.
2. Cognitive coping (defined as rationalization, acceptance, and optimism) did NOT mediate the relationship between family accommodation and anxiety severity — suggesting the FA→anxiety link operates through mechanisms other than deficits in cognitive reappraisal.
3. Families of children with a primary diagnosis of Separation Anxiety Disorder reported significantly higher levels of family accommodation than families of children with GAD or Social Anxiety Disorder (ANOVA F=3.836, p<.05), consistent with prior research.
4. Distraction coping showed a marginal positive correlation with family accommodation (r=0.235, p=0.089), suggesting that high-accommodation families may inadvertently model or reinforce distraction-based rather than engagement-based coping.
5. A preliminary cognitive coping measure (CQ-cog) was developed with a three-factor structure (rationalization, acceptance, optimism) and a higher-order cognitive coping factor, though it showed low concurrent validity with the CCSC-R1 Positive Cognitive Restructuring domain.

## Numerical findings
- Family accommodation → anxiety severity: β=0.309, SE=0.098, p=0.002
- Cognitive coping mediation: β=−0.007, SE=0.016, p=0.676 (non-significant)
- FA significantly higher in SAD vs. GAD and Social Anxiety: F=3.836, p<.05
- Distraction coping ↔ family accommodation: r=0.235, p=0.089 (trend)
- n=93 participants, ages 7–17, 50% female, 80% White Non-Hispanic
- Primary diagnoses: GAD 57%, Social Anxiety 28%, SAD 15%; mean comorbidity M=2.39

## Study design
Cross-sectional SEM using baseline data from a CBT treatment study (TCSC at University of Texas Austin); n=93 anxious youth ages 7–17 diagnosed with GAD, SAD, or Social Anxiety Disorder via ADIS-IV; measures: FASA (family accommodation), PARS (anxiety severity), experimental CQ-cog (cognitive coping).

## Main limitation
The cognitive coping measure (CQ-cog) was experimental, unvalidated, and showed low concurrent validity, meaning the null mediation result may reflect poor measurement rather than a true absence of a coping mechanism; sample was predominantly White, high-income, and drawn from a single specialist clinic, limiting generalisability.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Supports the argument that family accommodation is a robust predictor of child/adolescent anxiety severity across multiple disorder subtypes, and specifically that SAD is associated with highest accommodation; also useful as a counterpoint suggesting the mechanism linking FA to anxiety may not be cognitive coping per se.
