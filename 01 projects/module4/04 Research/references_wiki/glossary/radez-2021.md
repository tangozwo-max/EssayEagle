# Radez et al. (2022) Adolescents' perceived barriers and facilitators to seeking and accessing professional help for anxiety and depressive disorders: a qualitative interview study. *European Child & Adolescent Psychiatry*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Radez, J., Reardon, T., Creswell, C., Orchard, F. and Waite, P. (2022) 'Adolescents' perceived barriers and facilitators to seeking and accessing professional help for anxiety and depressive disorders: a qualitative interview study', *European Child & Adolescent Psychiatry*, 31(6), pp. 891–907. doi: 10.1007/s00787-020-01707-0

**Evidence tier:** Qualitative interview study (n=22 adolescents with a diagnosed anxiety/depressive disorder) — **Tier T2 (qualitative)**
**Validity estimate:** 72/100 (rich thematic depth; small purposive sample, not generalisable in frequency terms)
**DOI:** 10.1007/s00787-020-01707-0
**PDF status:** full-pdf

## Key usable claims
1. Four barrier themes to accessing help: (i) **difficulty making sense of difficulties** (normalising distress as "just me/just a phase"); (ii) **disclosure barriers** (shame, fear of being a burden); (iii) **ambivalence** (especially adolescent autonomy concerns); (iv) **dependency on adult gatekeepers**.
2. The gatekeeper point is decisive: adolescents typically **cannot self-refer** to CAMHS — they need an adult (parent, teacher) to notice, understand and act, and those adults face their own barriers.
3. Explains *why* the structural access gap (Ball, Abel) persists even where services exist: the route in is mediated and fragile.

## Main limitation
Small qualitative sample (n=22); perceptions not behaviours; no frequency/effect estimates.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
§3 GAP — supplies the *mechanism* of the access gap (the human/relational reasons help-seeking fails), adding critical depth beyond the headline waiting-list statistics. Also motivates the §4 solution of **parent-mediated** delivery (work through the gatekeeper rather than around them).
