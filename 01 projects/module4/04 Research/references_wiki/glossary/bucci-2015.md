# Bucci, Roberts, Danquah and Berry (2015) Using attachment theory to inform the design and delivery of mental health services: a systematic review. *Psychology and Psychotherapy: Theory, Research and Practice*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Bucci, S., Roberts, N.H., Danquah, A.N. and Berry, K. (2015) 'Using attachment theory to inform the design and delivery of mental health services: a systematic review of the literature', Psychology and Psychotherapy: Theory, Research and Practice, 88(1), pp. 1–20. DOI: 10.1111/papt.12029

**Evidence tier:** Systematic review (thematic synthesis) — conceptual/qualitative
**Validity estimate:** 62/100
**DOI:** 10.1111/papt.12029
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. "The attachment system is designed to be a help-seeking system (Bowlby, 1982)" — how a person seeks help when presenting to services reflects their attachment style.
2. "If caregivers are unresponsive or insensitive to distress, the individual either escalates distress to get attachment needs met (insecure ambivalent/anxious) or learns to de-escalate their attachment system (insecure avoidant/dismissing)" — i.e. anticipated unresponsiveness produces withdrawn/avoidant engagement.
3. "Patients with a dismissing attachment style may find it difficult to engage in the therapeutic process," which "is often interpreted by staff as failure to engage but is often understandable based on difficult childhood relational experiences."
4. Stress/vulnerability activates the attachment system; attachment-informed services should provide a secure base, consistency and relational safety across the service-user journey (referral → assessment → intervention → moving on).
5. Staff–patient relational conflict can be a therapeutic opportunity because it signals an activated internal working model available for repair.

## Numerical findings
- 1,105 articles screened → 14 met inclusion criteria; 8 key themes + 4 subthemes extracted (1960–2013 search).

## Study design
Systematic review with thematic analysis of literature applying attachment theory to the design/delivery of general mental-health services "for adults and adolescents."

## Main limitation
Only 14 included papers; the evidence base is **predominantly adult** mental-health services (forensic, inpatient, rehabilitation; effectively one adolescent-specific study), so application to adolescents is a defensible-but-soft extension (attachment is a lifespan framework). Thematic/conceptual synthesis, not outcome evidence.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
"Limitations of current treatments": attachment-informed account of *why adolescents disengage* — needing care is itself a stressor that activates the attachment system, so young people who have learned to anticipate unresponsive support may withdraw from services. Frame explicitly as an "attachment-informed lens" (the essay does) because the evidence base is adult-weighted; for a fully youth-anchored claim, pair with a youth help-seeking source (e.g. Gulliver et al. 2010). Keep shame/normalised-distress/burden on Radez 2022.
