# Skinner et al. (2022) Population mental health improves with increasing access to treatment: evidence from a dynamic modelling analysis. *BMC Psychiatry*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Skinner, A., Occhipinti, J.-A., Song, Y.J.C. and Hickie, I.B. (2022) 'Population mental health improves with increasing access to treatment: evidence from a dynamic modelling analysis', *BMC Psychiatry*, 22, 692. doi: 10.1186/s12888-022-04352-w

**Evidence tier:** Dynamic systems modelling fitted to Australian national data — **Tier T2 (modelling study)**
**Validity estimate:** 76/100 (rigorous system-dynamics method; model assumptions + Australian adult-distress data)
**DOI:** 10.1186/s12888-022-04352-w
**PDF status:** full-pdf

## Key usable claims (the prevention-paradox keystone)
1. Between 2008 and 2019 in Australia, **increasing access to mental-health care produced a real benefit** — but this benefit was **masked by a concurrent rise in the per-capita incidence** of psychological distress that more than offset the treatment gains.
2. The model estimates that **without** the incidence increase, prevalence would have **fallen from 14.4% to 13.6%**. The treatment signal is present; it is simply overwhelmed by rising incidence.
3. Core implication for the essay: **treatment scale-up alone cannot reduce population prevalence while incidence keeps rising** — prevention must run alongside access.

## Numerical findings
- Australian national data, 2008–2019; counterfactual prevalence 14.4% → 13.6% absent the incidence rise.

## Main limitation
Australian, population-level **psychological distress** (not youth-anxiety-specific); model-dependent counterfactuals; no UK youth-anxiety equivalent exists (flagged evidence gap).

## Cite for (essay relevance)
§3/§4 — the bridge between the access gap and the prevention argument: explains *why* fixing access is necessary but not sufficient, and sets up indicated prevention (Skinner 2023) as the second lever. The single best citation for the "prevention paradox."
