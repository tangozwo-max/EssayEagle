# Peris, Thamrin & Rozenman (2021) Family intervention for child and adolescent anxiety. *Journal of Affective Disorders*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Peris, T.S., Thamrin, H. and Rozenman, M.S. (2021) 'Family Intervention for Child and Adolescent Anxiety: A Meta-analytic Review of Therapy Targets, Techniques, and Outcomes', Journal of Affective Disorders, 286, pp. 282–295. DOI: 10.1016/j.jad.2021.02.053

**Evidence tier:** Meta-analysis / SR — Meta-analytic review (k=11 RCTs, CBT+FAM vs. individual CBT)
**Validity estimate:** 62/100
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jad.2021.02.053
**PDF status:** abstract-only

## Key usable claims
1. Family-based approaches for treating child and adolescent anxiety are generally considered efficacious for symptom reduction, but they vary substantially in format and therapeutic approach, making it difficult to determine whether they offer a meaningful advantage over individual child CBT alone.
2. Across the 11 RCTs reviewed, family intervention components (CBT+FAM) most commonly targeted anxious cognitive style (9 of the studies), followed by overprotection/overcontrol/intrusiveness and hostility/criticism (6 each), anxious modelling (5), and family accommodation was targeted in only one study — despite accommodation being a well-documented maintenance mechanism.
3. Only half of the identified RCTs (n=6) included a parent or family functioning measure at both pre- and post-treatment, meaning that most trials lacked the data needed to test whether family-focused components actually changed the parenting behaviours they were designed to target.
4. Only a single trial included anxiety outcome measures at a mid-treatment time point, and none included parent measures at a mid-treatment time point, leaving the change trajectory of family mechanisms during treatment unmapped.
5. The review highlights a critical mismatch between the theoretical mechanisms implicated in the development and maintenance of youth anxiety (e.g., accommodation, overcontrol, anxious modelling) and the mechanisms that are actually assessed in clinical trials of family intervention.
6. Family accommodation — despite strong evidence as a maintaining factor for youth anxiety — was the least targeted mechanism in the identified RCTs, representing a gap between theory and treatment practice.

## Numerical findings
- 11 RCTs identified that compared individual CBT to CBT plus a family component (CBT+FAM) and included a youth anxiety measure at pre- and post-treatment
- Only 6 of the 11 RCTs (approximately half) also included a parent/family functioning measure at both pre- and post-treatment
- 9 of the studies targeted anxious cognitive style
- 6 studies targeted overprotection/overcontrol/intrusiveness
- 6 studies targeted hostility/criticism
- 5 studies targeted anxious modelling
- Only 1 study targeted accommodation
- Only 1 study included anxiety measures at a mid-treatment time point
- 0 studies included parent measures at a mid-treatment time point
- Paper published in Journal of Affective Disorders, Volume 286, 1 May 2021, pages 282–295

## Study design
Meta-analytic review of 11 randomised controlled trials that directly compared individual CBT to CBT augmented with a family/parenting component (CBT+FAM) for child and adolescent anxiety, examining therapeutic targets, assessment timelines, and youth and family outcomes.

## Main limitation
The majority of included RCTs failed to measure parent or family functioning outcomes at both pre- and post-treatment, and no study tracked parenting mechanisms at a mid-treatment time point, making it impossible to determine whether family components produced their intended change in parenting behaviour.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Best used to support arguments about family accommodation and parental transmission as under-targeted but theoretically central maintenance mechanisms in child and adolescent anxiety treatment, and to critique the evidence base for family CBT by showing that most RCTs do not adequately measure the family-level change processes they are designed to produce.
