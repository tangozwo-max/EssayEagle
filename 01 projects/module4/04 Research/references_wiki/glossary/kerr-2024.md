# Kerr et al. (2025) Associations between social media use and anxiety among adolescents. *Journal of Adolescent Health*.

## Key usable claims
1. More than half of the 32 included studies (56.3%) reported positive associations between adolescent social media use and anxiety, indicating that social media use is more often linked to elevated anxiety than not.
2. The association between social media use and anxiety is strongest for problematic use measures (75.0% of studies reporting positive associations) and screen time (72.7%), suggesting that how social media is measured substantially shapes conclusions.
3. For social media measures other than problematic use and screen time, only 40.9% of studies showed positive associations, highlighting that the direction of association varies considerably depending on the construct measured.
4. Across the 32 included studies, 18 different anxiety measures were used, indicating substantial heterogeneity in outcome assessment that limits direct comparability of findings.
5. Only 4 studies (12.5%) stratified findings by gender identity, revealing a significant gap in understanding how social media-anxiety associations differ across demographic subgroups.
6. The mean study quality score on the STROBE checklist was 34.1 out of 46 (SD=4.3), suggesting moderate but imperfect reporting quality across the included observational literature.

## Numerical findings
- 32 studies included in the review
- 56.3% of studies reported positive associations between social media use and anxiety
- Problematic use measures: 31.4% most common social media measure type
- Problematic use measures associated with positive anxiety outcomes in 75.0% of studies
- Screen time measures associated with positive anxiety outcomes in 72.7% of studies
- Other social media measures showed positive associations in 40.9% of studies
- 18 different anxiety measures used across included studies
- 4 studies (12.5%) stratified findings by gender identity
- Mean STROBE score: 34.1 (SD=4.3) out of 46

## Study design
Systematic review of 32 English-language studies published before 2021 examining associations between adolescent social media use and anxiety, with dual-reviewer screening and data extraction, assessing direction of association, social media and anxiety measure types, demographic stratification, and study quality via STROBE.

## Main limitation
The review is limited to studies published before 2021 and the full methods/results are behind a paywall in the retrieved PDF; additionally, the predominance of cross-sectional designs among included studies prevents causal inference about the direction of the social media-anxiety relationship.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support the argument that social media is a systemic environmental risk factor for adolescent anxiety, particularly when framing how problematic or excessive use patterns — rather than mere exposure — are most consistently linked to elevated anxiety symptoms in young people.
