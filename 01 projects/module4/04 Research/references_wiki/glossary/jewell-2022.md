# Jewell, Wittkowski and Pratt (2022) Parent-only interventions for child anxiety. *Journal of Affective Disorders*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Jewell, C., Wittkowski, A. and Pratt, D. (2022) 'The impact of parent-only interventions on child anxiety: A systematic review and meta-analysis', Journal of Affective Disorders, 309, pp. 324–349. DOI: 10.1016/j.jad.2022.04.082

**Evidence tier:** Meta-analysis / SR — Systematic review and meta-analysis (k=29 studies, including 13 RCTs)
**Validity estimate:** 65/100
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jad.2022.04.082
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Parent-only interventions produce a small but statistically significant reduction in child anxiety symptoms compared to waitlist controls when measured by parent report (g = -0.48), supporting them as a viable low-intensity treatment option.
2. Parent-only interventions are not significantly inferior to child-focused CBT or combined parent-and-child interventions: anxiety symptoms reduced across all active treatment conditions with no significant between-group differences.
3. No significant treatment effect was found for child self-reported anxiety outcomes when comparing parent-only interventions to waitlist controls (g = -0.16, p = .20), indicating that children's own ratings do not consistently reflect parent-observed gains.
4. Treatment gains from parent-only interventions are generally maintained or improved at follow-up, with 13 out of 15 studies reporting maintained diagnostic outcomes and 13 out of 16 reporting maintained symptom reductions.
5. Up to 60% of children whose parents have an anxiety disorder themselves also meet diagnostic criteria for an anxiety disorder, highlighting parental anxiety as a key aetiological and maintaining factor for child anxiety.
6. Parent-only interventions can target specific parenting mechanisms linked to child anxiety — including overprotective and controlling parenting styles, parental modelling of anxious cognitions, and family accommodation — that child-focused CBT alone does not directly address.

## Numerical findings
- k = 29 studies included (published 1999–2020); 13 RCTs, 4 pilot trials, 2 feasibility trials, 2 cohort studies, 3 uncontrolled pre/post, 5 case series
- Total N across all studies = 2,916 participants (range 6–1,253)
- Meta-analysis (parent-rated, vs. waitlist): g = -0.48, 95% CI -0.90 to -0.07, z = 2.28, p = .02; I² = 83%
- Meta-analysis (parent-rated, vs. active comparison): g = 0.13, 95% CI -0.08 to 0.34, z = 1.22, p = .22; I² = 20%
- Meta-analysis (child-rated, vs. waitlist): g = -0.16, 95% CI -0.40 to 0.08, z = 1.29, p = .20; I² = 49%
- Meta-analysis (child-rated, vs. active comparison): g = 0.16, 95% CI -0.04 to 0.36, z = 1.58, p = .11; I² = 2%
- 19/29 studies (65.5%) rated 'weak' on EPHPP global quality rating; only 4/29 (13.8%) rated 'strong'
- Over 70% of parent-rated outcomes showed significant change post-treatment vs. 50% of child-rated outcomes
- Up to 60% of children of anxious parents also met diagnostic criteria for an anxiety disorder (Ginsburg and Schlossberg, 2002, cited within)
- Intervention duration ranged from 4 to 22 weeks; most interventions ran 10 or 12 weeks
- Uncontrolled pre/post effect sizes (parent-rated): d = -4.054 (Mayer-Brien et al.), d = -1.193 to -1.417 (Esbjørn et al., mothers), d = -0.923 (Thienemann et al.)
- Egger's test for publication bias: parent-rated non-significant (z = -0.902, p = .367); child-rated significant (z = -2.119, p = .034)

## Study design
Systematic review and random-effects meta-analysis of 29 quantitative studies (including 13 RCTs) identified through searches of five databases (PsycINFO, CINAHL Plus, EMBASE, Medline, Web of Science; inception to March 2021), enrolling a total of 2,916 parents of school-aged children (ages 4–17) with diagnosed anxiety disorders across studies conducted in the USA, Australia, UK, Canada, and Europe.

## Main limitation
The methodological quality of 19 out of 29 included studies was rated 'weak' using the EPHPP tool, primarily due to selection bias from participant self-referral, and the meta-analyses were based on a small number of studies (minimum n=5, maximum n=8), which can produce unstable estimates of between-study variance and potentially overinflated summary effects.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Cite to support arguments about parental transmission and family-level maintaining factors in child anxiety: this review provides quantitative evidence that parent-only interventions targeting parental behaviours (overprotection, modelling of anxious cognitions, family accommodation) can significantly reduce child anxiety symptoms, and it documents that up to 60% of children of anxious parents meet criteria for an anxiety disorder themselves — directly supporting the case for systemic, family-focused approaches within a stepped-care model.
