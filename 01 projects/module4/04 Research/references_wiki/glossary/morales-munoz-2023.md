# Morales-Muñoz et al. (2023) Impact of anxiety and depression across childhood and adolescence on adverse outcomes in young adulthood: a UK birth cohort study. *British Journal of Psychiatry*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Morales-Muñoz, I., Mallikarjun, P.K., Chandan, J.S., Thayakaran, R., Upthegrove, R. and Marwaha, S. (2023) 'Impact of anxiety and depression across childhood and adolescence on adverse outcomes in young adulthood: a UK birth cohort study', *British Journal of Psychiatry*, 222(5), pp. 212–220. doi: 10.1192/bjp.2023.23

**Evidence tier:** Longitudinal birth cohort (ALSPAC, n=8,122; DAWBA diagnostic assessment at 8/10/13 → outcomes at 24) — **Tier T1 (cohort)**
**Validity estimate:** 82/100 (large UK cohort, formal diagnosis; attrition + observational)
**DOI:** 10.1192/bjp.2023.23
**PDF status:** full-pdf

## Key usable claims
1. Latent-class trajectories of persistent anxiety, persistent depression, and **comorbid** anxiety-and-depression were each significantly associated with mental-health problems and education/employment difficulties at age 24.
2. The **comorbid trajectory carried the heaviest burden** by every outcome (highest odds ratios), and was the *only* group significantly associated with substance misuse and with physical-health problems.
3. Persistent high anxiety *alone* did not independently predict physical-health problems — the damage accrues once the anxiety→depression comorbidity sets in.
4. Strongly supports intervening **before** the anxiety–depression cascade locks in (the "why act early" keystone).

## Numerical findings
- n = 8,122 (ALSPAC); diagnostic assessment via DAWBA at ages 8, 10, 13; outcomes at 24.
- Comorbid group: highest ORs for adverse young-adult outcomes across domains; only group linked to substance misuse.

## Main limitation
Observational cohort (no causal attribution); ALSPAC attrition and the usual healthy-volunteer bias; trajectories are latent-class estimates.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
§1 NEED — grounds the urgency: untreated childhood anxiety does not stay contained; it cascades (especially into comorbid depression) and predicts worse young-adult mental health, education/employment, and substance outcomes. The strongest UK evidence for "act early."
