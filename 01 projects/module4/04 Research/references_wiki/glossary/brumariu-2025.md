# Brumariu et al. (2025) Parental emotion socialization and internalizing problems. *Development and Psychopathology*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Brumariu, L.E., Nair, T.K., Waslin, S.M., Rodrigues, G.A., Moore, M.T. and Kerns, K.A. (2025) 'Parental emotion socialization and internalizing problems in childhood and adolescence: A meta-analytic review', Development and Psychopathology, 37(5), pp. 2806–2826. DOI: 10.1017/S095457942510059X

**Evidence tier:** Meta-analysis / SR — Meta-analytic review (three meta-analyses: supportive ES k=50, nonsupportive ES k=47, elaboration k=6)
**Validity estimate:** 78/100
**DOI:** 10.1017/S095457942510059X
**PDF status:** abstract-only

## Key usable claims
1. Nonsupportive parental emotion socialisation (ES) behaviours — such as dismissing, punishing, or minimising children's emotions — show a medium positive association with children's internalising problems (anxiety and depression) across childhood and adolescence.
2. Supportive ES behaviours show only a very small negative (protective) association with internalising problems, suggesting that the absence of support alone is not sufficient to explain internalising risk.
3. Parental elaboration on children's emotional experiences (emotion-coaching style of co-reminiscing) shows a small negative association with internalising problems, indicating a modest protective effect.
4. Very few significant moderators were identified, and no differences based on parent gender (mother vs. father) were found, suggesting the associations are broadly consistent across family roles.
5. The findings suggest that incorporating an emotion socialisation framework into prevention and intervention programmes could benefit children at risk for anxiety and depression.
6. Anxiety and depression are identified as the leading mental health problems among children in Western societies, framing ES as a key modifiable family-level risk factor.

## Numerical findings
- Supportive ES behaviours: r = -.06 (very small negative association with internalising problems; k = 50, N = 10,698)
- Nonsupportive ES behaviours: r = .18 (medium positive association with internalising problems; k = 47, N = 10,970)
- Elaboration: r = -.11 (small negative association with internalising problems; k = 6, N = 867)
- Total sample for supportive ES meta-analysis: N = 10,698
- Total sample for nonsupportive ES meta-analysis: N = 10,970
- Total sample for elaboration meta-analysis: N = 867

## Study design
Systematic review and three separate meta-analyses synthesising studies examining parental emotion socialisation behaviours (supportive, nonsupportive, and elaboration) in relation to internalising problems (anxiety and depression) in children and adolescents aged birth to 18 years.

## Main limitation
The review is limited to abstract-visible information; the full limitations section is paywalled, but a likely design limitation is that most constituent studies are cross-sectional, precluding causal inference about the direction of effects between ES behaviours and child internalising problems.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support the argument that parental emotion socialisation — particularly nonsupportive parental responses to children's negative emotions — constitutes a significant family-level systemic risk factor for child and adolescent anxiety and depression, with nonsupportive ES showing a medium effect size (r = .18) across nearly 11,000 participants.
