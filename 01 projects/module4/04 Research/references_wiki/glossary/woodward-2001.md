# Woodward & Fergusson (2001) Life course outcomes of young people with anxiety disorders in adolescence. *Journal of the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Woodward, L.J. and Fergusson, D.M. (2001) 'Life course outcomes of young people with anxiety disorders in adolescence', *Journal of the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry*, 40(9), pp. 1086–1093. doi: 10.1097/00004583-200109000-00018

**Evidence tier:** Longitudinal birth cohort (Christchurch Health and Development Study, n=1,265) — **Tier T1 (cohort)**
**Validity estimate:** 80/100 (long follow-up, confounder-adjusted; older study, NZ cohort)
**DOI:** 10.1097/00004583-200109000-00018
**PDF status:** full-pdf

## Key usable claims
1. Anxiety disorder in adolescence (ages 14–16) predicted, in young adulthood (age 21), elevated rates of **anxiety, depression, illicit drug dependence, and educational underachievement** — broad **heterotypic continuity**.
2. Associations survived adjustment for confounding social/family factors, with odds ratios typically in the **2–3** range.
3. The breadth of adverse outcomes (not just later anxiety) makes this a strong citation for the long shadow of untreated youth anxiety.

## Numerical findings
- n = 1,265; anxiety assessed at 14–16, outcomes at 21; adjusted ORs ≈ 2–3 across outcome domains.

## Main limitation
Observational; single NZ birth cohort; older diagnostic criteria; cannot isolate causal effect of anxiety from correlated adversity despite adjustment.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
§1 NEED — the classic heterotypic-continuity citation: adolescent anxiety forecasts a spread of adult problems, reinforcing that failing to treat early is costly across the life course.
