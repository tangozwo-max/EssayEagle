# Rapee et al. (2017) Stepped care vs single CBT for youth anxiety. *Journal of the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry*.

## Key usable claims
1. Stepped care CBT and a single empirically validated CBT programme for youth anxiety produced equivalent outcomes — no significant differences on any outcome measure.
2. Stepped care delivered treatment in significantly less total therapist time (774 minutes) compared with best-practice standard CBT (897 minutes), suggesting efficiency advantages without sacrificing efficacy.
3. Within the stepped-care model, the first two steps (low-intensity intervention and standard CBT) accounted for the strongest treatment gains, suggesting that most children may not require the more intensive third step.
4. Restricting stepped care to only steps 1 and 2 would have produced considerable time savings with only a modest loss in overall efficacy, supporting a resource-allocation rationale for tiered service delivery.
5. Stepped care is widely advocated as an ideal model of mental health service delivery for young people but had been minimally evaluated by empirical trial prior to this study.
6. The study used a three-step stepped-care framework (low intensity; standard CBT; individually tailored treatment) with therapist qualifications increasing at each step, testing the principle that treatment intensity should be matched to clinical need.

## Numerical findings
- n = 281 youth with anxiety disorders, aged 6–17 years
- Total therapist time per child: stepped care = 774 minutes; best-practice standard CBT = 897 minutes
- No significant differences between conditions on any outcome measure
- Three-step model: step 1 = low intensity; step 2 = standard CBT; step 3 = individually tailored treatment
- Trial registration: ACTRN12612000351819

## Study design
Randomised clinical trial allocating 281 youth (aged 6–17) with anxiety disorders to either a single empirically validated CBT programme or a three-step stepped-care CBT framework, comparing clinical outcomes and therapist time across conditions.

## Main limitation
Full limitations section is behind a paywall and not visible; from the design, a key limitation is that the study does not report long-term follow-up data in the abstract, and the generalisability of therapist time savings to routine clinical settings (rather than a research context) cannot be assessed from the abstract alone.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use to support the argument that stepped-care CBT is a viable and efficient systemic delivery model for child and adolescent anxiety, offering equivalent clinical outcomes to gold-standard single-protocol CBT while reducing therapist resource demands — directly relevant to discussions of service-level responses to anxiety disorders in young people.
