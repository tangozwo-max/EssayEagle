# Krause, Zhang & Schneider (2024) Long-term effectiveness of cognitive behavioral therapy in routine outpatient care for youth with anxiety disorders. *Psychotherapy and Psychosomatics*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Krause, K., Zhang, X.C. and Schneider, S. (2024) 'Long-term effectiveness of cognitive behavioral therapy in routine outpatient care for youth with anxiety disorders', *Psychotherapy and Psychosomatics*, 93(3), pp. 181–190. doi: 10.1159/000537932

**Evidence tier:** Longitudinal effectiveness study (routine outpatient care, n=210; mean 4.3-year follow-up) — **Tier T2 (effectiveness cohort)**
**Validity estimate:** 78/100
**DOI:** 10.1159/000537932
**PDF status:** full-pdf

## Key usable claims
1. **61% in full remission at end of treatment, 64% at mean 4.3-year follow-up** — i.e. gains were maintained, not eroded.
2. Long-term effect sizes were large (**Hedges' g = 2.34** in child report) and comparable to post-treatment, with **no deterioration**.
3. The protocol included **booster sessions and high fidelity** — suggesting durable maintenance is achievable as a matter of *care quality/continuity*, not an inherent limit of CBT.

## Numerical findings
- n = 210; remission 61% → 64% (end of treatment → 4.3y); long-term g = 2.34 (child report).

## Main limitation
German routine-care sample of treatment *completers* (not ITT), which favours maintenance estimates; no inactive control at follow-up.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
§3 — the counter-point that reconciles the durability debate: where CAMELS shows ~48% relapse, well-delivered routine care with boosters maintains gains. Frames durability as a **continuity-of-care** problem the access-scaling solutions must not neglect.
