# Li et al. (2025) Screen time and adolescent depression and anxiety. *British Journal of Clinical Psychology*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Li, S.H., Batterham, P.J., Whitton, A.E., Maston, K., Khan, A., Christensen, H. and Werner-Seidler, A. (2025) 'Cross-sectional and longitudinal associations of screen time with adolescent depression and anxiety', British Journal of Clinical Psychology, 64, pp. 873–887. DOI: 10.1111/bjc.12547

**Evidence tier:** Longitudinal cohort — Longitudinal prospective cohort (n=4,058; 12-month follow-up nested within a 5-year study)
**Validity estimate:** 72/100
**DOI:** 10.1111/bjc.12547
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Screen time is associated with adolescent depression and anxiety cross-sectionally, but longitudinal associations over 12 months are markedly weaker for depression and absent for anxiety, challenging the assumption that screen time causes these conditions.
2. The discrepancy between strong cross-sectional and weak longitudinal associations suggests a bidirectional relationship: adolescents with higher symptoms may increase screen use (e.g., via social withdrawal, mood regulation through distraction) rather than screen time driving symptoms.
3. Maladaptive social media use — defined as using social media for negative social comparisons — did not substantially moderate or independently predict screen time associations with depression or anxiety in longitudinal analyses, contrary to expectations.
4. Gender influenced cross-sectional associations, with females showing a stronger link between screen time and depression than males, but this gender difference was not present in longitudinal analyses nor for anxiety.
5. Gender diverse adolescents reported substantially higher depression and anxiety than both male and female peers at both time points, representing a significant mental health disparity.
6. The study concludes that screen time is unlikely to be a direct cause of adolescent depression and anxiety; future research should use experimental designs and more granular measures of specific digital behaviours rather than generic screen time.

## Numerical findings
- n = 4,058 Australian Year 8 students; mean age 13.9 years (SD = 0.58); recruited from 134 schools
- Cross-sectional: each additional hour of screen time corresponded with a 1.25-point increase in PHQ-A depression scores (p < .001, 95% CI 1.12–1.37)
- Cross-sectional: each additional hour of screen time corresponded with a 0.79-point increase in CAS-8 anxiety scores (p < .001, 95% CI 0.68–0.90)
- 5+ hours daily screen time estimated to correspond with a 6.25-point increase in PHQ-A relative to 0–1 h cross-sectionally
- 5+ hours daily screen time estimated to correspond with a 3.95-point increase in CAS-8 relative to 0–1 h cross-sectionally
- Longitudinal (12 months): each additional hour of screen time corresponded with only a 0.15-point increase in PHQ-A (p = .007, 95% CI 0.04–0.26)
- Longitudinal (12 months): screen time was not significantly associated with anxiety at 12 months (p = .443)
- Gender diverse adolescents had PHQ-A mean 15.97 (SD 6.99) vs. female 8.39 (SD 6.12) and male 5.31 (SD 5.08) at Time 1
- Gender diverse adolescents had CAS-8 mean 13.85 (SD 5.84) vs. female 10.04 (SD 5.20) and male 5.85 (SD 4.45) at Time 1
- 27.1% of the total sample reported 5+ hours of daily screen time; 51.8% of gender diverse participants reported 5+ hours
- Correlation between screen time and PHQ-A at baseline: r = .312 (p < .01); screen time and CAS-8 at baseline: r = .230 (p < .01)
- Correlation between screen time and PHQ-A at 12 months: r = .216 (p < .01); screen time and CAS-8 at 12 months: r = .156 (p < .01)

## Study design
5-year prospective cohort study (Future Proofing Study) drawing on Time 1 baseline and 12-month (Time 2) follow-up data from 4,058 Australian Year 8 adolescents (mean age 13.9 years) recruited from 134 schools, using linear mixed models with school as a random effect to examine cross-sectional and longitudinal associations of self-reported screen time with depression (PHQ-A) and anxiety (CAS-8), controlling for baseline symptom severity in longitudinal analyses.

## Main limitation
Screen time was measured with a single, unvalidated self-report item that does not distinguish between different types or motivations for screen use (e.g., gaming, passive scrolling, social communication), limiting the ability to identify which digital behaviours specifically drive or do not drive mental health outcomes.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to challenge the claim that social media and screen time are direct systemic causes of adolescent anxiety: it provides large-cohort longitudinal evidence that cross-sectional associations are substantially inflated and that the relationship is more likely bidirectional, with anxious/depressed adolescents increasing screen use as a coping mechanism rather than screen time producing anxiety.
