# Yoshizawa et al. (2026) Screen time, child depression, and anxiety during COVID-19. *JMIR Pediatrics and Parenting*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Yoshizawa, M., Rafeedie, J., Tang, J.J., Lei, B.T., Durazo-Arvizu, R., Azucar, D., Hudson, S., Rao, S., Imagawa, K.K. and Deavenport-Saman, A. (2026) 'Screen time, child depression, and anxiety during the COVID-19 pandemic: systematic review and meta-analysis', JMIR Pediatrics and Parenting, 9, e83228. DOI: 10.2196/83228

**Evidence tier:** Meta-analysis / SR — Systematic review and meta-analysis (k=23 studies, N=29,581)
**Validity estimate:** 72/100
**DOI:** 10.2196/83228
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Higher levels of screen time during the COVID-19 pandemic were significantly associated with increased depression and anxiety in children and adolescents, with small but consistent pooled effect sizes across 23 global studies.
2. Problematic or disordered screen use (e.g., gaming disorder, compulsive smartphone use) showed a stronger association with anxiety than screen time measured simply as duration of use, with approximately a 0.15 higher correlation coefficient.
3. The association between screen time and poor mental health held across different types of screen activity, including social media use, television viewing, video gaming, and general digital media time.
4. Age and gender did not significantly moderate the relationship between screen time and either depression or anxiety outcomes in meta-regression analyses.
5. The COVID-19 pandemic functioned as a natural experiment that greatly amplified youth screen exposure — one cited meta-analysis found a 52% increase in screen time, with adolescents averaging nearly 6 hours per day — creating conditions that magnified mental health risks.
6. The findings underscore the importance of ongoing parental monitoring of screen quality and quantity, and family-level media planning, particularly during national or global emergencies when screen use intensifies.

## Numerical findings
- Pooled correlation between screen time and depression: r=0.175, 95% CI 0.124–0.226, P<.001 (k=22 studies)
- Pooled correlation between screen time and anxiety: r=0.157, 95% CI 0.0994–0.214, P<.001 (k=19 studies)
- Total participants across included studies: N=29,581 children and adolescents
- Number of included studies: k=23
- High heterogeneity for depression: I²=95%, Q=644.22, P<.001
- High heterogeneity for anxiety: I²=95%, Q=760.16, P<.001
- Problematic use studies had approximately 0.15 higher correlation with anxiety than duration-based studies (Fisher z=0.1585)
- Sensitivity analysis depression (outlier removed): r=0.158, 95% CI 0.114–0.202, P<.001
- Sensitivity analysis anxiety (outlier removed): r=0.135, 95% CI 0.0890–0.181, P<.001
- Mean age of participants across studies: 13.54 years (SD 0.43)
- 49% male (n=14,390), 51% female (n=15,191)
- Studies from 10 countries: US (n=6), China (n=6), Canada (n=3), Hong Kong (n=1), Hungary (n=1), Italy (n=2), India (n=1), Iran (n=1), Poland (n=1), South Korea (n=1)
- Global pandemic prevalence of anxiety and depression in youth doubled to 20–25% (cited background statistic)
- US emergency visits for child mental health rose 31–50% since pandemic onset (cited background statistic)

## Study design
Systematic review and meta-analysis of 23 observational studies (predominantly cross-sectional) involving 29,581 children and adolescents aged 8 to under 18 years, drawn from 10 countries across North America, Europe, and Asia, examining associations between screen time and validated measures of depression and/or anxiety during the COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2023).

## Main limitation
The majority of included studies were cross-sectional, preventing causal inference about the direction of the relationship between screen time and mental health; additionally, results are most representative of adolescents (mean age 13.54 years) and may not generalise to younger children aged 0–12 years, and screen time was measured via self- or parent-report, introducing recall bias.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support the argument that social media and digital screen use constitute a systemic environmental risk factor for child and adolescent anxiety and depression, particularly in contexts (such as pandemic-related social isolation) where screen exposure intensifies and protective social connectedness is reduced — directly relevant to discussions of social media as a driver of the adolescent mental health crisis.
