# Dunn et al. (2024) Unguided online intervention for anxious parents prevents child anxiety. *The Lancet Regional Health – Europe*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Dunn, A., Alvarez, J., Arbon, A., Bremner, S., Elsby-Pearson, C., Emsley, R., Jones, C., Lawrence, P., Lester, K.J., Morson, N., Simner, J., Thomson, A. and Cartwright-Hatton, S. (2024) 'Effectiveness of an unguided modular online intervention for highly anxious parents in preventing anxiety in their children: a parallel group randomised controlled trial', The Lancet Regional Health – Europe, 45, 101038. DOI: 10.1016/j.lanepe.2024.101038

**Evidence tier:** RCT — Parallel-group RCT (n=1,811 parent–child dyads, pre-registered)
**Validity estimate:** 82/100
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.lanepe.2024.101038
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. An unguided, fully online CBT-based intervention for highly anxious parents significantly reduced parent-reported child anxiety at 6-month follow-up, demonstrating that intergenerational transmission of anxiety can be interrupted without any clinician input.
2. The intervention also reduced parent anxiety (SCARED-A) and child internalising symptoms, but showed no effect on externalising or attentional symptoms, suggesting a specificity consistent with its anxiety-focused mechanism.
3. A clear dose–response relationship was observed: parents who completed all eight modules produced a child anxiety effect size of −0.91, comparable to face-to-face CBT for children with existing anxiety diagnoses.
4. Intervention effects were maintained at the longer-term follow-up (9–25 months post-consent), indicating durable impact rather than short-term fluctuation.
5. The study is the first known RCT of an online intervention specifically designed to limit the intergenerational transmission of anxiety from parent to child, extending earlier face-to-face trial evidence to a scalable digital format.
6. Children with higher baseline anxiety (particularly separation anxiety, social phobia, and generalised anxiety subscales) showed greater intervention benefit, suggesting the approach is especially valuable for already at-risk children of anxious parents.

## Numerical findings
- N = 1,811 randomised (intervention = 900; control = 911)
- 92.7% of participants female; 85.3% White British; 66.8% university educated
- Retention at T2 (6 months): 67.6% overall; intervention arm 57.3% vs control arm 77.8%
- Primary outcome effect size (Cohen's d) = −0.16 (95% CI: −0.23 to −0.08, p < 0.001)
- Standardised SCAS score difference between arms: −0.15 SDs
- Raw score reduction: SCAS-P −2.38 (95% CI: −3.59 to −1.16); Preschool SCAS −2.68 (95% CI: −4.05 to −1.31)
- PSC internalising subscale effect size d = −0.10 (95% CI: −0.43 to −0.07, p = 0.007)
- Parental anxiety (SCARED-A) effect size d = −0.17 (95% CI: −5.10 to −1.93, p < 0.001)
- T3 (9–25 months) SCAS effect size d = −0.15 (95% CI: −0.22 to −0.08, p < 0.001)
- CACE analysis: effect size for full completers (8 modules) increased from −0.16 to −0.91
- 73.7% of intervention participants started at least 1 module; only 19.0% completed all 8 modules
- 96.8% of participants scored at or above the clinical cut-off on SCARED-A at baseline
- Societal cost of childhood anxiety cited as £4,679 per child per annum
- A child whose mother has an anxiety disorder is twice as likely to develop anxiety than a peer whose mother does not (cited statistic from prior literature)

## Study design
Parallel-group, block-randomised controlled trial (1:1 allocation, unblinded participants) conducted entirely online with n=1,811 UK community-based parents with self-reported substantial anxiety who had a child aged 2–11 years; the intervention comprised 8 CBT-based online modules (~20 min each, unguided); primary outcome was parent-reported child anxiety (SCAS-P / Preschool SCAS) at 6-month follow-up with an additional exploratory follow-up at 9–25 months.

## Main limitation
The trial suffered substantial differential attrition between arms (intervention 57.3% vs control 77.8% retained at 6 months), which the authors attribute to a carry-over effect from intervention disengagement, though sensitivity analyses suggested the primary conclusions were robust; additionally, the sample was predominantly White British, female, and university-educated, limiting generalisability.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support the argument that parental transmission of anxiety is modifiable through targeted parenting interventions — specifically to evidence that anxiogenic parenting behaviours mediate intergenerational anxiety transfer and that even a brief, low-cost, clinically unguided digital intervention addressing these behaviours can significantly reduce anxiety in children of highly anxious parents.
