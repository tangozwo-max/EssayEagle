# Skinner et al. (2023) Population-level effectiveness of alternative approaches to preventing mental disorders in adolescents and young adults. *Scientific Reports*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Skinner, A., Occhipinti, J.-A., Song, Y.J.C. and Hickie, I.B. (2023) 'Population-level effectiveness of alternative approaches to preventing mental disorders in adolescents and young adults', *Scientific Reports*, 13, 19982. doi: 10.1038/s41598-023-47322-2

**Evidence tier:** Dynamic modelling analysis — **Tier T2 (modelling study)**
**Validity estimate:** 76/100
**DOI:** 10.1038/s41598-023-47322-2
**PDF status:** full-pdf

## Key usable claims
1. **Indicated prevention** (targeting subthreshold/at-risk youth) is often **more effective at reducing population disorder prevalence than universal prevention** — challenging the assumed superiority of whole-population strategies.
2. The **subthreshold group is a key leverage point**: intervening just below clinical threshold yields larger population returns than universal screening alone.
3. Provides the evidence base for *which* prevention to invest in — supports indicated programmes (brief CBT/SSIs for screen-identified high-risk youth + parent psychoeducation).

## Main limitation
Modelling study with structural assumptions; population-level (not anxiety-disorder-specific) outcomes; results depend on prevention-effect parameters drawn from the wider literature.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
§4 SOLUTION — directs the prevention recommendation toward **indicated** rather than universal programmes, which fits the essay's "be honest about prevention's limits" stance (universal underdelivers for anxiety; indicated is the better bet for the incidence overlay).
