# Lebowitz et al. (2020) SPACE vs CBT noninferiority RCT for childhood anxiety. *Journal of the American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Lebowitz, E.R., Marin, C., Martino, A., Shimshoni, Y. and Silverman, W.K. (2020) 'Parent-Based Treatment as Efficacious as Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Childhood Anxiety: A Randomized Noninferiority Study of Supportive Parenting for Anxious Childhood Emotions', Journal of the American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry, 59(3), pp. 362–372. DOI: 10.1016/j.jaac.2019.02.014.

**Evidence tier:** RCT — RCT — randomized controlled noninferiority trial (N=124, two active treatment arms)
**Validity estimate:** 82/100
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jaac.2019.02.014
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. A parent-only treatment (SPACE) targeting family accommodation was found to be non-inferior to individual CBT on all primary and secondary anxiety outcomes in children aged 7–14, establishing it as a viable alternative to the current standard of care.
2. Family accommodation — parental behaviors that help children avoid or escape anxiety-provoking situations — is linked to greater child anxiety severity, poorer functional outcomes, and may predict worse response to CBT, making it a central treatment target.
3. SPACE produced significantly greater reductions in parent-rated family accommodation than CBT, and accommodation reductions occurred earlier and more linearly in SPACE (48% in the first half) compared to CBT (65% in the second half), suggesting accommodation reduction may precede and drive anxiety improvement rather than follow it.
4. Both SPACE and CBT produced significant reductions in parenting stress, with no significant difference between treatments, indicating that parent-focused intervention does not uniquely burden parents.
5. Treatment response rates (SPACE 87.5%; CBT 75.5%) and remission rates (SPACE 68.8%; CBT 63.3% on ADIS C/P) did not differ significantly between groups, supporting equivalent clinical benefit.
6. High treatment credibility and client satisfaction ratings were reported for both conditions and did not differ significantly, supporting SPACE as broadly acceptable to families.

## Numerical findings
- N=124 children aged 7–14 (mean age 9.6 years, SD=2.45; 53% female); SPACE n=64, CBT n=60
- 78% (n=97) completed all treatment sessions and post-treatment assessments
- Treatment response (CGI-I): SPACE 87.5%, CBT 75.5% (chi-squared=3.2, p=0.7, ns)
- Remission (CGI-S): SPACE 58.3%, CBT 59.2% (chi-squared=0.02, p=0.88, ns)
- Remission (ADIS C/P): SPACE 68.8%, CBT 63.3% (chi-squared=0.32, p=0.57, ns)
- Family accommodation interaction effect: F(INTERACTION)=3.51, p<0.01 (SPACE superior to CBT)
- 48% of accommodation reduction in SPACE occurred in the first half of treatment; 65% of reduction in CBT occurred in the second half
- Baseline PARS mean: SPACE 19.89 (SD=4.36), CBT 18.65 (SD=4.43); post-treatment PARS: SPACE 7.88 (SD=3.79), CBT 8.98 (SD=4.69)
- Baseline FASA total: SPACE 16.7 (SD=7.89), CBT 14.2 (SD=8.39); post-treatment FASA: SPACE 8.52 (SD=6.8), CBT 7.68 (SD=6.11)
- Baseline PSI: SPACE 131.32 (SD=19.53), CBT 137.63 (SD=20.96); post-treatment PSI: SPACE 82.69 (SD=13.14), CBT 83.7 (SD=11.73)
- 75% of the sample had at least two anxiety disorders; 48.4% had at least one non-anxiety comorbid diagnosis
- Primary diagnoses: GAD 35.2%, social phobia 34.8%, separation anxiety disorder 18.2%, specific phobia 11.8%
- PARS noninferiority margin set at 6 points; 97.5% CI for mean difference between SPACE and CBT lay entirely below this margin (p<0.001)
- Inter-rater reliability for PARS: r=0.9; ICC>0.9 for all pre- and post-treatment ratings

## Study design
Randomized controlled noninferiority trial comparing 12 sessions of SPACE (parent-only, N=64) to 12 sessions of exposure-based individual CBT (child-only, N=60) in children aged 7–14 with primary DSM-5 anxiety disorders, with blinded independent evaluators assessing outcomes at baseline, mid-treatment, and post-treatment.

## Main limitation
The trial lacked an inactive or waitlist control arm (common in noninferiority designs), the sample was predominantly white (83%) and of medium-to-high socioeconomic status, and parental involvement in the CBT arm was restricted to ensure treatment differentiation, differing from typical CBT practice.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support the argument that family accommodation is a clinically significant and modifiable systemic factor in childhood anxiety: it provides RCT-level evidence that a parent-only intervention explicitly targeting accommodation reduction achieves outcomes equivalent to gold-standard CBT, while producing significantly greater reductions in accommodation, demonstrating that parental behaviour change is itself a sufficient mechanism for child anxiety improvement.
