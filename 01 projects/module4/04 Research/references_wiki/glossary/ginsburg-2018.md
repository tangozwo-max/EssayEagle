# Ginsburg et al. (2018) Results from the Child/Adolescent Anxiety Multimodal Extended Long-Term Study (CAMELS): primary anxiety outcomes. *Journal of the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Ginsburg, G.S., Becker-Haimes, E.M., Keeton, C., Kendall, P.C., Iyengar, S., Sakolsky, D., Albano, A.M., Peris, T., Compton, S.N. and Piacentini, J. (2018) 'Results from the Child/Adolescent Anxiety Multimodal Extended Long-Term Study (CAMELS): primary anxiety outcomes', *Journal of the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry*, 57(7), pp. 471–480. doi: 10.1016/j.jaac.2018.03.017

**Evidence tier:** Long-term naturalistic follow-up of an RCT (CAMS → CAMELS, n=288) — **Tier T1 (RCT follow-up)**
**Validity estimate:** 80/100 for the study; **abstract/image-only PDF on file**
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jaac.2018.03.017
**PDF status:** abstract (image-only scan; relapse figures verified from abstract)

## Key usable claims
1. Across the extended ~4-year follow-up, only **~22% were in stable remission**, **~30% chronically ill**, and **~48% relapsers**.
2. In the 6-year CAMELS analysis, **46.5% were in remission** and **~48% of acute responders had relapsed** at least once.
3. Durability of youth-anxiety treatment is **real but imperfect**: a substantial minority relapse over years, re-entering the "anxious and untreated" pool.

## Numerical findings
- n = 288; stable remission ~22%; relapsers ~48%; 6-yr remission 46.5%.

## Main limitation
Naturalistic follow-up (post-randomisation treatment uncontrolled); US CAMS sample; abstract-level access for this copy.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
§3 — the durability nuance within the gap: even those who *do* access and respond to treatment often relapse, so the access gap is compounded by a maintenance gap. Balance against Krause (2024), which shows good maintenance is achievable.
