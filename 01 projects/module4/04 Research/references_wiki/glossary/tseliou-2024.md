# Tseliou et al. (2024) Childhood correlates and young adult outcomes of trajectories of emotional problems from childhood to adolescence. *Psychological Medicine*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Tseliou, F., Riglin, L., Thapar, A.K., Heron, J., Dennison, C.A., Armitage, J.M., Thapar, A., Rice, F. and Collishaw, S. (2024) 'Childhood correlates and young adult outcomes of trajectories of emotional problems from childhood to adolescence', *Psychological Medicine*, 54(11), pp. 2504–2514. doi: 10.1017/S0033291724000631

**Evidence tier:** Longitudinal birth cohort (ALSPAC, n=8,286; SDQ emotional-problem scores across 7 waves, ages 4–17) — **Tier T1 (cohort)**
**Validity estimate:** 80/100
**DOI:** 10.1017/S0033291724000631
**PDF status:** full-pdf

## Key usable claims
1. Children on a **"decreasing"** emotional-problems trajectory were **not** at elevated risk of any poor adult outcome.
2. Children on **"persistent"** and **"increasing"** trajectories had substantially higher risks of adult depression, self-harm, and educational impairment.
3. Key implication: children who improve (with or without treatment) do fine; **risk accrues specifically when problems persist** — so the clinical task is to move children off the persistent trajectory.

## Numerical findings
- n = 8,286 (ALSPAC); SDQ emotional problems across 7 assessments, ages 4–17; trajectories → adult outcomes.

## Main limitation
Observational; SDQ is a symptom screen not a diagnosis; latent-trajectory modelling sensitive to specification.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
§1 NEED — sharpens Morales-Muñoz: it is *persistence* that does the damage, which is exactly what effective early intervention prevents. Frames the whole essay's "why act, and act early" rationale.
