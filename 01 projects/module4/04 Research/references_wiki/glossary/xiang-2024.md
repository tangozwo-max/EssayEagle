# Xiang et al. (2024) Depression and anxiety among US children and young adults. *JAMA Network Open*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Xiang, A.H., Martinez, M.P., Chow, T., Carter, S.A., Negriff, S., Velasquez, B., Spitzer, J., Zuberbuhler, J.C., Zucker, A. and Kumar, S. (2024) 'Depression and anxiety among US children and young adults', *JAMA Network Open*, 7(9), e2436906. doi: 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.36906

**Evidence tier:** Health-services cohort (Kaiser Southern California, n≈1.7 million, 2017–2021) — **Tier T2 (large administrative cohort)**
**Validity estimate:** 78/100 (very large; clinical diagnoses reduce self-report bias; single US system)
**DOI:** 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.36906
**PDF status:** full-pdf

## Key usable claims
1. **Clinically-diagnosed** anxiety incidence (from healthcare records, not self-report) **increased ~31%** from 2017 to 2021, with acceleration during COVID.
2. Because it uses billing/clinical diagnosis rather than surveys, it **reduces the detection/stigma confound** that complicates self-report trend data.
3. Corroborates the genuine-incidence-rise side of the Q5 debate from an independent US clinical source.

## Numerical findings
- n ≈ 1.7 million; diagnosed anxiety incidence +31% (2017–2021).

## Main limitation
Single integrated US health system; clinical diagnosis still partly reflects help-seeking/access, so not fully free of ascertainment effects; period spans COVID.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
§3 — strengthens the "incidence is genuinely rising" claim with clinical (not survey) data, supporting the prevention-paradox overlay without over-relying on self-report trend studies.
