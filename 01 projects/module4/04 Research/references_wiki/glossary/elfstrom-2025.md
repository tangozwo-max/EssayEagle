# Elfström et al. (2025) CPBC parent-only prevention RCT. *Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Elfström, S., Rosengren, A., Andersson, R., Engelbrektsson, J., Isaksson, A., Meregalli, M., van Leuven, L., Lalouni, M., Öst, L-G., Ghaderi, A. and Ahlén, J. (2025) 'Evaluating a program to prevent anxiety in children of anxious parents: a randomized controlled trial', Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry, 66(9), pp. 1345–1356. DOI: 10.1111/jcpp.14151

**Evidence tier:** RCT — Pre-registered RCT (N=215 parents, N=277 children; 12-month follow-up; active comparator control)
**Validity estimate:** 75/100
**DOI:** 10.1111/jcpp.14151
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Children of anxious parents are at particularly elevated risk for anxiety disorders, with parental transmission occurring through both genetic and environmental pathways including overprotection, anxious modelling, and parental criticism or lack of warmth.
2. A brief, video-conferencing-based parent-only group program (CPBC, 6 sessions) targeting anxious parents did not produce a statistically significant reduction in anxiety disorder incidence or clinical severity ratings (CSR) in children aged 5-9 over 12 months compared to a self-help parenting book.
3. A significant age-moderated effect was found: children aged 5-6 years in the CPBC condition showed substantially lower risk of developing an anxiety disorder and of increased clinical severity compared to controls, while no significant effect was found for 7-9-year-olds, suggesting parent-only interventions may be more potent in early childhood.
4. Across all ages, parent-rated child anxiety symptoms (SCARED-41) showed a significant between-group reduction favouring CPBC at 12 months, with CPBC children's scores decreasing from 16.4 to 13.2 while control children's scores remained flat (15.1 to 15.0).
5. CPBC parents showed significantly larger reductions in overprotection and parental criticism compared with control parents, supporting the intervention's logic model linking maladaptive parenting behaviours to child anxiety.
6. The study was underpowered relative to original calculations because the observed anxiety disorder incidence in control children (15%) was substantially lower than the anticipated 25%, indicating the trial may not have successfully identified a sufficiently high-risk sample.

## Numerical findings
- N=215 parents (91% female), N=277 children (48% girls, mean age 7.0 years) randomised 1:1
- Primary outcome (CSR change): OR=0.67 (95% CI: 0.30, 1.48), not statistically significant
- Anxiety disorder prevalence at 12 months, overall: OR=0.57 (95% CI: 0.24, 1.31), not statistically significant
- Anxiety disorder incidence at 12 months: 15% in control group (lower than anticipated 25%)
- Age moderation (5-6-year-olds): lower risk of increased CSR, OR=0.24 (95% CI: 0.08, 0.77); lower risk of anxiety diagnosis, OR=0.23 (95% CI: 0.05, 0.84)
- Age moderation (7-9-year-olds): risk of increased CSR OR=1.77 (95% CI: 0.57, 5.50), not significant
- Between-group effect on SCARED-41 (parent-rated anxiety symptoms) at 12 months: Cohen's d=0.35 (95% CI: 0.15, 0.55)
- SCARED-41 scores: CPBC pre=16.4, 12m=13.2; Control pre=15.1, 12m=15.0
- 12-month assessment retention: 204/215 parents (94.9%)
- Inter-rater reliability for CSR: Krippendorff's alpha=0.93; for anxiety disorder diagnosis: alpha=0.82
- Manual adherence mean score: 9.63/10 (SD=0.81)
- Mean sessions attended (CPBC): 5.01/6 (SD=1.64); 90% attended 3 or more sessions
- Three-way interaction (condition x time x age): OR=1.81 (95% CI: 1.01, 3.27)

## Study design
Parallel, pre-registered (NCT04722731), randomised controlled trial (1:1 allocation) conducted at Karolinska Institutet, Stockholm, Sweden; 215 anxious parents with 277 children aged 5-9 with subclinical anxiety symptoms randomised to a 6-session video-conferencing parent group programme (CPBC) versus a self-help parenting book active comparator, with assessments at baseline, post-intervention, and 12 months.

## Main limitation
The trial was underpowered because the observed rate of anxiety disorder onset in control children at 12 months (15%) was substantially lower than the 25% rate assumed in the power calculation, indicating the study may have failed to recruit a sufficiently well-defined high-risk sample; additionally, the predominantly female, highly educated, and Swedish-born sample limits generalisability.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Cite to support arguments about parental transmission of anxiety and the potential of targeting maladaptive parenting behaviours (overprotection, anxious modelling, family accommodation) as a preventive mechanism — particularly to illustrate that brief, scalable parent-only interventions addressing these transmission pathways show promise for young children (5-6 years) but that age and risk-group precision matter for intervention effectiveness.
