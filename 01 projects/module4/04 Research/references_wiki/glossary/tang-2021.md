# Tang et al. (2021) Screen time and mental health in young people: SR of longitudinal studies. *Clinical Psychology Review*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Tang, S., Werner-Seidler, A., Torok, M., Mackinnon, A.J. and Christensen, H. (2021) 'The relationship between screen time and mental health in young people: A systematic review of longitudinal studies', Clinical Psychology Review, 86, p. 102021. DOI: 10.1016/j.cpr.2021.102021.

**Evidence tier:** Meta-analysis / SR — Systematic review of longitudinal studies (k=35 studies)
**Validity estimate:** 72/100
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.cpr.2021.102021
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. The longitudinal association between screen time and subsequent depressive symptoms is small to very small in size (effect sizes around r = 0.10), casting doubt on claims that rising screen use explains recent substantial increases in adolescent depression.
2. Evidence for a longitudinal association between screen time and anxiety, self-esteem, or other internalising symptoms is limited; the clearest signal is for depression, not anxiety.
3. The direction of association is stronger from screen time to depression than from depression to subsequent screen time, providing only partial support for a causal screen-time hypothesis.
4. The relationship between screen time and depression varies substantially by screen type: newer interactive technologies (mobile phone, computer/internet) show relatively stronger associations than passive/older ones (television, videogames).
5. Social media use shows mixed longitudinal associations with internalising symptoms; passive use (browsing) and use driven by social comparison appear more consistently harmful than active posting or general time spent.
6. Sex moderates the relationship inconsistently across studies, with some studies finding effects only in females and others only in males, suggesting the mechanism is not uniform across gender.

## Numerical findings
- 35 longitudinal studies included; sample sizes per study ranged from 126 to 12,866 participants
- Follow-up durations across studies ranged from 6 months to 12 years
- Effect sizes for screen time to depression where estimable: approximately r = 0.10
- Unadjusted OR for total non-television screen time on subsequent depression (Zink et al., 2019): 1.47, corresponding to Cohen's d = 0.21, r = 0.11
- Reverse association (depression on subsequent screen time) unadjusted OR: 1.59, Cohen's d = 0.26, r = 0.13
- Largest OR in Zink, Ebrahimian et al. (2020): 1.29 (negative affect on non-television screen use), Cohen's d = 0.14, r = 0.07
- Houghton et al. (2018) path a (screen time to depression) estimate: 0.12; path b (depression to screen time) estimate: 0.19 for males
- Screen time accounted for only 0.4% of variation in mental wellbeing in one US adolescent study (Orben & Przybylski, 2019, cited)
- Between 2010 and 2015, 33% increase in US adolescents with high depressive symptoms and 31% increase in adolescent suicide deaths (cited context)
- Only 11.4% of included studies reported a baseline participation rate above 80%
- Only 1 of 35 included studies met all 9 quality criteria

## Study design
Systematic review (PRISMA/PROSPERO-registered, CRD42020180145) of 35 longitudinal studies examining bidirectional associations between screen time and internalising mental health symptoms (depression, anxiety, self-esteem) in young people aged 10–24, with a minimum 6-month follow-up, published 2005–2020, searched across PsycINFO, PubMed/Medline, and EMBASE.

## Main limitation
The review relied exclusively on self-report or parent-report measures of both screen time and mental health; no studies used clinician-rated mental health outcomes, and quantitative meta-analytic synthesis was not feasible due to wide heterogeneity in statistical models, making effect comparisons across studies approximate and potentially unreliable.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to challenge oversimplified claims that social media or screen time straightforwardly causes adolescent anxiety: the best available longitudinal evidence shows effects on depression are small to very small, and evidence linking screen time specifically to anxiety is limited and inconsistent, suggesting systemic and individual vulnerability factors carry greater explanatory weight than screen exposure alone.
