## Key usable claims
1. Stepped care (SC) for adolescent anxiety has previously shown clinical and cost efficacy in university clinic settings, but had not been evaluated in real-world community mental health services prior to this study.
2. In this pilot RCT, SC produced similar clinical outcomes to treatment as usual (TAU), with both conditions resulting in significant improvements in anxiety symptoms.
3. SC was associated with meaningfully less therapist time compared to TAU, suggesting a resource efficiency advantage even when clinical outcomes are equivalent.
4. No significant differences were found between SC and TAU in quality-adjusted life years (QALYs), meaning cost-effectiveness in terms of health utility was comparable.
5. Significant barriers limited adolescent engagement with the early, lower-intensity therapy steps of the SC model, representing a major implementation challenge in community settings.
6. The study is the first controlled evaluation of SC for anxious youth in real-world public mental health services, highlighting a critical gap between efficacy in controlled clinic settings and effectiveness in routine care.

## Numerical findings
- n=53 anxious adolescents randomised to SC or TAU
- Age range: 12-18 years; M=15.04 years, SD=1.60
- Female participants: 40 out of 53
- SC associated with 4.5 hours less therapist time than TAU
- No significant differences between groups in QALYs
- Trial registration: ACTRN12620000653965
- Cited by 8 articles (as of page capture)

## Study design
Pilot randomised controlled trial comparing psychological stepped care to treatment as usual in 53 anxious adolescents (aged 12-18) recruited from two community mental health services in Australia.

## Main limitation
Small pilot sample (n=53) limits statistical power and generalisability, and significant barriers to engagement with early SC steps were identified, indicating the model as implemented faced fidelity and acceptability challenges in routine community settings.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Best cited to support arguments about CBT-based stepped care as a scalable intervention model for adolescent anxiety in public health systems, particularly when discussing the gap between efficacy in controlled settings and real-world effectiveness, or the systemic and service-level barriers to delivering evidence-based anxiety treatment to young people.
