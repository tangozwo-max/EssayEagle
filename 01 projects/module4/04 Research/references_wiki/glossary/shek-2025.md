# Shek et al. (2025) Parental anxiety and child anxiety: four-wave longitudinal study. *Frontiers in Psychiatry*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Shek, D.T.L., Li, X., Yang, B. and Yang, J. (2025) 'Is parental anxiety related to child anxiety? Insights from a four-wave longitudinal study in a Chinese context', Frontiers in Psychiatry, 16, article 1570652. DOI: 10.3389/fpsyt.2025.1570652

**Evidence tier:** Longitudinal cohort — Four-wave longitudinal panel study (RI-CLPM, n=6,117, 2019–2022)
**Validity estimate:** 72/100
**DOI:** 10.3389/fpsyt.2025.1570652
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Parental anxiety consistently predicted child anxiety at every subsequent time point across all four waves, supporting a 'parent effects' model of intergenerational anxiety transmission.
2. Child anxiety also predicted parental anxiety, but only from Time 3 to Time 4, partially supporting a 'reciprocal effects' model — the relationship is dynamic and bidirectional at certain developmental transitions.
3. The influence of parental anxiety on anxiety was significantly stronger for girls than for boys, consistent with girls' greater sensitivity to negative emotional cues and tendency toward ruminative thinking.
4. Parental anxiety had a significantly greater impact on younger children (under 10) than on adolescents, because children rely more heavily on parental emotional states and learn coping strategies through direct imitation.
5. At the between-person level, there was a strong positive correlation (r = .51) between the random intercepts for parental and child anxiety, indicating that families with chronically anxious parents tend to have chronically anxious children.
6. Mechanisms proposed include emotional contagion and modelling, over-controlling or insufficiently warm parenting, disruption to attachment security, and parents conveying the environment as threatening.

## Numerical findings
- n = 6,117 students (49.00% girls; 61.10% adolescents; M age = 10.32 years, SD = 2.14)
- Between-person correlation of random intercepts for parental anxiety and child anxiety: r = .51, p < .001
- Cross-lagged path T1 parental anxiety → T2 child anxiety: b = .06, p < .01
- Cross-lagged path T2 parental anxiety → T3 child anxiety: b = .05, p < .01
- Cross-lagged path T3 parental anxiety → T4 child anxiety: b = .07, p < .01
- Cross-lagged path T3 child anxiety → T4 parental anxiety: b = .04, p < .01 (only significant reverse path)
- T1 child anxiety did not predict T2 parental anxiety: b = .02, p > .05
- Gender difference (girls): T1 PA → T2 CA b = .08, p < .01; T2 PA → T3 CA b = .09, p < .001; T3 PA → T4 CA b = .06, p < .01
- Gender difference (boys): T1 PA → T2 CA b = .003, p > .05 (non-significant)
- Age difference: T2 PA → T3 CA significant for children b = .06, p < .01; non-significant for adolescents b = .02, p > .05
- T3 PA → T4 CA for children b = .07, p < .001; for adolescents b = .02, p > .05 (non-significant)
- ICC for parental anxiety = 0.711 (71.1% between-person variance)
- ICC for child anxiety = 0.588 (58.8% between-person variance)
- SCARED Cronbach's alpha: 0.945 (T1), 0.953 (T2), 0.954 (T3), 0.943 (T4)
- SAS Cronbach's alpha: 0.793 (T1), 0.862 (T2), 0.817 (T3), 0.795 (T4)
- Model fit (full sample RI-CLPM): CFI = .97, TLI = .93, RMSEA = .05, SRMR = .03

## Study design
Four-wave longitudinal panel study (2019–2022, Sichuan Province, China) of 6,117 children and adolescents (mean age 10.32 years) and their parents, using Random Intercept Cross-Lagged Panel Modelling (RI-CLPM) to distinguish within-person and between-person effects of parental anxiety (SAS) on child anxiety (SCARED), with multi-group analyses by gender and developmental stage (children vs. adolescents).

## Main limitation
The study relied entirely on self-report questionnaires, which are susceptible to social desirability and recall bias, and did not explore mediating mechanisms or different subtypes of anxiety (e.g., trait vs. state vs. educational anxiety); future work should incorporate objective measures such as cortisol levels and teacher-rated anxiety.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support the argument that parental anxiety is a key systemic family-level driver of child and adolescent anxiety through intergenerational transmission — providing longitudinal, within-person evidence for the 'parent effects' model — and to document gender and developmental moderators (girls and younger children are disproportionately affected).
