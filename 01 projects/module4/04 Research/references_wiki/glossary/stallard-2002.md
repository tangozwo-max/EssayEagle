# Stallard (2002) CBT with children and young people: a selective review of key issues. *Behavioural and Cognitive Psychotherapy*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Stallard, P. (2002) 'Cognitive behaviour therapy with children and young people: a selective review of key issues', Behavioural and Cognitive Psychotherapy, 30(3), pp. 297–309. DOI: 10.1017/S1352465802003053

**Evidence tier:** Selective / narrative review (conceptual) — not systematic, not a meta-analysis
**Validity estimate:** 58/100
**DOI:** 10.1017/S1352465802003053
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. CBT models and techniques developed for adults have typically been "extended downwards and applied to children"; the review explicitly questions the appropriateness of this approach.
2. There is an absence of developmentally appropriate theoretical models of the onset/maintenance of maladaptive cognition in children; as a result child CBT is a loose umbrella whose "predominant emphasis [is] behavioural, rather than cognitive."
3. Developmental factors and "the important role of the parent in the onset and treatment of childhood problems have received comparatively little attention"; in practice the parent's role varies as facilitator, co-therapist or client.
4. Therapeutic materials for children "need... a greater visual emphasis and use simple understandable language" — i.e. techniques must be made more concrete and visual for younger children.
5. Children with anxiety disorders tend to misperceive ambiguous events as threatening and are overly self-focused/hypercritical (threat-biased cognition) — a child-specific cognitive profile treatment must address.

## Numerical findings
- Identifies eight core CBT treatment components (problem-solving, self-instruction, role-play, rewards, social-cognition training, social skills, etc.).
- No effect sizes (conceptual review).

## Study design
Author-selective narrative review of key conceptual issues in CBT for children and young people (developmental appropriateness, theoretical models, the role of cognition vs behaviour, the role of parents).

## Main limitation
Narrative and selective (not systematic); published 2002, so predates most of the developmental-adaptation and neuroimaging evidence; raises questions and flags gaps rather than providing prescriptive, age-graded protocols. The specific claim that *metacognitive capacity matures gradually* is NOT made here (that belongs to Grave & Blissett 2004 / Sauter et al. 2009) — do not attribute it to Stallard.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
New chapter "Childhood Anxiety and the Developing Brain": supports *why CBT must be developmentally adapted* rather than extended "downwards" — more concrete and visual techniques for younger children, with parents given a central role in treating the youngest, in whom self-directed cognitive techniques fit poorly. Use for the developmental-adaptation rationale only; pair with a developmental-cognition source if the metacognition-maturation mechanism is asserted.
