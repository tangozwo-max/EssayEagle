# Gladstone et al. (2023) Youth Anxiety: Moderating Effects of Accommodation and Emotional Warmth. *Child Psychiatry & Human Development*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Gladstone, T.R., Wilton, E.P., Biscarri Clark, S.D., Lahoud, A.A. and Flessner, C.A. (2023) 'Youth Anxiety: The Moderating Effects of Accommodation and Emotional Warmth', Child Psychiatry & Human Development, 56, pp. 126–133. DOI: 10.1007/s10578-023-01544-7

**Evidence tier:** Cross-sectional — Cross-sectional survey with moderation analysis (N=526 parents)
**Validity estimate:** 52/100
**DOI:** 10.1007/s10578-023-01544-7
**PDF status:** abstract-only

## Key usable claims
1. Parental accommodation — defined as modifying parental behaviour to reduce a child's distress — is among the most empirically supported parenting practices that enhance anxiety in children and adolescents.
2. Emotional warmth (parental support and affection) alone has a less clear relationship to child anxiety, but its effect is substantially shaped by the level of accommodation present in the family.
3. Accommodation significantly moderates the relationship between emotional warmth and child anxiety symptoms: when accommodation is high, greater emotional warmth significantly predicts higher child anxiety.
4. Adding the accommodation-by-warmth interaction term substantially improved model fit, suggesting that the combination of high warmth and high accommodation is a meaningful risk configuration — not high warmth alone.
5. The findings highlight that seemingly positive parenting qualities (warmth, affection) can inadvertently reinforce anxiety when they co-occur with accommodation behaviours, pointing to the importance of addressing accommodation in family-based intervention.

## Numerical findings
- N = 526 parents of youth aged 7–17
- Accommodation moderation coefficient B = 0.03, 95% CI (0.01, 0.05), p = 0.01
- Model R² = 0.47 after adding the interaction term, p < 0.001

## Study design
Cross-sectional parent-report survey of N=526 parents of youth aged 7–17, using a simple moderation analysis to examine whether parental accommodation moderates the relationship between emotional warmth and child anxiety symptoms.

## Main limitation
The authors themselves acknowledge that the study is limited by its reliance on parent-report data and non-clinical convenience sampling, which restricts causal inference and generalisability to clinically anxious populations.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support the argument that family accommodation is the critical contextual variable that determines whether parental warmth exacerbates or is neutral to child/adolescent anxiety — directly relevant to discussions of parenting transmission pathways and the rationale for targeting accommodation in family-based CBT.
