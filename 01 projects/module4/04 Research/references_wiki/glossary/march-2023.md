# March et al. (2023) Stepped-care vs therapist-guided ICBT for child/adolescent anxiety. *Internet Interventions*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
March, S., Spence, S.H., Myers, L., Ford, M., Smith, G. and Donovan, C.L. (2023) 'Stepped-care versus therapist-guided, internet-based cognitive behaviour therapy for childhood and adolescent anxiety: A non-inferiority trial', Internet Interventions, 34, p. 100675. DOI: 10.1016/j.invent.2023.100675.

**Evidence tier:** RCT — Preregistered non-inferiority RCT (n=137, two active arms, 9-month follow-up)
**Validity estimate:** 78/100
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.invent.2023.100675
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. A stepped-care internet-delivered CBT model (ICBT-SC), in which only non-responders at mid-treatment are stepped up to therapist guidance, is non-inferior to fully therapist-guided ICBT (ICBT-TG) on both primary (clinician severity rating) and secondary (parent- and child-reported anxiety) outcomes at 12 weeks and 9 months.
2. Both ICBT-SC and ICBT-TG produced large within-group improvements in anxiety symptoms, with completer remission rates for the primary anxiety diagnosis of approximately 77% in both conditions at 9-month follow-up.
3. Stepped-care ICBT requires substantially less therapist time than therapist-guided ICBT, because roughly 27% of children in the stepped-care arm responded after self-guided sessions and needed no therapist input at all; of these non-stepped-up completers, 90% were free of their primary diagnosis at 9 months.
4. Treatment satisfaction was moderate to high and statistically equivalent across both conditions, indicating that reducing therapist involvement does not meaningfully reduce acceptability to children and families.
5. Less than 50% of children and adolescents with anxiety disorders receive professional care, and internet-delivered CBT addresses barriers such as long waiting lists, CBT therapist shortages, high costs, and stigma.
6. The stepped-care model has implications for service delivery in primary care and under-resourced settings, potentially freeing therapist time for those who need it most while maintaining clinical outcomes comparable to more intensive approaches.

## Numerical findings
- n=137 participants aged 8–17 years (M=10.67, SD=2.43); 67 allocated to ICBT-SC, 70 to ICBT-TG
- 73% (n=44) of ICBT-SC participants were classified as non-responders at mid-point and stepped up to therapist guidance; 27% (n=16) continued self-guided
- Completer remission rate for primary anxiety diagnosis at 9 months: 76.9% ICBT-SC vs 77.5% ICBT-TG (no significant difference, χ²=0.004, p=.951)
- ITT remission rate for primary anxiety diagnosis at 9 months: 50.7% ICBT-SC vs 57.1% ICBT-TG (χ²=0.56, p=.453)
- Within-group effect sizes (pre-treatment to 12 weeks): SCAS-C d=1.16 [0.84,1.47] for ICBT-SC; d=1.19 [0.91,1.47] for ICBT-TG
- Within-group effect sizes (pre-treatment to 12 weeks): CSR d=3.58 [2.92,4.24] for ICBT-SC; d=3.51 [2.88,4.13] for ICBT-TG
- Of the 27% non-stepped-up self-guided completers, 90% were free of their primary diagnosis at 9 months
- ITT remission for any anxiety diagnosis at 9 months: 38.8% ICBT-SC vs 48.6% ICBT-TG (χ²=1.32, p=.249)
- Inter-rater reliability for primary diagnosis Kappa=0.94; r=0.87 for CSR ratings
- Drop-out rate at 9 months: 26.9% ICBT-SC vs 31.4% ICBT-TG (OR=0.95, p=.557)
- Comparison meta-analysis figures cited: CBT/ICBT remission rates for primary diagnosis 49.4% ITT and 56.4% completers (James et al., 2020)

## Study design
Preregistered two-arm non-inferiority RCT (ACTRN12618001415291) comparing stepped-care ICBT (ICBT-SC) to therapist-guided ICBT (ICBT-TG) in 137 clinically diagnosed Australian children and adolescents aged 8–17 with a primary anxiety disorder (SAD, GAD, separation anxiety, or specific phobia), with stratified block randomisation, blinded assessors, and assessments at baseline, 12 weeks, and 9 months.

## Main limitation
The study lacked a wait-list or no-treatment control arm, so spontaneous remission cannot be fully ruled out; additionally, cost-effectiveness of the two models was not quantified, the sample was over-represented by higher socioeconomic groups limiting generalisability, and the clinical step-up decision required clinician judgement, constraining scalability.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support the argument that CBT for child and adolescent anxiety can be delivered effectively via internet-based stepped-care models that reduce therapist burden without sacrificing outcomes — directly relevant to discussions of access barriers, service-delivery innovation, and the evidence base for structured CBT (including graded exposure and cognitive restructuring) as a frontline intervention for anxiety disorders in young people.
