# Gabrielle, Sonne and Indolo (2024) 'The impact of social media on adolescent mental health: a meta-analysis'. *Scientia Psychiatrica*.

## Key usable claims
1. Increased social media use is associated with small but statistically significant increases in depressive symptoms, anxiety, and loneliness, and a decrease in self-esteem in adolescents aged 10–19.
2. Effect sizes are uniformly small (r = 0.08–0.15), indicating that social media is one of many contributing factors rather than a primary determinant of adolescent mental health.
3. Substantial heterogeneity across studies (I² > 70% for all outcomes) indicates that the relationship is complex and moderated by individual, social, and platform-specific factors.
4. Gender moderates the relationship: females show stronger negative associations (r = 0.13) than males (r = 0.09), potentially due to body image pressures, cyberbullying, and relational aggression specific to girls' online experiences.
5. Family dynamics function as a significant moderator: adolescents from unsupportive family environments show markedly stronger negative associations (r = 0.17) compared to those from supportive families (r = 0.07), underscoring family cohesion as a protective factor.
6. Passive or high-frequency social media use and image-focused platforms (Instagram, Snapchat) are associated with stronger negative mental health effects than lower-frequency use or less image-centric platforms.

## Numerical findings
- Depression: pooled r = 0.12 (95% CI: 0.09–0.15), I² = 78.23%
- Anxiety: pooled r = 0.10 (95% CI: 0.07–0.13), I² = 75.51%
- Loneliness: pooled r = 0.15 (95% CI: 0.12–0.18), I² = 72.45%
- Self-esteem: pooled r = −0.08 (95% CI: −0.11 to −0.05), I² = 71.18%
- Total sample: N = 153,285 adolescents across 45 studies (2018–2024)
- Mean participant age: 15.2 years (SD = 2.1)
- Studies by region: North America n=18, Europe n=15, Asia n=8, Australia n=4
- Unsupportive family dynamics subgroup: r = 0.17 (95% CI: 0.14–0.20)
- Supportive family dynamics subgroup: r = 0.07 (95% CI: 0.04–0.10)
- Female subgroup: r = 0.13 (95% CI: 0.10–0.16); Male subgroup: r = 0.09 (95% CI: 0.06–0.12)
- High-frequency use subgroup: r = 0.15 (95% CI: 0.12–0.18)
- Instagram subgroup: r = 0.14 (95% CI: 0.11–0.17)
- Cyberbullying prevalence in cited studies: 10%–40% of adolescents

## Study design
Pre-registered systematic review and random-effects meta-analysis of 45 peer-reviewed quantitative studies (cross-sectional, longitudinal, and experimental designs) published 2018–2024, identified via PubMed, PsycINFO, Scopus, and Web of Science, examining social media use and mental health outcomes in adolescents aged 10–19 (total N = 153,285).

## Main limitation
Substantial between-study heterogeneity (I² > 70% for all outcomes) was observed across all outcomes, and most included primary studies relied on cross-sectional designs, precluding causal inference about the direction of the relationship between social media use and adolescent mental health.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support the argument that social media constitutes a systemic environmental risk factor for adolescent anxiety and depression, and to demonstrate that family environment significantly moderates this risk — adolescents in unsupportive families are disproportionately vulnerable — which directly connects to arguments about parental and family-level protective and transmission factors in child and adolescent anxiety.
