# Li et al. (2025) Screen time and adolescent depression and anxiety. *British Journal of Clinical Psychology*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Li, S.H., Batterham, P.J., Whitton, A.E., Maston, K., Khan, A., Christensen, H. and Werner-Seidler, A. (2025) 'Cross-sectional and longitudinal associations of screen time with adolescent depression and anxiety', British Journal of Clinical Psychology, 64, pp. 873–887. DOI: 10.1111/bjc.12547

**Evidence tier:** Longitudinal cohort — Prospective longitudinal cohort (n=4,058; 12-month follow-up from 5-year Future Proofing Study)
**Validity estimate:** 72/100
**DOI:** 10.1111/bjc.12547
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Screen time is significantly associated with both depression and anxiety in adolescents cross-sectionally, but longitudinal associations at 12 months are markedly weaker (depression) or absent (anxiety), suggesting the relationship is more likely correlational or bidirectional than causal.
2. Each additional hour of daily screen time corresponded with a 1.25-point increase in PHQ-A depression scores and a 0.79-point increase in CAS-8 anxiety scores cross-sectionally, but only a 0.15-point increase in depression at 12 months and no significant association with anxiety at 12 months.
3. The study argues that the pattern of weak longitudinal effects is consistent with a bidirectional relationship: adolescents with elevated symptoms may increase screen use (e.g., via mood regulation or social withdrawal) as much as screen use may worsen symptoms.
4. Neither gender nor maladaptive social media use substantially moderated the cross-sectional or longitudinal associations between screen time and depression or anxiety, contrary to predictions.
5. Gender diverse adolescents showed substantially higher depression and anxiety severity than females, who in turn showed higher severity than males, at both time points.
6. A single self-report item measuring total screen time may be too blunt an instrument; the study calls for future research that distinguishes between specific digital behaviours (e.g., passive scrolling, doom scrolling, friend interaction) and uses experimental designs to establish causality.

## Numerical findings
- n = 4,058 Australian Year 8 students (mean age 13.9 years, SD = 0.58; range 11–17.5 years) from 134 schools
- Cross-sectional: each additional hour of screen time = +1.25 PHQ-A points (95% CI 1.12–1.37, p < .001)
- Cross-sectional: each additional hour of screen time = +0.79 CAS-8 points (95% CI 0.68–0.90, p < .001)
- Cross-sectional (estimated): 5+ hours vs 0–1 hour daily screen time = ~6.25-point increase in PHQ-A; ~3.95-point increase in CAS-8
- Longitudinal (12-month): each additional hour of screen time = +0.15 PHQ-A points (95% CI 0.04–0.26, p = .007), controlling for baseline depression
- Longitudinal (12-month): screen time not significantly associated with CAS-8 at 12 months (p = .443)
- Longitudinal (estimated): 5+ vs 0–1 hours = ~0.75-point increase in 12-month PHQ-A
- Gender diverse group mean PHQ-A at Time 1: 15.97 (SD 6.99); females: 8.39 (SD 6.12); males: 5.31 (SD 5.08)
- Gender diverse group mean CAS-8 at Time 1: 13.85 (SD 5.84); females: 10.04 (SD 5.20); males: 5.85 (SD 4.45)
- 27.1% of total sample reported 5+ hours daily screen time; 51.8% of gender diverse individuals reported 5+ hours
- Cronbach's alpha for PHQ-A at Time 1: .88; CAS-8 at Time 1: .90
- Pearson r between screen time and PHQ-A baseline: .312 (p < .01); screen time and CAS-8 baseline: .230 (p < .01)
- Pearson r between screen time and 12-month PHQ-A: .216; screen time and 12-month CAS-8: .156 (both p < .01)
- Screen time × male interaction on cross-sectional depression: −0.45 (95% CI −0.68 to −0.22, p < .001)

## Study design
5-year prospective cohort study (Future Proofing Study) using linear mixed models with data from 4,058 Australian secondary school students (Year 8; mean age 13.9) recruited from 134 schools across NSW and other Australian states, with cross-sectional (Time 1) and 12-month longitudinal (Time 2) assessments of self-reported screen time, depression (PHQ-A), anxiety (CAS-8), and maladaptive social media use.

## Main limitation
Screen time was measured using a single unvalidated self-report categorical item that does not distinguish between types of use (e.g., gaming, social media, passive scrolling), making it impossible to identify which specific digital behaviours drive the observed associations; retrospective self-report is also prone to recall bias.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to argue that the assumed causal link between social media/screen time and adolescent anxiety is not well supported by longitudinal evidence — cross-sectional associations are significant but weak longitudinal effects suggest the relationship is bidirectional or correlational, cautioning against simple dose-reduction narratives in intervention and policy discussions.
