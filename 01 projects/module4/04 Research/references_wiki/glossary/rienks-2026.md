# Rienks et al. (2026) Supporting parents to reduce child anxiety: meta-analysis of interventions and effective components. *Gedragstherapie*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Rienks, K., Salemink, E., Laas Sigurðardóttir, L.B., Melendez-Torres, G.J., Staaks, J. and Leijten, P. (2026) 'Ouders ondersteunen om angst bij kinderen te verminderen: Een meta-analyse van interventies en effectieve componenten', *Gedragstherapie*, 59(1), pp. 34–55. DOI: 10.5553/TG/016774542026059001003

**Evidence tier:** Meta-analysis / SR — Meta-analysis with network meta-analysis (k=26 RCTs, N=4,098 participants)
**Validity estimate:** 72/100
**DOI:** 10.5553/TG/016774542026059001003
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Parent-focused interventions (without any direct child-therapist contact) produce a medium-sized significant reduction in child anxiety across treatment and prevention contexts, confirming that working exclusively through parents is a viable treatment pathway.
2. Behavioural components (exposure, modelling, positive reinforcement, consequence management) were present in every cluster of components that produced statistically significant reductions in child anxiety, making them the single most consistently effective element.
3. No individual intervention component was significantly superior to any other when tested in isolation; differential effects only emerged when components were examined as clusters, suggesting that component combinations matter more than single techniques.
4. Family accommodation and parental overprotection are identified as key parental behaviours that inadvertently maintain child avoidance and anxiety, providing the theoretical rationale for why parent-focused intervention can interrupt the anxiety cycle.
5. Adding cognitive and emotion-regulation components to a behavioural base appeared to enhance outcomes, and multi-component interventions generally outperformed single-component ones — with the exception of behavioural-only interventions, which were effective on their own.
6. Overall evidence quality was rated very low (GRADE), with 12 of 26 studies assessed as high risk of bias and funnel-plot asymmetry suggesting possible publication bias, indicating the field is still developing and high-quality trials are needed.

## Numerical findings
- Overall effect across prevention and treatment: d = -0.59 (95% CI [-0.92; -0.26]), N=25 studies, k=154 effect sizes
- Treatment subsample: d = -0.66 (95% CI [-1.18; -0.14]), n=11 studies, k=57 effect sizes
- Indicated prevention subsample: d = -1.13 (95% CI [-2.67; 0.42]), n=6 studies, k=59 — large but not significant
- Selective prevention: d = -0.26 (95% CI [-0.57; 0.06]), n=4 — not significant
- Universal prevention: d = -0.12 (95% CI [-0.58; 0.34]), n=4 — not significant
- Total N across included studies: 4,098 participants (range 18–734 per study)
- 26 included studies from 31 articles; 2,674 records identified in database search
- Behavioural component present: d = -0.62** (95% CI [-0.99; -0.26]), n=22 studies
- Behavioural component absent: d = -0.39 (95% CI [-1.84; 1.07]), n=3 studies — not significant
- Best-performing component cluster COG+GED+EMO+ACC: d = -1.38 (95% CI [-2.37; -0.38]), n=1 study
- GED alone: d = -1.10 (95% CI [-1.64; -0.56]), n=3 studies
- Mean child age: 6.84 years (range 2.70–14.00); 49% female
- Mean intervention duration: 8.19 hours (range 1–20 hours)
- Inter-rater reliability for component coding: r=0.85, κ=1.00
- Independent screening agreement: 90%
- 14 studies rated 'some concerns' for risk of bias; 12 rated 'high risk'
- Only 35% of included studies reported parenting outcomes alongside child anxiety outcomes

## Study design
Systematic review and meta-analysis (with network meta-analysis for component clusters) of 26 RCTs (N=4,098 parents/caregivers of children aged up to 16, mean age 6.84 years) evaluating parent-only interventions — no child-therapist contact — targeting child anxiety across prevention and treatment contexts; searched PsycINFO, Medline, and Web of Science (October 2022); Robust Variance Estimation used for main analyses.

## Main limitation
The overall evidence quality was rated very low (GRADE), with 12 of 26 studies assessed as high risk of bias and funnel-plot asymmetry pointing to likely publication bias, meaning precise effect estimates for individual components and clusters remain uncertain and require replication in higher-quality trials.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use to support the argument that family accommodation and parental transmission of anxiety are modifiable risk factors: this meta-analysis directly demonstrates that intervening through parents alone — targeting parental behaviours such as accommodation, modelling, and overprotection via behavioural components — produces clinically meaningful reductions in child anxiety (d ≈ 0.59–0.66), providing strong meta-analytic evidence for the parental transmission and CBT-based family intervention arguments.
