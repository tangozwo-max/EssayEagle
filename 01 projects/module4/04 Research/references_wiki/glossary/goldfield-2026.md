# Goldfield et al. (2026) Reducing social media use decreases loneliness regardless of gender or level of social comparisons in youth with anxiety and depression: a randomized controlled trial. *Journal of Affective Disorders*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Goldfield, G.S., Lopes, M.V.V., Mahboob, W., Perry, S. and Davis, C.G. (2026) 'Reducing social media use decreases loneliness regardless of gender or level of social comparisons in youth with anxiety and depression: a randomized controlled trial', *Journal of Affective Disorders*, 403, 121331. doi: 10.1016/j.jad.2026.121331

**Evidence tier:** RCT (experimental; n=260 randomised, n=219 analysed; 3-week intervention) — **Tier T1**
**Validity estimate:** 74/100 (RCT design strong; but undergraduate sample, self-report SMU, short follow-up)
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jad.2026.121331
**PDF status:** full-pdf

## Key usable claims
1. Experimental (not merely correlational) evidence that *reducing* social media use causally lowers loneliness in young people already experiencing anxiety/depressive symptoms — addressing the causality gap that observational SMU–distress studies (e.g. Boers 2020, Fassi 2024) cannot close.
2. The benefit was **not moderated by gender** nor by baseline **social-comparison** tendency — i.e. reducing SMU helped broadly, not only girls or high social-comparers, contradicting the common assumption that only specific subgroups are harmed by/benefit from SMU change.
3. Effect magnitude was **small**, so SMU reduction is best framed as *one component* of a comprehensive approach to loneliness in a vulnerable population, not a standalone treatment.
4. Useful as an *intervention* citation (a modifiable behavioural lever in the digital ecology) rather than an aetiology citation — fits the v009+ intervention-majority framing.

## Numerical findings
- n = 260 randomised; 219 completed and analysed (102 control, 117 intervention)
- Sample: 77% female; youth with pre-existing anxiety and/or depressive symptoms
- Design: 1-week baseline (daily SMU screenshots) → 3 weeks; intervention = voluntarily reduce SMU to 1 h/day; control = no restriction
- Baseline SMU ~2.5 h/day; intervention achieved ~50% reduction
- Loneliness (UCLA Loneliness Scale; baseline Cronbach's alpha = 0.93)
- Group x time interaction: **beta = -0.11, 95% CI [-0.21, -0.005]** (intervention > control reduction in loneliness)
- Gender moderation: beta = 0.23, 95% CI [-0.16, 0.63] (ns)
- Baseline social-comparison moderation: beta = -0.04, 95% CI [-0.24, 0.15] (ns)

## Study design
Randomised controlled trial. After a one-week baseline with daily SMU screenshots, participants were randomised to reduce SMU to 1 h/day or to an unrestricted control for three weeks. Loneliness measured by UCLA Loneliness Scale; moderation by gender and baseline social comparison tested via mixed models (group x time). No external funding; no competing interests declared.

## Main limitation
Sample restricted to **undergraduate psychology students**, predominantly female and presenting with distress — so this is a young-adult sample, **not children/adolescents (<=18)**; generalisability to the school-age population at the centre of this essay is limited. SMU exposure self-reported (screenshot-based) and short 3-week follow-up; small effect size; single-site convenience sample.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use as **experimental** evidence that modifying the digital ecology (reducing social media use) is itself an intervention lever that improves wellbeing (loneliness) in distressed young people — strengthening the "interventions must now address the digital context" strand without overclaiming causation. Pair with the explicit caveat that the sample is undergraduates, so it informs the direction of travel rather than proving paediatric efficacy.
