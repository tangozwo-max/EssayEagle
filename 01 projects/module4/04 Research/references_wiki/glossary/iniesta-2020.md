# Iniesta-Sepúlveda et al. (2021) Family accommodation and pediatric anxiety severity. *Child Psychiatry & Human Development*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Iniesta-Sepúlveda, M., Rodríguez-Jiménez, T., Lebowitz, E.R., Goodman, W.K. and Storch, E.A. (2021) 'The relationship of family accommodation with pediatric anxiety severity: meta-analytic findings and child, family and methodological moderators', Child Psychiatry & Human Development, 52(1), pp. 1–14. DOI: 10.1007/s10578-020-00987-6

**Evidence tier:** Meta-analysis / SR — Meta-analysis (k=22 studies, N=2,353)
**Validity estimate:** 78/100
**DOI:** 10.1007/s10578-020-00987-6
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Family accommodation (FA) shows a moderate positive association with pediatric anxiety severity when measured by parent-report (r=0.47), indicating that higher levels of parental accommodation are reliably linked to greater child anxiety symptoms.
2. The association between FA and anxiety severity is disorder-specific: studies with higher proportions of children with separation anxiety disorder showed stronger FA–anxiety correlations, because parental involvement is inherent to that disorder.
3. When anxiety was rated by children rather than parents, effect sizes were substantially smaller (r=0.27) and non-significant when FA was parent-rated and anxiety child-rated (r=0.10), suggesting informant perspective materially shapes the observed relationship.
4. FA operates through operant mechanisms: it negatively reinforces both the child (distress reduction) and the parent (relief from witnessing suffering), thereby maintaining anxiety by preventing the child from learning that feared stimuli pose no real risk and inhibiting adaptive coping skill development.
5. Reductions in FA following CBT predict improvement in child anxiety symptoms, and parent-only interventions targeting FA (e.g., the SPACE programme) demonstrated non-inferiority to child-centred CBT in a randomised trial (response rates 87.5% vs. 75.5%).
6. No demographic moderators (child age, gender, ethnicity; parent age, income, marital status) significantly influenced the FA–anxiety association, suggesting the relationship is robust across sociodemographic variation.

## Numerical findings
- Global effect size (parent-report FA and anxiety): r=0.47, 95% CI [0.39, 0.54], z=9.94, p<0.001
- Effect size range across individual studies: r=0.25 to 0.77
- Child-report FA and anxiety: r=0.27, 95% CI [0.18, 0.35], z=5.97, p<0.001
- Parent-rated FA × child-rated anxiety: r=0.10, 95% CI [-0.02, 0.23], z=1.60, p=0.11 (non-significant)
- Heterogeneity: Q(19)=88.98, p<0.001; I²=78.65%
- Total N across 22 studies: 2,353 children and adolescents; mean age 10.20 (SD=2.5); 50.12% male
- 86–100% of parents of anxious children endorsed at least some accommodation; 61–80.7% reported daily accommodation
- SPACE parent-based treatment vs child-centred CBT: response rates 87.5% vs. 75.5%; remission 58.3% vs. 59.2%
- Fail-safe N=2,461 (publication bias robustness check)
- Trim-and-fill adjusted effect: r=0.49, 95% CI [0.41, 0.56]
- Separation anxiety as moderator: QR=7.74, p<0.05
- Selective mutism as moderator (inverse): QR=5.14, p<0.05

## Study design
Pre-registered (PROSPERO CRD42019117248) random-effects meta-analysis of 22 observational studies (predominantly clinical cross-sectional samples) comprising 2,353 children and adolescents under 19 years, examining Pearson correlations between standardised measures of family accommodation and anxiety severity, with moderator analyses for child, parent, and methodological variables.

## Main limitation
All included studies were cross-sectional, making it impossible to establish the direction of causality between family accommodation and anxiety severity, and the limited number of studies constrained the statistical power of moderator analyses.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to substantiate the argument that family accommodation is a key systemic family-level mechanism that maintains and amplifies child and adolescent anxiety, and to evidence that parent-focused interventions targeting accommodation reduction are as effective as child-centred CBT.
