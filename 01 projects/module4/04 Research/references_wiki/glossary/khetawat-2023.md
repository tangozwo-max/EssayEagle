# Khetawat and Steele (2023) Digital stress components and psychological wellbeing: a meta-analysis. *Clinical Child and Family Psychology Review*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Khetawat, D. and Steele, R.G. (2023) 'Examining the Association Between Digital Stress Components and Psychological Wellbeing: A Meta-Analysis', *Clinical Child and Family Psychology Review*, 26(4), pp. 957–974. doi: 10.1007/s10567-023-00439-y. Published online July 2023.

**Evidence tier:** Meta-analysis (multi-component; k=7–73 per DS component)
**Validity estimate:** 72/100
**DOI:** 10.1007/s10567-023-00439-y
**PDF status:** full-pdf

## Key usable claims
1. All five digital stress (DS) components — availability stress, approval anxiety, FOMO (fear of missing out), connection overload, and online vigilance — show significant medium-sized associations with psychosocial distress (r=.26–.34), meaning digital stress, not mere screen time, is the proximal driver of mental health risk.
2. Digital stress is defined as the stress and anxiety accompanying notifications and use of social media and communication technologies — it is a subjective appraisal, not an objective exposure quantity, consistent with Lazarus and Folkman's stress-coping framework.
3. Age and sex did not significantly moderate the association between most DS components and psychosocial distress, suggesting the DS-to-distress link is robust across demographic subgroups.
4. The exception: age moderated the connection overload → psychosocial distress association, suggesting that information overwhelm may be particularly harmful at specific developmental stages.
5. No statistically significant differences existed among the five DS components in their association with psychosocial distress — all components carry similar-sized risk, so targeting any single component (e.g., FOMO alone) is insufficient.
6. SMU (social media use) alone explains only small and mixed variance in psychosocial outcomes; DS mediates or moderates this relationship, explaining why simple screen-time reduction without addressing the appraisal of digital demands may be clinically insufficient.

## Numerical findings
- Studies included per DS component: availability stress k=7; approval anxiety k=73; FOMO k=60; connection overload k=19; online vigilance k=16
- Pooled effect sizes (r): all five components in range .26–.34 (p<.001)
- Age and sex: non-significant moderators for most components
- Age significantly moderated connection overload → distress association
- No statistical differences among DS component effect sizes (all r values within range)

## Study design
Systematic review and meta-analysis; databases searched: PubMed, PsycINFO, Communication and Mass Media Complete; search scope targeted each of five DS components; effect sizes pooled using meta-analytic methods; moderation analyses by age and sex conducted; published in *Clinical Child and Family Psychology Review*, 2023.

## Main limitation
Variable k across components (k=7 for availability stress vs k=73 for approval anxiety) limits confidence in less-studied components; heterogeneity across studies in DS measurement instruments; most studies cross-sectional, precluding causal inference; predominantly Western samples; DS literature uses varied nomenclature making synthesis imprecise.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use to support the argument that social media's impact on adolescent anxiety operates specifically through digital stress — a subjective appraisal of excessive online demands — rather than through exposure quantity alone. Particularly useful for arguing that (a) the SMU-anxiety link is mediated by DS, making simple screen-time caps insufficient; (b) the effect is consistent across age and sex, making DS a systemic rather than individual-level risk factor; and (c) all five DS components carry equivalent risk, meaning intervention should address the full stress appraisal pattern.
