# Treadwell (2024) Family factors in the development and management of anxiety disorders. *Psychiatric Clinics of North America*.

Treadwell, K.R.H. (2024) 'Family Factors in the Development and Management of Anxiety Disorders', Psychiatric Clinics of North America, 47(4), pp. 787–800. DOI: 10.1016/j.psc.2024.04.018

## Key usable claims
1. Family heritability accounts for 30% to 40% of the variance in child anxiety disorders, based on twin and adoption studies.
2. Family accommodation — parental behaviours that modify routines or situations to reduce a child's anxiety — explains 20% to 50% of the impact of child anxiety disorders on functional impairment.
3. Child anxiety and family environment/parenting behaviours influence each other reciprocally, with this bidirectional relationship being particularly pronounced during earlier childhood.
4. Meta-analyses of anxiety treatment in children conclude that parent inclusion in treatment shows similar overall efficacy to child-centred interventions alone, though some studies indicate that active parent involvement produces superior outcomes.
5. Models of child anxiety recognise three interacting layers: genetic predisposition, reinforcement of fear by parental anxiety and parenting behaviours, and parents coaching children in coping strategies — together warranting a systematic focus on parent involvement in treatment.
6. Assessment of family contributions to child anxiety should encompass environmental factors such as parental divorce, parental psychopathology, substance abuse, and adverse childhood events.

## Numerical findings
- 30% to 40% of variance in anxiety disorders attributed to heritability (twin and adoption studies)
- 4-fold increase in risk observed in offspring of anxious parents (meta-analyses of family aggregate studies)
- Family accommodation explains 20% to 50% of the impact of child anxiety disorders on functional impairment
- Article: Volume 47, Issue 4, December 2024, Pages 787–800
- 68 references cited

## Study design
Narrative review synthesising existing literature on family-level risk and protective factors in the aetiology and treatment of child and adolescent anxiety disorders; no primary data collected; 68 references cited.

## Main limitation
As a narrative (non-systematic) review without a registered protocol or systematic search strategy, it is susceptible to selection bias in the literature included and cannot provide pooled effect estimates; full methods are behind a paywall so the scope of included studies cannot be fully verified.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Best cited to support arguments about family accommodation as a systemic maintainer of child anxiety (accounting for up to 50% of functional impairment), parental transmission of anxiety risk (4-fold offspring risk; 30–40% heritability), and the rationale for including parents actively in CBT-based interventions.
