# Ho et al. (2016) Efficacy and cost-effectiveness of stepped care for anxiety/depression. *Scientific Reports*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Ho, F.Y.-Y., Yeung, W.-F., Ng, T.H.-Y. and Chan, C.S. (2016) 'The Efficacy and Cost-Effectiveness of Stepped Care Prevention and Treatment for Depressive and/or Anxiety Disorders: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis', Scientific Reports, 6, 29281. DOI: 10.1038/srep29281.

**Evidence tier:** Meta-analysis / SR — Systematic review and meta-analysis (k=10 RCTs: 6 prevention, 4 treatment)
**Validity estimate:** 68/100
**DOI:** 10.1038/srep29281
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Stepped care treatment was significantly more effective than care-as-usual (CAU) in reducing anxiety symptoms at immediate posttreatment, with a small but consistent pooled effect across three RCTs.
2. The treatment response rate for anxiety disorders was significantly higher in stepped care than in CAU, supporting stepped care as a viable delivery model for anxiety treatment.
3. Stepped care prevention did not significantly reduce the incidence of anxiety or depressive disorders compared to CAU, meaning stepped care works better as a treatment than as a preventive strategy.
4. Stepped care showed no significant advantage over CAU in reducing depressive symptoms, suggesting its benefit may be more specific to anxiety than to depression.
5. The cost-effectiveness evidence was inconsistent: one study found stepped care prevention affordable for elderly populations (depression/anxiety-free year at an incremental cost of €563 per recipient), while another found no significant cost advantage over CAU.
6. The model's two core features — 'least restrictive' (starting with self-help) and 'self-correcting' (stepping-up criteria to escalate care) — are identified as essential structural elements that determine both reach and efficacy.

## Numerical findings
- Stepped care treatment vs. CAU on anxiety symptoms: SMD = -0.29, 95% CI: -0.48 to -0.10, p < 0.01, I² = 0% (k=3 RCTs)
- Treatment response rate for anxiety disorders: OR = 2.38, 95% CI: 1.25 to 4.52, p < 0.01, I² = 0% (k=2 RCTs)
- Incidence of anxiety/depressive disorders (prevention): OR = 0.75, 95% CI: 0.41 to 1.38, p = 0.36, I² = 45% (k=3 RCTs) — non-significant
- Total k=10 RCTs included; prevention arm n=731 (sample sizes 136-240); treatment arm n=488 (sample sizes 30-180)
- Prevention attrition: stepped care 15.0% vs. CAU 8.9%; risk ratio 1.7, 95% CI: 1.2 to 2.4, p < 0.01, I² = 40%
- Treatment attrition: stepped care 19.4% vs. CAU 5.0%; risk ratio 1.9, 95% CI: 0.9 to 4.3, p = 0.11, I² = 0%
- Inter-rater reliability for quality assessment: Cohen's Kappa = 0.76, p < 0.001
- Cost-effectiveness (prevention, Study 3): incremental cost of €563 per recipient; average €4,367 for a depression/anxiety-free year
- Cost-effectiveness (prevention, Study 2): stepped care total mean cost €4,284 vs. CAU €3,446 — no significant difference
- Stepped care for bulimia nervosa (cited comparison): cost per abstinent subject USD $12,146 (stepped care) vs. USD $20,317 (CBT)

## Study design
Systematic review and meta-analysis of 10 RCTs (identified from 3,093 citations across 5 databases up to April 2015) comparing stepped care prevention or treatment (with self-help as a required first step) against care-as-usual or waiting-list control for adults with depressive and/or anxiety disorders; random effects models used throughout.

## Main limitation
The number of included RCTs was small (k=10), limiting statistical power and the ability to assess publication bias via funnel plots; the majority of trials were conducted in Netherlands and US healthcare contexts, making generalisability to low-resource or non-Western settings uncertain.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support arguments about CBT-based stepped care as a systemic, scalable treatment model for child and adolescent anxiety — specifically to evidence that structured, least-restrictive-first delivery (self-help escalating to face-to-face CBT) significantly reduces anxiety symptoms and improves treatment response rates compared to standard care, while also addressing healthcare resource constraints.
