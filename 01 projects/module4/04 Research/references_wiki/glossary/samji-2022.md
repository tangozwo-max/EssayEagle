# Samji et al. (2022) Mental health impacts of COVID-19 on children and youth: systematic review. *Child and Adolescent Mental Health*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Samji, H., Wu, J., Ladak, A., Vossen, C., Stewart, E., Dove, N., Long, D. and Snell, G. (2022) 'Review: Mental health impacts of the COVID-19 pandemic on children and youth — a systematic review', *Child and Adolescent Mental Health*, 27(2), pp. 172–189. doi: 10.1111/camh.12501

**Evidence tier:** Systematic review (k=116 studies; N=127,923 children and adolescents)
**Validity estimate:** 72/100
**DOI:** 10.1111/camh.12501
**PDF status:** full-pdf

## Key usable claims
1. Children and adolescents experienced significantly elevated depressive and anxious symptoms during the COVID-19 pandemic compared to pre-pandemic estimates, driven by a confluence of pandemic-specific stressors (fear of infection, social isolation, disruption to schooling and routines, economic insecurity).
2. Older adolescents, girls, and young people living with neurodiversities or chronic physical conditions were more likely to experience negative mental health outcomes — identifying priority subgroups for monitoring and early intervention.
3. Pandemic control measures (home confinement, lockdowns, school closures) directly disrupted access to social support, mental health services, peer networks, and protective routines — each of which independently confers mental health protection when present.
4. Physical exercise, access to entertainment, positive familial relationships, and social support were all independently associated with better mental health outcomes during the pandemic, highlighting protective factors at multiple ecological levels.
5. The relationship between pandemic adversity and child mental health was moderated by existing vulnerabilities: children with pre-existing mental health conditions experienced symptomatic worsening, especially when service access was disrupted.
6. The pandemic's impact constitutes a natural experiment demonstrating that systemic disruptions to social and institutional protective factors substantially increase child and adolescent mental health risk.

## Numerical findings
- 116 peer-reviewed and preprint articles included; search period January 2020 – February 22, 2021
- Total data: 127,923 children and adolescents; 50,984 child/adolescent proxy reports; >3,000 chart reviews
- High prevalence of COVID-19-related fear documented across studies
- More depressive and anxious symptoms compared to pre-pandemic rates across studies
- UK longitudinal survey (N>50,000): proportion reporting clinically significant mental distress rose from 19% (2018) to 27% (April 2020, one month into lockdown)
- Older adolescents, girls, and those with neurodiversities or chronic conditions disproportionately affected
- Protective factors: physical exercise, entertainment access, positive family relationships, social support

## Study design
Systematic review (CAMH, 2022); searched peer-reviewed and preprint databases (January 2020 – February 2021); included studies collecting primary data on COVID-19-related mental health impacts on children and adolescents (<19 years); quality graded using Oxford Centre for Evidence-Based Medicine rating scheme; outcomes: anxiety, depression, PTSD, fear, general mental health.

## Main limitation
Rapid-review timeframe captures early pandemic data only (pre-March 2021); many studies used non-validated outcome measures or lacked control groups; inclusion of preprints introduces quality variability; predominantly cross-sectional designs limit causal inference; heterogeneity in pandemic responses across countries complicates synthesis.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use to support the argument that systemic social disruptions — analogous to the chronic adversity many anxious children already experience — rapidly translate into measurable increases in child and adolescent anxiety and depression, and that protective factors at family and community level (positive relationships, routine access) buffer this risk. Particularly relevant to discussions of COVID-19's role in exacerbating pre-existing anxiogenic family dynamics and increasing mental health service demand among young people.
