# Nan et al. (2024) Is Social Media Use Related to Social Anxiety? A Meta-Analysis. *Mass Communication and Society*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Nan, Y., Qin, J., Li, Z., Kim, N.G., Kim, S.S.Y. and Miller, L.C. (2024) 'Is Social Media Use Related to Social Anxiety? A Meta-Analysis', Mass Communication and Society, 27(3), pp. 441–474. DOI: 10.1080/15205436.2024.2321533

**Evidence tier:** Meta-analysis / SR — Meta-analysis (k=27 independent study samples, N=38,163)
**Validity estimate:** 72/100
**DOI:** 10.1080/15205436.2024.2321533
**PDF status:** abstract-only

## Key usable claims
1. Social media use is significantly positively associated with social anxiety, with a small but reliable overall effect size (r = .14) across 27 independent samples.
2. Prior reviews yielded inconclusive results partly because they examined social anxiety or social media use in relation to broader categories (e.g., psychological well-being, computer-mediated communication) rather than the specific pairing directly.
3. Social media may function as an alternative, potentially less anxiety-provoking communication channel for individuals with social anxiety, which could complicate interpretation of the positive association.
4. The effect size was smaller in adolescent-only samples compared to mixed-age or adult samples, suggesting the relationship may be attenuated or differ in nature among younger populations.
5. Studies using White-majority or North American samples, and studies with less reliable measures of social media use, showed smaller positive effect sizes — pointing to moderation by demographic and methodological factors.
6. The authors argue that social media affords both risks and benefits that are specific to each mental health challenge, and that future reviews should disaggregate findings by specific condition rather than broad psychological well-being categories.

## Numerical findings
- r = .14 (overall meta-analytic correlation between social media use and social anxiety)
- k = 27 independent study samples included
- Total N = 38,163 participants across included studies
- Random-effects model used for primary analysis
- Smaller effect sizes found for adolescent-only samples, White-majority samples, North American samples, and studies with less reliable social media use measures (specific moderator r values not visible in abstract)

## Study design
Meta-analysis using a random-effects model, synthesising 27 independent study samples (total N = 38,163) that specifically examined the relationship between social media use and social anxiety, with moderation analyses for age group, racial composition, geographic region, and measure reliability.

## Main limitation
The full limitations section is behind a paywall; from the abstract and moderation findings, a key limitation is that the overall positive effect is small (r = .14) and effect sizes vary substantially across demographic groups and measurement quality, indicating that the aggregate estimate may obscure important heterogeneity and that causal direction cannot be established from cross-sectional constituent studies.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support the argument that social media use is a systemic environmental risk factor for social anxiety in children and adolescents — specifically to quantify the association (r = .14) and to note that the effect, though small, is consistent across a large aggregated sample, while also acknowledging that the relationship is weaker in adolescent-only samples, which nuances claims about age-specific vulnerability.
