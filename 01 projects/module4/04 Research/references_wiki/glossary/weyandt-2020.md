# Weyandt et al. (2020) Neuroplasticity in children and adolescents in response to treatment intervention. *Clinical & Translational Neuroscience*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Weyandt, L.L., Clarkin, C.M., Holding, E.Z., May, S.E., Marraccini, M.E., Gudmundsdottir, B.G., Shepard, E. and Thompson, L. (2020) 'Neuroplasticity in children and adolescents in response to treatment intervention: A systematic review of the literature', Clinical & Translational Neuroscience, July-December 2020, pp. 1–21. DOI: 10.1177/2514183X20974231

**Evidence tier:** Meta-analysis / SR — Systematic review (k=28 neuroimaging studies, PRISMA-adherent)
**Validity estimate:** 62/100
**DOI:** 10.1177/2514183X20974231
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Experience-dependent interventions — both cognitive and sensory-motor — are associated with measurable neuroplastic changes (structural and functional) in children and adolescents across neurotypical, neurodevelopmental, and neurological-injury populations.
2. Negative environmental triggers such as poverty, neglect, and chronic stress can produce morphological and functional brain changes that increase vulnerability to psychological disorders, underscoring how environmental context shapes neurobiological development.
3. Cognitive behaviour therapy for children with OCD and anxiety was associated with neuroplastic changes in both grey and white matter, and these changes correlated positively with symptom severity, providing neuroimaging evidence that psychological interventions alter brain structure in anxious youth.
4. 22 of the 28 included studies found evidence of both structural and functional neuroplastic changes following intervention; the three studies showing only functional improvement (no neural change) and the three showing only neural change (no functional improvement) highlight the still-unclear relationship between brain change and behavioural outcome.
5. Longer-duration interventions (beyond 16 weeks) appear to produce more robust neuroplastic effects than shorter ones, suggesting that treatment intensity and duration are clinically meaningful variables for intervention design.
6. The field is hampered by high methodological heterogeneity — inconsistent definitions of neuroplasticity, variable preprocessing pipelines, predominantly small samples, and infrequent reporting of effect sizes — which limits the clinical utility and replicability of findings.

## Numerical findings
- k=28 studies included in the final qualitative synthesis from an initial yield of 1,122 articles
- 7 different neuroimaging techniques employed across the 28 studies (MRI, fMRI, DTI, fNIRS, EEG, and multimodal combinations)
- Total participants across included studies: N=962 (range 4–235 per study; M=34.36, SD=41.63)
- Mean of mean ages across studies: 9.43 years
- 57% of studies (n=16) conducted in the United States
- 82% of studies (n=23) published within the last 5 years of the review window (2013–2018)
- Median intervention duration: 8 weeks; 79% of interventions were 16 weeks or less
- 22 of 28 studies (79%) found evidence of both structural and functional neuroplastic changes
- 35.7% of studies (10/28) had total sample sizes of 20 or fewer participants
- Only 14% of studies (4/28) had sample sizes greater than 50
- Only 17.8% of studies (5/28) acknowledged statistical power or effect size
- Only 7% of volumetric studies reported intracranial volume (ICV) correction methods
- 75% of studies used 1:1 intervention delivery; 11% group delivery; 14% compared both

## Study design
PRISMA-adherent systematic review of 28 neuroimaging studies (published 2008–2018) examining experience-dependent neuroplasticity in children and adolescents (aged 0.59–17.3 years; total N=962) across neurotypical, neurodevelopmental, and neurological-injury clinical populations, using cognitive or sensory-motor interventions with pre- and post-neuroimaging measures.

## Main limitation
High methodological heterogeneity across included studies — in neuroplasticity definitions, imaging modalities, preprocessing pipelines, sample sizes, and statistical approaches — combined with predominantly small samples and infrequent effect-size reporting prevents meta-analytic synthesis and limits the clinical interpretability of the findings.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Cite to support the claim that the developing brain retains neurobiological plasticity in response to both therapeutic intervention (including CBT for anxiety) and adverse environmental exposures (poverty, neglect, stress), grounding arguments about neurobiological vulnerability and the potential for intervention-induced change in anxious children and adolescents.
