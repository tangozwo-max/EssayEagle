# Ask et al. (2021) Intergenerational transmission of parental neuroticism to emotional problems in 8-year-olds. *JCPP Advances*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Ask, H., Eilertsen, E.M., Gjerde, L.C., Hannigan, L.J., Gustavson, K., Havdahl, A., Cheesman, R., McAdams, T.A., Hettema, J.M., Reichborn-Kjennerud, T., Torvik, F.A. and Ystrom, E. (2021) 'Intergenerational transmission of parental neuroticism to emotional problems in 8-year-old children: Genetic and environmental influences', JCPP Advances, 1(4), e12054. DOI: 10.1002/jcv2.12054

**Evidence tier:** Longitudinal cohort — Longitudinal population-based cohort with children-of-twins design (MoBa, N offspring = 26,091)
**Validity estimate:** 82/100

**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Maternal neuroticism has a substantial direct (environmentally mediated) effect on offspring neuroticism, depressive symptoms, and anxiety symptoms in 8-year-olds, even after accounting for genetic transmission and assortative mating — the direct maternal path explained 78–98% of the mother–child similarity.
2. Genetic transmission from mothers explained only 16–18% of mother–offspring correlations for neuroticism and depressive symptoms, and none for anxiety symptoms, indicating that the intergenerational link for anxiety is predominantly environmental rather than genetic.
3. Father–child similarities were largely explained by assortative mating (partners resembling each other phenotypically, r = 0.26) and by maternal genetic and direct influences, rather than by independent paternal effects; genetic transmission from fathers accounted for approximately 39–41% of father–offspring correlations for neuroticism and depression but none for anxiety.
4. The total variance in child anxiety, depression, and neuroticism explained by all intergenerational influences (direct and genetic combined) was modest: 2.7%, 8.8%, and 8.5% respectively, highlighting the importance of child-specific factors.
5. Assortative mating for neuroticism inflated heritability estimates of neuroticism, depression, and anxiety in the offspring generation by approximately 18–19% compared to a hypothetical random-mating population.
6. The results support the conclusion that interventions targeting maternal functioning and parenting quality could reduce transmission of emotional problems to children, because the primary transmission pathway is environmental rather than genetic.

## Numerical findings
- Mother–child correlations: neuroticism r = 0.31 (95% CI = 0.29–0.33); depressive symptoms r = 0.31 (95% CI = 0.29–0.33); anxiety symptoms r = 0.16 (95% CI = 0.14–0.19)
- Father–child correlations: neuroticism r = 0.13 (95% CI = 0.11–0.15); depressive symptoms r = 0.13 (95% CI = 0.11–0.15); anxiety symptoms r = 0.05 (95% CI = 0.03–0.08)
- Direct maternal path explained 80% (95% CI = 47–95%) of mother–child similarity for neuroticism, 78% (95% CI = 66–89%) for depressive symptoms, and 98% (95% CI = 45–112%) for anxiety symptoms
- Genetic transmission from maternal neuroticism explained 16% (95% CI = 9–23%) of offspring neuroticism correlation and 18% (95% CI = 11–25%) of depressive symptoms correlation; 0% for anxiety
- Genetic transmission from paternal neuroticism explained 39% (95% CI = 22–56%) of offspring neuroticism correlation and 41% (95% CI = 25–58%) of depressive symptoms correlation; 0% for anxiety
- Parental assortative mating phenotypic correlation: r = 0.26 (95% CI = 0.24–0.28); genetic component rg = 0.16, environmental component re = 0.10
- Total intergenerational influences explained 8.5% of variance in child neuroticism, 8.8% in depressive symptoms, 2.7% in anxiety symptoms
- Total sample: 11,088 extended family units, 26,091 children (48.9% girls), 22,176 parents
- Adult neuroticism heritability estimated at 43% (95% CI = 0.41–0.46)
- Offspring neuroticism heritability 26.3%; depressive symptoms 43.7%; anxiety symptoms 27.8%
- Assortative mating increased heritability of neuroticism, depression, and anxiety in offspring generation by 18%, 19%, and 19% respectively

## Study design
Extended children-of-twins design using the Norwegian Mother, Father and Child Cohort Study (MoBa), a prospective population-based pregnancy cohort; parental generation comprised 11,088 sibling pairs (including MZ twins, DZ twins, full siblings, and half-siblings), their partners (N = 22,176), and their 8-year-old children (N = 26,091); structural equation modelling in OpenMx was used to partition intergenerational associations into direct environmental and genetic transmission components while adjusting for assortative mating.

## Main limitation
Outcome measures were obtained exclusively by maternal self-report, which introduces shared method variance that could inflate mother–child similarity and artificially inflate the apparent direct maternal effect relative to paternal effects; the study acknowledges this as a potentially confounding limitation and calls for future inclusion of paternal or child self-report data.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support the argument that parental transmission of anxiety and emotional problems to children operates primarily through environmental (not genetic) mechanisms — specifically maternal influences such as parenting style, modelling, and home environment — which implies that targeting parental functioning is a viable preventive intervention for child anxiety.
