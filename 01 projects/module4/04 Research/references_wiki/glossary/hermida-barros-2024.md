# Hermida-Barros et al. (2024) Family accommodation in OCD: systematic review and meta-analysis. *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Hermida-Barros, L., Primé-Tous, M., García-Delgar, B., Forcadell, E., Lera-Miguel, S., Fernández de la Cruz, L., Vieta, E., Radua, J., Lázaro, L. and Fullana, M.A. (2024) 'Family accommodation in obsessive-compulsive disorder: An updated systematic review and meta-analysis', Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews, [vol. to verify], p. 105678. DOI: 10.1016/j.neubiorev.2024.105678

**Validity estimate:** 82/100

## Key usable claims
1. Family accommodation — defined as familial participation in a person's compulsions or avoidant behaviours and modification of family routines due to OCD symptoms — is moderately prevalent across both paediatric and adult OCD populations.
2. There is a significant positive correlation between family accommodation and OCD symptom severity (r = 0.42), confirming that higher accommodation is consistently associated with more severe OCD.
3. Baseline levels of family accommodation do not predict pre- to post-treatment change in OCD severity (g = -0.03), meaning clinicians cannot use accommodation as a prognostic marker for treatment response.
4. CBT for OCD — both individual-focused (g = 2.00) and family-focused (g = 1.17) formats — significantly reduces family accommodation, establishing treatment as a mechanism for changing accommodation behaviour.
5. Family accommodation is a multi-domain construct (participation, modification, distress and consequences) and different family members — parents, partners, siblings — exhibit different types of accommodation behaviours.
6. Parental-rearing styles and parental mental disorders have previously been associated with increased OCD risk, situating family accommodation within a broader picture of family-level systemic factors in OCD.

## Numerical findings
- 108 studies involving 8,928 individuals with OCD included (abstract figure); final selection snippet reports 117 studies and 9,787 individuals (44.77% female; 68.21% paediatric)
- Correlation between family accommodation and OCD severity: r = 0.42
- Effect of baseline family accommodation on treatment outcome: g = -0.03 (non-significant)
- Reduction in family accommodation via individual CBT: g = 2.00
- Reduction in family accommodation via family-focused CBT: g = 1.17
- Prior meta-analysis (Wu et al., 2016a; k = 41): r = 0.42 for accommodation-severity association
- Prior meta-analysis (Strauss et al., 2015; k = 14): r = 0.35 for accommodation-severity association
- 76 new studies added relative to the most recent comparable meta-analysis

## Study design
Pre-registered (PROSPERO CRD42021264461) PRISMA-compliant systematic review and meta-analysis of 117 studies (N = 9,787 individuals with OCD, 68% paediatric) covering adults, children, and adolescents, searching PubMed, Scopus, and Web of Science.

## Main limitation
The review combined studies with heterogeneous assessment instruments and mixed RCT/non-RCT designs, with the prior literature showing evidence of significant heterogeneity and potential publication bias that may also affect the updated estimates.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support the argument that family accommodation is a systemic family-level factor that reinforces OCD symptom severity (r = 0.42) and that CBT — especially family-focused formats — is an effective intervention for reducing accommodation behaviour, making it directly relevant to discussions of parental transmission, systemic maintenance of anxiety, and the role of CBT in child and adolescent OCD treatment.
