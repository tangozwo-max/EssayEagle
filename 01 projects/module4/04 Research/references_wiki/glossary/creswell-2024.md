# Creswell et al. (2024) Digitally augmented parent-led CBT vs treatment as usual for child anxiety. *Lancet Psychiatry*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Creswell, C., Taylor, L., Giles, S., Howitt, S., Radley, L., Whitaker, E., Brooks, E., Knight, F., Raymont, V., Hill, C., van Santen, J., Williams, N., Mort, S., Harris, V., Yu, S., Pollard, J., Violato, M., Waite, P. and Yu, L-M. (2024) 'Digitally augmented, parent-led CBT versus treatment as usual for child anxiety problems in child mental health services in England and Northern Ireland: a pragmatic, non-inferiority, clinical effectiveness and cost-effectiveness randomised controlled trial', Lancet Psychiatry, 11(3), pp. 193–209. DOI: 10.1016/S2215-0366(23)00429-7

**Evidence tier:** RCT — Pragmatic non-inferiority RCT (n=444, 34 CAMHS sites)
**Validity estimate:** 82/100
**DOI:** 10.1016/S2215-0366(23)00429-7
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Therapist-supported, digitally augmented, parent-led CBT (OSI) was non-inferior to treatment as usual on parent-reported anxiety interference in children aged 5–12 at 26-week follow-up, with a standardised mean difference of 0.01 (95% CI –0.15 to 0.17), well within the pre-specified non-inferiority margin of 0.33.
2. OSI plus therapist support required substantially less therapist contact time than treatment as usual: mean delivery time was 182 minutes versus 307 minutes, meaning OSI required only 59% of the therapist time of usual care.
3. Non-inferiority was demonstrated across all secondary clinical outcomes including child-reported anxiety interference, parent- and child-reported anxiety symptoms, overall functioning, and emotional and behavioural problems.
4. Parent and therapist acceptability of OSI was high; most parents found it user-friendly, flexible, and effective, and many reported increased parental self-efficacy and skills that they could apply to future difficulties beyond the trial.
5. 85% of treatment-as-usual participants received CBT, yet the digitally augmented parent-led approach achieved equivalent outcomes, supporting parent-led delivery as a viable and scalable first-line model for child anxiety in routine CAMHS.
6. Cost-effectiveness analyses suggested OSI plus therapist support was likely to be cost effective under several scenarios, though uncertainty was high and results were sensitive to the QALY value set used.

## Numerical findings
- n=444 children aged 5–12 randomised (222 per arm) across 34 CAMHS sites in England and Northern Ireland
- Primary outcome SMD at 26 weeks: 0.01 (95% CI –0.15 to 0.17); p<0.0001 for non-inferiority
- Non-inferiority margin: SMD 0.33
- Mean therapist delivery time: OSI 182 minutes vs treatment as usual 307 minutes (OSI = 59% of TAU time)
- 85% of treatment-as-usual participants received CBT
- Mean child age: 9.20 years (SD 1.79); 58% female, 90% White
- 79% of OSI arm and 76% of TAU arm completed the week 14 assessment; 79% and 74% completed week 26 assessment
- Only 79% of enrolled families started treatment within 12 weeks of randomisation; 13% received no treatment within 26 weeks
- 15% of children had a recognised special educational need
- No serious adverse events reported in either arm; 10 adverse events per arm

## Study design
Pragmatic, unblinded, two-arm, multisite, non-inferiority RCT comparing therapist-supported digitally augmented parent-led CBT (OSI platform) with treatment as usual in 444 children aged 5–12 with primary anxiety problems across 34 CAMHS in England and Northern Ireland, with outcomes assessed at 14 and 26 weeks post-randomisation.

## Main limitation
Data collectors were not blind to treatment arm, and only 79% of families started their allocated treatment within the required 12-week window, with 13% receiving no treatment at all within 26 weeks, limiting confidence in per-protocol analyses; cost-effectiveness conclusions were also highly sensitive to the QALY value set chosen.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use to support arguments about the scalability of CBT delivery for child anxiety through stepped-care and parent-led models: the paper provides direct RCT evidence that therapist-supported, parent-led digital CBT achieves equivalent outcomes to standard CAMHS treatment at significantly lower therapist cost, directly addressing the structural barriers (long waiting lists, workforce capacity) that prevent anxious children from accessing evidence-based care.
