# Zhou et al. (2025) Moderators of CBTs for child/adolescent anxiety. *Clinical Psychology Review*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Zhou, S., Creswell, C., Košir, U. and Reardon, T. (2025) 'Moderators of cognitive and behaviour therapies for prevention and treatment of anxiety disorders in children and adolescents: A systematic review and meta-analysis', Clinical Psychology Review, 116, 102548. DOI: 10.1016/j.cpr.2025.102548

**Evidence tier:** Meta-analysis / SR — Systematic review and meta-analysis (k=86 RCTs, 98 CBT trial arms)
**Validity estimate:** 78/100
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.cpr.2025.102548
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Child age moderates CBT outcomes differently across intervention types: older age (adolescent samples) was associated with greater effect sizes for universal prevention, while mixed child/adolescent samples showed larger treatment effects than preadolescent-only samples, though clear conclusions are limited by data availability and potential confounders.
2. Parental involvement enhances CBT effectiveness for universal anxiety prevention in preadolescent children: child-focused CBT with limited parental involvement produced a significant pooled effect versus non-active controls (Hedge's g = -0.25), whereas child-only CBT did not.
3. Facilitator background matters for universal prevention: CBT delivered by mental health professionals produced a medium, significant effect (Hedge's g = -0.43) on child-reported outcomes, significantly larger than school staff-delivered CBT, which was not distinguishable from non-active controls.
4. For treatment, CBT involving 9-16 hours of facilitator contact time produced the largest child-reported effect size (Hedge's g = -1.02) and was significantly more effective than CBT with less than 9 hours of contact, suggesting a minimum threshold of therapist contact for treatment efficacy.
5. Child baseline anxiety level did not significantly moderate CBT outcomes across universal prevention, targeted prevention, or treatment conditions, contrary to some prior individual-trial findings.
6. Significant publication bias was detected across included trials, with missing studies concentrated in non-significant or negative result regions, indicating the overall evidence base skews positive.

## Numerical findings
- 86 studies meeting eligibility criteria; 98 CBT trial arms versus non-active controls (31 universal prevention, 31 targeted prevention, 36 treatment)
- 89 child-reported and 54 parent-reported effect sizes extracted
- Universal prevention: CBT by mental health professionals Hedge's g = -0.43 (p < 0.001, 95% CI -0.62 to -0.24, I2 = 67.0%, k = 16) vs school staff Hedge's g = 0.05 (ns, k = 9), between-group Q = 5.88, p = 0.02
- Universal prevention meta-regression: older child age associated with larger effect sizes (k = 22, b = -0.07, p = 0.03, R2 = 29.11%)
- Targeted prevention: adolescent subgroup Hedge's g = -0.96 (p = 0.05, 95% CI -1.89 to -0.03, k = 8) vs mixed samples Hedge's g = -0.08 (ns), Q = 4.56, p = 0.03
- Treatment with 9-16 h facilitator contact: Hedge's g = -1.02 (p < 0.001, 95% CI -1.45 to -0.60, I2 = 85.7%, k = 14) vs <9 h: Hedge's g = -0.27 (ns), Q = 7.50, p = 0.006
- Parental involvement (preadolescent universal prevention): child-focused with limited parental involvement Hedge's g = -0.25 (p = 0.01, 95% CI -0.41 to -0.10, k = 4) vs child-only Hedge's g = -0.02 (ns, k = 8), Q = 6.02, p = 0.01
- Publication bias Egger's test: child-reported k = 81, t = -4.68, p < 0.0001; parent-reported k = 53, t = -4.10, p < 0.0001
- Anxiety disorder prevalence in childhood/adolescence: 6.5% (cited from Polanczyk et al., 2015)
- 73.3% of all anxiety disorders begin by age 25; median onset age 17 years; peak onset age 5.5 years (cited from Solmi et al., 2021)
- Inter-rater agreement on study screening: 98.7%, kappa = 0.91 (p < 0.001)
- 62% of included trials showed some risk of bias due to randomisation process; 44% showed risk of bias related to missing data

## Study design
Pre-registered systematic review and meta-analysis of 86 RCTs (98 CBT arms vs non-active controls) evaluating CBT for universal prevention, targeted prevention, and treatment of anxiety disorders in children and adolescents under 19 years, using meta-regression and subgroup analyses to test moderators including child age, baseline anxiety, parental anxiety, intervention duration, facilitator contact time, facilitator background, delivery format, and parental involvement.

## Main limitation
The ability to draw firm conclusions about any moderator was substantially limited by the small number of studies within many subgroups, wide heterogeneity in outcomes (I2 frequently 60-90%), potential confounding between moderators (e.g., delivery format and facilitator contact time were conflated), inconsistencies between child- and parent-reported outcomes, and significant publication bias toward positive results across the entire evidence base.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support arguments about what makes CBT more or less effective for child and adolescent anxiety, particularly: that parental involvement enhances prevention outcomes for younger children; that adequate therapist contact time (9-16 hours) is associated with stronger treatment effects; and that CBT delivered by mental health professionals outperforms school staff-delivered programmes for universal prevention — directly relevant to discussions of stepped care, optimal CBT delivery parameters, and the role of parental factors in treatment outcomes.
