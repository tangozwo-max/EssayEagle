# Lester &amp; Michelson (2024) Perfect storm: EBSA in the post-COVID-19 context. *BMJ Mental Health*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Lester, K.J. and Michelson, D. (2024) 'Perfect storm: emotionally based school avoidance in the post-COVID-19 pandemic context', BMJ Mental Health, 27, pp. 1–4. DOI: 10.1136/bmjment-2023-300944

**Evidence tier:** Theory / narrative review — Perspective / narrative review (expert commentary with literature synthesis)
**Validity estimate:** 52/100
**DOI:** 10.1136/bmjment-2023-300944
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. The COVID-19 pandemic created a 'perfect storm' of conditions that amplified known school-, family-, and child-based risk factors for emotionally based school avoidance (EBSA) while simultaneously reducing access to support services.
2. Post-pandemic persistent absenteeism rates approximately doubled compared with pre-pandemic levels in England, and the adverse mental health impacts were most sustained for children with SEND, neurodevelopmental disorders, fewer friendships, and those from lower-income families.
3. School closures reinforced avoidance for children with pre-existing EBSA, and a fundamental breakdown in home-school relationships — partly attributable to pandemic restrictions — is an important predictor of absenteeism.
4. CAMHS access has been severely rationed post-pandemic, with nearly 250,000 children (32% of referrals) turned away or redirected by NHS CAMHS in 2021/22, creating an urgent need for scalable, early-access interventions.
5. Family accommodation behaviours (repeated reassurance-giving that maintains anxious avoidance) are a key treatment target within CAMHS-delivered CBT for EBSA, though evidence for CBT's effectiveness in improving school attendance is mixed.
6. Parent-focused, contextually sensitive, brief online interventions — potentially peer-led — are proposed as a scalable complement to specialist services, especially for multiply stressed and disadvantaged families.

## Numerical findings
- Overall absence rate in English schools during 2022/2023 was 7.5%, compared with approximately 5% pre-pandemic.
- Rate of persistent absenteeism (missing >10% of sessions) was 22.3% in 2022/2023, approximately double the pre-pandemic rate.
- Only 50% of pupils with attendance below 90% achieved expected Key Stage 2 standards in reading, writing and mathematics, compared with 71% of pupils with >99% attendance.
- EBSA results in missed schooling for an estimated 1%–5% of the total school population.
- UNESCO estimated 10.9 million primary and secondary students worldwide were at risk of not returning to school after the pandemic.
- Nearly 250,000 children (32% of all referrals) were turned away or redirected by NHS CAMHS during the 2021/22 financial year.

## Study design
Expert perspective article (narrative review and commentary) synthesising epidemiological statistics, qualitative findings, and intervention literature on EBSA in England in the post-COVID-19 context; no primary data collection.

## Main limitation
As a perspective/commentary piece rather than a systematic review or primary study, the article does not present a formal evidence synthesis methodology, limiting the rigour with which causal claims about pandemic effects on EBSA can be evaluated.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support arguments about systemic and family-level risk factors amplifying child anxiety and school avoidance post-pandemic, particularly the role of family accommodation in maintaining anxious avoidance and the case for accessible, parent-focused stepped-care interventions as an alternative to specialist CBT when CAMHS access is constrained.
