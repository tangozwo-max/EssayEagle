# Iniesta-Sepúlveda et al. (2017) CBFT meta-analysis for pediatric OCD. *Journal of Anxiety Disorders*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**

(2017) 'Cognitive-behavioral high parental involvement treatments for pediatric obsessive-compulsive disorder: A meta-analysis', Journal of Anxiety Disorders, [to verify](vol/issue), [to verify](pp). DOI: 10.1016/j.janxdis.2017.03.010

**Evidence tier:** Meta-analysis / SR — Meta-analysis (k=27 studies, CBT with high parental involvement for pediatric OCD)
**Validity estimate:** 72/100
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.janxdis.2017.03.010
**PDF status:** abstract-only

## Key usable claims
1. Cognitive-Behavioral Family Treatment (CBFT) — CBT with high parental involvement — produces a large and clinically significant reduction in obsessive-compulsive symptoms in children and adolescents, with an adjusted mean effect size of d=1.464 at post-treatment.
2. CBFT also reduces family accommodation, but the effect is substantially smaller (d=0.511), indicating that parental participation in treatment has limited impact on accommodation behaviours even when parents attend the majority of sessions.
3. Individual CBFT outperforms group CBFT in reducing OCD symptoms, with effect sizes of d+=2.429 vs d+=1.409 respectively, suggesting that treatment format is a meaningful moderator.
4. Family accommodation has been observed as a predictor of symptom severity and poor response to CBT in children with OCD, providing the theoretical rationale for including parental components in treatment.
5. No differential efficacy was found across the specific CBFT techniques used, either for child OCD outcomes or for parent outcomes, suggesting that the presence of structured parental involvement matters more than which specific techniques are employed.
6. Publication bias was assessed and discarded as a threat to the validity of the meta-analytic results, and large heterogeneity among effect sizes was detected across the included studies.

## Numerical findings
- Adjusted mean effect size for OCD symptoms (CY-BOCS) at post-test: d_adj=1.464
- Adjusted mean effect size for family accommodation (FAS) at post-test: d_adj=0.511
- Individual CBFT effect size: d+=2.429
- Group CBFT effect size: d+=1.409
- Number of studies included: k=27
- OCD prevalence in children and adolescents: approximately 2%
- Mean follow-up duration: 4.35±5.04 months (range 1–18 months)
- Discussion section reports global adjusted CY-BOCS effect size d_adj=1.45, consistent with previous meta-analyses reporting ds ranging from 1.68 to 2.03
- Prior meta-analysis: parental involvement levels associated with d+=1.45 (minimal), d+=1.54 (moderate), d+=2.26 (high), explaining 34% of variance

## Study design
Meta-analysis of 27 studies examining the efficacy of CBT with high parental involvement (parents attending at least 70% of sessions and trained in accommodation-reduction and exposure coaching) in children and adolescents under 19 years with a DSM/ICD diagnosis of OCD, using CY-BOCS (OCD symptoms) and FAS (family accommodation) as standardised outcome measures at post-treatment and follow-up.

## Main limitation
CBFT shows only medium effectiveness for reducing family accommodation (d=0.511), and the authors conclude that additional targeted modules must be incorporated into CBFT to improve outcomes for accommodation specifically; large heterogeneity across effect sizes also limits generalisability of the pooled estimate.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support the argument that family accommodation is both a driver of OCD severity in children and a target for CBT-based intervention, and to quantify the differential effectiveness of parental-involvement CBT on symptoms versus accommodation — specifically that CBT with high parental involvement produces large symptom gains but only moderate reductions in accommodating behaviour, indicating that accommodation requires dedicated therapeutic targeting beyond standard parental inclusion.
