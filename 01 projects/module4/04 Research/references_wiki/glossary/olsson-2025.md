# Olsson et al. (2025) Stepped vs stratified care for youth anxiety: pilot RCT. *Child Psychiatry & Human Development*.

## Key usable claims
1. A pre-registered pilot RCT found that a full-scale trial comparing stepped care (all patients start with internet-delivered CBT) and stratified care (complex cases start directly with in-person CBT) for youth with anxiety disorders in CAMHS is feasible, with high retention and acceptable adherence.
2. Preliminary efficacy data, not powered for between-group comparisons, showed notably higher treatment response rates in stratified care (75%) than stepped care (40%) at the six-month primary endpoint, suggesting that matching treatment intensity to clinical complexity from the outset may produce better outcomes.
3. Internet-delivered CBT (ICBT) produced lower response rates (28%) and smaller within-group effect sizes (Cohen's d = 0.37) than in-person CBT (35% response; d = 0.69) as a first treatment course, indicating that a meaningful proportion of youth — approximately 60–72% — require more intensive intervention after ICBT.
4. The sample was highly comorbid: 80% of enrolled youth met criteria for more than one mental or neurodevelopmental disorder, with 24% meeting criteria for ADHD, illustrating the real-world clinical complexity of anxiety-disordered youth seeking CAMHS treatment.
5. Resource use was lower in stepped care (13.70 hours per patient) than in stratified care (15.11 hours), with ICBT requiring only 30% of the therapist time needed for in-person CBT, suggesting potential cost-effectiveness advantages for stepped models.
6. Adverse events were largely non-serious; the most common were increased stress and anxiety, and only one serious adverse event (a suicide attempt not attributed to trial participation) was recorded, supporting the safety of both care models.

## Numerical findings
- n=51 randomised (25 stepped care, 26 stratified care); mean age 14.18 years (range 9.25–17.50); 71% girls
- 80% of participants met criteria for more than one psychiatric disorder at baseline
- Treatment response at primary endpoint (30 weeks): 40% stepped care vs 75% stratified care
- Within-group Cohen's d on PARS at primary endpoint: 0.91 (95% CI 0.43–1.37) stepped care; 1.17 (95% CI 0.64–1.69) stratified care
- At 14-week follow-up: 20% response stepped care vs 42% stratified care; Cohen's d 0.37 vs 0.81
- ICBT treatment response (Treatment 1): 28%; in-person CBT (Treatment 1): 35%
- Treatment adherence (>=50% completion): 78% youth and 84% caregivers for ICBT; 74% for in-person CBT Treatment 1; 82% in-person CBT Treatment 2
- Masked outcome assessments completed by 96% of participants; 99% within pre-specified windows
- Resource use: 13.70 hours/patient stepped care; 15.11 hours/patient stratified care; ICBT averaged 3.96 hours vs 13.18 hours for in-person CBT
- Therapist adherence ICC2 = 0.85 (95% CI 0.79–0.89, p<.001); competence ICC2 = 0.73 (95% CI 0.61–0.82, p<.001)
- Recruitment rate: 2 participants per week; 127 screened, 84 (66%) eligible, 51 (61% of eligible) randomised
- 763 patient-reported and 532 caregiver-reported adverse events via AEQ; 26% and 39% respectively linked to treatment; 1 serious adverse event

## Study design
Single-blind, parallel-group, pre-registered pilot RCT (NCT06016907) conducted at a CAMHS research unit in Sweden; 51 youth aged 8–17 with principal DSM-5-TR anxiety disorder randomly assigned 1:1 to stepped care (all start with 12-week ICBT, step up to in-person CBT if insufficient response) or stratified care (complex cases allocated directly to in-person CBT based on a five-domain baseline risk score); primary endpoint at 30 weeks post-randomisation.

## Main limitation
The trial was not powered to detect efficacy differences between groups, and most participants were recruited from specialist CAMHS clinics with relatively high socioeconomic status and Swedish descent, limiting generalisability to the full diversity of families seen in routine services.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Cite this source to support arguments about CBT delivery models and systemic access barriers: it provides direct evidence that matching treatment intensity to clinical complexity (stratified care) outperforms a one-size-starts-at-low-intensity approach (stepped care) for anxious youth, and that approximately 60–72% of young people do not respond sufficiently to ICBT alone, underscoring the inadequacy of low-intensity-first pathways for complex presentations.
