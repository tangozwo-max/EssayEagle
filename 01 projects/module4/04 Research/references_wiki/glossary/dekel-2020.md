# Dekel et al. (2020) Feasibility of parent group treatment for youth anxiety/OCD. *Child Psychiatry & Human Development*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Dekel, I., Dorman-Ilan, S., Lang, C., Bar-David, E., Zilka, H., Shilton, T., Lebowitz, E.R. and Gothelf, D. (2020) 'The Feasibility of a Parent Group Treatment for Youth with Anxiety Disorders and Obsessive Compulsive Disorder', *Child Psychiatry & Human Development*, 52(6), pp. 1044–1049. doi: 10.1007/s10578-020-01082-6

**Evidence tier:** Feasibility / pilot — single-group pre-post design, no control condition (N=25 parents)
**Validity estimate:** 40/100
**DOI:** 10.1007/s10578-020-01082-6
**PDF status:** full-pdf

## Key usable claims
1. CBT is insufficiently effective in approximately 50% of childhood anxiety and OCD cases in clinical trials, and many children refuse to participate altogether — creating a therapeutic gap that parent-only group interventions may fill.
2. The SPACE (Supportive Parenting for Anxious Childhood Emotions) protocol, delivered in group format, targets parental accommodation reduction as the primary mechanism of child anxiety change, without requiring direct child participation in therapy.
3. 88% of parents in the sample were offered SPACE because of long waiting lists for individual CBT — illustrating that group parent-training is a pragmatic response to real-world service constraints.
4. Following group SPACE, parents reported significant reductions in parental accommodation (FASA) and in family power struggles, indicating the family system can shift even without the child in the room.
5. Parental sense of helplessness decreased significantly post-treatment, reflecting a benefit to the parent system beyond symptom change.
6. Clinician-rated anxiety and OCD symptom severity (CGI) in children decreased significantly post-treatment, supporting the indirect treatment pathway through parental behaviour change alone.

## Numerical findings
- N = 30 children initially; N = 25 parents provided pre-post outcome data
- Children: 19 boys; mean age 11.14 (SD 4.15)
- Diagnoses: anxiety disorder n=22, OCD n=6, both n=2
- 22/25 (88%) referred due to waiting lists; 3/25 (12%) because child refused individual CBT
- Mothers in 28/30 (93%) cases; fathers in 2/30 (7%)
- Comorbidities: ADHD 14 children (46%), ODD 4 (16%)
- Significant pre-post reductions in: FASA total score, Family Power Struggles total, Parental Sense of Helplessness total, CGI anxiety/OCD severity
- Instruments: FASA (13-item, 0–4 Likert), Family Power Struggles Questionnaire (16 items), Parental Sense of Helplessness (18 items, 0–6 Likert), CGI (clinician-rated)

## Study design
Single-group pre-post feasibility pilot; group-format SPACE parent training (Israel; Sheba Medical Centre); assessment conducted by independent senior psychologist from medical records; Israeli ethics approval (Declaration of Helsinki); no control condition; outcomes parent-reported except CGI (clinician-rated).

## Main limitation
No control condition; very small sample (N=25); reliance on parent self-report and retrospective clinician file review; cannot rule out spontaneous remission or regression to the mean; no follow-up data reported; sample from a tertiary clinical centre limits generalisability; authors explicitly call for controlled RCT research.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support the argument that family accommodation is a modifiable systemic factor in child and adolescent anxiety, and that parent-only group interventions targeting accommodation behaviour can reduce children's anxiety symptoms even without direct child participation in therapy — relevant to discussions of parental transmission mechanisms and family-level CBT adaptations. Also useful for illustrating service-level realities (long waiting lists) that motivate group parent-training formats within a stepped-care model.
