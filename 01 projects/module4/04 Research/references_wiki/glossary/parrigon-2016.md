# Stuart Parrigon & Kerns (2016) Family Processes in Child Anxiety: Long-Term Impact of Fathers and Mothers. *Journal of Abnormal Child Psychology*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Stuart Parrigon, K.L. and Kerns, K.A. (2016) 'Family Processes in Child Anxiety: the Long-Term Impact of Fathers and Mothers', Journal of Abnormal Child Psychology, 44(7), pp. 1253–1266. DOI: 10.1007/s10802-015-0118-4

**Evidence tier:** Longitudinal cohort — Longitudinal cohort study (9-year follow-up, N=661, 3 time points, path modelling)
**Validity estimate:** 65/100

**PDF status:** abstract-only

## Key usable claims
1. Lower attachment security to fathers — not only mothers — predicts which children maintain anxiety symptoms into adolescence, highlighting fathers as a distinct long-term risk factor.
2. Restriction of maternal autonomy granting (i.e., maternal overcontrol) predicted persistence of child anxiety across the 9-year follow-up period.
3. Partner conflict between parents mediated the association between earlier and later parental anxiety symptoms, representing a novel mechanism in the literature.
4. The longitudinal path model including both fathers and mothers showed good fit for the data, supporting the importance of examining both parents simultaneously rather than mothers alone.
5. Together, family process factors — attachment security, autonomy granting, and interparental conflict — explain meaningful variance in the developmental trajectory of child anxiety from middle childhood into adolescence.

## Numerical findings

## Study design
Secondary analysis of the NICHD Study of Early Child Care and Youth Development (N=661), using a longitudinal path model across three time points (ages 6, 10, and 15) to examine how partner conflict, parent–child attachment security, and parental autonomy granting predict changes in child, mother, and father anxiety symptoms over 9 years.

## Main limitation
Full text is paywalled and the limitations section is not visible; from design, a key limitation is that the sample is drawn from a single large US cohort (NICHD SECCYD) with predominantly Caucasian families, limiting generalisability, and the correlational path model cannot establish causal direction between family processes and child anxiety.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Best cited to support the argument that parental transmission of anxiety operates through identifiable family-level mechanisms — specifically father–child attachment insecurity, maternal overcontrol, and interparental conflict — and that these systemic factors predict the persistence of anxiety from childhood into adolescence.
