# Ahmadzadeh et al. (2023) Mothers' anxiety/depression symptoms and child temperament development. *JCPP Advances*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Ahmadzadeh, Y.I., Eilertsen, E.M., Cheesman, R., Rayner, C., Ystrom, E., Hannigan, L.J. and McAdams, T.A. (2023) 'Mothers' symptoms of anxiety and depression and the development of child temperament: A genetically informative, longitudinal investigation', JCPP Advances, 3(4), e12171. DOI: 10.1002/jcv2.12171

**Evidence tier:** Longitudinal cohort — Longitudinal cohort with genetically informative extended family design (MoBa, n = 42,526 offspring, n = 34,060 mothers; 3 time-points)
**Validity estimate:** 78/100
**DOI:** 10.1002/jcv2.12171
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Maternal anxiety and depression symptoms are weakly but significantly correlated with child emotional temperament (emotionality, shyness, sociability) in the first five years of life, but show no association with child activity level.
2. The intergenerational associations between maternal emotional symptoms and child temperament are driven primarily by stable, trait-like factors shared across time (latent intercept correlations), not by dynamic co-development — change in maternal symptoms did not meaningfully predict change in child temperament.
3. Approximately half of the stable association between maternal emotional symptoms and offspring emotional temperament is attributable to shared genetic factors (50.7%), and the other half to environmentally mediated direct effects (49.3%), establishing that both genetic and non-genetic pathways are operative.
4. This is described by the authors as the first study to demonstrate genetic overlap between adult emotional symptoms and early child emotional temperament using a longitudinal, genetically informative framework.
5. Elevated child emotional temperament (high emotionality) may serve as an early familial marker of risk for later internalising psychopathology, given its associations with both maternal symptoms and subsequent child anxiety and depression outcomes.
6. Environmental, potentially causal, influence accounts for roughly half the mother-child emotional similarity, suggesting that family-level intervention targeting social interactions could reduce intergenerational transmission of emotional symptoms.

## Numerical findings
- n = 42,526 offspring born to 34,060 mothers (Norwegian MoBa cohort)
- Cross-sectional correlation between maternal emotional symptoms and child emotionality: r = 0.15–0.18 across ages 1.5, 3, and 5 years (all p < 0.001)
- Cross-sectional correlation between maternal symptoms and child shyness: r = 0.03–0.05 (p < 0.001)
- Cross-sectional correlation between maternal symptoms and child sociability: r = −0.02 to −0.04 (significant at ages 3 and 5)
- Longitudinal stability correlation (latent intercept): maternal symptoms and offspring emotionality r = 0.143
- Longitudinal stability correlation: maternal symptoms and child shyness r = 0.031; sociability r = −0.015
- Latent slope (change) correlations were all r < 0.005, indicating negligible co-development
- Total correlation between stable maternal emotional symptoms and stable offspring emotionality in genetic decomposition model: r = 0.191
- Genetic relatedness accounted for 50.7% (CI 36.1%–64.6%) of the r = 0.191 association
- Environmentally mediated direct effect accounted for 49.3% (CI 35.4%–63.9%)
- Residual environmentally mediated standardised path coefficient after genetic adjustment: β = 0.094 (CI 0.067–0.123)
- SCL-8 internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha): 0.85, 0.87, 0.86 at ages 1.5, 3, 5 years respectively
- Assessment Ns: 1.5 years = 38,939 children; 3 years = 31,704 children; 5 years = 23,450 children

## Study design
Population-based longitudinal cohort study (Norwegian MoBa) with three measurement waves (child ages 1.5, 3, 5 years), using autoregressive latent growth-curve structural equation models and a Multiple-Children-of-Twins/Siblings extended family design to genetically decompose intergenerational associations between maternal anxiety/depression symptoms (SCL-8) and child temperament (EAS questionnaire) in 42,526 offspring of 34,060 mothers.

## Main limitation
All phenotypes were reported by the same informant (mothers), creating the potential for shared-rater bias that may inflate intergenerational correlations between maternal emotional symptoms and child temperament ratings, even though the extended family design partially mitigated this by using avuncular comparisons.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support the argument that parental transmission of anxiety risk to children involves both genetic and environmental pathways — specifically that roughly half of the stable mother-child similarity in emotional reactivity reflects shared genetic vulnerability (neurobiological transmission) while the other half reflects environmentally mediated social influence, implying that family-level intervention can address the non-genetic portion of intergenerational risk.
