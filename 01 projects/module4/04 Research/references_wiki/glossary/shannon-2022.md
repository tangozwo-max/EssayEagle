# Shannon et al. (2022) Problematic Social Media Use in Adolescents and Young Adults. *JMIR Mental Health*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Shannon, H., Bush, K., Villeneuve, P.J., Hellemans, K.G.C. and Guimond, S. (2022) 'Problematic Social Media Use in Adolescents and Young Adults: Systematic Review and Meta-analysis', JMIR Mental Health, 9(4), e33450. DOI: 10.2196/33450

**Evidence tier:** Meta-analysis / SR — Systematic review and meta-analysis (k=18 studies, N=9,269 participants)
**Validity estimate:** 68/100
**DOI:** 10.2196/33450
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Problematic social media use — defined by addiction-like symptoms rather than mere hours of use — shows moderate but statistically significant positive correlations with depression, anxiety, and stress in adolescents and young adults aged 12–30.
2. The strongest association found was between problematic social media use and anxiety symptoms (r=0.348), exceeding both the depression (r=0.273) and stress (r=0.313) correlations, all significant at p<.001.
3. Problematic social media use shows consistently larger effect sizes than simple measures of time spent on social media, which typically yield correlations below r=0.20 in prior meta-analyses, suggesting that the quality/pattern of use matters more than quantity.
4. Age, gender, and year of publication did not significantly moderate any of the three relationships, indicating the association between problematic social media use and poor mental health is broadly consistent across the adolescent-to-young-adult age range and across genders.
5. Adolescents and young adults are a particularly vulnerable population because increased social media use coincides with a critical period of identity formation and ongoing brain development in circuits governing emotion regulation and motivation.
6. The direction of causality cannot be established from the available cross-sectional evidence; bidirectional effects between poor mental health and problematic use are plausible, and longitudinal studies are needed.

## Numerical findings
- Summary correlation with depression: r=0.273 (95% CI 0.215–0.332), p<.001, I²=83.2%
- Summary correlation with anxiety: r=0.348 (95% CI 0.270–0.426), p<.001, I²=91.6%
- Summary correlation with stress: r=0.313 (95% CI 0.203–0.423), p<.001, I²=92.6%
- Total sample: 18 studies, N=9,269 participants
- Age range across included studies: 12–30 years
- Age as moderator: p=.75 (not significant)
- Gender as moderator: p=.25 (not significant)
- Year of publication as moderator: p=.09 (not significant)
- Egger's test for depression: p=.35 (not significant); anxiety: p=.30; stress: p=.79
- Initial database search yielded 2,846 articles; 18 included after PRISMA screening

## Study design
Pre-registered (PROSPERO CRD42021222309) systematic review and random-effects meta-analysis of 18 cross-sectional studies (N=9,269 adolescents and young adults aged 12–30) examining associations between validated measures of problematic social media use and depression, anxiety, and stress symptoms.

## Main limitation
All included studies are cross-sectional, so causal direction cannot be inferred; the authors explicitly acknowledge that bidirectional effects between poor mental health and problematic social media use are plausible, and the lack of clinical diagnostic data in primary studies limits interpretation.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support the argument that social media functions as a systemic environmental risk factor for adolescent anxiety and depression, specifically to distinguish problematic (addiction-like) use patterns from simple screen-time metrics and to cite pooled effect sizes demonstrating a moderate, consistent association across age, gender, and cultural contexts.
