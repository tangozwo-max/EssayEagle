# Fassi et al. (2024) Social media use and internalizing symptoms in clinical and community adolescents. *JAMA Pediatrics*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Fassi, L., Thomas, K., Parry, D.A., Leyland-Craggs, A., Ford, T.J. and Orben, A. (2024) 'Social media use and internalizing symptoms in clinical and community adolescent samples: a systematic review and meta-analysis', JAMA Pediatrics, 178(8), pp. 814–822. DOI: 10.1001/jamapediatrics.2024.2078

**Evidence tier:** Meta-analysis / SR — Systematic review and meta-analysis (k=143 studies, N=1,094,890 adolescents, 886 effect sizes)
**Validity estimate:** 78/100
**DOI:** 10.1001/jamapediatrics.2024.2078
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Social media use is positively and significantly associated with internalizing symptoms (anxiety, depression) in adolescents, including in clinical populations, though effect sizes are small.
2. Only 11% of effect sizes in the existing literature examine clinical populations; 88% come from community samples, representing a critical evidence gap.
3. In clinical samples, time spent on social media shows a small but significant positive meta-correlation with internalizing symptoms (r=0.08), and social media engagement shows a slightly stronger association (r=0.12).
4. Contrary to expectation, the strength of the social media–internalizing symptom association does not significantly differ between clinical and community samples; sample type was not a significant moderator.
5. Neither type of mental health measure (anxiety vs. depression vs. internalizing), COVID-19 timing, age, nor sex explained heterogeneity in the associations, suggesting the effect is broadly consistent across subgroups.
6. The lack of research on clinical adolescent populations limits the ability to draw clinically informative conclusions and to design targeted interventions or regulations for the most vulnerable young people.

## Numerical findings
- 143 studies included; combined N=1,094,890 adolescents; 886 effect sizes
- Only 11% of effect sizes (99 effect sizes from 12 studies) examined clinical samples; 88% (774 effect sizes from 133 studies) examined community samples
- Clinical samples, time spent meta-correlation: r=0.08 (95% CI, 0.01–0.15; P=.03; I²=57.83)
- Clinical samples, social media engagement meta-correlation: r=0.12 (95% CI, 0.09–0.15; P=.002; I²=82.67)
- Community samples, time spent meta-correlation: r=0.12 (95% CI, 0.09–0.15; P<.001; I²=98.23)
- Community samples, social media engagement meta-correlation: r=0.14 (95% CI, 0.10–0.18; P<.001; I²=94.85)
- Sample type as moderator for time spent: β=0.05, SE=0.03, P=.145 (non-significant)
- Sample type as moderator for engagement: β=0.01, SE=0.02, P=.52 (non-significant)
- 55% of included studies rated acceptable quality; 45% rated questionable quality on the Quality of Survey Studies in Psychology Checklist
- UK adolescents with probable mental health condition increased from 10% to 25% between 2017 and 2022 (cited background statistic)
- 97% of young people are daily social media users (cited background statistic)

## Study design
Pre-registered systematic review (PROSPERO CRD42022321473) and meta-analysis of 143 cross-sectional and longitudinal observational studies (N=1,094,890 adolescents aged 10–24 years), using random-effects models with robust variance estimation (RVE) to pool correlations between social media use and internalizing symptoms (anxiety, depression, or combined) across clinical and community samples published 2007–2023.

## Main limitation
The meta-analysis pooled only correlational (cross-sectional) data, so no causal inferences can be drawn about whether social media use increases symptoms or vice versa; reliance on inaccurate self-reported social media use measures may also have obscured real differences between clinical and community samples.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use this source to support the claim that social media use is a systemic environmental risk factor for adolescent anxiety and depression, and to contextualise the evidence base — specifically that even clinically diagnosed adolescents show the same small but significant positive association between social media use and internalizing symptoms as community samples, justifying attention to social media as a contextual factor in stepped-care and clinical treatment planning.
