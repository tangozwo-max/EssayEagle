# Sigurvinsdóttir (2018) Effectiveness of CBT for child and adolescent anxiety disorders. *University of Iceland MS Thesis*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Sigurvinsdóttir, A.L. (2018) Effectiveness of cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) for child and adolescent anxiety disorders across different CBT modalities and comparisons: A systematic review and meta-analysis. MS thesis, University of Iceland, Reykjavík. [to verify: no DOI assigned to thesis]

**Evidence tier:** Meta-analysis / SR — Systematic review and meta-analysis (k=74 RCTs, n=5,439 participants)
**Validity estimate:** 68/100
**DOI:** not found
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. CBT is an effective treatment for child and adolescent anxiety disorders across all modalities (individual, group, family, remote) compared to wait-list control, with large odds ratios consistently favouring CBT.
2. Individual-based CBT is significantly superior to both wait-list control and attention control (active placebo), meaning its effects cannot be explained by expectancy alone; children receiving individual CBT were approximately 2.6 times more likely to be free from anxiety than those in attention control.
3. Family-based CBT shows particularly large effects versus wait-list (OR = 26.21), and also outperforms attention control, suggesting that involving parents adds meaningful therapeutic value beyond non-specific factors.
4. Remote-delivered CBT (internet/computer-based) is as effective as face-to-face individual CBT in head-to-head comparisons, and is superior to both wait-list and attention control, supporting its potential as a scalable low-barrier alternative.
5. Individual-based CBT is equivalent in efficacy to SSRIs alone, but combined CBT plus SSRI treatment is significantly more effective than CBT alone (OR = 0.39 favouring combination), indicating pharmacotherapy adds incremental benefit over psychotherapy.
6. No CBT delivery modality (individual, group, family, remote) was found to be significantly superior to any other in head-to-head comparisons, suggesting clinicians can match format to patient and family circumstances without sacrificing efficacy.

## Numerical findings
- 74 RCTs included; 3,132 CBT participants and 2,307 control participants (5,439 total)
- Individual CBT vs. wait-list: OR = 9.53 [95% CI 5.48–16.58]
- Group CBT vs. wait-list: OR = 9.86 [95% CI 3.97–24.48]
- Family CBT vs. wait-list: OR = 26.21 [95% CI 6.62–103.79]
- Remote CBT vs. wait-list: OR = 6.14 [95% CI 2.97–12.71]
- Individual CBT vs. attention control: OR = 2.55 [95% CI 1.35–4.93]
- Individual CBT vs. TAU: OR = 3.70 [95% CI 0.84–16.40], non-significant
- CBT + SSRI combination vs. individual CBT alone: OR = 0.39 [95% CI 0.24–0.64], favouring combination
- Individual CBT vs. SSRIs: OR = 1.01 [95% CI 1.11–2.19], non-significant (single study)
- Individual CBT vs. group CBT (head-to-head): OR = 2.73 [95% CI 0.98–7.61], non-significant
- Individual CBT vs. family CBT (head-to-head): OR = 0.75 [95% CI 0.44–1.26], non-significant
- Individual CBT vs. remote CBT (head-to-head): OR = 0.91 [95% CI 0.47–1.74], non-significant
- Continuous measure, individual CBT vs. WL: SMD = 0.98 [95% CI 0.50–1.47]
- Continuous measure, group CBT vs. WL: SMD = 0.74 [95% CI 0.41–1.08]
- Continuous measure, family CBT vs. WL: SMD = 0.56 [95% CI 0.27–0.86]
- Continuous measure, remote CBT vs. WL: SMD = 1.63 [95% CI 0.60–2.65]
- Mean age of participants across studies: 10.8 (SD 1.6) years; age range 3–18 years
- 64.9% of included studies rated low risk of bias for random sequence generation; performance bias high in all trials due to lack of blinding

## Study design
Systematic review and meta-analysis of 74 randomised controlled trials (RCTs) of manualized CBT for anxiety disorders in children and adolescents aged 18 years or younger, including 3,132 CBT participants and 2,307 control participants, comparing individual, group, family, and remote CBT modalities against wait-list, treatment as usual, attention control, SSRIs, and combined CBT plus SSRIs.

## Main limitation
The comparative efficacy of CBT versus SSRIs and versus combination therapy rests on only a single included study (Walkup et al., 2008), making those effect size estimates unreliable and precluding heterogeneity calculation; additionally, performance bias was rated high across all trials due to the impossibility of blinding participants and therapists in psychotherapy research.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Cite to support the argument that CBT is a robustly evidenced first-line intervention for child and adolescent anxiety across multiple delivery formats, and to demonstrate that family-based CBT in particular shows strong effects (suggesting parental involvement is a meaningful therapeutic mechanism), while combination CBT plus medication outperforms CBT alone — directly relevant to discussions of treatment efficacy, stepped care, and the role of family factors in intervention outcomes.
