# Rienks et al. (2025) Supporting parents to reduce children's anxiety: A meta-analysis. *Behaviour Research and Therapy*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Rienks, K., Salemink, E., Sigurðardóttir, L.B.L., Melendez-Torres, G.J., Staaks, J.P.C. and Leijten, P. (2025) 'Supporting parents to reduce children's anxiety: A meta-analysis of interventions and their theoretical components', Behaviour Research and Therapy, 185, 104692. DOI: 10.1016/j.brat.2025.104692

**Evidence tier:** Meta-analysis / SR — Meta-analysis of RCTs (k=26 trials, N=4,098), PRISMA-compliant, preregistered on PROSPERO
**Validity estimate:** 72/100
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.brat.2025.104692
**PDF status:** full-text

## Key usable claims
1. Parent-focused interventions — where therapists work only with parents, without any direct therapist-child contact — produce a significant medium effect on children's anxiety symptoms, indicating they are a meaningful treatment pathway in their own right.
2. Behavioral components (exposure, modeling, contingency management/positive reinforcement) were present in every cluster of intervention components that produced statistically significant reductions in child anxiety, suggesting they are the most critical active ingredient in parent-focused programs.
3. Combining multiple theoretically distinct components (e.g., behavioral plus cognitive plus emotional) tends to yield better effects than using any single component alone, with the exception that behavioral components alone were already effective.
4. Well-intentioned parental behaviors such as family accommodation, overprotection, and modeling of anxious behavior can inadvertently maintain and worsen child anxiety, providing the theoretical rationale for targeting parents as the primary change agent.
5. Parent-focused interventions are argued to be more accessible, cost-effective, and capable of facilitating generalization of treatment gains compared to child-only or combined interventions, partly because they can reduce waitlists and be delivered remotely.
6. The certainty of evidence across all included trials was rated as very low by GRADE criteria, due to high risk of bias, substantial heterogeneity (I² up to 95%), imprecision, and suspected publication bias, meaning conclusions must be treated as preliminary.

## Numerical findings
- Overall effect across prevention and treatment settings: d = −0.59 (95% CI [−0.92, −0.26]), I² = 91%, k = 154 effect sizes, N = 25 trials
- Effect in treatment settings: d = −0.66 (95% CI [−1.18, −0.14]), I² = 90%, n = 11 trials
- Effect at indicated prevention level: d = −1.13 (95% CI [−2.67, 0.42]), I² = 93%, n = 6 trials (not significant)
- Effect at selective prevention level: d = −0.26 (95% CI [−0.57, 0.06]), I² = 65%, n = 4 trials (not significant)
- Effect at universal prevention level: d = −0.12 (95% CI [−0.58, 0.34]), I² = 95%, n = 4 trials (not significant)
- Sensitivity analysis excluding 2 outlier studies (dark phobia): d = −0.42 (95% CI [−0.64, −0.20])
- NMA I² = 82%; strongest cluster COG+BEH+EMO+ACC: d = −1.38 (95% CI [−2.37, −0.38]); BEH alone: d = −1.10 (95% CI [−1.64, −0.56])
- Total N = 4,098 parents across 26 trials; mean child age = 6.84 years; 49% girls
- Mean intervention duration = 8.19 hours (range 1–20 hours)
- Interrater reliability for theoretical component coding: κ = 1.00; for data extraction: r = 0.85

## Study design
PRISMA-compliant systematic review and meta-analysis of 26 RCTs (k=157 effect sizes, N=4,098 parents) of parent-only interventions targeting child anxiety (mean child age 6.84 years, up to age 16), using robust variance estimation for main and component effects and network meta-analysis for component clusters; preregistered on PROSPERO (CRD42022362983).

## Main limitation
The overall certainty of evidence was rated as very low by GRADE criteria due to high risk of bias in most trials, large heterogeneity, imprecision from wide confidence intervals, and suspected publication bias, meaning all conclusions about effective components must be regarded as preliminary and hypothesis-generating rather than definitive.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use to support arguments about the role of parental behavior (family accommodation, overprotection, anxious modeling) in maintaining child anxiety, and to evidence that parent-focused behavioral interventions (exposure-based, contingency management) are an effective and accessible treatment mechanism — particularly relevant to discussions of CBT components, stepped-care accessibility, and parental transmission of anxiety.
