# Storch et al. (2015) Phenomenology and clinical correlates of family accommodation in pediatric anxiety. *Journal of Anxiety Disorders*.

## Key usable claims
1. Family accommodation is virtually universal in pediatric anxiety disorders: some form of accommodation was present in all 112 families in the sample.
2. The most common forms of accommodation were providing reassurance (100% of families) and facilitating avoidance of anxiety triggers (85.6% of families).
3. Family accommodation is associated with greater anxiety severity, elevated externalizing behaviours, a diagnosis of separation anxiety disorder, and greater functional impairment.
4. Family accommodation partially mediated the relationship between anxiety severity and functional impairment, and between externalising behaviours and functional impairment — suggesting it is a mechanism through which symptoms translate into disability.
5. By negatively reinforcing avoidance, accommodation prevents children from having corrective learning experiences with feared stimuli, thereby maintaining or escalating anxiety symptoms over time.
6. The findings highlight the clinical necessity of directly targeting parental accommodation practices within treatment, rather than treating child anxiety in isolation from family-systemic factors.

## Numerical findings
- N=112 anxious youth aged 7–13 years (M=9.87, SD=1.85)
- Reassurance-giving present in 100% of families
- Facilitating avoidance present in 85.6% of families
- Changes in family routine and family/child distress without accommodation present in more than 75% of families
- Accommodation occurs in approximately 95–97% of families with an anxious child (citing Benito et al., 2015; Lebowitz et al., 2013; Thompson-Hollands et al., 2014)
- Up to 97% of adults with OCD report family accommodation (cited background literature)
- Cited by 69 articles (Scopus)

## Study design
Cross-sectional study of 112 treatment-seeking youth aged 7–13 years with clinically significant anxiety disorders (identified via the Anxiety Disorders Interview Schedule), and their parents, who completed measures of anxiety severity, family accommodation, functional impairment, and internalising/externalising symptoms at screening for a randomised controlled trial of computer-assisted CBT.

## Main limitation
The cross-sectional design prevents causal inference about the direction of the relationships between family accommodation, anxiety severity, and functional impairment; the authors explicitly note that longitudinal data are needed to validate the proposed mechanistic models.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Best used to support the argument that family accommodation is a near-universal systemic factor in pediatric anxiety that independently contributes to functional impairment and must be targeted explicitly in CBT-based interventions.
