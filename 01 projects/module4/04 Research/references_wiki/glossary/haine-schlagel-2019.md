# Haine-Schlagel et al. (2019) Parent mental health problems and motivation as predictors of their engagement in community-based child mental health services. *Children and Youth Services Review*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Haine-Schlagel, R., Dickson, K.S., Shapiro, A.F., May, G.C. and Cheng, P. (2019) 'Parent mental health problems and motivation as predictors of their engagement in community-based child mental health services', *Children and Youth Services Review*, 104, 104370. doi: 10.1016/j.childyouth.2019.06.005

## Key usable claims
1. Parent **internalizing symptoms (anxiety/depression) are the most common parental mental-health problems** among parents of children receiving mental-health treatment.
2. Higher parental internalizing symptoms predict **lower therapist-reported engagement** in the child's treatment (attendance and active participation).
3. Parent motivation is associated with fewer perceived barriers to participation, which in turn supports engagement — so the parent's own mental state shapes whether the child's treatment proceeds.

## Numerical findings
- US community-based child mental-health services sample (confirm exact N from PDF).

## Study design
Observational study using parent- and therapist-reported measures to predict parent engagement (attendance, active participation) in community-based child mental-health treatment.

## Main limitation
Observational (associational, not causal); US community-services sample; engagement partly therapist-rated; not anxiety-specific (general child MH services).

"Structural limitations / closing the gap" — the **direct evidence for the access half of the parental "double disadvantage"**: a parent's own mental-health difficulties predict lower engagement in the child's treatment, so the parent-mediated route reaches the highest-risk children least well.
