# Boers, Afzali and Conrod (2020) Temporal associations of screen time and anxiety symptoms among adolescents. *The Canadian Journal of Psychiatry*.

**Citation (Harvard-WMS):**
Boers, E., Afzali, M.H. and Conrod, P. (2020) 'Temporal Associations of Screen Time and Anxiety Symptoms Among Adolescents', *The Canadian Journal of Psychiatry*, 65(3), pp. 206–208. doi: 10.1177/0706743719885486

**Evidence tier:** Longitudinal cohort (N=3,826; 4-year annual assessment; multilevel modelling)
**Validity estimate:** 72/100
**DOI:** 10.1177/0706743719885486
**PDF status:** full-pdf

## Key usable claims
1. Social media use and computer use are associated with anxiety symptoms in adolescents across both between-person (stable heavy users vs. lighter users) and within-person (when a teen uses more than their personal average) dimensions — both associations are significant and independent.
2. Critically, lagged effects — where prior-year screen time predicts future anxiety — were NOT significant for social media or computer use, suggesting the association may be bidirectional or contemporaneous rather than screen time straightforwardly causing subsequent anxiety.
3. Video gaming showed NO significant associations with anxiety at any level of analysis (between-person, within-person, or lagged), distinguishing it from social media and passive screen exposure.
4. Television viewing showed a negative between-person association with anxiety (heavy TV users report less anxiety) but a positive within-person association (when an individual watches more than usual, anxiety is slightly elevated) — consistent with passive viewing providing relative stress relief as a stable pattern, but being used reactively under stress.
5. Gender and SES did not significantly affect the screen time–anxiety associations, suggesting the effect is broadly applicable across adolescent subgroups.
6. The findings challenge simple causal narratives: the strength of within-person associations suggests screen use may increase alongside anxiety concurrently, rather than screen use linearly causing anxiety accumulation.

## Numerical findings
- N = 3,826 seventh graders (47% female, mean age 12.8 years, SD=0.4); 3,659 with complete data
- 4-year longitudinal study: annual assessments from 7th to 10th grade
- Data source: Co-Venture Trial (personality-targeted prevention; Sainte-Justine Hospital, Montreal)
- Multilevel linear modelling (random intercepts and slopes, individual and school level)
- Social media — between-person: B=0.43 (SE=0.05, p<.001); within-person: B=0.21 (SE=0.03, p<.001); lagged: B=0.06 (p=.052, ns)
- Computer use — between-person: B=0.42 (SE=0.07, p<.001); within-person: B=0.11 (SE=0.03, p=.002); lagged: B=0.07 (p=.406, ns)
- Television — between-person: B=–0.12 (p=.041); within-person: B=0.15 (p=.001)
- Video gaming: no significant associations at any level
- Anxiety measured: Brief Symptom Inventory Anxiety subscale (7 items, 0–4 scale, past 12 months)
- SES and gender: did not significantly affect associations

## Study design
Secondary longitudinal analysis within the Co-Venture randomised trial; annual surveys (grades 7–10); multilevel linear modelling tested between-person, within-person, and lagged effects of four screen types (social media, video gaming, television, other computer use) on anxiety; trial exposure included as covariate; Institutional Review Board approved.

## Main limitation
Research Letter format — abbreviated reporting; screen time measured by self-report, subject to social desirability bias; single-item per screen category; lagged effects only one-year prospective (longer lags untested); anxiety measure is general symptom scale not diagnostic; sample drawn from a drug/alcohol prevention trial, limiting generalisability; no measure of screen content quality or purpose.

## Cite for (essay relevance)
Use to support a nuanced argument that social media use is associated with adolescent anxiety — particularly as a concurrent and within-person phenomenon — while cautioning against overly simplistic causal claims. Particularly valuable for noting that lagged (prospective) effects are non-significant, which challenges the narrative that screen time simply accumulates to cause anxiety over time, and for distinguishing social media from video gaming (no effect) and television (more complex pattern).
