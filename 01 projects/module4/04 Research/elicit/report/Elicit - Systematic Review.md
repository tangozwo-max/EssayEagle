# Systemic Maintenance of Anxiety in Children and Adolescents and the Added Value of Family and Environmental Interventions

## Abstract

Pediatric anxiety is frequently conceptualised as embedded within family processes and broader environments, rather than residing solely within the individual child, and family accommodation has been explicitly framed as important for the course, treatment, and prognosis of pediatric anxiety[^1]. This review evaluated whether evidence from (i) family-system processes (accommodation, emotion socialisation, parent anxiety transmission, family conflict/attachment) and (ii) digital environments (social media/screen time, including neurodevelopmental context) supports a systemic/ecological framing, and whether interventions that engage parents/families and the wider environment improve outcomes relative to child-only treatment.

We synthesised findings from meta-analyses and systematic reviews of family factors and digital exposures[^1][^2][^3][^4][^5][^6][^7][^8], longitudinal cohorts examining intergenerational and family-process pathways[^9][^10][^11][^12], and randomised trials of parent-led, parent-only, and family-inclusive interventions[^13][^14][^15]. Across the family domain, family accommodation showed a moderate association with anxiety severity (overall $$r=0.47$$), although cross-informant associations were weaker and sometimes non-significant[^1]. Parental anxiety predicted child anxiety across repeated assessments, and some evidence suggested bidirectionality (child anxiety influencing later parental anxiety)[^9]. In genetically informed analyses, most of the observed mother–child association in offspring anxiety symptoms (mother–child $$r=0.16$$) was attributed to “potential maternal effects” after accounting for assortative mating and genetic transmission, supporting environmentally mediated transmission pathways[^10]. Digital exposures showed consistent cross-sectional associations with anxiety (e.g., problematic social media use $$r=0.348$$), but longitudinal evidence for anxiety outcomes was described as lacking, and a cohort study found no adjusted 12‑month prediction of anxiety by screen time[^2][^16][^5]. For interventions, parent-focused programs showed pooled anxiety reductions (meta-analytic $$d=-0.59$$) albeit with very low certainty, and parent-led/digitally augmented approaches were non-inferior to usual care while reducing therapist time, suggesting feasible systemic/stepped-care delivery models[^6][^13]. Overall, the evidence supports systemic framing most strongly for family processes and for the efficacy of parent/family-engaged interventions versus no-treatment controls, while superiority over active comparators and causal claims for digital drivers remain mixed[^7][^8][^3][^5].

## Rationale

A systemic/ecological framing of pediatric anxiety posits that anxiety symptoms are shaped and potentially maintained by interactions among child vulnerabilities and recurring interpersonal and environmental contingencies, including parent responses and broader contexts that can reinforce avoidance. Family accommodation has been explicitly presented as important for the course, treatment, and prognosis of anxiety in pediatric populations, motivating quantitative synthesis of its association with symptom severity[^1]. This framing is strengthened when family processes prospectively predict persistence of child anxiety, when parent symptom trajectories and family conflict patterns help explain sustained parent anxiety, and when intergenerational similarity in anxiety is attributed largely to environmental pathways beyond genetic transmission[^12][^10].

A complementary ecological strand concerns adolescents’ immersion in digital environments. Evidence syntheses have operationalised this in terms of problematic social media use, time spent, and engagement measures, and have quantified associations with anxiety and broader internalising symptoms[^2][^3]. Importantly, this line of work has also highlighted a neurodevelopmental context: social media use is occurring during a period when neural circuitry relevant to emotion regulation and motivation is still developing, a point used to contextualise why exposure might plausibly interact with developmental vulnerability[^2]. However, observational designs and cross-sectional measurement dominate many digital studies, and authors frequently emphasise that causal directions are uncertain and potentially bidirectional (poor mental health driving more problematic use, and/or use exacerbating symptoms)[^2][^3][^17].

This review therefore focuses on two linked questions: whether the empirical record supports systemic maintenance/amplification pathways for pediatric anxiety (family processes and digital environments), and whether treatments that change the family system or parents’ behaviours can improve child outcomes at least as well as child-only approaches and potentially with greater scalability[^1][^6][^13].

## Methods

This review used a structured synthesis of the provided evidence base consisting of meta-analyses/systematic reviews, longitudinal cohorts, and randomised trials spanning the family, digital, and intervention domains[^1][^9][^10][^11][^12][^2][^3][^4][^5][^6][^7][^8][^13][^14][^15]. Study designs were treated as providing graded evidential strength, prioritising meta-analyses and systematic reviews over individual observational studies for estimating typical associations, and prioritising RCTs and longitudinal modelling over cross-sectional designs for inference about change and directionality[^1][^3][^5][^13][^14].

Data were extracted as reported in the supplied records, including sample sizes and pooled or trial effect sizes when available (e.g., correlations $$r$$, standardised mean differences, Cohen’s $$d$$, odds ratios), and limitations were interpreted as described by the original authors (e.g., lack of longitudinal data, shared-rater bias, unblinded trials, correlational evidence)[^1][^10][^11][^2][^3][^13][^15]. The synthesis was organised into four domains aligned to the research question: family accommodation and related family processes, parental anxiety transmission, digital environment exposures with neurodevelopmental context, and family-inclusive/parent-focused interventions[^1][^9][^2][^6].

## Results

### Family processes

Evidence from meta-analysis indicates that family accommodation is positively associated with pediatric anxiety severity. Across 22 studies with 2,353 children/adolescents, the global correlation between family accommodation and anxiety severity was $$r=0.47$$ (95% CI 0.39–0.54), supporting a moderate association at the level of pooled estimates[^1]. However, informant and measurement factors moderated the strength of observed associations, because correlations based on child-administered measures were smaller (6 studies; $$r=0.27$$, 95% CI 0.18–0.35), and cross-informant pairings could be weak and non-significant (4 studies pairing parent-rated accommodation with child-rated anxiety; $$r=0.10$$, 95% CI −0.02–0.23; $$p=0.11$$)[^18][^1]. This pattern supports systemic relevance of accommodation while also highlighting that rater concordance and method variance can shape estimates of the accommodation–severity linkage[^1].

The ability to interpret accommodation as a maintaining factor (rather than simply a response to child severity) is constrained by the lack of longitudinal studies in the accommodation meta-analysis, which was explicitly identified as limiting causal inference about links between accommodation and anxiety severity over time[^1]. In the broader family-process literature, longitudinal cohort evidence indicates that specific family relational processes can predict which children maintain anxiety into adolescence, providing prospective support for systemic maintenance pathways. In NICHD SECCYD data (N=661), lower attachment security to fathers and restriction of maternal autonomy granting were reported to predict which children maintained anxiety into adolescence, and partner conflict was reported to explain the association between earlier and later parental anxiety[^12]. These findings were interpreted as indicating long-term roles of both fathers and mothers in child anxiety, aligning with a systemic view that family relationships can shape persistence rather than only co-occur with symptoms[^12].

A related family-system pathway concerns parents’ emotion socialisation practices. In a synthesis incorporating three meta-analyses, supportive emotion socialisation behaviours were very weakly negatively associated with child internalising problems (supportive ES $$r=-0.06$$), nonsupportive ES behaviours showed a medium positive association (nonsupportive ES $$r=0.18$$), and elaboration showed a small negative association (elaboration $$r=-0.11$$), across birth–18 years[^19]. The authors explicitly suggested that incorporating an emotion socialisation framework could be beneficial in intervention and prevention efforts for children at risk of internalising problems, which conceptually includes anxiety outcomes and supports systemic targets beyond child-only cognition/behaviour change[^19].

### Parental anxiety transmission

Longitudinal modelling supports an intergenerational association between parental anxiety and child anxiety that is consistent with systemic transmission mechanisms. In a four-wave longitudinal design using a random intercept cross-lagged panel model (RI-CLPM) to mitigate confounding, data collected across 2019–2022 in Sichuan Province (N=6,117 students) showed a strong between-person correlation between parental and child anxiety “traits” (random intercepts; $$r=0.51$$, $$p<0.001$$)[^9]. The same study reported that parental anxiety predicted child anxiety across all time points, supporting a “parent effects” model, while child anxiety also influenced parental anxiety from Time 3 to Time 4, partially supporting reciprocal effects[^9]. The parental-to-child pathway was reported to be stronger for girls than boys and stronger for children than adolescents, suggesting heterogeneity in transmission across gender and developmental stage[^9]. The study noted reliance on self-report questionnaires as a limitation due to potential social desirability and recall bias, which is relevant when interpreting the magnitude and specificity of observed cross-lagged effects[^9].

Genetically informed cohort evidence further supports the plausibility of environmental/systemic transmission beyond genetic inheritance. Using the Norwegian Mother, Father and Child Cohort Study (MoBa), a prospective population-based pregnancy cohort, investigators analysed data involving parental-generation sibling pairs and partners, with offspring N=26,091 children[^10]. After accounting for assortative mating (phenotypic $$r=0.26$$) and genetic transmission (explaining 0%–18% of mother–offspring correlations), “potential maternal effects” were estimated to explain 98% (95% CI 45–112) of the association with offspring anxiety symptoms (mother–child $$r=0.16$$), supporting environmentally mediated pathways in intergenerational association estimates for child anxiety symptoms[^10]. Limitations noted included reliance on maternal reports for outcome measures and low internal consistency of the SCARED measure, with the authors explicitly noting that increased measurement error could partly explain differences in model fit for anxiety compared with neuroticism and depression, underscoring the importance of measurement quality in intergenerational inference[^10].

Large-scale longitudinal work also suggests that intergenerational associations may be small and may operate more through stability in traits than through correlated change. In a cohort where mothers (n=34,060) reported symptoms and offspring temperament (n=42,526) at ages 1.5, 3, and 5 years, longitudinal stability in mothers’ emotional symptoms was associated with stability in offspring emotionality ($$r=0.143$$), shyness ($$r=0.031$$), and sociability ($$r=-0.015$$), while correlations involving linear change (slope–slope) were reported as very small (all $$r<0.005$$) with minimal evidence for co-development of mother symptoms and child temperament change[^11]. The authors additionally noted that longitudinal change correlations were negligible and raised concerns that shared-rater bias could inflate intergenerational correlations because all phenotypes were mother-reported, which qualifies interpretation of systemic transmission mechanisms in this dataset[^11].

### Digital environments and neurodevelopmental context

Meta-analytic evidence suggests that youth digital experiences—particularly problematic or dysregulated forms of use—are associated with anxiety symptoms, although causal and temporal directionality remain uncertain. A systematic review/meta-analysis of problematic social media use (18 studies; N=9,269) reported a summary metaregression correlation between problematic social media use and anxiety symptoms of $$r=0.348$$ (95% CI 0.270–0.426; $$p<0.001$$), and concluded that there is evidence for a significant relationship between problematic use and higher anxiety symptoms[^2]. The authors contextualised these findings by noting that social media use occurs while critical brain circuits involved in emotion regulation and motivation are continuing to undergo development, providing a neurodevelopmental vulnerability frame that could plausibly interact with environmental exposures[^2]. At the same time, the same review emphasised that the evidence is cross-sectional and correlational, precluding causal inference; it explicitly raised the possibility of bidirectional effects between poor mental health and social media use and identified reliance on self-report measures as a limitation[^2].

When social media is operationalised more broadly as time spent or engagement (rather than problematic use), associations with internalising symptoms appear smaller in clinical adolescent samples. In a pre-registered systematic review/meta-analysis of 143 studies including 1,094,890 adolescents (with 11% clinical samples), meta-correlations in clinical samples were reported as $$r=0.08$$ for time spent and $$r=0.12$$ for engagement with internalising symptoms, and these meta-correlations did not substantially differ from those in community samples[^3]. The authors noted that the meta-analysis captured relationships using correlations and therefore could not support causal inferences about whether increased social media use leads to internalising symptoms or vice versa[^3]. The small magnitude of these correlations and their similarity across clinical and community samples suggest that, at least as typically measured, social media exposure may be a weak correlate of internalising severity within clinical populations, or that measurement approaches dilute stronger effects limited to specific subgroups or usage patterns[^3].

For social anxiety specifically, a random-effects meta-analysis (27 studies; N=38,163) reported a pooled association between social media use and social anxiety of $$r=0.14$$ (95% CI 0.06–0.22; $$p=0.001$$), indicating a small but statistically significant relationship across studies[^17]. However, the same synthesis noted that the relationship varies in direction and magnitude, and that most included studies were cross-sectional (25/27), limiting inference about the direction of effects[^17].

Screen time during acute contextual disruptions (COVID-19) was also associated with anxiety in pooled analyses. A systematic review/meta-analysis of studies during 2020–2023 (23 studies; N=29,581) reported a pooled correlation between screen time and anxiety of $$r=0.157$$ (95% CI 0.0994–0.214; $$p<0.001$$) and concluded that higher screen time was associated with increased anxiety during the pandemic context[^4]. Meta-regression indicated that screen time measured as problematic use of devices had a 0.15 higher correlation with anxiety compared to duration-based measures, reinforcing the theme that “how” devices are used may matter more than duration alone[^4]. The authors recommended ongoing parent, professional, and self-monitoring of youth screen behaviours, which positions parents and systems as relevant actors in mitigating potential harms during emergencies[^4].

Longitudinal evidence for screen time as a maintaining factor for anxiety is less consistent. In the Future Proofing Study cohort (N=4,058 adolescents; mean age 13.9), each additional hour of screen time was associated cross-sectionally with a $$0.79$$ increase in CAS-8 anxiety score (95% CI 0.68–0.90; $$p<0.001$$), but screen time did not significantly predict subsequent anxiety at 12 months after controlling for baseline anxiety (reported $$p=0.443$$), and the authors concluded that none of the models showed an association between screen time or maladaptive social media use and subsequent anxiety after 12 months, with longitudinal associations described as markedly weaker[^16]. Measurement limitations were emphasised, including the use of a bespoke unvalidated measure and a single self-report screen time question, with noted concerns about retrospective bias and failure to capture heterogeneity of screen activities[^16]. Consistent with these findings, a systematic review of longitudinal studies (35 studies) concluded that evidence supporting longitudinal associations between screen time and anxiety (and other internalising symptoms beyond depression) was lacking, and called for studies examining screen content and motivations to better discern relationships with internalising mental health symptoms[^5].

### Family-inclusive and parent-focused interventions

Across intervention studies, parent-focused and family-inclusive approaches generally show benefits compared with no-treatment controls, while comparisons against active comparators more often indicate equivalence rather than clear superiority. A component/network meta-analytic approach to parent-focused interventions identified 26 studies (N=4,098) and reported a significant medium effect on children’s anxiety (pooled $$d=-0.59$$; 95% CI −0.92 to −0.26), with the additional finding that all effective component clusters contained a behavioural component[^6]. The authors interpreted this as highlighting the potential of parent-focused interventions and the importance of behavioural elements, but explicitly noted that the analysis was limited by “very low certainty of evidence,” which constrains confidence in point estimates and comparative claims across component clusters[^6].

A systematic review/meta-analysis focusing on parent-only interventions in school-aged children (29 eligible studies) concluded that parent-only interventions had a significant treatment effect compared to waitlist controls, but found no significant differences when comparing parent-only interventions with other active interventions; additionally, no significant treatment effects were found for child-rated outcomes[^7]. This pattern suggests that parent-only approaches can be beneficial relative to no treatment but may not outperform other active treatments, and it highlights the possibility that rater perspective (child vs parent) is a key moderator of apparent efficacy[^7]. The authors further reported that methodological quality was a concern, with 19 of 29 studies rated “weak,” limiting the strength of inference regarding comparative efficacy and generalisability[^7].

Family-based CBT has been synthesised against various controls, with evidence of strong effects versus waitlist but mixed results versus attention controls and no evidence that individual CBT is superior to family-based CBT. In a systematic review/meta-analysis including 81 studies (CBT participants n=3,386; controls n=2,527), family-based CBT showed a significant benefit versus waitlist (OR=26.21; 95% CI 6.62–103.79)[^8]. However, for attention control, the estimated effect favoured family-based CBT but was not statistically significant (OR=1.88; 95% CI 0.87–4.09), and individual-based CBT was reported as not superior to family-based CBT[^8]. Limitations included performance bias across trials due to lack of blinding and the fact that only one study compared family-based CBT with attention control, constraining the precision of that comparison[^8].

Pragmatic trials also support the feasibility and non-inferiority of parent-led/digitally augmented CBT models in routine care. In a multisite pragmatic unblinded non-inferiority RCT (N=444 families), an online supportive intervention (OSI) plus therapist support was non-inferior to treatment as usual for parent-reported anxiety interference (CAIS-P) at week 26 (SMD=0.01; 95% CI −0.15 to 0.17; reported $$p<0.00001$$ for the non-inferiority finding) and all secondary outcomes[^13]. The intervention was reported to bring substantial reductions in therapist time without compromising outcomes, and to be associated with lower costs while yielding approximately zero mean difference in QALYs across trial arms, supporting a stepped-care/scalability argument for systemic interventions that leverage parent involvement and digital augmentation[^13]. Trial limitations included inability to blind participants and reliance on participant-reported outcomes with prioritisation of parent report, which could influence perceived change and comparability to clinician-rated outcomes[^13].

Interventions targeting anxious parents for prevention show small but significant improvements in child anxiety compared with no intervention, suggesting a potential mechanism for limiting intergenerational transmission. In an unblinded parallel-group RCT of an unguided modular online intervention for highly anxious parents (N=1,811), child anxiety was lower in the intervention group at 6 months with an adjusted effect size estimate of −0.15 SD (95% CI −0.23 to −0.08; $$p<0.001$$) and Cohen’s $$d=-0.16$$ (95% CI −0.23 to −0.08; $$p<0.001$$)[^14]. The authors reported similar effects in longer-term models (e.g., standardised SCAS scores, internalising, and SCARED-A), supporting maintained effects within the analytic framework, and framed the program as promising for limiting intergenerational transmission of severe anxiety given low resource intensity and positive effects[^14]. Differential retention was substantial (57.3% intervention vs 77.8% control at 6 months), which raises risk of attrition bias and limits confidence that observed effects generalise to all enrolled families[^14].

Prevention via broader parent-focused programs can yield mixed outcomes depending on endpoints and age. In an open-label parallel RCT of a parent-focused prevention program (CPBC) including 215 parents and 277 children, there was no statistically significant difference in the primary outcome (change in clinical severity rating at 12 months; OR=0.67; 95% CI 0.30–1.48), but CPBC children showed larger decreases in anxiety symptoms than controls on secondary outcomes (Cohen’s $$d=0.35$$; 95% CI 0.15–0.55)[^15]. Age-stratified analyses suggested potentially stronger effects for younger children (5–6 years), including lower risk of increased CSR (OR=0.24; 95% CI 0.08–0.77) and lower risk of anxiety diagnosis (OR=0.23; 95% CI 0.05–0.84)[^20]. The authors noted lack of participant blinding as a limitation, which is relevant for interpretation of symptom outcomes and potential expectancy effects in parent-reported measures[^15].

## Discussion

### How strongly does the evidence support systemic maintenance

Across family-focused observational evidence, the accommodation literature provides a quantitatively robust association with pediatric anxiety severity (global $$r=0.47$$), and the framing of accommodation as relevant to course/treatment/prognosis positions it as a plausible maintaining factor and treatment target within systemic models[^1]. Nonetheless, cross-informant and child-reported associations are smaller and sometimes non-significant, which could reflect both measurement limitations and the possibility that accommodation’s most direct effects operate through parent–child interaction patterns that are best captured by specific informants or contexts[^18][^1]. The absence of longitudinal studies in the accommodation meta-analysis is a major evidential gap for causal maintenance claims, because correlations alone cannot establish whether accommodation contributes to persistence or is merely a response to severity[^1].

Parental anxiety transmission evidence is more explicitly longitudinal and therefore more directly relevant to maintenance and directionality questions. RI-CLPM modelling reported that parental anxiety predicted child anxiety across time points, while also finding partial reciprocity (child anxiety predicting later parental anxiety), which aligns with systemic feedback-loop formulations in which parent and child anxiety mutually reinforce each other over time[^9]. The finding that the parental-to-child impact was stronger for girls and for children compared to adolescents suggests that systemic transmission may vary by developmental stage, potentially interacting with children’s dependence on parents and differential socialisation contexts, though such interpretations remain bounded by reliance on self-report data in the cohort[^9].

Genetically informed findings add plausibility to modifiable environmental mechanisms of transmission by attributing most of the observed mother–child association in anxiety symptoms to potential maternal effects after accounting for assortative mating and genetic transmission, rather than to inherited liability alone[^10]. However, measurement limitations (maternal reporting of outcomes, low internal consistency) complicate inference because shared-rater bias and measurement error can inflate or obscure intergenerational effects, and the authors explicitly raised the possibility that measurement error could affect anxiety modelling results relative to other traits[^10]. The very small slope–slope correlations in maternal symptom–child temperament change (all $$r<0.005$$) further suggest that, at least in early childhood temperament markers, correlated change over time may be limited even when stability correlations exist, reinforcing a nuanced systemic view in which stable family risk can matter even if dynamic co-development is weak in some measures[^11].

Digital exposure evidence supports the ecological premise that youth anxiety co-varies with digital behaviours, but it is comparatively weaker for maintenance/causal claims. Problematic social media use shows a moderate association with anxiety symptoms (meta-analytic $$r=0.348$$), and the developmental framing explicitly ties this exposure to ongoing maturation of emotion-regulation and motivational neural circuitry, which supports a neurobiologically informed ecological hypothesis[^2]. Yet the same evidence base highlights cross-sectional correlational data, inability to infer causality, and likely bidirectional effects between mental health and social media use, which limits conclusions that digital environments maintain or amplify anxiety over time[^2]. Broader measures of social media time/engagement show small associations with internalising symptoms in clinical samples (e.g., $$r=0.08$$ to $$r=0.12$$), and the authors again emphasised that correlational evidence cannot adjudicate directionality, suggesting that a systemic framing here may need to focus on specific patterns (problematic use) and mediating mechanisms rather than duration alone[^3].

Longitudinal findings further qualify the digital-maintenance hypothesis. A large adolescent cohort found cross-sectional associations between screen time and anxiety but no adjusted 12‑month prediction, and the authors characterised longitudinal associations as markedly weaker, which is consistent with the longitudinal systematic review’s conclusion that evidence for longitudinal associations with anxiety is lacking[^16][^5]. Measurement challenges (single-item retrospective screen time, unvalidated bespoke measures, limited content specificity) provide a plausible explanation for inconsistent longitudinal results and indicate that future ecological research should prioritise content, motivations, and problematic/dysregulated use constructs over coarse duration metrics[^16][^5].

### Do systemic interventions improve outcomes relative to individual treatment

Intervention evidence most directly addresses whether changing the family system or parent behaviours improves child anxiety outcomes. Meta-analytic evidence indicates that parent-focused interventions reduce child anxiety with a medium pooled effect ($$d=-0.59$$), and component findings emphasise behavioural elements as common to effective packages, supporting the plausibility that altering parent responses and contingencies is therapeutically relevant[^6]. However, the explicit “very low certainty of evidence” qualification limits confidence in the robustness of the pooled effect size and implies that additional high-quality trials and better standardisation are needed for definitive comparative claims[^6].

When compared with waitlist/no intervention, parent-only approaches generally show benefit, but comparative advantage over active treatments is less clear. The parent-only systematic review/meta-analysis reported significant effects versus waitlist but no significant differences versus other active interventions, and no significant effects in child-rated outcomes, implying that systemic interventions may be “at least as good” as alternatives but not reliably superior across outcome reporters[^7]. In family-based CBT syntheses, strong effects versus waitlist (OR=26.21) are juxtaposed with non-significant effects versus attention control and the conclusion that individual CBT is not superior to family-based CBT, which indicates equivalence between child-only and family-inclusive CBT in available comparisons rather than clear superiority of either modality[^8].

Pragmatic RCT evidence strengthens the case for systemic engagement in scalable delivery models, even when superiority is not shown. The non-inferiority trial of OSI plus therapist support found outcomes comparable to treatment as usual (SMD=0.01 on CAIS-P interference) while reducing therapist time and lowering costs, which supports using parent-led, digitally augmented interventions as part of stepped care to increase access without compromising outcomes[^13]. This finding is particularly relevant to the systemic framing because it shifts part of therapeutic action to parents and home environments while leveraging digital tools, although interpretation should consider that participants could not be blinded and outcomes were participant-reported with prioritisation of parent report[^13].

Preventive parent interventions targeting anxious parents provide additional evidence that changing parent factors can yield child benefits. The large online intervention trial for highly anxious parents showed small but significant reductions in child anxiety (Cohen’s $$d=-0.16$$) and framed the approach as promising for limiting intergenerational transmission, directly aligning with the systemic transmission premise supported by longitudinal parent–child anxiety findings[^14][^9]. However, differential retention between arms introduces uncertainty, and future trials should evaluate strategies to improve engagement and assess outcomes using multi-informant and clinician-rated measures to reduce susceptibility to attrition and reporting biases[^14][^13].

## Limitations

The family accommodation evidence base synthesised here is limited for causal inference because no longitudinal studies were included in the accommodation meta-analysis, explicitly restricting conclusions about whether accommodation maintains anxiety over time[^1]. Multiple intergenerational studies highlight shared-rater and measurement issues, including reliance on maternal reports for child outcomes and explicit concerns that shared rater bias may inflate associations, as well as low internal consistency in anxiety measures, which can distort both effect sizes and model comparisons[^10][^11].

Digital-environment evidence is consistently constrained by correlational designs and reliance on self-report, with multiple reviews explicitly stating that causal inferences cannot be drawn and that bidirectional effects are likely, and a separate longitudinal review concluding that evidence for anxiety-specific longitudinal associations is lacking[^2][^3][^5]. Intervention trials and syntheses indicate additional risks of bias, including lack of blinding (performance bias) in CBT trials and open-label delivery in prevention trials, which can influence subjective symptom reporting and expectations, and meta-analytic conclusions that overall certainty or methodological quality is weak in a substantial portion of the parent-only intervention literature[^8][^15][^7].

## Conclusions

Overall, the evidence supports a systemic framing of pediatric anxiety most strongly in the family domain, where family accommodation correlates moderately with anxiety severity and longitudinal family-process factors (e.g., father attachment security, autonomy granting, partner conflict) help explain persistence and parent anxiety stability[^1][^12]. Intergenerational findings further support environmentally mediated pathways, with longitudinal modelling showing parental anxiety predicting child anxiety and genetically informed analyses attributing most of the observed mother–child association in anxiety symptoms to potential maternal effects beyond genetic transmission alone[^9][^10].

Digital environments show reliable cross-sectional associations with anxiety—especially for problematic social media use—but longitudinal evidence for anxiety maintenance is weaker and causal direction remains unresolved, indicating that digital exposures should be treated as plausible contextual correlates and potential amplifiers rather than established maintaining causes on current evidence[^2][^16][^5].

Intervention evidence indicates that engaging parents and the family system can reduce child anxiety relative to waitlist/no-intervention comparators and can achieve outcomes comparable to established care models, including via parent-led and digitally augmented delivery that reduces therapist time and costs, supporting family-inclusive and stepped-care approaches as clinically meaningful and potentially scalable systemic strategies[^6][^7][^13].
