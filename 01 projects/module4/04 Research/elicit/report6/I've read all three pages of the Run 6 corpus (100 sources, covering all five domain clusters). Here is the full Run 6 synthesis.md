## Run 6 Synthesis: Why Does the Burden of Youth Anxiety Rise Despite Effective Treatments?

### Q1: Persistence and sequelae — why acting early matters

The longitudinal evidence that childhood anxiety, if not effectively treated, casts a long shadow is strong and consistent.

The most comprehensive UK evidence comes from ALSPAC (Avon Longitudinal Study of Parents and Children, n = 8,122). Morales-Muñoz et al. (*British Journal of Psychiatry*, 2023) tracked participants from ages 8, 10, and 13 through to 24 years using formal DAWBA diagnostic assessment. All three latent-class trajectories involving persistent anxiety, persistent depression, or comorbid anxiety-and-depression were significantly associated with mental health problems and education/employment difficulties at 24. Critically, the comorbid group carried the heaviest burden by every outcome — the highest odds ratios for adverse outcomes, and the only group significantly associated with substance misuse. Persistent high anxiety alone did not independently predict physical health problems, but the comorbid trajectory did. This strongly supports the claim that early intervention matters most *before* the anxiety–depression cascade locks in. [^1]

The trajectory data are complemented by ALSPAC work by Tseliou et al. (*Psychological Medicine*, 2024, n = 8,286) using SDQ emotional problem scores across seven assessments from age 4 to 17. Children in the "decreasing" trajectory were *not* at elevated risk of any poor adult outcome, while those in the "persistent" and "increasing" trajectories had substantially higher risks of adult depression, self-harm, and educational impairment. The key implication: children who improve — with or without treatment — do fine; children who don't improve accumulate risk. The question is what causes trajectories to diverge. [^2]

A mechanistic framework for why anxiety transitions to depression is provided by Silk et al. (*Psychological Medicine*, 2012), who propose that social evaluative threat sensitivity and pubertal reward-processing changes combine at adolescence to create a negative spiral. Critically, they argue early intervention could interrupt this pathway specifically. This is supported by Silk et al. (*JAACAP*, 2019), where CBT responders in a longitudinal follow-up showed significantly fewer depressive symptoms at 2-year follow-up compared with non-responders, with effects driven mainly by girls. [^3][^4]

The older but closely cited Woodward & Fergusson (2001, JAACAP, n = 1,265, Christchurch cohort) remains important for its scope: anxiety in adolescence predicted anxiety, depression, illicit drug dependence, and educational underachievement in young adulthood even after adjustment for confounders. The magnitude (OR typically 2–3) and breadth of outcomes make this one of the stronger heterotypic continuity findings in the literature. [^5]

On developmental timing and neuroplasticity: the developmental neuroscience literature converges on early-to-mid adolescence as a plastic window for intervention. Ladouceur (*American Journal of Psychiatry*, 2024) synthesises trial evidence showing CBT normalises fronto-parietal network activation in unmedicated early adolescents. Kim (2017, Q1 journal) reviews rodent–human parallel evidence that extinction-based learning (the mechanism of exposure CBT) is particularly efficient in childhood, and argues this is a genuine neurobiological window, not merely convenient labelling. The practical implication: waiting is costly in a very specific neurobiological sense, not just a clinical one. [^6][^7]

### Q2: The treatment-access gap

This is the most empirically solid explanation for the persistent burden, and the UK data are stark.

The most directly relevant UK figure I found comes from a 2024 *British Journal of Child Health* brief report (Henshaw) drawing on the England Children's Commissioner's investigation: **more than 1 in 4 children referred to CAMHS in 2022/23 were still waiting for support at year-end, with approximately 32,000 having waited over two years**. [^8]

Ball et al. (2022, *International Journal of Population Data Science*, NHS Grampian, n = ~100,000 children/year, 2015–2021) show that CAMHS referrals rose 20% over the study period, but the proportion of referrals *rejected* rose from 18% to 31% — meaning the total number of children reaching specialist care stayed roughly flat while demand increased. Mental health prescriptions rose 40% over the same period. The divergence between demand and capacity is structural, not incidental. [^9]

The EN-CAMHS study (Abel et al., 2025, NIHR Health and Social Care Delivery Research, 9 CAMHS sites, n = 497,502 referrals 2020–21) found that **nearly one-quarter of CAMHS referrals were unsuccessful**, often after long waits and without redirection to alternative services. Focus groups with over 100 stakeholders identified confusion about CAMHS remit, lack of referral support, and a failure to communicate during the waiting period as the primary problems. These are not resource-only problems; some are fixable organisational failures. [^10]

Globally, a systematic review and meta-analysis by Ghafari et al. (2021, *Archives of Psychiatric Nursing*, 15 studies, n = 51,309) estimated the pooled prevalence of unmet mental health care need among adolescents at **54%** (95% CI 41–67%), rising to 58% among those with any diagnosed mental disorder. This is broadly consistent with US data: a 2026 JAMA Pediatrics paper (Burnett et al.) using 2023–24 National Survey of Children's Health found that 53.6% of children with an MEB condition had difficulty getting mental health care, up from 44.0% in 2018. [^11][^12]

Barriers are not only structural. Radez et al. (*European Child and Adolescent Psychiatry*, 2021, qualitative study, n = 22 diagnosed adolescents) identified four barrier themes: difficulty making sense of difficulties (normalisation of distress), disclosure barriers (shame, fear of being a burden), ambivalence (especially autonomy concerns at adolescence), and dependency on adult gatekeepers (parents, teachers) as the necessary route in. The last is critical: adolescents with anxiety can't typically self-refer to CAMHS; they need an adult to notice, understand, and act, and those adults face their own barriers. [^13]

The US trend data from Lebrun-Harris et al. (*Journal of Developmental and Behavioral Pediatrics*, 2025, National Survey of Children's Health, n = 155,178, 2016–2021) show this is not improving: children with a current MEB increased from 18.1% to 20.4% (10+ million) over five years, while unmet need rates rose from 4.3% to 6.4%. Disparities are large and widening: access was substantially lower for publicly insured, low-income, and racial/ethnic minority children. [^12]

### Q3: The efficacy–effectiveness gap and long-term durability

This is a more complicated picture than the access narrative would suggest.

For the core efficacy–effectiveness question, the most relevant evidence is Wergeland, Riise & Öst (*Clinical Psychology Review*, 2020, systematic review and meta-analysis, 58 routine care studies, n = 4,618 youth). Large effect sizes for routine CBT at post-treatment (g = 1.28–2.54) and follow-up (g = 1.72–3.36). Remission rates at post-treatment ranged 50.7–77.4%, and the benchmarking analysis found similarities between routine care and efficacy trials were **greater than the differences**. In other words, for youth who access CBT in routine settings, outcomes are largely comparable to those achieved in RCTs. This is an important result: it means the efficacy–effectiveness gap is *not* the primary explanation for the rising burden. The problem is who gets in, not what happens once they arrive. [^14]

The German KibA study (Krause, Zhang & Schneider, *Psychotherapy and Psychosomatics*, 2024, n = 210) is the most rigorous long-term follow-up I found: 61% in full remission at end of treatment, 64% at mean 4.3-year follow-up. The effect sizes at long-term follow-up (Hedges' g = 2.34 in child report) were striking and comparable to post-treatment figures, with no deterioration. Walter et al. (*Behavioural and Cognitive Psychotherapy*, 2023, n = 220, mean follow-up 5.3 years) replicated this, finding 57–70% reporting decreased emotional symptoms and 80% improved life satisfaction at follow-up. [^15][^16]

The durability picture is less uniformly positive from the CAMS/CAMELS data. Ginsburg et al. (*JAMA Psychiatry*, 2014) — the CAMELS 6-year naturalistic follow-up of CAMS participants (n = 288) — found only 46.5% in remission at a mean of 6 years post-randomisation, with 48% of acute responders having relapsed. The CAMELS extended long-term study (Ginsburg et al., *JAACAP*, 2018) found that across a 4-year follow-up, only 22% were in stable remission, 30% chronically ill, and 48% were relapsers. Bai et al. (*JCPP*, 2023) identified three trajectory groups: "durable responders" (stable gains), "short-term responders" (relapsed), and "delayed remitters" — with caregiver strain differentiating the first two. [^17][^18]

The reconciliation between these datasets is that German routine-care studies show better long-term maintenance than the US CAMS/CAMELS data, possibly because the KibA protocol included booster sessions, had high protocol fidelity, and followed children who had already completed treatment rather than following an ITT sample. The meta-analytic picture (Rith-Najarian et al., 2019, 110 CBT groups) found large effect sizes at 2+ years (g = 1.23–1.82), but noted that outcome data availability diminished substantially at later follow-ups — a methodological warning about optimistic meta-analyses. [^19]

**Summary for Q3:** Routine CBT achieves efficacy-comparable results when delivered well. Durability is real but imperfect — a meaningful minority relapse within 2–6 years. The efficacy–effectiveness gap is not absent, but it is not the primary driver of the treatment–prevalence paradox.

### Q4: Developmental and transfer-fit issues

The most direct evidence on whether youth anxiety treatments are downward extensions of adult protocols with limited fit comes from two complementary literatures.

The individual patient data meta-analysis by Bennett et al. (*Depression and Anxiety*, 2013, 24 RCTs, 1,700 children) found **no overall age effect** on CBT outcomes within the child/adolescent range, except that individual CBT outperformed group CBT for adolescents but not children — suggesting the delivery format matters more than age per se for treatment response. 

Waite & Creswell (*Journal of Affective Disorders*, 2014, n = 200, children 6–12 vs adolescents 13–18, routine referral sample) showed that adolescents had significantly higher clinician-rated anxiety, more social anxiety, more comorbid depression, and more school refusal than children — profiles that mapped poorly onto the cognitive content of protocols developed for older children or adults. Waite et al. (*JAD*, 2015, n = 120) showed that interpretation biases associated with anxiety were present in anxious adolescents but not anxious children under 10, questioning whether cognitive restructuring components are developmentally appropriate for younger children. [^20][^21]

Baker et al. (*Clinical Child and Family Psychology Review*, 2021, meta-analysis of 16 studies, n = 766 adolescents) found only 36% in remission post-treatment compared with typical rates of 50–60% for mixed child/adolescent samples, noting this as "a clear need to develop more effective treatments for adolescents" specifically. [^22]

The developmental-fit critique has generated a productive treatment adaptation literature. Abel et al. (*Psychiatric Annals*, 2025) review family-based CBT for under-7s (15 protocols), noting that pure downward extension fails but parent-mediated versions work well. Carpenter et al. (*Clinical Child and Family Psychology Review*, 2014) describe PCIT-based adaptations for early childhood internalising, showing this class of interventions delivers comparable effect sizes to standard CBT without requiring children to have adult-level cognitive capacity. [^23][^24]

Frankel, Gallerani & Garber (chapter in a standard text, 2012) make the developmental uniformity myth critique explicit: the assumption that children across ages respond similarly to CBT has never been adequately tested, and most protocols derive from either adult models or older-child models extended upward to adolescents. A meta-analysis noted by these authors found that children aged 11–13 benefited more from CBT than those aged 5–11, consistent with the cognitive-capacity account. 

**Summary for Q4:** Developmental fit issues are real and specifically pronounced at two ends of the age range: under-10s (insufficient cognitive capacity for standard cognitive restructuring) and mid-to-late adolescents (higher comorbidity, different disorder profile, possible need for different protocol emphasis). This is a partial explanation for suboptimal outcomes but not the primary driver of rising burden — it explains why some who access treatment don't fully recover, rather than why most don't access treatment at all.

### Q5: The rising-incidence hypothesis and the prevention paradox

This question generated the most directly relevant sources for the framing question, and the evidence supports a genuine incidence increase — though the detection debate cannot be fully resolved.

**The incidence data.** The UK CPRD study by Cybulski et al. (*BMC Psychiatry*, 2021, n = 9.1 million individuals, 117 million person-years, 2003–2018) is the strongest primary care incidence dataset. Anxiety disorder incidence in under-20s in England increased by an **incidence rate ratio of 3.51** (95% CI 3.18–3.89) between 2003 and 2018 — a 3.5-fold rise. The authors explicitly note they cannot rule out true incidence increases, but acknowledge that diagnostic changes and reduced stigma partly explain results. [^25]

From the GBD data, Bie et al. (*Frontiers in Psychiatry*, 2024, GBD 2021 data, 204 countries, ages 10–24, 1990–2021) report a **52% increase in global anxiety disorder incidence** from 1990 to 2021 in adolescents/young adults, with a sharp acceleration post-2019. The 10–14 age group showed the steepest rise. [^26]

Piao et al. (*European Child and Adolescent Psychiatry*, 2022, GBD 2019 analysis) confirm that age-standardised DALY rates for anxiety disorders in under-20s increased significantly from 1990 to 2019, and that the number of affected young people grew substantially, partially driven by population growth but not entirely explainable by it. [^27]

For the US, Xiang et al. (*JAMA Network Open*, 2024, n = ~1.7 million, Southern California, 2017–2021) found clinically-diagnosed anxiety incidence (not just self-report) increased **31%** from 2017 to 2021, with acceleration during COVID. This uses healthcare billing, not self-report, reducing the detection/stigma confound somewhat. [^28]

Counterpoint: Phillips & Yu (*Journal of Affective Disorders*, 2020, Ontario administrative data, all Ontarians aged 5–25, 1997–2017) found diagnosed anxiety/depression rates were stable until ~2012, then showed modest increases in 11–20 year olds. They explicitly caution against "upsurge" narratives, noting the increases are small and sociodemographically patterned. However, their data predate COVID and end at 2017, missing the apparent 2019+ acceleration. [^29]

Parodi et al. (*Social Psychiatry and Psychiatric Epidemiology*, 2021, n = 37,360 adolescents, Dane County, 2012–2018) found anxiety screening-criteria prevalence increased from 34.1% to 44.0% between 2012 and 2018, with widening disparities for sexual minority youth and girls. This is a rapid survey-based prevalence rise, but is school-based screening rather than clinical diagnosis. [^30]

Moreover, the Norwegian national registry analysis (Bang, 2025, *European Journal of Public Health*, population-level data, 2010–2022) shows anxiety disorder incidence in diagnosed specialist care rose sharply in adolescent girls, with trends largely predating COVID. This is difficult to explain purely by reduced stigma, since the rise was in specialist care rather than community screening. [^31]

**The prevention paradox.** The most important theoretical source is Skinner et al. (*BMC Psychiatry*, 2022, dynamic systems model fitted to Australian national data). They show that between 2008 and 2019 in Australia, increasing access to mental health care had a **real but obscured benefit** — but this benefit was masked by a concurrent increase in the per-capita incidence of psychological distress that more than offset the treatment gains. They estimate that without the incidence increase, prevalence would have fallen from 14.4% to 13.6%. The signal is present; it is just overwhelmed. [^32]

Skinner et al. (*Scientific Reports*, 2023) extend this with a dynamic modelling analysis showing that indicated prevention (targeting subthreshold symptoms) is often *more* effective at reducing population disorder prevalence than universal prevention — challenging the widely assumed superiority of whole-population strategies and highlighting that the early subthreshold group is an important leverage point. 

The "treatment-prevalence paradox" is articulated clearly for depression by Ezawa, Robinson & Hollon (*Annual Review of Clinical Psychology*, 2024, Q1), who conclude that access failure — particularly in the delivery gap between efficacious protocols and real-world treatment — is the dominant explanation, with rising incidence as a contributing factor. Their prescription: lay therapists and digital technologies. [^33]

Patton, Raniti & Reavley (*World Psychiatry*, 2021) make the population-level argument most forcefully: expanding individual treatment continuously without addressing structural and social risk factors cannot reduce burden at population scale; the analogy is alcohol — individual-level counselling has not moved population rates; population-level risk-factor reduction has. They point to digital technology, shifts in peer relationships, and social determinants as key structural drivers. 

Twenge (*Psychiatric Research and Clinical Practice*, 2020) proposes a specific mechanism for the post-2012 rise in US adolescents — smartphone/social media adoption at population scale, operating via social displacement, sleep disruption, cyberbullying, and online social comparison — and reviews large trend study evidence. This account remains contested (Orben & Przybylski, not retrieved in this corpus, challenge effect size magnitude), but the post-2012 temporal coincidence with smartphone diffusion and the gender pattern (girls' mental health deteriorated more sharply) are hard to dismiss entirely.

### Q6: The adjudication

Weighting the five explanations against the evidence:

**1. The access gap is the dominant proximate driver.** A majority of anxious young people — roughly 50–60% globally, with UK CAMHS data suggesting closer to 70–75% when attrition, rejection, and non-referral are combined — never reach evidence-based care. This alone would produce rising prevalence even in the complete absence of rising incidence. It is the most direct, best-evidenced, and most actionable explanation. Uncertainty: the figures conflate unmet need broadly and anxiety-specific access gaps; most data are not anxiety-diagnosis-specific.

**2. Rising incidence is real and adds a prevention paradox overlay.** The convergence across CPRD, GBD, US clinical records, Norwegian registry data, and survey studies makes a genuine incidence increase in the post-2010/2012 period hard to dismiss. The mechanism is not definitively established, but the digital/social environment hypothesis is the most plausible for the specific gender and timing pattern. This incidence rise means treatment scale-up alone cannot reduce population prevalence even if access were fully solved — a prevention investment is also required. Uncertainty: some of the rise reflects reduced stigma and diagnostic expansion; the exact split between real incidence and ascertainment change is unresolved.

**3. Relapse/durability is a secondary driver.** CAMELS data indicate ~48% relapse within 6 years; this contributes to cumulative burden by re-circulating individuals back into the "anxious and untreated" pool. However, German routine-care data suggest good maintenance is achievable with well-delivered protocols, so this is a quality/continuity-of-care problem rather than a fundamental treatment limitation. Uncertainty: follow-up data beyond 2–3 years are sparse in routine settings.

**4. Developmental fit is a partial and solvable modifier.** The evidence that standard protocols are suboptimally effective for under-10s and for mid-to-late adolescent profiles with high comorbidity is real but quantitatively modest. The effect size difference (adolescent CBT remission ~36% vs ~50–60% in mixed samples) is meaningful clinically but insufficient to explain population-level burden trends. Better protocol adaptation is warranted but should not be the policy lever.

**5. Developmental timing windows provide the strongest argument for urgency, not a new explanation.** The neuroplasticity window evidence strengthens the case for acting on explanation 1 (access gap) earlier — not a separate explanation for rising burden, but a reason the temporal dimension of the access gap is especially important.

**Evidence gaps to flag:** No study in this corpus directly addresses the treatment–prevalence paradox using anxiety-specific incidence data in under-18 UK samples. The Skinner modelling work uses Australia-wide adult psychological distress data. A UK-specific dynamic modelling of youth anxiety incidence vs CAMHS capacity would be a high-value evidence gap to flag.

### Q7: Solution mapping for the dominant gaps

**For the access gap (primary target):**

The three best-evidenced delivery strategies:

1. **Parent-led/parent-mediated treatment (notably SPACE and parent-only CBT).** The strongest scalability evidence. Lebowitz et al. SPACE trial (JAACAP 2019, n = 124): parent-only treatment non-inferior to child CBT on anxiety outcomes, with greater reduction in family accommodation. Parent-only delivery removes the child as the bottleneck — parents can engage without requiring a child appointment slot. Effect sizes comparable to standard CBT. Limitation: requires engaged, non-anxious-enough parents; harder in high-adversity families.

2. **Guided low-intensity digital CBT (internet-delivered or blended).** Most relevant for closing the access gap is guided (not unguided) delivery with therapist support — small-to-medium effects (g ≈ 0.4–0.7 across this corpus's Run 4 findings) in youth samples, better engagement than unguided, and scalable. The evidence base is weaker in under-12s than in adolescents. Specific example: Wuthrich and Rapee's Cool Kids Online, iChill. Limitation: engagement/dropout remain problems; effects vs active controls are smaller.

3. **School-based universal/indicated CBT programmes as a first-rung step-care solution.** For closing the access gap at population scale, FRIENDS-based and transdiagnostic school programmes offer the best coverage. Effects on anxiety are small (SMD ≈ 0.2–0.3 from Run 4 meta-analyses), durability limited, but as a first-level filter identifying and mildly reducing anxiety before it requires specialist referral, the cost-effectiveness argument is strong. OurFutures (Australia) showed a significant 3-month anxiety reduction; Climate Schools data showed attenuated growth up to 30 months before long-term effects were lost.

**For the prevention paradox/rising incidence (secondary target, longer horizon):**

The Skinner et al. (2023) dynamic modelling result favours **indicated prevention** — targeting subthreshold anxious youth *before* clinical threshold — over universal screening alone. The most evidence-supported indicated intervention is brief cognitive intervention (e.g., CBT-based workshops or single-session interventions for high-risk youth identified by school screening), combined with parent psychoeducation. The evidence is modest but the mechanism is well-specified. Sleep interventions in school settings (CBT-I, as per the Run 5 synthesis, school-based g = 0.37 for anxiety) are the single best-supported mechanism-targeted component that can sit within a universal/indicated tier.

For population-level structural factors (social connection, digital environment), the evidence base is still too thin to specify a scalable intervention, but the case for policy-level action (sleep guidelines, social media platform standards, school social climate) is supported by the prevention paradox modelling and the Patton et al. World Psychiatry argument.
