The best causal evidence I found clusters into two areas: (1) experiments showing that brief exposure to idealized, comparison-triggering social media content can acutely increase state anxiety/social anxiety, and (2) longitudinal evidence (including meta-analyses) that cybervictimization predicts later internalizing symptoms, including anxiety, though the effect is usually small and sometimes bidirectional.

One fairly clean adolescent-specific longitudinal result is a two-wave cross-lagged panel study (N=257; ages 13–14) separating active, passive, and problematic use. It reported that problematic social media use predicted later anxiety symptoms, and that “the reverse was not the case.” [^1] The main limitation is power and measurement: the sample is modest and outcomes are self-reported.

The strongest counterweight in my results is a large UK longitudinal cohort in The Lancet (N=3,228; ages 10–15). In adjusted analyses, time spent on social media at ages 12–13 did not predict later mental health problems, and the authors explicitly interpret this as “little evidence to suggest a causal relationship … 2 years later.” [^2] This study is well-powered, but it measures mental health via a broad difficulties score (not anxiety-specific) and uses a coarse hours-on-social-media scale, which can miss mechanism-relevant exposure (what content was seen, what interactions occurred).

On social comparison and idealized content, the experimental evidence in my quick pull is mostly in older samples (college students or adults), but it is mechanistically informative. In one lab experiment in Frontiers in Psychology, participants exposed to typical social media content reported higher social anxiety than controls exposed to documentaries, and mediation analyses supported an “upward social comparison → (lower self-esteem) → social anxiety” pathway. [^3] A separate randomized Instagram-scrolling experiment found that viewing appearance-focused accounts increased state anxiety relative to control content. [^4] These experiments support short-term causality for the comparison mechanism, but they do not directly establish that ordinary adolescent use causes long-run anxiety disorders.

Cyberbullying is where the longitudinal synthesis is clearest. A 2025 meta-analysis focused specifically on longitudinal studies (27 studies; N=13,497; ages 8–19) concluded the victimization→mental health association is “weakly positive and consistent across time and age.” [^5] Another meta-analysis of 56 longitudinal studies emphasized bidirectionality: cybervictimization predicts later internalizing problems (including anxiety), but baseline depression/anxiety also predicts later cybervictimization. [^6] Individual longitudinal studies align with that picture; for example, a Journal of Adolescent Health cohort (N=2,480; ages 12–13) found cybervictims were more likely to report social anxiety a year later even after adjusting for baseline mental health. [^7] Cross-sectional cybervictimization meta-analyses show larger associations (e.g., r≈0.3 with anxiety), but by design they cannot separate cause from selection. [^8]

For “reduced face-to-face interactions” and “decreased quality of real-life friendships,” the work I found in this pass is suggestive but not strongly causal. A large cross-cohort U.S. time-series analysis reports that adolescents in the 2010s spent less time in face-to-face interaction and that loneliness increased after 2011. [^9] That is consistent with a displacement story at the cohort level, but it is not an individual-level causal test, and it addresses loneliness more directly than anxiety.

Overall, the evidence differentiating correlation vs causation looks like this: time-on-platform measures often weaken substantially in adjusted longitudinal models (sometimes to null), [^2][^10] while mechanism-specific exposures (idealized content and cybervictimization) have stronger support, including experiments for short-term effects and longitudinal meta-analytic evidence for prospective risk (with bidirectional feedback loops especially for cyberbullying). [^3][^5][^6] This is from a quick search over a large literature, so treat it as an orientation; a deeper pass could pull in additional adolescent-only experiments, multi-wave longitudinal mediation models, and EMA-based studies that test mechanisms closer to real-world use.
