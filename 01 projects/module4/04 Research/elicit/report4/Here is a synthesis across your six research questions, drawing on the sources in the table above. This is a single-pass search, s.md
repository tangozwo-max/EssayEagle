**Q1 — Prevalence & epidemiology (replacing Polanczyk 2015)**

The most up-to-date current figure comes from GBD 2021 data. A 2025 analysis in *Psychological Medicine* (Liu et al.) found that by 2021 anxiety disorders had become the single leading cause of nonfatal disability in children and adolescents aged 5–24, accounting for 12.9 million years lived with disability globally — up from third place pre-pandemic, with an estimated 165.9 extra cases per 100,000 attributable to COVID beyond pre-pandemic trends. [^1] The 52% increase in incidence among 10–24-year-olds between 1990 and 2021 reported by Bie et al. (2024, *Frontiers in Psychiatry*, GBD data across 204 countries) shows a sharp inflection post-2019; bullying victimisation was the most significant identified risk factor. [^2] For COVID-period prevalence specifically, Racine et al.'s JAMA Pediatrics meta-analysis (2021, 29 studies, N = 80,879) estimated that roughly 1 in 5 youth globally had clinically elevated anxiety symptoms during the first pandemic year — approximately double prepandemic estimates, and rising across successive waves. [^3]

For the UK specifically, your best recent sources are: Cybulski et al. (2021, *BMC Psychiatry*, CPRD cohort of 9.1 million, 2003–2018), which found the incidence of diagnosed anxiety disorders in CYP tripled over that period (IRR 3.51) [^4]; and Taxiarchi et al. (2025, *British Journal of Psychiatry*), showing English MHCYP survey data that prevalence of anxiety disorders rose from 3.5% to 5.4% of 5–16-year-olds between 2004 and 2017, driven predominantly by social phobia, separation anxiety, and panic disorder, with the increase confined to White CYP in the sample. [^5] The 2023/4 APMS update (McManus, 2025) found rates continuing to rise particularly in 16–24-year-old women, though the full data remains embargoed pending government release. One important caveat across UK trend data: rising incidence rates in primary care records likely reflect a mixture of true increases, reduced stigma, and improved detection — the three are difficult to disentangle.

**Q2 — Established psychological interventions: efficacy and durability**

The case for CBT is solid and well-replicated. A 2025 meta-analysis by Alemdar & Karaca (29 studies, 2015–2024) reported a large overall SMD of −1.51 for CBT vs. controls in children with anxiety disorders, with effects maintained at 3-, 6-, and 12-month follow-up. [^6] The largest network meta-analysis in this space (Zhou et al., 2018, JAMA Psychiatry, 101 RCTs, N = 6,625) found that most therapies outperformed waitlist (SMDs −1.43 to −0.61), but group CBT was the only modality that also significantly outperformed other active therapies. [^7]

On individual vs. group format: a 2021 meta-analysis of 9 head-to-head RCTs found no overall difference, but individual CBT was more effective than group for adolescents specifically (SMD −0.77), while in younger children the formats were equivalent. [^8] The implication for module design is that group format is a reasonable default for children; in adolescents, individual delivery may yield stronger outcomes.

Family-based CBT is well-supported. Sigurvinsdóttir et al.'s 2019 meta-analysis (81 RCTs) showed all major CBT modalities outperformed waitlist, with family-based CBT superior to treatment-as-usual, waitlist, and attention control; combination CBT + medication outperformed individual CBT. [^9]

On SPACE (parent-only treatment): Lebowitz et al.'s 2019 JAACAP noninferiority RCT (N = 124, ages 7–14) established that SPACE produced outcomes equivalent to child-focused CBT across all primary and secondary anxiety measures, with significantly greater reductions in family accommodation. [^10] A 2023 RCT by Storch et al. found that a light-dose version of SPACE (bibliotherapy + 4 sessions) was not significantly inferior to standard 12-session SPACE on primary outcomes, though children self-reported higher anxiety in the light-dose arm. [^11] A 2022 systematic review of parent-only interventions broadly (29 studies, Jewell et al.) confirmed significant effects over waitlist but found child-rated outcomes non-significant and no evidence for any superior delivery format. [^12]

Durability: across the literature, CBT gains generally hold at 6–12 month follow-up, though longer-term maintenance (2+ years) is less consistently studied.

**Q3 — Pharmacological interventions**

The most recent and comprehensive source is a 2025 meta-analysis by Karawekpanyawong et al. (15 RCTs, N = 2,083, median age ~11.6): SSRIs and SNRIs significantly reduced anxiety (SMD −0.49 vs. placebo) with good tolerability, but were associated with a 3.5-fold higher risk of suicidal ideation (RR 3.51, 95% CI 1.51–8.16). [^13] The larger 2017 JAMA Pediatrics meta-analysis (Wang et al., 115 studies, N = 7,719) found SSRIs increased remission (RR 2.04) and response (RR 1.96) vs. placebo, and that CBT outperformed fluoxetine on primary symptoms and outperformed sertraline on remission. Combination sertraline + CBT was superior to either alone. Benzodiazepines and tricyclics showed no significant benefit. [^14]

For combination treatment timing: Strawn et al.'s 2021 individual patient data analysis of 3 NIH RCTs found that SSRI + CBT surpassed SSRI alone at week 12 but not earlier — the additive value of CBT accrues late. Fastest responders to combination treatment were younger, milder at baseline, and had depressive disorders; slowest were boys, adolescents, and those with severe symptoms. [^15]

The stepped-care implication that emerges from these data: CBT is appropriate as a first-line standalone; SSRIs are indicated for moderate-to-severe cases or CBT non-responders; combination is most appropriate for severe or treatment-refractory presentations, bearing in mind the latency to benefit and the suicidality signal requiring monitoring.

**Q4 — Digital and emerging interventions (your priority area)**

*Internet-delivered CBT (iCBT).* The evidence base is now reasonably mature, though effect sizes for under-18s are smaller than for adults. A 2023 meta-analysis specifically in under-18s (Csirmaz et al., 18 RCTs, N = 1,290) found medium effects on clinician-rated outcomes (g = 0.66) but small effects on self-report (g = 0.28). [^16] Grist et al.'s 2018 meta-analysis (34 RCTs, N = 3,113, ages 6–18) found technology-delivered CBT yielded g = 0.66 over waitlist; non-CBT digital approaches (attention bias modification, CBM) showed small or non-significant effects. Key moderators: therapist guidance, parental involvement, and problem severity all increased effect sizes. [^17] A 2021/22 meta-analysis (Eilert et al.) found a smaller pooled effect on anxiety (g = −0.25) using a broader but stricter inclusion approach. [^18]

*Engagement and dropout.* A 2024 RCT in Finland (Kaajalaakso et al., N = 234, ages 10–13, telephone-assisted ICBT) found 23.9% dropout, with dropout predicted by absence of an anxiety diagnosis and poor therapeutic alliance — demographic and pandemic factors were not significant predictors. Average engagement was 127 minutes/week on the platform. Satisfaction was 87% (children) and 95% (parents). [^19] A broader meta-analysis of internet anxiety treatment engagement (mostly adult, Bisby et al. 2022) found that once enrolled, ~81% of participants completed treatment and only ~21% dropped out at post-test. Clinical guidance significantly increased engagement.

*App / smartphone interventions.* The evidence base in under-18s is thin and heterogeneous. A notable 2026 RCT (Hagalwadi et al., N = 107, ages 12–21) of the SMILE app, which uses mobile sensing to personalise CBT delivery, found significant anxiety reduction at 4 weeks vs. controls (b = −0.48, p = 0.006), with marginal maintenance at 12 weeks. [^20] Older app RCTs show mostly pilot-level feasibility data with small samples.

*Single-session interventions (SSIs).* An umbrella review by Schleider et al. (2025, *Annual Review of Clinical Psychology*, 24 systematic reviews, 415 unique trials) found 83% of reviews reported significant positive effects; pooled SMD = −0.25. SSIs showed the largest effects for anxiety specifically (g = 0.56) and for children over adolescents. Self-administered and therapist-administered SSIs did not differ significantly. Digital SSIs delivered anonymously have shown better reach into underserved populations (LGBTQ+, ethnic minorities). [^21] The scalability argument for SSIs is compelling given that up to 80% of youth with mental health needs receive no services. Limitations: effects are smaller vs. active controls (g = 0.14) than vs. no treatment (g = 0.41), and effects may not persist beyond 13 weeks.

*Chatbot / conversational agents.* The evidence base is still largely feasibility-stage for youth specifically. A 2024 scoping review (Park et al., 39 studies) found most chatbots for youth are prototype rule-based systems delivering prewritten content; only preliminary evidence of benefit, and 90% of studies ignored ethical considerations. Youth flagged privacy as a concern. [^22] A 2025 scoping review of chatbots in treatment-seeking youth (Hawke et al., 10 studies, 2016–2025) found 8/10 studies reported high acceptability; 3 RCTs showed potential reductions in depressive symptoms. No studies focused specifically on anxiety as a primary outcome in rigorous RCT designs. [^23] The Woebot RCT (Fitzpatrick et al. 2017, N = 70, young adults 18–28) remains the most-cited trial; it reported significant depression reduction but the sample is above your ≤18 cutoff and the trial was unblinded. A 2022 pilot RCT of a CBT chatbot app for adolescents (Nicol et al., N = 18, ages 13–17) was too small to demonstrate effectiveness but showed feasibility. [^24]

*VR exposure therapy.* A 2026 systematic review and meta-analysis for youth (ages 4–18, Baschab et al., 5 studies) found a large pre-post effect on subjective distress (Hedges g = −1.51), but only 3 studies entered the meta-analysis, heterogeneity was high (I² = 81%), and no active-control RCTs were identified. Social anxiety and public speaking anxiety are the most studied targets. [^25] A 2025 mixed-age meta-analysis (Zeng et al., 33 RCTs, N = 3,182) found VR significantly outperformed conventional interventions (SMD −0.95), but does not separate child/adolescent subgroups cleanly. [^26] The headline finding is promise rather than established efficacy: the technology is ahead of the trials.

*JITAIs / EMA-based interventions.* JITAIs for mental health in youth are at an early development stage. A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis (von Lützow et al., 23 studies, N = 2,563) found a small between-group effect (g = 0.15) for JITAIs/EMIs for mental health broadly, with effects persisting at 1 and 3–6 months. Notably, shorter JITAIs (<6 weeks) showed larger follow-up effects (g = 0.71). Most studies targeted adults; no child-specific subgroup analysis was reported. [^27] A 2025 systematic review specifically of JITAIs in adolescent/young adult substance use (Koike et al., 6 studies, N = 168, ages 13–25) found preliminary efficacy but noted the field is underdeveloped and lacks a consistent JITAI definition. Anxiety-specific JITAI trials in youth appear essentially absent from the current literature.

*Engagement / adherence across digital interventions: the pattern.* Across modalities, engagement is consistently the Achilles heel. Guided formats substantially outperform unguided ones. Parental involvement improves outcomes for children. The strongest sustained engagement figures come from ICBT with telephone or therapist support rather than purely self-directed platforms. App-only designs without scaffolding tend to show rapid dropoff.

**Q5 — Preventive and early interventions**

Universal school-based prevention programmes produce modest effects. A 2018 meta-analysis (Johnstone et al., 14 RCTs, N = 5,970, children ≤13) found significant small effects on depressive symptoms (g = 0.17 post-program; g = 0.18 long-term) but no significant preventive effect on anxiety at any time point. FRIENDS and longer programmes were exceptions with some anxiety benefit. [^28] For targeted (indicated) approaches, a meta-analysis of 20 RCTs (N = 2,076) found a small but significant effect on anxiety (g = −0.28) favouring indicated school programmes over controls. [^29] The Schwartz et al. 2019 review (*Evidence-Based Mental Health*, 60 years of RCTs) singled out the CBT programme "Coping and Promoting Strength" for reducing anxiety diagnoses in a prevention context, and identified nine CBT programmes (including Cool Kids, Coping Cat, and One-Session Treatment) as reliably effective for treatment. Fluoxetine was effective pharmacologically but with adverse events requiring management.

**Q6 — Temporal/generalisability question: have pre-digital-era effect sizes held up?**

This is the most honest gap in the literature I found. No study in my search directly compared effect sizes across cohorts or tested whether digital-era youth respond differently to interventions designed pre-digitally. What the evidence can offer indirectly is: (a) the GBD and UK trend data document a clear secular increase in anxiety burden among youth, with the sharpest post-2019 inflection; (b) recent CBT meta-analyses (e.g., Alemdar & Karaca, 2025) continue to report large effects (SMD −1.51), suggesting that CBT efficacy has not obviously declined, though trials have not been designed to detect cohort effects; (c) several authors (including Khanna & Carper's 2021 review of digital mental health for child anxiety) note that pre-digital-era trial evidence may not generalise to today's digitally immersed youth, particularly regarding engagement, avoidance behaviours mediated by smartphones, and the new social-comparison dynamics of image-focused platforms — but this remains largely a theoretical argument without empirical testing. The question of whether interventions have kept pace with a digitally transformed childhood is, for now, posed more clearly than it is answered in the evidence base.
