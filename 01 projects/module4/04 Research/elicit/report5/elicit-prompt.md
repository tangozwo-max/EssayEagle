**Project:** Module 4 Part B — *Evidence-based approaches and interventions for anxiety in children and young people*
**Purpose:** Test the essay's central claim — *the maintaining mechanisms of youth anxiety have shifted to
digital-era drivers (social comparison, FOMO, cybervictimisation, displaced friendship/loneliness, sleep), but
do any interventions actually target these new mechanisms, or do they still treat the generic model?* This is
the bridge between "what changed" (runs 2–3) and "what works" (run 4). **Stay on interventions** — we are
asking what *treats/prevents* these drivers, not re-litigating whether they cause anxiety.
**Date:** 2026-06-07

## Paste this into Elicit (deep research / systematic review)

**Topic:** Interventions for children and young people (aged ≤18) that specifically target the *digital-era
maintaining mechanisms* of anxiety — and their effect on anxiety/internalising outcomes. The aim is to
establish whether treatment and prevention have adapted to the changed risk architecture of youth anxiety, or
still target generic anxiety only.

**Research questions:**

1. **Social-media reduction / restriction / "digital detox" interventions.** RCTs and meta-analyses where
   reducing, limiting, or abstaining from social media use is the intervention — effects on anxiety,
   internalising symptoms, and wellbeing in young people. Do they work, for whom, and do effects last?

2. **Interventions targeting social comparison and FOMO / approval anxiety directly.** Social-media-literacy,
   self-compassion-on-social-media, cognitive-reappraisal-of-social-feedback, or "positive social media use"
   programmes — efficacy on anxiety/social anxiety in youth.

3. **Anti-cyberbullying interventions and their mental-health outcomes.** Programmes to prevent/reduce
   cyberbullying victimisation — do they improve anxiety/internalising outcomes (not just bullying rates)?
   Recent meta-analyses preferred. *(Context: GBD analyses identify bullying victimisation as a leading driver
   of rising youth anxiety burden.)*

4. **Sleep-focused interventions for adolescent anxiety.** CBT-for-insomnia (CBT-I) and sleep-hygiene/sleep
   interventions in young people — effects on anxiety, given sleep disruption as a digital-mediated pathway.

5. **Loneliness / social-connection interventions.** Interventions to rebuild in-person connection or reduce
   loneliness in children/young people (incl. social prescribing, peer-connection, befriending) — effects on
   anxiety/internalising outcomes.

6. **Digital-citizenship / media-literacy school programmes** with mental-health (anxiety) outcomes — do they
   reduce anxiety, or only change knowledge/behaviour?

7. **Synthesis question:** Across the above, is there an established, evidence-based class of interventions that
   targets the *digital-era mechanisms* of youth anxiety — or is this still an emerging/under-evidenced frontier
   relative to generic CBT/SSRI treatment?

**Inclusion preferences:** systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and RCTs; **2018–2026** preferred; child/
adolescent samples (≤18 — exclude adult-only); high-tier peer-reviewed journals. For each study, capture sample
size, design, the *intervention* and its *target mechanism*, effect size, follow-up, and limitations. Where a
mechanism has **no** intervention evidence, say so explicitly (a documented gap is a useful finding).

**Exclude:** studies that only establish risk/causation without an intervention; adult-only samples;
non-peer-reviewed/low-tier sources.

**Output:** a synthesis organised by the seven questions, with full citations, **and an RIS export** for Zotero.
