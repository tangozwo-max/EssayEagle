## Run 5 Synthesis: Mechanism-Targeted Interventions for Youth Anxiety in the Digital Age

### Cross-domain summary first

The honest answer to the framing question — *is there an evidence-based class of mechanism-targeted digital-era interventions that rivals CBT?* — is no, not yet. What exists is a patchwork of promising but mostly small, often adult-skewed trials, with a common pattern: short-term signal, uncertain durability, and conspicuous gaps in under-18 samples with diagnosed anxiety. The strongest signals come from sleep (CBT-I) and social connection/inclusion domains, where the intervention logic is clearest and the effect sizes most consistent. Social media restriction, FOMO/social-comparison training, and cyberbullying programmes all show measurable effects, but effect sizes are small and the child/adolescent anxiety-specific evidence base is thin. Digital citizenship and media literacy programmes remain largely untested for mental health outcomes in RCT-controlled designs.

---

### Q1: Social media restriction / digital detox interventions

The most important source I found is a 2025 meta-analysis by Burnell et al. (*SSM–Mental Health*, Q1) covering 32 RCTs and 5,544 individuals. The pooled effect on subjective well-being was significant but small (g̃ = 0.17, 95% CI 0.08–0.27). Critically, **all 32 included studies used college-student or adult samples** (mean age 23); no under-18 meta-analytic pool exists. A reanalysis by Thrul et al. (2025, *Psychology of Popular Media*, Q1) clarifies that intervention length is a key moderator: studies shorter than one week show *worse* outcomes (d = –0.18), while those of one week or longer show benefit (d = +0.16), with an apparent optimum around three weeks. This is a methodologically important finding for anyone designing a youth detox protocol.

For under-18 specifically, Goldfield et al. (2026, *Journal of Affective Disorders*, Q1) ran an RCT limiting social media to 1 h/day in youth with pre-existing anxiety or depression symptoms (n = 219 completers). Restricting social media produced significantly greater reductions in loneliness compared to controls, and the conference abstract version (Davis et al., 2023, JAACAP) indicates that the same group found reductions in anxiety and FoMO. A Czech experimental study (Višvaderová, 2025) using an eight-week protocol in adolescents and young adults found significant anxiety reductions and flagged a potential yo-yo rebound at four-week follow-up — a recurrent theme across this literature. The *JAMA Network Open* cohort study by Calvert et al. (2025) evaluated a one-week detox in youth and is notable for its venue quality, though the design is observational rather than a true RCT. Marciano et al. (2024, *Pediatrics*, Q1) conducted the most rigorous narrative review of digital detox interventions and concluded that: (1) no consistent definition of "detox" exists; (2) partial reduction is more beneficial than total abstinence; (3) effects vary by gender, age, and outcome type; and (4) available evidence is insufficient for policy guidance. Several other detox studies in this corpus have clear quality problems — no peer review, implausible effect sizes, or obvious fabrication signals — and should be rejected at triage.

**For v009:** Social media restriction is a feasible, lightly evidence-based intervention for anxious youth, but the under-18 RCT evidence is sparse, the duration question is unresolved, and durability beyond the intervention period is uncertain. Frame as a promising component strategy, not a standalone treatment.

### Q2: Interventions targeting social comparison, FoMO, and approval anxiety

This is the most frontier domain. The clearest conceptual finding I found is that FoMO moderates the social-media–anxiety relationship in adolescents: Einstein et al. (2023, *Australian Journal of Psychology*, Q1) showed that social media use was associated with increased anxiety only among adolescents *high* in FoMO (n = 951, mean age 13.7), and reduced anxiety among those low in FoMO. This supports a precision framing: generic detox may miss the mechanism; targeting FoMO cognitions may be more efficient.

For actual intervention evidence, the SoSelf training (Buehler et al., 2026, *Body Image*, Q1) combined social media literacy and self-compassion in a 30-day EMA study with 26 young women aged 15–21. EMA compliance was high (91%), outcomes included reductions in social appearance anxiety and increases in body appreciation maintained at one-month follow-up. This is promising as a proof-of-concept but it is a feasibility study: n = 26, no control group, and the outcome is appearance anxiety rather than clinically significant general anxiety. Gill et al. (2018, *Journal of Adolescence*, Q1) established that self-compassion is inversely related to social anxiety in adolescents (r = –.55), mediated by fear of negative evaluation — providing mechanistic support for self-compassion targeting. However, I found no RCT of a self-compassion training programme specifically for FoMO/social-comparison anxiety in under-18 youth with clinical anxiety outcomes. The pilot by Carter et al. (2025, *Psychological Reports*) explored self-compassion deficits in relation to social media self-control failure and FoMO but is preliminary (n = 61, no intervention component). The Digital Bodies intervention (Bell et al., 2021, *British Journal of Educational Psychology*) — a brief classroom-based programme for appearance concerns — showed improved body satisfaction in 290 British adolescents at 8-week follow-up, but body satisfaction is not the same as anxiety.

**For v009:** The mechanistic case for targeting FoMO and social comparison is strong; the intervention evidence for doing so in a way that reduces anxiety in under-18 clinical samples is largely absent. This is a genuine and important evidence gap.

### Q3: Anti-cyberbullying interventions — anxiety and internalising outcomes

The key meta-analytic paper is Guzman-Holst et al. (2022, *Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry*, Q1) — 22 studies, 58,091 participants. Anti-bullying interventions produced a very small reduction in anxiety (ES = 0.08, 95% CI 0.01–0.16) and depression (ES = 0.06) at post-intervention. Critically, **bullying victimisation did not mediate the anxiety reduction** — meaning these programmes are not reducing anxiety *by* reducing bullying exposure, but through some other mechanism (possibly general social-climate effects). The intervention component most associated with internalising symptom reduction was "working with peers"; CBT techniques within anti-bullying programmes were paradoxically associated with *worse* internalising outcomes. This is an important finding: even when anti-bullying programmes work, their mental health benefits are small and mechanistically unclear.

A review in *Annual Review of Clinical Psychology* (Nickerson & Fredrick, 2025, Q1) confirmed that cyberbullying-specific prevention evidence is more limited than general bullying programmes, particularly for anxiety outcomes. A longitudinal mixed-methods study by Klettke et al. (2022, *Psych*) of a UK Year 7 cyberbullying intervention found significant reductions in victimisation and perpetration at follow-up, with mental health improving but not reaching significance. The PRISMA-compliant systematic review (Casa ñ a Mohedo et al., 2026, *Children*, Q2) across 42 studies confirmed that cybervictimisation is associated with elevated anxiety in 87% of studies, reinforcing the importance of the mechanism, even if the intervention effects are small.

**For v009:** Anti-cyberbullying programmes reduce cyberbullying behaviour modestly, but their effect on anxiety/internalising symptoms is small and not clearly mediated by victimisation reduction. They are better framed as school climate and social safety interventions than anxiety treatments.

### Q4: Sleep-focused interventions and anxiety outcomes

This is the most clinically developed mechanism-targeted domain. Multiple meta-analyses confirm CBT-I efficacy for insomnia in adolescents on sleep outcomes: Galgut et al. (2024, *Journal of Sleep Research*, Q1) across four RCTs (n = 527) found Hedges' g = 0.4 for sleep quality and g = 1.04 for insomnia symptoms; Mei et al. (2024, *Frontiers in Public Health*, Q1) across 8 RCTs (n = 599) confirmed significant improvements in insomnia, sleep onset latency, total sleep time, and sleep efficiency, with effects maintained at follow-up. A 2025 systematic review (Ewart et al., *Behavioural Sleep Medicine*, Q2) of school-based CBT-I specifically found significant improvements in subjective sleep and, importantly, **a significant effect on anxiety** (g = 0.37, p < 0.01) — making school-based sleep intervention one of the few mechanism-targeted approaches with direct anxiety outcome evidence in under-18 populations.

The critical limitation is the directionality question. Haugland et al. (2021, *European Child & Adolescent Psychiatry*) randomised 313 anxious adolescents to CBT vs waitlist and found that anxiety-focused CBT reduced insomnia and sleep onset latency within groups, but showed no significant sleep advantage vs waitlist — suggesting anxiety and sleep co-improve but the direction of therapeutic action is unclear. Lawrence-Sidebottom et al. (2024, *Digital Health*, Q2) found that in 1,219 children and adolescents using a digital mental health intervention (not sleep-specific), 77% of those with elevated sleep problems improved — and mental health improvement mediated sleep improvement. This suggests digital anxiety interventions may have secondary sleep benefits, and vice versa, but the mechanistic pathway remains entangled.

A small digital detox study (Pan et al., 2025, *JAYPS*) with 30 Chinese adolescents randomised to a device-at-bedtime intervention found significant sleep hygiene improvements maintained at five-month follow-up, but the study is too small and the journal too obscure to carry evidential weight alone.

**For v009:** CBT-I for adolescents is the most robustly evidence-based mechanism-targeted intervention in this entire domain. School-based delivery shows promise (anxiety g = 0.37). The sleep–anxiety reciprocal relationship is real, and sleep-integrated components are undersupplied in existing anxiety programmes.

### Q5: Loneliness and social connection interventions

Hunt et al. (2023, *International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health*, Q2) conducted a systematic review of social inclusion interventions for adolescent anxiety and depression (12 studies), finding a medium-to-large pooled effect (SMD = –0.62, 95% CI –1.23 to –0.01). This is a notable effect size, though the review pooled very heterogeneous interventions (social skills training, life skills, cash transfers) and the confidence interval barely excludes zero, suggesting instability. Raniti et al. (2022, *BMC Public Health*, Q1) reviewed 34 longitudinal studies of school connectedness and found protective relationships with anxiety and depression in most; two intervention studies targeting school connectedness both improved depression (via self-esteem and relationship mechanisms), though anxiety outcomes were less well-measured.

For social prescribing specifically — a model of growing UK policy relevance — Hayes et al. (2026, *medRxiv* preprint; the prospectively registered Wellbeing While Waiting trial) is the most important source. This multi-site non-randomised trial across 11 CAMHS in England (n = 558, aged 11–18 on CAMHS waiting lists) found **no significant effect on anxiety or depression symptoms** from social prescribing, but *did* find significant improvements in conduct difficulties, hyperactivity, peer problems, prosocial behaviour, and resilience. The Linking Leeds evaluation (Brettell et al., 2022, *IJERPH*, Q2) identified social connectedness and behavioural activation as mechanisms, consistent with the effect pattern seen in Hayes. A searchRxiv systematic review (Bailey & Simmons, 2022) of peer support for youth anxiety and depression found limited evidence from controlled trials, with the evidence base being described as insufficient.

**For v009:** Social inclusion/connection interventions show medium effects in meta-analytic synthesis, but the studies are heterogeneous and the most rigorous current UK trial (social prescribing in CAMHS) found no anxiety/depression effect. Social prescribing may improve broader social outcomes and resilience but is not currently an evidence-based anxiety treatment.

### Q6: Digital citizenship and media literacy programmes — anxiety outcomes

This is the least developed domain in terms of anxiety-specific outcomes. The Teesson et al. Climate Schools trials provide the most relevant data: the cluster-RCT (2020, *Lancet Digital Health*, 71 schools, 6,386 pupils) found that the combined online prevention programme reduced anxiety symptom growth over 30 months (SMD = –0.12 at 30 months), but the 72-month follow-up (2024, *Lancet Digital Health*) found **no lasting difference** in anxiety outcomes. A 2026 German RCT (Schröder et al., *Computers in Human Behavior Reports*, Q1) of a five-module digital health literacy e-learning programme for 14–21-year-olds found significant improvements in knowledge, digital health literacy, and digital competence vs controls, but did not measure anxiety outcomes. An Italian cluster-RCT of a media education programme targeting conscious mobile device use (Gui et al., 2021) showed reduced problematic smartphone use and improved general well-being in treated students, again without anxiety-specific measures. A Turkish RCT (Ünlü et al., 2025, *Children*, Q2) of a six-week mindfulness-based psychoeducational programme addressing social media burnout (n = 32) showed significant well-being improvements at 30-day follow-up, but the sample is tiny.

The OurFutures Mental Health trial (Grummitt et al., 2025, *EClinicalMedicine*, Q1) — a CBT-based school programme (n = 784, Australia) — did find a significant anxiety reduction at 3-month follow-up (β = –1.05, p = 0.024), though depression was unaffected and the trial was underpowered with substantial school dropout.

**For v009:** Digital literacy/citizenship programmes may reduce problematic use and improve general well-being, but evidence for anxiety reduction specifically is weak or absent. The more explicitly CBT-based school programmes have more convincing short-term anxiety data, though durability at 6+ years appears minimal.

### Q7: Cross-domain synthesis — is this a coherent evidence class?

No single mechanism-targeted intervention has accrued enough controlled, youth-specific, anxiety-outcome trial evidence to constitute a replicable evidence class comparable to CBT or SSRIs. What the literature reveals instead is a spectrum of maturity:

**Most developed:** Sleep-focused interventions (CBT-I), especially school-based delivery, which have multiple meta-analyses, consistent effect sizes, and direct anxiety outcome data in under-18 samples.

**Promising but under-powered:** Social media restriction (RCT evidence in youth exists but is small and adult-heavy); social inclusion (medium pooled effect but heterogeneous); FoMO/self-compassion training (mechanistic case strong, no anxiety-outcome RCTs in clinical-range youth samples).

**Largely observational or conceptual:** Digital citizenship/media literacy for anxiety; anti-cyberbullying as an anxiety treatment; existential and mindfulness-FOMO approaches.

**Critical evidence gap:** No study in this corpus directly compares a mechanism-targeted digital-era intervention against CBT or SSRI as an active control in a clinical (diagnosed anxiety disorder) youth sample. The field has not yet run that trial. The Wellcome Trust's 2021 *Nature Human Behaviour* commentary (Sebastian, Pote, & Wolpert) explicitly identifies this as the priority: identifying "active ingredients" rather than testing generic programmes, and argues for mechanism-targeted designs — but acknowledges the field has not yet produced them systematically.
