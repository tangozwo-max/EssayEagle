## 1. Prevalence & Significance
**Source:** wiki/anxiety-in-children.md

- Most common mental health disorder in children & adolescents (~6.5% globally; 1 in 12 in the US aged 3–17)
- Impacts: educational/social functioning, lifelong mental health risk, economic burden
- **Underrecognised and undertreated** — the majority of affected children do not access evidence-based care
- Youth-specific presentations: separation anxiety disorder, selective mutism
- **EBSA** (emotionally based school avoidance): anxiety-driven school non-attendance, doubled post-COVID; autistic young people at highest risk (~40% have an anxiety disorder)

## 2. Evidence-Based Treatments

### CBT — Gold standard
**Source:** wiki/cbt.md + curriculum M4 W4 P56 (not yet in wiki)

- Thoughts, feelings, behaviours are interconnected — changing negative thought patterns drives change
- Flexible, non-standardised; combines cognitive, behavioural, problem-solving techniques
- Initial phase: **psychoeducation** for child and family
- **Age adaptations** (curriculum M4 W4 P56):
  - *Under 7*: visual aids, cartoons, play, stories; parents as primary facilitators
  - *7–11*: mix of verbal/nonverbal; metaphors (e.g. "anger volcano"); parental support integral
  - *Adolescents*: autonomy emphasis; Socratic dialogue (objective empiricism); collaborative partnership; computerised CBT an option; parental involvement agreed at start
- **Reciprocal CBT formulation**: examines mutual influence of thoughts/behaviours across relational context (e.g. social anxiety within friendship group)
- SSRIs: effective short-to-medium term; long-term efficacy not proven

### Trauma-Focused CBT
**Source:** wiki/trauma-focused-cbt.md + wiki/tf-cbt.md

- Adapted for processing traumatic experiences; effective for abuse, violence, disasters
- Parent/caregiver components included in child/youth versions
- Evidence: Van Dis et al. meta-analysis — CBT for anxiety disorders effective to 12 months; beyond 12 months, effects larger for PTSD than GAD/SAD

### Psychodynamic Therapy
**Source:** curriculum M4 W4 P58 (not yet in wiki)

- Focuses on unconscious processes, early attachments, defence mechanisms
- Uses play and creative activities for younger children
- Limited RCT base; 2013 meta-analysis suggests possible "sleeper effect"
- Large NIHR RCT upcoming (CBT vs. STPP vs. specialist care for adolescent depression)

### Systemic Family Therapy
**Source:** curriculum M4 W4 P59 (not yet in wiki)

- Views child within family system; addresses relational dynamics
- Medium-to-large effect sizes; 2 out of 3 cases improve; comparable to individual therapy
- Subtypes relevant to anxiety: cognitive-behavioural family therapy, PCIT, MST

### DBT-A
**Source:** curriculum M4 W4 P60 (not yet in wiki)

- Four components: mindfulness, interpersonal effectiveness, emotion regulation, distress tolerance
- Designed for emotion dysregulation, self-harm, suicidality — **less central to primary anxiety**; relevant for complex comorbid presentations

## 3. Intervention Framework

### Continuum of Need
**Source:** wiki/targeted-early-help.md + wiki/early-intervention.md

- Universal → Selective → Indicated → Specialist
- Targeted early help: non-diagnostic, responsive, preventative — e.g. support groups for anxious-about-school young people
- Early intervention prevents escalation, produces sustained improvements, reduces need for intensive treatment

### Intervention categories for adolescents (wiki/early-intervention.md)
- **Family/individual-based**: CBT, psychotherapy, multisystemic therapy
- **School-based**: solution-focused brief therapy, interpersonal psychotherapy
- **Community-based**: creative activities, computerised CBT, environmental interventions

### School-Based Interventions
**Source:** wiki/school-based-interventions.md

- Schools as universal institutions reaching most young people
- Whole-school approach; universal, selective, indicated tiers within school setting
- Directly relevant given EBSA as a key anxiety presentation

### Digital Interventions
**Source:** wiki sources (src-m4w3-p42) + curriculum M4 W3 P42

- Computerised/online CBT with growing evidence base
- Important for access and scalability; raises questions of digital divide and therapeutic relationship

### WHO Recommendations (Universal)
**Source:** curriculum M4 W3 P39

- Social-emotional learning: emotional regulation, problem-solving, interpersonal skills, mindfulness, assertiveness, stress management
- Must reference when discussing preventive/early-intervention tier

### Stepped Care Model
**Source:** curriculum M4 W4 P55

- NHS framework for matching treatment intensity to need
- Frames how CBT, family therapy, specialist care relate to each other
