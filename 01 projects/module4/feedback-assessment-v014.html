The single most valuable insight — and it is a correction, not an edit.
The review's central premise is that the essay is "slightly over the stated limit" and that you should "cut 100–150 words". This is factually wrong. The brief sets 2,000 words +/- 10%, states "you may go beyond the word count by ±10% without penalty," and excludes the cover page, reference list, figures and tables from the count. At ~2,090 body words the essay sits inside the no-penalty band; the penalty only begins at ≥2,201. So there is ~110 words of headroom and no length pressure at all. Every "cut it to fit" recommendation rests on a premise that does not hold — which means none of the cutting is forced, and each cut must justify itself purely on clarity. Most cannot.

Why the cutting advice backfires here

Two structural facts about this assignment decide it:

  • Analysis & Evaluation is 50% of the grade. The A+ descriptor explicitly rewards "extremely insightful synthesis… material managed in an exceptionally creative way demonstrating innovation and evidence of originality of thought." The essay's distinctive voice — the paradox framing, the adjudication between competing explanations, the layered conclusion — is the originality the rubric is paying for. The review repeatedly asks to make these sentences "less rhetorical," "less lyrical," "more direct." For a generic essay that is safe advice; for a 50%-A&E rubric it trades the marked asset for blandness.
  • The reversal spine is the essay. Setup (strong evidence base + rising burden = paradox) → therapies work, even in routine care → why that is not enough (access / durability / incidence) → what could close the gap → the layered answer. The intro's epidemiology and developmental-neuroscience are the first leg of that spine (they create the paradox and the "act early" stakes), not padding. Gutting them — the review's biggest single ask — breaks the argument the FQA panel scored GREEN.

TASK 1 — Replace emotive / journalistic wording with precise academic wording.
Keep the meaning and every citation. Make exactly these changes:
- "why is the problem getting worse" → "why does population prevalence keep rising"
- "The heaviest burden falls on the minority" → "The greatest clinical burden falls on the minority"
- "The most striking evidence comes from parent-only treatment" → "The strongest evidence comes from

  so do not overclaim)
- "the evidence for that access gap is stark" → "the evidence for this access gap is well-documented"
- "even where services exist, the route in is fragile" → "even where services exist, the pathway to care
